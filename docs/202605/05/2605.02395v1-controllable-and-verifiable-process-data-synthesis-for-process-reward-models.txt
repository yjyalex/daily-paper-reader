# Controllable and Verifiable Process Data Synthesis for Process Reward Models 

Yinghui Chi and Lucien Wang *

Jilin University 

Abstract 

Process reward models (PRMs) rely on high-quality process supervision data, yet existing construction methods often provide limited con-trol over error location, error type, and trajec-tory consistency. We propose a controllable and verifiable framework for synthesizing pro-cess supervision data for PRMs. Our frame-work first constructs a correct symbolic rea-soning chain, injects a template-aware error into an intermediate step, recomputes subse-quent steps under the corrupted state, and veri-fies that the injected step is not derivable from its prefix. The resulting paired trajectories are prefix-invalid at the first error while remaining trajectory-consistent after symbolic recompu-tation, and are translated into aligned natural-language processes for PRM training and eval-uation. Experiments show that the synthesized data improve Best-of-8 reranking on logical reasoning benchmarks and transfer to mathe-matical reasoning. Step-level evaluation further shows that first-error localization remains sub-stantially more challenging than overall step classification, highlighting the need for fine-grained and verifiable process supervision. 

1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) have shown strong performance on multi-step reasoning tasks, espe-cially when they are prompted to produce inter-mediate reasoning traces (Wei et al., 2022). How-ever, final-answer accuracy alone does not reveal whether the intermediate reasoning process is reli-able. Process supervision addresses this limitation by providing step-level feedback, and has been shown to improve reasoning quality in complex set-tings (Uesato et al., 2022; Lightman et al., 2024). In this setting, process reward models (PRMs) and step-level verifiers are increasingly used for gen-eration, reranking, and search (Cobbe et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2024; Setlur et al., 2024).

The main bottleneck is data. Effective PRMs re-quire trajectories with reliable step-level signals, especially the position and validity of the first er-roneous step. Manual annotation can provide such supervision, but it is expensive to scale (Lightman et al., 2024). Automatic process annotation re-duces this cost, for example through Monte Carlo rollouts or search-based annotation (Wang et al., 2024a; Luo et al., 2024). However, such methods often provide limited control over error type, error location, and trajectory consistency. As a result, existing PRMs still struggle with first-error local-ization and fine-grained process evaluation (Zheng et al., 2025; Song et al., 2025). Symbolic reasoning systems offer a natural way to make process data more verifiable. Recent work combines LLMs with symbolic provers to construct logically grounded reasoning data (Qi et al., 2025), but these methods mainly focus on correct reason-ing chains. They do not explicitly synthesize er-roneous trajectories with controlled first-error in-jection, downstream error propagation, and prefix-level validity checking. To address this gap, we propose an automated framework for synthesizing controllable and verifi-able process supervision data for PRMs. The frame-work first constructs a verified correct symbolic reasoning chain, injects a template-aware error into an intermediate step, symbolically recomputes sub-sequent steps under the corrupted state, and verifies that the injected step is not derivable from its pre-fix. It then translates the correct and erroneous symbolic chains into aligned natural-language pro-cesses. This design enables verifiable correct and erroneous trajectory construction, controllable first-error generation with downstream recomputation, and fine-grained PRM supervision for reranking and process-level evaluation.

2 Related Work 

2.1 Process Supervision and Process Reward Models 

Process supervision evaluates intermediate reason-ing steps rather than only the final answer (Uesato et al., 2022; Lightman et al., 2024), and PRMs have become widely used for reranking and reasoning-time selection (Cobbe et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2024). Recent work extends process rewards to broader settings (Setlur et al., 2024; Khalifa et al., 2025; Zeng et al., 2025), but accurate first-error localization and fine-grained process evaluation remain difficult (Zheng et al., 2025; Song et al., 2025). 

2.2 Process Data Construction 

Process data are typically obtained through manual annotation, sampling-based estimation, or direct synthesis. Manual annotation is reliable but ex-pensive (Lightman et al., 2024), while rollout- or search-based methods reduce cost but provide lim-ited control over error type, position, and difficulty (Wang et al., 2024a; Luo et al., 2024). Direct syn-thesis can be more scalable (Zelikman et al., 2022; Zeng et al., 2025), but existing approaches gener-ally do not explicitly enforce controllable first-error generation or trajectory-level consistency of erro-neous reasoning. 

2.3 LLMs with Symbolic Systems 

LLM-based symbolic data construction has re-cently been explored for reasoning generation and verification. ProverGen (Qi et al., 2025), for exam-ple, combines LLMs with symbolic provers to build logically grounded reasoning data. However, this line of work is primarily aimed at generating cor-rect reasoning chains , whereas our work focuses on synthesizing erroneous trajectories with con-trolled first-error injection, symbolic verification, and downstream error propagation. 

3 Method 

Figure 1 shows our controllable and verifiable pro-cess data synthesis framework for PRM supervi-sion. Given a target conclusion G, the framework constructs a correct reasoning chain, synthesizes an erroneous chain with a verified first invalid step, and finally translates both chains into aligned natural-language processes. We represent each in-stance as (G, C +, C −, k, e ), where C+ and C−

denote the correct and erroneous chains, k is the first-error step, and e is the error type. Our correct-chain construction follows the general LLM-symbolic prover pipeline of ProverGen (Qi et al., 2025), which uses symbolic verification to obtain logically grounded reasoning structures. Un-like ProverGen, which primarily constructs verified correct chains, our framework focuses on erroneous process supervision by injecting a template-aware first error, recomputing downstream steps under the corrupted state, and filtering pseudo-errors through prefix non-derivability checking. 

3.1 Correct Chain Synthesis 

We first generate a correct symbolic reasoning chain from a rule template pool. Starting from the target conclusion, we recursively expand inter-mediate goals and use a symbolic prover to verify whether each step is derivable from the current premises. Only truth-consistent structures are re-tained, yielding a verifiable forward chain from base facts to the target. 

3.2 Error Injection and Symbolic Re-computation 

Given a verified correct chain C+ = ( s1, . . . , s T ),we construct an erroneous counterpart by first se-lecting an intermediate step k as the candidate first-error position. The perturbation type e is con-strained by the rule template instantiated at step 

k, ensuring that the injected error is structurally compatible with the local inference pattern rather than sampled from a global error distribution. De-pending on the template, the perturbation may alter the inferred conclusion, misuse the rule direction, or corrupt the local factual state. The corrupted conclusion produced at step k is then treated as part of the subsequent symbolic state. We recompute all downstream steps under this updated state with the symbolic prover. The resulting erroneous chain C− preserves the correct prefix before step k, replaces sk with a corrupted step, and recomputes all subsequent steps from the corrupted state. This construction ensures that later steps remain consistent with the corrupted prefix, avoiding unrealistic trajectories formed by isolated label flipping. To verify that the injected step is indeed the first invalid step, we apply error-type-specific checks. For non-structural errors, we perform a prefix non-derivability check: the corrupted conclusion ˜sk

is tested against the original prefix (s1, . . . , s k−1),Figure 1: Overview of the proposed controllable and verifiable process data synthesis framework for PRM supervision. 

and the sample is rejected if the conclusion is still derivable from that prefix. For structural errors, we instead verify that the corrupted step violates the intended rule direction, dependency order, or acyclic trajectory constraints, since the local truth values may still be compatible with the instantiated rule. Detailed error type definitions and their dis-tribution in the 20k training set are provided in the appendix. 

3.3 Natural-Language Translation 

Finally, we convert the symbolic trajectories into natural-language reasoning processes. To preserve comparability between the correct and erroneous chains, both translations are conditioned on the same background context, entity realizations, and predicate mappings. This shared semantic ground-ing ensures that the two processes differ primarily in their reasoning behavior rather than in unrelated surface realizations. The translation is performed in a step-aligned man-ner: each symbolic step is mapped to a correspond-ing natural-language inference step, and the cor-rect and erroneous trajectories maintain matched positions wherever possible. The final instance therefore contains a paired correct and erroneous process, the first-error position, and the associated error type, providing supervision signals for both PRM training and fine-grained process evaluation.

We evaluate our method on three aspects: Best-of-K reranking for logical reasoning, transfer to mathematical reasoning, and step-level evaluation on our synthesized data. We use Llama-3.1-8B (Grattafiori et al., 2024) and Qwen-2.5-7B (Qwen Team, 2024) as the main model families. Unless otherwise noted, our PRMs are trained on 20k syn-thesized process-supervision instances. 4.5 Discussion 

Overall, the results indicate that controllable and verifiable process supervision can improve PRM-based reranking, transfer to more open-ended rea-soning tasks, and provide a useful diagnostic set-ting for first-error localization. These findings sup-port constructing erroneous trajectories that are prefix-invalid and downstream-consistent rather than arbitrary local perturbations. 

5 Limitations 

While our framework improves controllability and verifiability, it has several limitations. First, the diversity of synthesized errors is still limited by the predefined templates and may not fully cover the range of errors in open-ended LLM reasoning. In addition, the current framework is built on sym-bolic reasoning structures, which makes it most nat-urally applicable to domains with relatively explicit logical form. Finally, while the current results are encouraging, evaluating the framework on broader tasks and model families would further strengthen its generality.

C Best-of-8 Generation and Evaluation Protocol 

For each test instance, we pair the input problem with eight sampled candidate solutions from the corresponding base model. During reranking, the PRM scores every candidate trajectory step by step. We aggregate step-level positive scores with the 

min rule, which makes the trajectory score sensitive to the weakest step in the reasoning chain. The highest-scoring trajectory is selected as the final prediction. We also report two reference selectors. Major-ity@8 selects the answer that appears most fre-quently among the eight sampled candidates. It provides a simple self-consistency baseline that does not use step-level supervision. Oracle@8 

checks whether the candidate pool contains at least one correct solution, and therefore estimates the upper bound imposed by the sampled candidates. The gap between our PRM and Oracle@8 reflects remaining headroom in step-level scoring and tra-jectory selection.

G Error Type Definitions and Minimal Examples 

This section presents the error taxonomy used by our synthesis framework in a more structured form. We define errors from a truth-state satisfaction per-spective. A non-structural error either produces an assignment that violates the instantiated rule or current symbolic state, or performs a local update that is not licensed by the active rule. By contrast, a structural error may be locally truth-satisfying, but it violates the rule direction, the dependency order, or the well-formedness of the reasoning trajectory. This distinction matters for PRM supervision: a step may look locally plausible while still being invalid because the prefix does not support it. We use ASCII operators for readability: and , or ,

xor , and -> . The examples below are minimal symbolic cases designed to clarify the boundary between categories. 

Group Main criterion 

Truth-state errors The corrupted step either violates the local truth condition or current state, or performs an unauthorized local update. Structural errors The local conclusion may be truth-compatible, but the transition uses an in-valid rule direction or an unsupported de-pendency.

I Prompt Templates 

This section summarizes the prompts used in PRM training, Best-of-8 reranking, and natural-language translation. We present cleaned versions of the implementation prompts in a compact dialogue for-mat. Placeholders such as {goal} , {question} ,and {current_step} are filled automatically by the pipeline. Control fields such as step status and error type are used only during offline data synthe-sis and annotation; they are not included as label-leaking text in the PRM input. The prompts are grouped by function rather than by source file for readability. 

I.1 Step-Level PRM Prompts Training prompt. During fine-tuning, each sym-bolic trajectory is expanded into step-level in-stances. The model receives the target goal, initial information, previous steps, and the current step, and is trained to generate one of two label words. In the main experiments, the label words are true 

and false .

PRM training prompt 

User Task: Determine whether the current reasoning step is logically correct. 

Goal: {goal} 

Initial Information: 

{initial_information} 

Previous Steps: {previous_steps} 

Current Step: {current_step} 

Answer: 

Assistant true or false 

Each step is serialized with its supporting facts, the applied rule, and the resulting conclusion. An auxil-iary reasoning field can also be appended, though it is not required in the main experiments. The same dialogue format is used for both correct and erro-neous chains; the label is determined by whether the current step is valid under its prefix. 

Best-of-8 scoring prompt. At evaluation time, each sampled candidate solution is split into steps. For the t-th step, the prompt contains the original question, the candidate prefix before that step, and the current step. The PRM scores the probability of the positive label word, and the final trajectory score is obtained by aggregating step-level scores with the min rule. 

Best-of-8 step scoring prompt  

> User

Task: Determine whether the current reasoning step is logically correct. 

Question: {question} 

Previous Steps: {previous_steps} 

Current Step: {current_step} 

Answer:  

> Assistant

true or false 

This prompt differs from the training prompt mainly in its context field: it uses the original prob-lem question instead of the translated symbolic goal. The previous steps are the candidate’s own generated prefix, so the judge evaluates whether the current step remains valid in that candidate-specific context. 

I.2 Natural-Language Translation Prompts 

The translation pipeline converts symbolic PRM instances into aligned natural-language data. All stages share the same entity name, background story, and predicate mapping for the correct and er-roneous chains. This shared semantic space keeps paired trajectories comparable: their differences should reflect reasoning discrepancies rather than unrelated wording changes. 

Background generation. The first stage creates a short scenario for the sampled entity. This sce-nario provides a coherent semantic context for all predicates in the same instance. 

Background generation  

> System

Generate a brief background story for a person. The story should be natural, concise, and provide context for logical reasoning about the person’s attributes and actions. Output only the story.  

> User

Generate a background story for some-one named {name} . 

> Assistant

{background_story} 

Predicate mapping. The second stage maps fact symbols to natural-language predicates. For each symbol, the model must produce a predicate name, a positive realization, and a negative realization. This allows the translator to render truth values consistently across the goal, base facts, rules, and both chains. 

Predicate mapping prompt  

> System

Step-level translation and refinement. The final stage translates each symbolic step into a natural-language reasoning step. For correct steps, the text states why the conclusion follows from the facts and rule. For erroneous steps, the translator may produce an explanatory annotation of the process problem, but the text used as PRM input is further naturalized to avoid revealing the label. Control fields such as step status and error type guide the transla-tor, but they are not included in the final PRM input. For error-chain steps from the first invalid step on-ward, the naturalized text avoids explicit markers such as “error”, “mistake”, “wrong”, “invalid”, and similar meta-commentary. This preserves the in-tended propositions while keeping the generated process usable for PRM training.
