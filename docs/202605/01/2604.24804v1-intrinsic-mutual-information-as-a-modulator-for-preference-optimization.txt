Abstract 

Offline preference optimization methods, such as Direct Preference Optimization (DPO), of-fer significant advantages in aligning Large Language Models (LLMs) with human values. However, achieving optimal performance with these methods typically involves additional hy-perparameter tuning, resulting in substantial time overhead. Although prior work has pro-posed a range of improvements, these meth-ods remain limited in effectiveness and have not fully eliminated reliance on hyperparam-eter tuning. In this work, we propose RMiPO ,a lightweight and efficient framework for of-fline preference optimization. RMiPO leverages intrinsic Response-level Mutual information for Preference Optimization with hyperparam-eter modulation, dynamically decoupling pref-erence contributions at negligible additional computational cost. Extensive experimental results demonstrate that RMiPO achieves con-sistently superior performance over existing methods while reducing training overhead by more than 15%. Our code is available at 

https://github.com/liavonpenn/rmipo .

1 Introduction 

Large Language Models (LLMs) demonstrate re-markable generation and comprehension capa-bilities across diverse NLP tasks (Llama, 2025; DeepSeek, 2025; OpenAI, 2025). However, when faced with complex tasks such as mathematical analysis and logical reasoning, LLMs may take incorrect reasoning paths or generate inconsistent conclusions. To address this issue, researchers have proposed Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022). This ap-proach fine-tunes pretrained LLMs using carefully curated human preference data, improving their rea-soning fidelity and alignment with human values. To mitigate these limitations while preserv-ing alignment quality, researchers have introduced offline preference optimization methods such as DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023), IPO (Azar et al., 2024), and KTO (Ethayarajh et al., 2024). 3 Methodology 

In this section, we address two core design ques-tions: (1) Which hyperparameters necessitate adap-tive adjustment, and (2) how to design effective mechanisms for such adaptation. §3.1 presents two key insights, and in §3.2 we introduce RMiPO , a novel offline preference optimization framework that effectively resolves both of these challenges. 

3.1 Motivation and Key Insights 

The performance of offline preference optimization depends on hyperparameter choice, particularly the trade-off between policy regularization and reward scaling. Through extensive experiments and theo-retical analysis, we have distilled two key insights. 

Insight I . In offline preference optimization, opti-mal performance is primarily determined by γ. As shown in Appendix Table 4, the offline preference learning paradigm involves more than two hyper-parameters, but its core remains β, which scales reward differences, and γ, which controls reward margins. We fine-tuned Mistral-7B-Base and con-ducted sensitivity analyses on MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2023). Although training curves and in-domain validation are similar across configurations, MT-Bench scores vary sharply (Figure 2). In DPO, except for β = 0 .01 , which performs relatively well but is not a universal solution (Wu et al., 2025), all other β settings lead to substantial performance degradation, in some cases falling even below the SFT initialization baseline. In contrast, in SimPO, adjusting either β or γ improves performance, with 

γ exerting an especially pronounced positive effect. While incorporating γ into DPO yields negligi-ble improvements (Meng et al., 2024), it is indis-pensable for SimPO, as its removal degrades per-formance. 3.2 Proposed Framework: RMiPO 

These insights establish two principles: treating 

γ as the key adaptive variable and updating it via mutual information differences. To this end, we propose RMiPO , a novel optimization framework. 

Overview . Figure 4 illustrates the RMiPO train-ing workflow. Given a prompt x and a preference pair (yw, y l), the policy model πθ computes point-wise mutual information for each response, denoted as PMI( y|x). Unlike the raw log-likelihood dif-ference, which entangles semantic relevance with response priors, the PMI difference ∆pmi more directly disentangles conditional relevance from marginal popularity. This signal (Step ❸) thus en-ables reliable and efficient adaptation of the reward margin γ and offers advantages over prior work. Details are provided in the following sections. 

Objective . In offline preference optimization, the policy πθ increases ∆R by maximizing the log-likelihood ratio between preferred and less-preferred responses, defining the gradient direc-tion for preference learning. The reward margin 

γ gates updates based on semantic separability: samples with ∆R > γ are suppressed, others con-tribute. To this end, RMiPO employs the pointwise mutual information difference, ∆pmi( x, y w, y l) = PMI( yw|x) − PMI( yl|x), as an intrinsic metric for semantic separability. This signal eliminates inter-ference from non-semantic factors, such as prior frequencies. Building on this, RMiPO introduces a key design: the reward margin γ is negatively correlated with ∆pmi . Under this mechanism, semantically distinct pairs (high ∆pmi ) receive smaller margins to promote saturation and reduce redundancy. Conversely, ambiguous samples (low 

∆pmi ) retain larger margins to sustain gradients on semantic differences at the decision boundary. As shown in Fig. 3, in early training the policy πθ

lacks reliable preference discrimination, resulting in an effectively ungated margin regime ( ∆R < γ )where most samples remain gradient-active. As learning progresses, the margin γ progressively re-allocates gradients, yielding a smooth transition from coarse discrimination to fine-grained align-ment. To model this nonlinear dependency, we define the dynamic margin as a rectified exponen-tial decay of the pointwise mutual information dif-ference: γ(x, y w, y l) = γmin + ( γmax − γmin ) ·

exp( − max(0 , ∆pmi)) . The rectification intro-duces asymmetry: when ∆pmi ≤ 0, the margin is fixed at γmax , enforcing a strong constraint that con-tinuously penalizes inverted semantic preferences; otherwise, the margin decays exponentially with increasing confidence. This design preserves opti-mization signals in low- and medium-confidence re-gions while preventing premature margin collapse. Moreover, inspired by SimPO (Meng et al., 2024), we incorporate length normalization into the reward signal to further stabilize training dynamics. Notably, as shown in the Appendix Table 5, even in settings without length normalization, our method remains effective and consistently improves perfor-mance. 5 Related Work 

Preference Optimization for LLM . Preference optimization is a key technique for aligning LLMs with human values. This paradigm was originally proposed by (Ouyang et al., 2022) and employs proximal policy optimization for online training. However, the high complexity of online algorithms has driven researchers to shift toward more effi-cient offline methods. Offline preference optimiza-tion (Park et al., 2024; Rafailov et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Azar et al., 2024; Ethayarajh et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024; Zeng et al., 2024; Amini et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2025a,c) directly leverages paired preference data to guide policies toward preferred behaviors. Building on this line of work, (Meng et al., 2024; Hong et al., 2024) proposed an approach that does not require a reference model. Despite their effectiveness, these methods require extensive hyperparameter tuning. 

Efficiency Improvement in DPO . Unlike prior work on data filtering (Muldrew et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2025; Corrado et al., 2025), this study focuses on minimizing hyperpa-rameter tuning overhead in DPO and its variants. Existing research has explored several strategies: (Wu et al., 2024) and (Lee et al., 2025) dynamically adjust hyperparameters via KL regularization to ac-count for instance-level preference intensity; (Wu et al., 2025; Sun et al., 2025) introduces an implicit reference model, reducing hyperparameters that re-quire tuning; and (Xiao et al., 2025b) proposes the first parameter-free preference optimization ob-jective based on perplexity. While these methods demonstrate success, they remain limited in effec-tiveness. To address this, we propose RMiPO , an adaptive hyperparameter tuning mechanism based on intrinsic mutual information that improves align-ment while reducing optimization overhead. 

6 Conclusion 

In this paper, we propose RMiPO , a lightweight and efficient offline preference optimization framework. Its core innovation is an adaptive modulation mech-anism based on intrinsic response-level mutual in-formation. This mechanism dynamically decou-ples the contribution of different samples to the overall preference signal, enabling finer-grained alignment control. Theoretical analysis shows that this mutual information–driven strategy offers strong interpretability, establishing a new princi-pled paradigm for sample weighting in preference optimization. Extensive experiments demonstrate that RMiPO achieves superior alignment gains with lower training costs and favorable convergence. 

Limitations 

The core advantage of RMiPO lies in its ability to obviate the manual tuning of task-sensitive hyper-parameters, rather than reducing the number of tunable parameters. This property substantially en-hances the practicality of preference optimization algorithms. Nevertheless, RMiPO still exhibits sev-eral limitations that warrant further investigation. 

Sensitivity . While γ is the primary driver of adap-tivity, its efficacy presupposes that β lies within a reasonable range, as typically ensured by stan-dard model-scaling practices. Although extreme β

values destabilize all preference-optimization base-lines, RMiPO exhibits greater robustness, with per-formance degrading more gracefully than static-margin methods. By dynamically adjusting the reward margin γ (and its bounds) as a function of 

β, the proposed exponential decay mechanism par-tially mitigates mis-scaled preference signals and reduces performance degradation under suboptimal settings. Although RMiPO cannot compensate for arbitrarily ill-chosen β, its adaptive margin con-sistently offers a safety buffer over fixed-margin baselines. The coupled dynamics between β and 

γ therefore warrant further study in the context of optimization stability and generalization. 

Scalability . Recent studies suggest increasing policy optimization iterations can further improve instruction-following performance. However, due to computational constraints, this work, like most existing studies, does not conduct extensive valida-tion under large-scale regimes. Our experiments focus on billion-parameter models, which suffice to elucidate RMiPO ’s key mechanisms and provide empirical support for its effectiveness. The gener-alization of RMiPO to ten-billion-parameter models remains to be systematically evaluated. 

Adaptability . This work focuses on offline prefer-ence optimization with fixed datasets, which lim-its adaptation to dynamic environments. Extend-ing RMiPO to an online learning framework with real-time interaction feedback could improve its applicability to scenarios such as human–computer interaction and autonomous agents, for example by using an online oracle to provide preferences and intrinsic mutual information to guide exploration. 

Ethical Considerations 

This work proposes a lightweight framework for LLM alignment. The study does not involve hu-man participants or personal or sensitive data, nor does it enable malicious use; therefore, it raises no ethical concerns. The proposed method aims to improve the robustness and efficiency of value alignment, supporting the reliable deployment of large language models. Furthermore, LLM-based assistants were employed solely for linguistic re-finement, with the authors explicitly retaining full responsibility for the research design, technical content, and final conclusions.

A Revisiting Baseline Methods 

This section revisits SOTA baseline methods for offline preference optimization by examining the underlying design motivations and the functional roles of hyperparameters in their loss formulations. Based on this analysis, we summarize and contrast the resulting optimization objectives in Table 4. • DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023): By introducing a difference term of log-likelihood ratios, DPO implicitly models the reward function, thereby avoiding reliance on an explicit reward model in RLHF. This method introduces a tunable hyperparameter β to control the deviation of the policy model from the reference model, enabling a controllable trade-off between pref-erence alignment and policy updates. • SLiC (Zhao et al., 2023): The optimization objective of SLiC incorporates a supervised fine-tuning (SFT) term and is governed by two hyperparameters: the margin parameter δ ,which sets the boundary of the ranking loss, and the weighting coefficient λ , which bal-ances the contribution of the SFT term within the overall objective. • IPO (Azar et al., 2024): IPO transforms pref-erence learning into a regression problem with a squared loss by defining a surrogate reward function, thereby avoiding the instability as-sociated with reinforcement learning training. This approach involves a hyperparameter β

that controls the magnitude of the reward sig-nal. • CPO (Xu et al., 2024): CPO directly uses the log-likelihood as the reward signal and jointly optimizes it with a SFT term. This method introduces two hyperparameters: β , which scales the log-probability term, and λ , which adjusts the weight of the SFT term in the joint objective. • KTO (Ethayarajh et al., 2024): KTO learns from unpaired preference data, with its opti-mization objective governed by three hyper-parameters: β , which controls the degree of deviation of the policy model from the refer-ence model, and λw and λl , which weight the preference terms corresponding to win-ning and losing responses, respectively. • SimPO (Meng et al., 2024): SimPO does not rely on a reference model and is trained by optimizing a length-regularized objective over response pairs, with its objective function gov-erned by two hyperparameters: β , which scales the log-probability term, and γ , which adjusts the reward margin. A review of the above paradigms reveals that hy-perparameters are ubiquitous across different meth-ods; as demonstrated in prior studies, their tuning is not only unavoidable but also often incurs sub-stantial computational overhead. C Implementation Details 

Dataset : In this work, the training of the policy model primarily relies on two preference datasets: UltraFeedback-Binarized (Cui et al., 2024) and Anthropic-HH (Bai et al., 2022). • UltraFeedback-Binarized : The original Ul-traFeedback dataset contains 64,000 prompts, each with four responses from various open-and closed-source language models. All re-sponses are rated by GPT-4 based on overall quality (overall_score). In the UltraFeedback-Binarized version, a preference pair is formed by the highest-scoring response (the “pre-ferred” response) and one randomly selected response from the remaining three (the “less-preferred” response). Policy models trained on this dataset are evaluated on instruction-following benchmarks and downstream tasks. • Anthropic-HH : Anthropic-HH is a human-labeled dialogue preference dataset compris-ing two subsets, focusing on “helpfulness” and “harmlessness.” The dataset originates from manually constructed and red-teamed di-alogue samples, designed to study how crowd-sourced annotators perform red-team attacks and which strategies are effective. Policy mod-els trained on Anthropic-HH are primarily used for safety alignment evaluation. 

Training : RMiPO is designed for simplicity and strong performance, with the full procedure pro-vided in Algorithm 1. Its core lies in leveraging response-level PMI for hyperparameter adaptation. Specifically, πθ(y) denotes the prior popularity of response y, defined as the joint probability of the sequence: πθ(y) = Q|y| 

> t=1

πθ(yt | y<t ). Unlike 

πθ(y | x), this formulation removes dependence on the prompt x, enabling explicit decoupling between response priors and semantic relevance. For probability estimation, πθ(y) is computed exactly over the full sequence without trunca-tion or approximation, ensuring consistency with information-theoretic definitions. For datasets where prompts and responses are concatenated, we apply a lightweight string-matching heuristic to ex-tract y. Although this may introduce minor bound-ary inaccuracies, it preserves intended decoupling. Consequently, ∆pmi serves as an intrinsic measure of semantic separability, mitigating bias from non-semantic factors such as high-frequency tokens. All models are trained under a unified setup with 

β = 2 .0, γmin = 0 .3, and γmax = 1 .6, following the search space of SimPO. During SFT, we use a learning rate of 2 × 10 −5; across both SFT and preference optimization, we adopt a batch size of 128, a maximum sequence length of 2048, and train for one epoch using Adam with cosine annealing. All experiments are conducted on four 80GB A800 GPUs. Baselines follow the same search protocol as SimPO: method-specific hyperparameters are de-tailed in Table 4, and learning rates are tuned within 

[3 × 10 −7, 1 × 10 −6]. In contrast, RMiPO (and Sim-PER) require tuning only the learning rate, without introducing additional hyperparameters. 

Evaluation : Evaluation on AlpacaEval 2 adopts the same hyperparameter settings as SimPO: decod-ing temperatures are set to 0.7 and 0.5 for Mistral-Base and Mistral-Instruct, respectively, while both Llama-3 variants use a temperature of 0.9. Evalu-ation on MT-Bench follows the official decoding configuration, which specifies task-specific sam-pling temperatures. To systematically examine the impact of preference optimization methods on downstream task performance, we evaluate mod-els trained with each method on the standard tasks included in the Hugging Face Open LLM Leader-board, strictly following established evaluation pro-tocols. For safety evaluation, we assess models trained on the Anthropic HH dataset using its test set as the benchmark, measuring the probability that the model generates preferred responses over less-preferred ones.

Space and Time Complexity Analysis . To evalu-ate the practicality of RMiPO and baseline methods, we analyze space consumption (GPU memory) and time efficiency (computational throughput) under identical hardware settings, parallelization strate-gies, and model architectures. 

Space complexity . GPU memory during training is dominated by model parameters, optimizer states, gradients, and activations. DPO-based methods (e.g., DPO, β-DPO, ϵ-DPO) require an additional frozen reference model πref to compute the KL constraint. Although it does not require optimizer states, its parameters still occupy substantial mem-ory, leading to a peak utilization of 82%. In con-trast, SimPO, SimPER, and RMiPO eliminate the ref-erence model entirely. As a result, RMiPO reduces peak memory usage to 62% (an approximately 20% absolute reduction), enabling larger batch sizes or longer sequences and mitigating memory and com-munication bottlenecks in distributed training. 

Time complexity . Per-step runtime is determined by the number of forward and backward passes. DPO-style methods incur additional latency due to an extra forward pass through πref , although this overhead is limited because no gradients are com-puted. SimPO and SimPER require only a single forward and backward pass, achieving the highest efficiency. In contrast, α-DPO introduces complex dynamic offsets and nonlinear gradient operations, resulting in the highest per-step latency. RMiPO has a runtime of O(T ) + ∆ , where ∆ arises from computing the PMI-based margin term γ(x, y w, y l)

with lightweight logit-level operations. Despite this small overhead, RMiPO converges faster and achieves better alignment. Compared with DPO, which incurs an additional 30%–50% memory overhead, RMiPO maintains a lower memory footprint via its reference-free de-sign while introducing only minimal computational overhead, yielding a favorable trade-off between efficiency, scalability, and alignment performance. 

Mechanism analysis . This section provides ad-ditional theoretical clarification and empirical vali-dation for the exponential scheduling mechanism and constraint bounds adopted in RMiPO . We aim to further substantiate that these design choices are grounded in principled considerations rather than heuristic tuning. Preference optimization is inherently conducted in the log-probability space. Therefore, an exponential mapping naturally aligns with the log-ratio structure of the objective function. This alignment ensures that the adaptive scaling re-mains geometrically consistent with the additive formulation in the log domain. More specifically, the exponential transformation preserves the rela-tive ordering of preference signals while enabling controlled amplification of marginal differences. Compared with alternative mappings (e.g., linear or cosine), it provides a more balanced modulation be-havior: in high-confidence regions, it avoids under-modulation and preserves discriminative gradients; in low-confidence regions, it suppresses excessive scaling and prevents noisy updates. As a result, the exponential schedule facilitates a smoother and more stable gradient flow throughout training. The parameters γmin and γmax are introduced as stabil-ity constraints rather than task-specific hyperparam-eters. Their primary role is to restrict the adaptive scaling within a safe region. These bounds serve two key purposes: preventing gradient explosion during early training stages or under noisy PMI es-timation, and avoiding the dominance of marginal scaling over the main optimization objective. From a regularization perspective, the constraint interval enforces robustness across different random seeds and model architectures, ensuring consistent con-vergence behavior.
