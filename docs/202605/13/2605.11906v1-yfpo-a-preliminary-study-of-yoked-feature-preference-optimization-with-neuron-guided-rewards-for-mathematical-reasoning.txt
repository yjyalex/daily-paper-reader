# YFPO: A Preliminary Study of Yoked Feature Preference Optimization with Neuron-Guided Rewards for Mathematical Reasoning 

Yifan Le *

Zhejiang University 

leyifan@zju.edu.cn 

Abstract 

Preference optimization has become an impor-tant post-training paradigm for improving the reasoning abilities of large language models. Existing methods typically rely on externally constructed preference data, using preferred and dispreferred responses as sample-level su-pervision. However, such external signals rarely make explicit use of capability-related information contained in the model’s internal representations. For mathematical reasoning, certain neuron groups may exhibit activation patterns associated with mathematical knowl-edge, symbolic manipulation, or logical rea-soning. Similar to reflexive behavioral signals, these internal activations may provide a coarse indication of whether the model is engaging math-related capabilities.We introduce YFPO ,short for Yoked Feature Preference Optimiza-tion , a preliminary neuron-guided preference optimization framework for mathematical rea-soning. YFPO first uses AttnLRP to identify math-related neurons, and then constructs an auxiliary reward from their activation margin between preferred and dispreferred responses. This design augments external preference learn-ing with internal neuron-level signals. We con-duct preliminary experiments on a small-scale language model using GSM8K as the main benchmark. Results suggest that neuron-level signals can interact with preference optimiza-tion and occasionally improve reasoning per-formance, offering a promising direction for more fine-grained and interpretable reasoning-oriented post-training. 

1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated strong capabilities in mathematical reasoning, com-

plex problem solving, and instruction following, but these abilities often need to be further elicited and aligned through post-training. Supervised fine-tuning teaches models task formats and basic re-sponse patterns, while preference optimization im-proves model behavior by comparing the quality of different responses and encouraging outputs that better match human preferences or task ob-jectives. Recent methods, ranging from PPO-based RLHF (Schulman et al., 2017; Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022) to direct preference optimiza-tion algorithms such as DPO, IPO, KTO, ORPO, and SimPO (Rafailov et al., 2023; Azar et al., 2023; Ethayarajh et al., 2024; Hong et al., 2024; Meng et al., 2024), have made preference optimization an important route for post-training LLMs. In mathematical reasoning, preference optimization and reinforcement learning can use final-answer correctness, process supervision, verifiers, or cho-sen/rejected responses to encourage more reliable reasoning traces and final answers (Cobbe et al., 2021; Lightman et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2025). However, existing preference optimization meth-ods primarily rely on supervision signals con-structed outside the model. Whether the signal comes from human preferences, reward models, rule-based verifiers, or answer-based preference pairs, it essentially tells the model which output is better. Such sample-level supervision is effec-tive, but it usually does not explicitly consider what happens inside the model when these outputs are generated. In other words, the model is optimized to prefer a chosen response, while the training ob-jective rarely uses internal computational signals associated with a specific capability. At the same time, a growing body of inter-pretability work suggests that LLMs contain func-tional structure at the neuron or circuit level. Prior studies have identified neurons or neuron groups associated with factual knowledge, language behav-ior, task patterns, or reasoning processes (Dai et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2024). Multilingual neuron stud-ies show that different languages can correspond to different activation patterns, while relevance-based analysis and intervention further suggest that acti-vation correlation alone is not sufficient to establish functional importance. These findings imply that neuron-level internal signals may not only help ex-plain model behavior, but may also provide more fine-grained guidance for training. Motivated by this observation, we study a natu-ral question: if a model contains neuron-level sig-nals associated with mathematical reasoning, can these signals be introduced into preference opti-mization as a complement to external preference signals? We do not assume that the selected neu-rons fully characterize mathematical ability. In-stead, we treat them as internal statistical signals associated with mathematical reasoning behavior. Preference optimization naturally contains a cho-sen/rejected comparison structure, which provides a convenient interface for comparing whether high-quality and low-quality reasoning responses differ in their internal neuron signals. This paper explores whether math-related neuron activations can inter-act with external preference data and influence the preference learning process. To this end, we propose YFPO, a neuron-guided preference optimization framework for mathemati-cal reasoning. YFPO first identifies neurons associ-ated with mathematical reasoning, then constructs an auxiliary reward from the activations of these neurons during preference optimization. Unlike standard DPO-style methods that mainly use ex-ternal chosen/rejected responses, YFPO introduces internal capability-related signals in addition to sample-level preference supervision. Concretely, YFPO does not replace the original preference opti-mization objective. Instead, it adds a neuron reward 2as an auxiliary term so that the model is trained under both external preference supervision and in-ternal math-related guidance. We position YFPO as a preliminary study of neuron-guided preference optimization, with a fo-cus on the interaction between internal neuron sig-nals and preference learning objectives. Our goal is not to claim that neuron signals can replace ex-ternal preference data, but to explore whether such internal signals can provide additional informa-tion for reasoning-oriented preference optimiza-tion. We conduct preliminary experiments on a small-scale language model using mathematical preference data, and analyze final task accuracy, neuron reward trends, and chosen-rejected reward margins. Figure 1 provides an overview of the proposed framework. YFPO consists of two stages. In the offline stage, we use AttnLRP to estimate neuron-level relevance on mathematical reasoning samples and select a fixed set of top-K math-related neu-rons. In the training stage, these selected neurons are accessed through forward hooks to compute activation scores for preferred and dispreferred responses. The resulting neuron reward margin is then combined with the DPO objective, allow-ing external preference supervision and internal neuron-level signals to jointly shape the optimiza-tion process. Our main contributions are threefold. First, we formulate neuron-guided preference optimization as a problem, exploring whether internal math-related neuron signals can complement external preference data. Second, we propose YFPO, which converts math-related neuron activations into an auxiliary reward and combines it with a DPO-style preference objective. Third, we conduct prelim-inary experiments in a small-scale mathematical reasoning setting and analyze how neuron rewards interact with preference optimization, providing an initial step toward more fine-grained and inter-pretable reasoning-oriented post-training. 

2 Related Work 

Preference optimization. Preference optimiza-tion has become a central technique in LLM post-training. Early RLHF pipelines typically rely on reward models and PPO-style reinforcement learn-ing to improve model outputs according to hu-man preferences (Schulman et al., 2017; Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022). Although ef-fective, these methods involve complex training pipelines and require substantial compute and hy-perparameter tuning. To simplify this process, re-cent work has proposed direct preference optimiza-tion methods such as DPO, IPO, KTO, ORPO, and SimPO (Rafailov et al., 2023; Azar et al., 2023; Ethayarajh et al., 2024; Hong et al., 2024; Meng et al., 2024). These methods usually construct sample-level objectives from chosen/rejected re-sponses or preference-like labels, without explicitly training a separate reward model. Overall, exist-ing preference optimization methods mainly use externally constructed preference data to tell the model which answer is better. In contrast, our work studies whether internal neuron signals associated with mathematical reasoning can serve as auxiliary guidance during preference optimization. 

Preference optimization for reasoning. Pref-erence optimization and reinforcement learning have also been widely applied to improve math-ematical reasoning. Compared with general align-ment tasks, mathematical reasoning often provides clearer feedback sources, such as final-answer cor-rectness, process correctness, or verifier-based vali-dation (Cobbe et al., 2021; Lightman et al., 2023). As a result, reasoning-oriented post-training meth-ods often use outcome rewards, process rewards, automatic verifiers, or preference data to optimize model policies. For example, GRPO in DeepSeek-Math and DAPO show that reinforcement-learning-style post-training can substantially affect complex reasoning behavior (Shao et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2025). However, most of these methods still de-rive optimization signals from outside the model and rarely explicitly consider which internal com-putational components are invoked during reason-ing. YFPO explores a complementary direction by introducing math-related neuron activations into preference optimization. 

Neuron-level interpretability. Interpretability research has long studied how to understand inter-nal representations and computational mechanisms in LLMs. Prior work uses feature attribution, prob-ing, activation statistics, and neuron intervention to identify neurons associated with factual knowledge, language ability, or task-specific behavior (Sun-dararajan et al., 2017; Belinkov, 2022; Dai et al., 2022). For example, Knowledge Neurons studies factual-knowledge-related neurons, while LAPE analyzes language-selective neurons in multilin-gual LLMs (Dai et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2024). At-3tnLRP extends layer-wise relevance propagation to transformer models and enables efficient attribution over latent representations (Achtibat et al., 2024). Inspired by this line of work, we treat neuron-level signals not only as tools for explaining model be-havior, but also as potential signals that can par-ticipate in training. Specifically, YFPO introduces math-related neuron signals as an auxiliary reward in preference optimization, connecting internal ca-pability representations with external preference learning. 

3 Method 

We propose YFPO, a neuron-guided preference optimization method for mathematical reasoning. The key idea is to use internal neuron signals asso-ciated with mathematical reasoning as an auxiliary reward, in addition to the external preference signal provided by preferred and dispreferred responses. The overall procedure contains two stages. In the first stage, we use AttnLRP to identify math-related neurons offline on mathematical reasoning data and select the Top-K neurons with the highest relevance scores. In the second stage, during DPO training, we use forward hooks to extract the activa-tions of these neurons on preferred and dispreferred responses, and construct a neuron reward margin. YFPO then adds this margin as an auxiliary term to the DPO objective, encouraging the model to learn external preferences while also being guided by internal math-related signals. 

3.1 Identifying Math-Related Neurons 

We first identify a set of neurons associated with mathematical reasoning behavior. Following the relevance-based neuron selection procedure used in CRANE (Le and Li, 2026), we adopt At-tnLRP (Achtibat et al., 2024) to estimate neuron-level contributions on mathematical reasoning sam-ples. Given a mathematical problem and its reason-ing answer, AttnLRP propagates the relevance of output tokens backward through transformer mod-ules, assigning relevance scores to intermediate MLP neurons according to their contribution to the mathematical reasoning output. We treat each intermediate channel in a trans-former MLP as a neuron. Concretely, for each MLP layer, we focus on the intermediate activation dimension before down_proj . A neuron is there-fore specified by a layer index l and a neuron index 

j. For the j-th neuron in the l-th layer, we aggre-gate its relevance over mathematical samples and response tokens: 

rl,j = Ex,y,t [|Rl,j,t (x, y )|] , (1) where Rl,j,t denotes the relevance assigned to neu-ron (l, j ) at response token t. We use the absolute relevance value because we focus on the contribu-tion strength of a neuron to mathematical reasoning outputs rather than the sign of its contribution. Based on the aggregated relevance score, we se-lect the Top-K neurons as the math-related neuron set, denoted as Nmath . Unless otherwise stated, we set K = 1024 . The selected neuron set is kept fixed during preference optimization and is not updated with model parameters. We emphasize that these Top-K neurons are not assumed to fully characterize mathematical ability. Instead, we treat them as a set of internal statistical signals associated with mathematical reasoning be-havior and study whether such signals can provide useful auxiliary guidance for preference optimiza-tion. 

3.2 Constructing the Neuron Reward 

Given a preference example consisting of a prompt 

x, a preferred response y+, and a dispreferred re-sponse y−, we feed (x, y +) and (x, y −) into the current model and extract the activations of neurons in Nmath using forward hooks. For a response y, we define its math-neuron score as: 

sneu (x, y ) = E(l,j )∈N math , t ∈T y [|al,j,t (x, y )|] , (2) where al,j,t is the activation of neuron (l, j ) at re-sponse token t, and Ty denotes response tokens only. We aggregate only over response tokens rather than prompt tokens to avoid the length or content of the input problem dominating the neuron score. We also average over both token and neuron dimensions to reduce sensitivity to sequence length and neuron count. We then define the neuron reward margin be-tween preferred and dispreferred responses: 

∆neu = sneu (x, y +) − sneu (x, y −). (3) A larger ∆neu indicates that the preferred response more strongly activates the selected math-related neuron signals than the dispreferred response. The goal of YFPO is not to indiscriminately increase 4neuron activations for all responses. Instead, it en-courages preferred responses to have higher math-related internal signals than dispreferred responses. This pairwise design matches the chosen/rejected structure of preference optimization and reduces the risk of a shortcut where the model merely am-plifies internal activations without improving pref-erence separation. 

3.3 YFPO Objective 

YFPO uses DPO as the base preference optimiza-tion objective (Rafailov et al., 2023). For a pref-erence example (x, y +, y −), DPO compares the log-probability margin of the current model with that of a reference model: 

∆DPO = log πθ(y+|x) − log πθ(y−|x)

− log πref (y+|x) − log πref (y−|x) .

(4) The standard DPO loss is: 

LDPO = − log σ(β∆DPO ), (5) where β controls the preference strength. YFPO introduces the neuron reward margin as an auxiliary optimization signal: 

LYFPO = LDPO − λ∆neu , (6) where λ controls the strength of the neuron-guided auxiliary reward. When λ = 0 , YFPO reduces to standard DPO. When λ > 0, the model is op-timized to learn external preferences while also enlarging the internal math-neuron margin between preferred and dispreferred responses. Intuitively, DPO provides external preference su-pervision, telling the model which response is pre-ferred, while the neuron reward provides an internal capability-oriented signal, indicating whether the preferred response more strongly engages math-related neurons. YFPO does not replace the origi-nal preference learning objective, but augments it with internal neuron-level guidance. 

3.4 Implementation Details 

We first run AttnLRP offline on mathematical rea-soning samples to compute neuron relevance scores and select the Top-1024 math-related neurons. Dur-ing DPO training, we register forward hooks on the corresponding MLP modules and extract activa-tions on completion tokens for both preferred and dispreferred responses. Because we define neurons as MLP intermedi-ate channels, hooks should be applied to the input activation of down_proj , rather than the output ac-tivation of down_proj . This ensures that the acti-vation used during YFPO training is aligned with the neuron set selected during the AttnLRP stage. The reference model is used only for comput-ing the implicit DPO reward margin and does not participate in the neuron reward computation. The selected math-related neuron set remains fixed throughout training to ensure comparability across different training settings. 

4 Experiments 

We conduct preliminary experiments to examine whether neuron-guided rewards can interact with standard preference optimization for mathematical reasoning. Our goal is not to establish a strong performance claim under large-scale settings, but to test whether internal neuron-level signals can influence DPO-style training and lead to observable changes on math benchmarks. 

4.1 Experimental Setup Model. We use Qwen2-0.5B as the base model for our initial experiments. This small-scale model allows us to quickly validate the YFPO training pipeline, including offline neuron selection, activa-tion extraction with hooks, and the auxiliary neuron reward. 

Training data. We use 

xlinla/Math-Step-DPO-10K as the prefer-ence optimization dataset. Each example contains a math problem together with preferred and dispreferred responses. In addition to the full 10K training set, we also construct a randomly sampled 2K subset to study the behavior of YFPO under a lower-resource training setting. The 2K subset refers to a subset of training data, rather than a random-neuron baseline. 

Training methods. We compare standard DPO with DPO+YFPO. For YFPO, math-related neu-rons are selected offline using AttnLRP, with K =1024 by default. During training, we extract ac-tivations from the selected neurons for preferred and dispreferred responses, construct the neuron reward margin ∆neu , and combine it with the DPO objective. We sweep several values of λ to analyze the effect of the neuron reward strength. 5Evaluation. We evaluate models on GSM8K us-ing greedy decoding. We report results from three saved training checkpoints, denoted as Ckpt-1, Ckpt-2, and Ckpt-3. The original Qwen2-0.5B model obtains 0.3654 accuracy on GSM8K and 0.0264 on Math500. Since training-time Math500 results are not yet complete, we mainly report and analyze GSM8K in this preliminary study. 

4.2 Main Results on the Full 10K Set 

Table 1 reports GSM8K results on the full 10K training set. Since YFPO exhibits checkpoint-level variance, we report accuracy at three checkpoints, the average accuracy across checkpoints, and the best checkpoint accuracy. ∆Avg . and ∆Best denote improvements over the standard DPO baseline. On the full 10K training set, standard DPO does not substantially improve over the original model. Its best checkpoint reaches 0.3662, which is close to the original model accuracy of 0.3654. This sug-gests that, under the current small-model setting, standard DPO alone provides only limited improve-ment on GSM8K. Adding YFPO leads to stronger performance sig-nals. With λ = 0 .05 , DPO+YFPO reaches 0.3738 at Ckpt-1, improving over the best DPO checkpoint by 0.0076. With λ = 1 .0, YFPO also reaches a best accuracy of 0.3738, while obtaining a higher average accuracy of 0.3700 compared with 0.3649 for DPO. These results suggest that YFPO can not only improve a single checkpoint, but may also affect the overall optimization trajectory. At the same time, the performance drop of λ = 0 .05 at Ckpt-2 indicates that the benefit of the neuron reward remains sensitive to training stage and reward scale. 

4.3 Results on the 2K Data Subset 

Table 2 reports results on the 2K data subset. This setting examines whether math-related neuron re-wards can still provide useful auxiliary signals when fewer preference examples are used for train-ing. On the 2K subset, standard DPO obtains a best accuracy of 0.3669 and an average accuracy of 0.3639. Adding YFPO improves the best check-point accuracy under all tested λ values. For ex-ample, λ = 0 .1 reaches the highest best accuracy of 0.3730, improving over DPO by 0.0061. The settings λ = 0 .2 and λ = 0 .3 also reach 0.3715. In terms of average accuracy, λ = 0 .3 performs best, reaching 0.3674 and improving over DPO by 0.0035. This suggests that even with fewer preference examples, math-related neuron rewards can provide additional optimization signals and lead to observable performance gains. However, the best checkpoints appear at differ-ent training stages for different λ values. For ex-ample, λ = 0 .1 and λ = 0 .3 achieve their best results at Ckpt-1, while λ = 0 .2 achieves its best result at Ckpt-2. This indicates that YFPO remains sensitive to reward scaling and training dynamics.

This analysis focuses on YFPO runs with consistent neuron-margin log-ging, allowing us to examine how different λ values affect the internal reward during training. From the external preference margin, standard DPO and YFPO variants follow broadly similar op-timization trends. This suggests that adding the neuron reward does not collapse or replace the original preference optimization process. Instead, YFPO preserves the external DPO signal while adding an auxiliary internal constraint. From the internal neuron reward, different λ val-ues exhibit visibly different behaviors during train-ing. The neuron reward changes over optimization and shows λ-dependent patterns, indicating that it is not merely a static explanatory metric. Instead, it forms an additional internal optimization chan-nel that operates alongside the external preference signal. Combining these dynamics with the GSM8K summary shows that internal reward dynamics are not in a simple monotonic relationship with final accuracy. For example, λ = 0 .1 obtains the highest best-checkpoint accuracy, while λ = 0 .3 obtains a higher average accuracy. This is consistent with Ta-ble 2: a stronger neuron reward may more steadily alter the overall optimization trajectory, while a weaker or moderate reward may yield a better sin-gle checkpoint. This training-dynamics analysis complements the table results. Table 2 shows that YFPO im-proves best-checkpoint accuracy and, in some set-tings, average accuracy on the 2K subset. 4.5 Effect of Neuron Reward Strength 

The coefficient λ controls the strength of the auxil-iary neuron reward. A smaller λ keeps the training objective closer to standard DPO, while a larger λ

gives more weight to the neuron activation margin between preferred and dispreferred responses. The results show that YFPO does not follow a simple monotonic trend with respect to λ. On the full 10K set, both λ = 0 .05 and λ = 1 .0 achieve a best accuracy of 0.3738, while λ = 1 .0 gives a higher average score across checkpoints. On the 2K subset, λ = 0 .1 gives the highest best-checkpoint accuracy, whereas λ = 0 .3 gives the highest average accuracy. The training dynamics further show that λ af-fects not only the final accuracy, but also the evo-lution of the internal neuron reward. A larger λ

tends to more strongly change the internal neuron-level signal, but this does not necessarily lead to the highest single-checkpoint accuracy. Instead, the fi-nal performance depends on the balance between the external DPO objective and the internal neuron reward. These observations suggest that neuron reward scaling is important. A weak neuron reward may have limited influence, while an overly strong re-ward may interfere with the original DPO objective and encourage the model to over-optimize inter-nal activation signals. The current results support an intermediate conclusion: neuron rewards can provide useful auxiliary information for preference optimization, but their effectiveness depends on the balance among λ, data scale, and training stage. 

4.6 Preliminary Findings 

Overall, the results show that YFPO can provide an effective auxiliary signal for DPO under small-model mathematical reasoning settings. On both the full 10K training set and the 2K subset, YFPO obtains better best-checkpoint accuracy than stan-dard DPO. The highest GSM8K accuracy reaches 0.3738, outperforming both the original model and the DPO baseline. More importantly, YFPO improves not only the best checkpoint but also the average accuracy in several settings. For example, on the 10K set, 

λ = 1.0 improves the average accuracy from 0.3649 to 0.3700. On the 2K subset, λ = 0 .3 im-proves the average accuracy from 0.3639 to 0.3674. This suggests that neuron rewards may affect the overall optimization trajectory rather than merely producing isolated checkpoint-level gains. The training dynamics further support this in-terpretation. YFPO preserves the external pref-erence margin while introducing an independent internal neuron-level signal, and this internal signal changes differently across λ values. This indicates that math-related neuron signals are not only useful as internal explanatory features, but can also serve as auxiliary training signals in preference optimiza-tion. At the same time, the gains remain sensitive to reward scale and training stage. 5 Conclusion 

We introduced YFPO, a neuron-guided preference optimization method for mathematical reasoning. Unlike existing preference optimization methods that mainly rely on external chosen/rejected re-sponses, YFPO introduces internal neuron signals associated with mathematical reasoning and incor-porates them as an auxiliary reward into a DPO-style preference objective. Specifically, we use AttnLRP to identify Top-K math-related neurons offline, then compute an activation margin between chosen and rejected responses during training, en-couraging preferred responses to exhibit higher math-related internal signals. Preliminary experiments on Qwen2-0.5B and GSM8K show that YFPO can affect the prefer-ence optimization process and achieve higher ac-curacy than standard DPO at several checkpoints. Although the current results do not demonstrate stable or large performance gains, they suggest that neuron-level internal signals can meaningfully interact with external preference learning. This in-dicates that internal capability representations may be useful not only for interpreting model behavior, but also as auxiliary optimization signals during post-training. Overall, this work positions YFPO as an initial exploration of neuron-guided preference optimiza-tion. Our results suggest that combining external preference data with internal neuron signals may provide a path toward more fine-grained and inter-pretable reasoning-oriented post-training methods. Future work can extend the model scale, task cov-erage, and neuron selection strategy to more sys-tematically evaluate the role of internal capability signals in preference optimization. 

6 Limitations 

This work has several limitations. First, the exper-imental scale is small. Our current experiments mainly use Qwen2-0.5B and GSM8K, and do not yet systematically cover larger models, instruction-tuned models, or a wider range of mathematical reasoning benchmarks. Therefore, the current re-sults should be interpreted as preliminary evidence in a small-model setting rather than as a conclusion that generalizes to larger models or more complex reasoning tasks. Second, the current results are mixed but infor-mative. YFPO outperforms standard DPO at some checkpoints and λ settings, but the gains are small and vary across training stages. This suggests that the effectiveness of the neuron reward may depend on reward scale, training data size, model initializa-tion, and checkpoint selection. Future work should perform more systematic λ sweeps, multi-seed ex-periments, and training-dynamics analyses to deter-mine whether YFPO’s gains are stable. Third, we use AttnLRP to select math-related neurons offline and keep the neuron set fixed during training. This design is simple and easy to analyze, but it may ignore the dynamic evolution of internal 8representations during preference optimization. As training proceeds, the importance or function of the initially selected neurons may change. Future work can explore dynamic neuron selection, in-training relevance updates, or hybrid selection strategies that combine activation and attribution signals. Fourth, this work uses the activation margin of math-related neurons as an auxiliary reward. Al-though this design matches the chosen/rejected structure of preference optimization, activation is still an internal statistical signal and does not nec-essarily equal true mathematical ability. Over-optimizing activation rewards may introduce short-cut behavior, where the model learns to amplify cer-tain internal activation patterns without genuinely improving reasoning quality. Future experiments should include random-neuron baselines, causal intervention, and more behavioral evaluations to test whether the selected neurons have functional significance. Finally, this paper focuses on adding neuron signals to a DPO objective and does not fully ex-plore how YFPO interacts with other preference optimization or reinforcement learning methods. SimPO, ORPO, GRPO, and process reward learn-ing may provide different interfaces for neuron-guided optimization. Future work can investigate whether YFPO can serve as a general auxiliary term across different post-training frameworks and whether it applies to capabilities beyond mathemat-ical reasoning.
