# Abstract 

Large language model (LLM) agents have increasingly advanced service applications, such as booking flight tickets. However, these service agents suffer from unreliability in long-horizon tasks, as they often produce pol-icy violations, tool hallucinations, and misaligned actions, which greatly impedes their real-world deployment. To address these challenges, we propose NOD (Navigator-Operator-Director ), a heterogeneous multi-agent architecture for service agents. Instead of maintaining task state implicitly in dialogue context as in prior work, we externalize a structured Global State to enable explicit task state tracking and consistent decision-making by the Navigator . Besides, we introduce selective external oversight before 

critical actions , allowing an independent Director agent to verify execution and intervene when necessary. As such, NOD effectively mitigates error propagation and unsafe behavior in long-horizon tasks. Experiments on 

τ2-Bench demonstrate that our NOD achieves higher task success rates and 

critical action precision over baselines. More importantly, NOD improves the reliability of service agents by reducing policy violations, tool hallucina-tions, and user-intent misalignment. 

# 1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) have enabled a new class of agents that can interact with users through multi-turn dialogue and execute actions through tools (Schick et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024). One important instance is human-in-the-loop service agents, such as assistants for customer support (Yao et al., 2024; Balaji et al., 2026). In these settings, the agent must gather missing information, maintain user constraints over long interactions, and perform environment-changing operations under domain policies. The central challenge is therefore not only language generation, but reliable execution .Current service agents remain unreliable in long-horizon interactions (Sun et al., 2025; Larsson, 2026). As dialogue unfolds, users may provide incomplete, noisy, or evolving instructions, and errors in the agent’s task representation can propagate into external actions (Chen et al., 2023). In practice, these failures often appear as policy violations, tool hallucinations, and user-intent misalignment (Mishra et al., 2025; Xu et al., 2024b; Fang et al., 2025). Because service agents act on external environments, such errors are more consequential than ordinary text-generation mistakes. As illustrated in Figure 1, these failures are closely associated with two recurring limitations of single-model service agents. The first is implicit state fragility : task state is stored only implicitly in dialogue context, which makes long-horizon execution brittle and makes state inconsistencies harder to detect as the interaction grows more complex (Huang et al., 2022;

The second is unreliable intrinsic self-correction : once execution has already deviated, asking the same model to repair its own trajectory is often unreliable or highly condition-dependent (Huang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025; Li et al., 2024). The central challenge is therefore not only whether the agent is uncertain at the current turn, but whether it is already operating on an incorrect task state. To address these limitations, we propose NOD (Navigator-Operator-Director ), a heteroge-neous multi-agent architecture for reliable service agents. For implicit state fragility, the 

Navigator maintains a structured Global State , making task state explicit. For unreli-able intrinsic self-correction, the Director adds selective external oversight before critical actions , defined as consequential state-changing tool calls whose execution directly affects the external environment. Concretely, the Navigator maintains task state, the Operator 

handles routine dialogue and action proposal, and the Director reviews critical actions against dialogue context, user intent, and domain constraints, returning PASS , REVISE , or 

ABORT . In this way, NOD frames reliable service-agent execution as a structured control problem with explicit state tracking and selective pre-action verification. We evaluate NOD on the Retail and Airline domains of τ2-Bench (Barres et al., 2025). NOD 

improves task Success Rates and Critical Action Precision (CAP), a reliability-oriented metric for the correctness of executed critical actions, over baselines. NOD also reduces policy violations, tool hallucinations, and user-intent misalignment. Our contributions are summarized as follows: • We highlight implicit state fragility and unreliable intrinsic self-correction as two core obstacles to reliable long-horizon service-agent execution. • We propose NOD , a heterogeneous multi-agent architecture that combines explicit task-state tracking with selective oversight before critical action s.• We show on τ2-Bench that NOD improves task Success Rates and Critical Action Precision by up to 23.7 and 25.8 points over the baseline, respectively, while reducing policy violations, tool hallucinations, and user-intent misalignment. 

# 2 Related Work 

Tool-Using LLM Agents and Service Agents Recent work has turned LLMs into tool-using agents either by interleaving reasoning with action or by organizing execution through tool-centric architectures (Yehudai et al., 2025; Yao et al., 2022; Hao et al., 2024; Schick et al., 2023). Complementary efforts in training and evaluation have further improved API selection and tool-use correctness at scale (Qin et al., 2024; Polyakov et al., 2025; Hamad et al., 2025; Kokane et al., 2025; Patil et al., 2024). We focus on reliable long-horizon service execution, where agents must prevent unsafe actions under evolving user input (Xu et al., 2024a). Our setting is therefore closer to dialogue-based service systems than to more autonomous open-ended agents, such as coding or computer-use agents (Sobo et al., 2025). 

LLM Agents Reliability A parallel line improves reliability through self-correction and verification (Shinn et al., 2023; Madaan et al., 2023; Dhuliawala et al., 2024; Wu, 2025), while another studies uncertainty calibration and abstention as ways to reduce hallucination in single-turn generation (Liu et al., 2024; Tomani et al., 2024; Wen et al., 2025; Srey et al., 2Preprint. Under review. 2025). More recently, runtime guardrail work has begun to monitor tool invocations before execution (Mou et al., 2026).

We now describe NOD , a control architecture for reliable long-horizon service-agent ex-ecution. To address the two failure sources identified in Section 1— implicit state fragility 

and unreliable intrinsic self-correction —NOD separates three functions that are entangled in a standard single-agent design: explicit task-state tracking, routine interaction and action proposal, and oversight before critical actions. As shown in Figure 2, these functions are implemented by the Navigator , Operator , and Director , respectively. 

3.1 Explicit Task State 

Consider a service agent operating over a multi-turn dialogue and external tools. At turn 

t, the agent observes the dialogue history Ht and must either continue the interaction or execute an external action: 

at = π(Ht). (1) This formulation is standard, but brittle in long-horizon settings because task state remains implicit, leading to implicit state fragility . At any point in the interaction, the agent must maintain a task state : a representation of what the user wants, which constraints are in force, and how far execution has progressed. In standard single-agent systems, this state is not represented explicitly, but remains latent in Ht. As the dialogue grows longer, the model must repeatedly reconstruct the current goal, constraints, unresolved requirements, and progress from context alone. Once this latent state becomes incomplete or incorrect, subsequent actions can drift away from the intended task. NOD addresses this problem by externalizing task state into a structured Global State St, maintained by the Navigator :

St = {intent , constraints , missing_info , subtasks , entities , task_status , summary }.(2) Eq. 2 is an abstraction of the implementation schema in the Navigator prompt shown in Table 13. Together, these fields encode the current goal, constraints, unresolved requirements, intermediate subgoals, grounded entities, progress, and a compact interaction summary. The purpose of St is not merely to compress dialogue history, but to provide a persistent task representation for downstream reasoning and action selection. This design is related to dialogue state tracking in task-oriented dialogue (Jacqmin et al., 2022; Williams et al., 2013), but differs in scope and purpose. Standard dialogue state tracking mainly maintains a 3Preprint. Under review. belief state for dialogue management, typically centered on slot values. By contrast, Global State in NOD is execution-oriented: it also tracks missing requirements and intermediate subgoals, and is used to condition both action proposal and critical-action verification. 

3.2 Structured Control with Navigator, Operator, and Director 

Figure 2 summarizes the control flow. At each turn, the Navigator updates Global State 

from the dialogue history, the current user turn, and tool or environment feedback. Condi-tioned on this state, the Operator handles routine dialogue and proposes the next action. As in a standard single-agent system, the Operator remains the execution model, but now acts on an explicit task representation rather than raw dialogue history alone. The Director is invoked only for critical actions , i.e., tool calls that directly change the external environment and can therefore produce user-visible or hard-to-reverse consequences if executed incorrectly, such as creating orders or stored user information. Detailed critical actions are provided in Appendix A.2. By contrast, read-only retrieval calls and other non-mutating tools are not treated as critical. In our framework, the Director is instantiated with a frontier model , meaning a stronger external model reserved for selective verification rather than routine dialogue and action generation. The Director checks whether a proposed critical action is justified by the current Global State , dialogue context, user intent, and domain constraints. This added control is necessary because explicit state tracking does not resolve unreliable intrinsic self-correction once execution has already drifted. Formally, given the current state and a proposed critical action, the Director returns 

dt ∈ { PASS , REVISE , ABORT }. (3) A PASS decision approves execution. A REVISE decision blocks the current action and returns control to the Navigator, which updates Global State before the Operator proposes a revised action; if that proposal is again critical, the Director is consulted again. An ABORT 

decision blocks execution when the revised critical action is judged unsafe. In this way, NOD 

combines two complementary safeguards: REVISE enables recovery from inconsistent task state, while ABORT blocks unsafe critical actions when recovery is insufficient. Reliability is therefore treated not as a property of a single model output, but as a control process over state, action, and verification. 

# 4 Experiments 

We now conduct experiments to show the effectiveness of our NOD architecture. 

4.1 Experimental Setup Benchmarks and models. We evaluate NOD on the Retail and Airline domains of τ2-Bench (Barres et al., 2025), which require multi-turn interaction, policy-constrained ex-ecution, and environment-changing tool use. We exclude Telecom because it requires tool use on the user-simulator side, introducing additional variation beyond the agent itself. Unless otherwise specified, the Navigator and Operator are instantiated with six open-weight backbones: Qwen3-30B-A3B-Thinking-2507(Qwen3-30B-MoE) , Qwen3-32B ,

Qwen3-14B , Qwen3-8B (Yang et al., 2025), Ministral-3-14B-Instruct (Liu et al., 2026), and 

GPT-OSS-20B (Agarwal et al., 2025). The Director uses GPT-5.1 (Singh et al., 2025) in the main experiments. We use Qwen3-32B as the user simulator; comparisons with GPT-5.1 are deferred to Appendix A.1. Prompts are provided in Appendix A.9. 

Metrics. We report Success Rate (SR) and Critical Action Precision (CAP) , averaged over three independent trials. SR is the percentage of tasks successfully completed under the benchmark evaluator. CAP measures the precision of executed critical actions: CAP = # correct critical actions # executed critical actions . (4) Unlike precision over all predicted tool calls (Zhou et al., 2025), CAP is restricted to actions that actually reach the environment, making it directly sensitive to harmful executions. 4Preprint. Under review. 

Baselines. We compare against both single-agent and multi-agent baselines. The single-agent baselines are Vanilla LLM , Self-Reflection (Ji et al., 2023), and Self-aware Absten-tion (Wen et al., 2025). The multi-agent baselines are Multi-Agent Debate (Liang et al., 2024), where three agents independently propose actions and the final action is chosen by voting, and AutoGen (Wu et al., 2024), which uses a solver –verifier –finalizer pipeline in which the first two interact for iterative checking and the last produces the final action. Together, these baselines cover direct execution, intrinsic self-correction, uncertainty-aware abstention, deliberative interaction, and generic role-based coordination. 

Variants and analysis scope. To understand what drives NOD ’s gains, we study four variants, each designed to isolate one component of the architecture: (i) w/o Director , which removes the Director, isolating the effect of explicit task-state tracking alone; (ii) Frontier Re-navigation , which invokes the frontier model at the original revision points only to reconstruct Global State , testing whether stronger-model access at matched checkpoints is sufficient without an explicit revision signal; (iii) Weak Director , which keeps the full control flow but uses the same local backbone for the Director, isolating the role of Director strength; and (iv) REVISE-only , which disables ABORT while preserving selective external revision, isolating the contribution of ABORT -based blocking. 

4.2 Main Results 

Table 1 shows a consistent pattern across both domains and all six backbones: the strongest SR is achieved by either REVISE-only or full NOD , while the strongest CAP comes from 

Weak Director , REVISE-only , or full NOD . More importantly, the two main variants in this comparison— REVISE-only and full NOD —tend to improve different aspects of reliability. 

REVISE-only already delivers large SR gains and often attains the best SR, suggesting that externally guided revision is a major driver of task completion. Full NOD , by contrast, more consistently improves CAP, suggesting that ABORT helps sharpen reliability by preventing unsafe critical actions from reaching the environment. In short, REVISE appears to contribute most directly to task success, while ABORT contributes most directly to critical-action safety. This pattern is also informative relative to the baselines. NOD generally outperforms single-agent methods based on intrinsic self-correction or abstention, and is also stronger than generic multi-agent baselines such as Multi-Agent Debate and AutoGen . We additionally include Weak Director to provide a like-for-like comparison against methods that use only local models. Its results suggest that the gains are not explained solely by introducing 

GPT-5.1 : even in the all-local setting, explicit state tracking and selective verification remain competitive with, and often stronger than, the baselines. At the same time, the gap between 

Weak Director and full NOD indicates that Director strength still matters. We return to the roles of REVISE , ABORT , and Director strength in Sec. 4.4. We also report Critical Action Recall 

(CAR) in Appendix A.3, which reflects critical-action coverage.

4.4 What Drives NOD ’s Gains?

Calling a stronger model at revision checkpoints is not enough. We next ask whether the gains could be explained simply by consulting a frontier model at selected checkpoints. To test this, we introduce Frontier Re-navigation , a control-matched variant that invokes the frontier model at the same checkpoints as REVISE-only , but uses it only to rebuild 

Global State and continue execution, without issuing an explicit REVISE judgment. As shown by the w/o Director , Frontier Re-nav. , and REVISE-only bars in Figure 4, Frontier Re-navigation yields only limited gains over w/o Director , whereas REVISE-only produces a much larger improvement, especially in Airline. This comparison suggests that frontier-model access alone is not the main driver. What matters is the external corrective signal 

provided by the Director: instead of merely refreshing state and continuing, the system is explicitly told that the current trajectory is flawed and should be revised before proceeding. 

Director strength matters for effective oversight. We then isolate the role of Director’s strength by comparing Weak Director with full NOD . These two variants share the same control flow, including selective verification and ABORT ; the only difference is that Weak Director replaces the frontier Director with the same local backbone used by the Navigator 

and Operator . As shown by the Weak Director and NOD bars in Figure 4, this change leads to clear degradation in both domains. This comparison shows that oversight must also be strong enough to recognize when the maintained task state is incomplete, inconsistent, or no longer aligned with the user request.

helps isolate the contribution of ABORT . The main results already show that full NOD outperforms all-local baselines. The remaining question is narrower: are these gains fundamentally driven by heterogeneity, i.e., by pairing a stronger frontier Director with weaker local modules? Table 4 suggests that the answer is no. We first consider the homogeneous local setting, in which all three roles share the same local backbone. Here, Weak Director already improves substantially over Vanilla LLM. On Qwen3-14B, for example, SR rises from 40.7 to 47.4 in Retail. On Ministral-3-14B, the gains are even larger in CAP, increasing from 80.8 to 90.6 in Retail. Since Weak Director uses no frontier model at all, these results show that part of NOD ’s advantage comes from the control architecture itself: explicit task-state tracking and selective verification already help even in a fully homogeneous local system. We then ask the complementary question: if all three roles are upgraded to the same frontier model, does the architecture still matter? The GPT-5.1 shaded columns show that it does. Relative to a vanilla GPT-5.1 agent, full NOD improves both CAP and SR in both domains, from 86.6/60.8 to 91.5/69.3 in Retail and from 88.7/52.0 to 92.0/54.0 in Airline. This comparison is especially informative because the strongest competing CAP numbers come from Self-aware Abstention, which reaches 96.5 in Retail and 97.7 in Airline, but with much lower SR, at 25.4 and 41.3. Even when the backbone is fixed at GPT-5.1, NOD still improves the frontier model by organizing it differently. Heterogeneity appears helpful, but the results suggest that it is not the only source of NOD ’s gains.

# 6 Conclusion 

We study reliability in long-horizon service agents, where errors in task representation can propagate into external actions over the course of interaction. NOD addresses this problem by combining explicit task-state tracking with selective oversight before environment-changing actions. Across the Retail and Airline domains of τ2-Bench, NOD improves Success Rate and Critical Action Precision over single-agent and multi-agent baselines, while also reducing policy violations, tool hallucinations, and user-intent misalignment. Our analyses further suggest a division of labor within the architecture: REVISE primarily improves task completion, whereas ABORT mainly sharpens critical-action safety. 

Limitations. A main limitation is that the Navigator currently regenerates a full JSON 

Global State at every turn, which may increase token cost and introduce brittleness when the state becomes incomplete or inconsistent. We are therefore exploring more formal alternatives, such as updating an independent state maintainer through tool calls, to make state updates more robust and efficient.

A.3 Full Results of Main Experiments 

In Sec. 4.2, we report Success Rate (SR) and Critical Action Precision (CAP). Here we additionally report Critical Action Recall (CAR) for completeness. CAR measures the coverage of benchmark-required critical actions, and is defined as CAR = # correct critical actions # benchmark-required critical actions .While CAP measures the precision of executed critical actions, CAR measures whether the agent successfully covers the critical actions required for task completion. The two metrics are therefore complementary: CAP emphasizes action correctness among executed critical actions, whereas CAR emphasizes critical-action coverage. Table 7 reports the complete results of the main experiments with CAP, CAR, and SR across all six backbones in both Retail and Airline. Overall, the CAR results are broadly consistent with the main findings in the paper: NOD -family methods remain competitive with, and often stronger than, the single-agent and generic multi-agent baselines. At the same time, CAR reveals an aspect that is not fully captured by CAP alone. In particular, variants without ABORT , such as NOD (REVISE-only ), often obtain stronger CAR than full 

NOD , because they preserve a broader coverage of benchmark-required critical actions. By contrast, full NOD is more conservative: it may forgo some critical-action coverage by blocking actions that remain insufficiently justified, even when such conservatism improves the precision of actions that are actually allowed to reach the environment. This difference also clarifies the relation between CAR and SR. CAR is a recall-style metric defined only over critical actions, whereas SR is an end-to-end task metric defined over complete trajectories. As a result, the two do not necessarily move together. A.4 Full Ablation Results 

Table 8 reports the full ablation results with CAP, CAR, and SR. These results further support the mechanism analysis in the main text. First, w/o Director already improves over standard baselines in many settings, showing that explicit task-state tracking helps the agent cover required critical actions more reliably. Second, frontier re-navigation yields additional gains in several cases, but its effect is typically smaller and less consistent than that of the full NOD 

variants, suggesting that stronger model access alone does not explain the improvements. Third, Weak Director remains competitive but generally underperforms full NOD , indicating that selective oversight is useful in itself, while the strength of the Director still matters for robust verification. The CAR results are especially informative for understanding the role of ABORT . Relative to

A.8 Director Usage Statistics 

A practical concern is whether the gains of NOD are driven primarily by frequent Director intervention. Tables 11 and 12 report the full per-backbone Director usage statistics in Retail and Airline. For each NOD variant, we report total agent turns, Director triggers, and 

REVISE counts, aggregated over three trials. The full results are consistent with the pattern described in Sec. 5.3. Across backbones and domains, most turns still proceed without any Director check. Director-triggered turns remain a minority of all agent turns, and only a smaller subset of those checks results in a 

REVISE decision. This supports a more precise interpretation of NOD ’s advantage: its gains do not come from handing off a large fraction of the interaction to a stronger external model, but from applying selective oversight at consequential decision points. The comparison between full NOD and NOD (REVISE-only ) is also informative. In most settings, full NOD reduces the number of Director-triggered turns while keeping REVISE 

counts sparse. This is especially clear in Airline, where adding ABORT often shortens the interaction and reduces repeated repair cycles on persistently unsafe trajectories. The overall picture is therefore one of sparse and targeted intervention rather than high-frequency external control.
