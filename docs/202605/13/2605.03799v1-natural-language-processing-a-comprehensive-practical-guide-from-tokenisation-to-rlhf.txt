Guide from Tokenisation to RLHF  [Text ]:  a textbook  for  undergraduate  and 

graduate  students  of  higher  education  institutions  specialising  in  information

This preprint presents a systematic, research -oriented practicum that guides the reader 

through the entire modern NLP pipeline  — from tokenisation and vectorisation to fine -tuning 

of large language models, retrieval -augmented generation, and reinforcement  learning from 

human feedback. Twelve hands -on sessions combine concise theory with detailed 

implementation plans, formalised evaluation metrics, and transparent assessment criteria. The 

work is not a conventional textbook: it is designed as a reproducible  research artefact where 

every session requires publishing code, models, and reports in public repositories. All 

experiments are conducted on a single evolving corpus, and the work advocates open -weight 

models over commercial APIs, with special attention to  the Hugging Face ecosystem. The 

material is enriched by original research on low -resource languages, incorporating linguistic 

resources for Tajik and Tatar  — subword tokenisers, embeddings, lexical databases, and 

transliteration benchmarks  — demonstrating  how modern NLP can be adapted to data -scarce 

environments. Designed for senior undergraduates, graduate students, and practising 

developers seeking to implement, compare, and deploy methods from classical ML to 

state -of -the -art LLM -based systems.

Natural  Language  Processing  (NLP)  has  evolved  from  a specialised 

research  field  into  a key  interdisciplinary  technology  underpinning  search 

engines,  virtual  assistants,  machine  translation,  sentiment  analysis  and  numerous 

other  applications  [1,  2].  Modern  NLP  integrates  methods  from  linguistics, 

statistics  and  deep  learning  [3,  4],  demanding  of  the  specialist  not  only  theoretical 

grounding  but  also  confident  practical  skills  in  handling  data,  models  and 

computational  resources. 

This  textbook  has  been  developed  from  experience  of  teaching  the 

discipline  "Natural  Language  Processing"  to  master's  students  in  "Business 

Informatics"  and  "Applied  Mathematics  and  Informatics"  at  Kazan  Federal 

University.  It  aims  to  provide  a systematic  practicum  that  combines  the 

acquisition  of  fundamental  concepts  with  the  solution  of  applied  tasks,  and  has 

been  prepared  with  student  feedback  in  mind.  The  classical  foundations  of 

statistical  NLP  [5]  and  modern  deep  learning  approaches  [6]  have  informed  the 

structure  of  the  course.  For  practical  implementation,  the  book  draws  on 

established  Python  libraries  and  frameworks  [7,  8]. 

The  book  follows  a "simple  to  complex"  principle  and  guides  the  reader 

through  the  complete  cycle  of  an  NLP  project:  from  the  collection  and 

preprocessing  of  textual  data  to  the  construction  and  evaluation  of  complex 

models.  Each  of  the  twelve  sessions  comprises  aims,  brief  theoretical  background, 

a step -by -step  execution  plan,  report  requirements  and  assessment  criteria.  The 

theoretical  notes  are  introductory  in  nature;  for  in -depth  study,  the  reader  is 

advised  to  consult  the  fundamental  textbooks  and  scientific  literature  listed  at  the 

end  of  the  volume,  including  such  standard  works  as  Jurafsky  & Martin  [9]  and 

Eisenstein  [10]. 

The  material  is  organized  into  four  logical  sections  reflecting  the  key  stages 

of  competence  development  in  NLP.  The  first  covers  the  essentials  of  text 

preprocessing:  tokenisation,  normalisation,  and  the  compilation  and  analysis  of 

text  corpora.  When  dealing  with  morphologically  rich  languages,  state -of -the -art 

subword  tokenisation  methods  [11,  12]  are  emphasised,  as  well  as  the  use  of 

libraries  such  as  NLTK  [13]  and  spaCy. 

The  second  section  examines  vectorisation  methods  and  unsupervised 

analysis:  classical  and  modern  approaches  to  text  representation  [14,  15], 

including  Word2Vec  [16],  GloVe  and  FastText  [11],  clustering  and  the  evaluation 5

of  semantic  similarity.  Dimensionality  reduction  and  visualisation  techniques 

such  as  t-SNE  and  UMAP  are  also  covered. 

The  third  section  addresses  text  classification,  spanning  the  full  range  of 

approaches —from  classical  machine  learning  algorithms  and  AutoML  to  deep 

neural  networks  [17]  and  modern  transformer  models.  The  transformer 

architecture,  exemplified  by  BERT  [18],  revolutionised  NLP,  and  its  descendants 

such  as  ALBERT  [19],  DistilBERT  [20]  and  various  Russian -language 

adaptations  (RuBERT,  ruRoBERTa)  are  discussed  in  detail.  For  sequence 

modelling,  recurrent  architectures  [17]  are  also  presented. 

The  final  part  considers  contemporary  architectures  and  applied  systems: 

working  with  the  Hugging  Face  platform  [21,  22],  multi -task  scenarios 

(information  extraction,  text  generation),  the  application  of  large  language  models 

(fine -tuning  via  parameter -efficient  methods  [23],  construction  of 

retrieval -augmented  generation  (RAG)  systems  [24]),  their  domain -oriented 

evaluation  using  unified  text -to -text  frameworks  [25],  and  alignment  using 

reinforcement  learning  from  human  feedback  (RLHF).  Sentence -level 

embeddings  for  semantic  search  are  introduced  via  Sentence -BERT  [36].  For 

large  language  models,  recent  advances  in  few -shot  learning  [26]  and 

cross -lingual  transfer  [27]  are  taken  into  account. 

Particular  attention  is  devoted  to  the  use  of  the  Hugging  Face  ecosystem —

the  de  facto  standard  for  modern  NLP  development,  offering  access  to  an 

extensive  collection  of  models,  datasets  and  tools.  The  Hugging  Face  course  and 

documentation  [28,  22]  provide  essential  supplementary  material. 

The  textbook  also  reflects  the  author's  own  research  in  low -resource  and 

under -represented  languages.  In  particular,  the  creation  of  lexical  resources  for 

Tajik –Persian  [29],  the  development  of  subword  tokenisers  for  Tajik  [12],  the 

construction  of  word  embeddings  for  Tatar  [30],  the  analysis  of  safety  alignment 

for  the  Tajik  language  [31],  and  the  systematic  benchmarking  of  machine 

transliteration  models  for  the  Tajik –Farsi  language  pair  [39]  have  all  informed  the 

methodological  choices  made  in  the  practical  tasks.  Comparative  studies  of 

anomaly  detection  [32]  and  multilingual  benchmarks  [33]  further  enrich  the 

presented  material. 

The  textbook  is  aimed  at  senior  undergraduates,  master's  students,  doctoral 

candidates  and  practising  developers  who  seek  not  only  to  understand  the  core 

concepts  of  NLP  but  also  to  acquire  the  skills  for  independently  implementing, 

comparing  and  applying  diverse  methods  to  solve  practical  problems.  It  may  be 6

employed  within  taught  courses,  laboratory  sessions,  or  for  independent  project 

work. 

The  author  hopes  this  practicum  will  prove  useful  in  mastering  the 

dynamically  evolving  field  of  natural  language  processing  and  will  help  to 

prepare  specialists  ready  for  research  and  engineering  work  in  this  domain.

1.  Aim  and  Objectives  of  the  Work 

Aim  of  the  work  — to  give  the  student  a systematic  understanding  of  the 

pipeline  for  the  preliminary  processing  of  textual  data  in  Natural  Language 

Processing  (NLP)  tasks,  to  develop  practical  skills  in  implementing  and 

comparatively  analysing  tokenisation  and  normalisation  methods,  and  to  acquire 

competencies  in  preparing  representative  text  corpora,  training  subword  models 

and  ensuring  the  reproducibility  of  research. 

Main  objectives  of  the  work: 

1.  Master  methods  for  collecting  textual  data  from  web  sources  and  form  a

representative  corpus  of  news  texts  in  Russian. 

2.  Implement  software  modules  for  preliminary  cleaning,  standardisation  and 

normalisation  of  text  at  various  levels  of  processing. 

3.  Conduct  a comparative  analysis  of  classical  and  modern  algorithms  for 

tokenisation,  stemming  and  lemmatisation  on  the  basis  of  formalised 

metrics. 

4.  Train  subword  tokenisation  models  and  investigate  their  properties  in  terms 

of  vocabulary  compactness,  lexical  fragmentation  and  reversibility  of 

transformation. 

5.  Develop  an  interactive  web  tool  for  the  visual  and  functional  comparison 

of  text  processing  methods. 

6.  Ensure  reproducibility  and  openness  of  the  results:  publish  the  corpus, 

trained  models,  source  code  and  analytical  report  in  accordance  with 

modern  scientific  and  methodological  standards. 

2.  Theoretical  Prerequisites 

Tokenisation  is  a fundamental  stage  of  the  natural  language  processing 

pipeline  and  determines  the  quality  of  all  subsequent  stages  of  text  analysis.  The 

accuracy  of  frequency  characteristics,  the  effectiveness  of  search  and  filtering,  the 

quality  of  language  modelling,  and  the  robustness  of  subword  representations 

when  dealing  with  unknown  vocabulary  depend  on  the  correctness  with  which 

the  text  is  segmented  into  linguistically  or  statistically  meaningful  units.  The 

difficulty  of  the  tokenisation  task  varies  considerably  across  languages.  For 

languages  with  clear  word  boundaries  and  relatively  simple  morphology,  such  as 

English,  surface  heuristics  often  suffice.  For  morphologically  rich  languages  like 

Russian  or  Tatar —where  a single  lemma  may  generate  dozens  of  surface  forms 

through  inflection  and  derivation —the  choice  of  tokenisation  strategy  has  a direct 8

and  measurable  impact  on  downstream  task  performance.  Moreover,  the  problem 

of  out -of -vocabulary  words,  inevitable  in  any  real -world  application,  cannot  be 

resolved  by  dictionary -based  methods  alone  and  requires  a fundamentally 

different  approach  to  text  segmentation. 

In  contemporary  theory  and  practice,  the  following  classes  of  tokenisation 

methods  and  associated  normalisation  procedures  are  distinguished: 

1.  Surface  methods  — splitting  of  text  by  whitespace  and  punctuation 

characters  on  the  basis  of  heuristics.  They  are  characterised  by  high 

computational  efficiency,  yet  do  not  adequately  account  for  the 

morphological  complexity  of  languages  with  rich  inflection. 

2.  Morphologically  oriented  methods  — include  stemming  (reducing  a word 

to  its  stem  by  means  of  rules)  and  lemmatisation  (restoring  the  normal  form 

of  a word  using  dictionaries  or  language  models).  Such  approaches  provide 

greater  semantic  consistency  but  require  external  linguistic  resources  and 

computational  cost. 

3.  Subword  tokenisation  methods  — statistically  trained  algorithms  (BPE, 

WordPiece,  Unigram  LM,  etc.)  that  build  a vocabulary  from  subword  units. 

These  methods  make  it  possible  to  effectively  address  the  out -of -

vocabulary  (OOV)  problem,  flexibly  control  vocabulary  size,  and  ensure  a

balance  between  compactness  and  the  expressive  power  of  the  text 

representation. 

These  three  classes  represent  a trade -off  between  computational  efficiency 

and  linguistic  adequacy.  Surface  methods  are  fast  but  linguistically  naïve; 

morphologically  oriented  methods  introduce  linguistic  knowledge  but  depend  on 

external  resources  and  may  propagate  errors;  subword  methods  learn  an  optimal 

segmentation  directly  from  data,  offering  a principled  solution  to  the  OOV 

problem,  yet  their  behaviour  is  less  transparent  and  requires  careful  empirical 

validation.  Understanding  this  trade -off,  and  acquiring  the  ability  to  measure  it 

quantitatively,  is  one  of  the  central  learning  outcomes  of  the  present  work. 

3.  Procedure  for  Executing  the  Work 

The  work  is  performed  in  several  successive  stages,  each  directed  at 

attaining  specific  educational  and  research  outcomes. 

Task  1.  Collection  of  a representative  corpus  of  Russian -language  news 

texts  from  open  web  sources,  containing  no  fewer  than  100 –150 000  words, 

constitutes  the  starting  point  of  the  entire  project.  For  each  publication  it  is 

necessary  to  extract  the  headline,  body  text,  publication  date,  URL  and  category 9

(if  available),  thereby  furnishing  a rich  context  for  subsequent  analysis.  Sources 

may  include  nationwide  Russian  news  agencies  (TASS,  RIA  Novosti),  thematic 

portals,  as  well  as  regional  and  national -language  resources  of  the  student’s 

choosing.  The  use  of  synthetic  or  purely  instructional  corpora  lacking  external 

context  and  supplementary  attributes  is  strictly  forbidden.  Data  are  stored  in 

JSONL  format  (one  JSON  object  per  line),  and  collection  is  carried  out  by  means 

of  the  requests  and  BeautifulSoup4  tools  for  static  pages,  or  selenium  in  the  case 

of  dynamic  content.  Particular  attention  is  paid  to  the  correct  cleaning  of  HTML 

markup  and  the  removal  of  elements  extraneous  to  the  main  text  — navigation 

bars,  advertising  blocks  and  scripts. 

Task  2.  Implementation  of  a preliminary  cleaning  module  tailored  to  the 

structure  of  the  collected  data  ensures  the  unification  of  input  texts  and  their 

readiness  for  further  processing.  The  module  must  strip  all  HTML  tags,  scripts 

and  non -semantic  components,  normalise  whitespace  characters  and  excessive 

line  breaks,  optionally  lower -case  the  text  (preserving  linguistically  significant 

elements  such  as  abbreviations,  hyphens  in  compound  words  and  unit 

designations),  and  also  support  stop -word  filtering  using  standard  dictionaries 

(for  instance,  from  the  stop -words -ru  library).  All  code  is  extracted  into  a separate 

module,  text_cleaner.py,  with  a clearly  documented  interface,  which  guarantees 

its  reusability  and  testability. 

Task  3.  Development  of  a universal  preprocessing  module  that 

standardises  the  text  before  it  is  fed  to  the  tokeniser  is  a key  step  towards  creating 

a flexible  and  configurable  pipeline.  The  module  must  ensure  uniform  handling 

of  punctuation  and  hyphens,  replace  URLs,  email  addresses,  numerical  values 

and  other  special  tokens  with  standard  markers  (e.g.,  <URL>,  <EMAIL>, 

<NUMBER>),  as  well  as  support  the  optional  expansion  of  common  contractions 

and  abbreviations.  The  implementation  is  based  on  regular  expressions  for 

robustness  to  the  variability  of  input  data,  and  all  logic  supports  the  loading  of 

rules  from  an  external  configuration  file  in  YAML  or  JSON  format,  which 

enhances  reproducibility  and  ease  of  tuning. 

Task  4.  Conducting  descriptive  statistical  analysis  and  visualisation 

permits  a deeper  understanding  of  the  structure  and  properties  of  the  prepared 

corpus.  Within  this  task,  basic  statistics  are  computed:  total  word  count,  number 

of  unique  tokens,  mean  text  and  headline  length,  and  proportion  of  stop  words 

before  and  after  filtering.  Histograms  of  the  distributions  of  text  and  headline 

lengths  are  plotted.  Additionally,  the  distribution  of  text  length  across  categories 

(if  the  category  is  available)  is  analysed  by  means  of  box  plots  or  summary 10 

statistics.  The  structure  of  missing  values  (for  example,  absence  of  category  or 

publication  date)  is  also  investigated  using  the  missingno  library  or  its  analogues, 

which  helps  to  uncover  potential  data  problems. 

Task  5.  Comparative  analysis  of  classical  tokenisation  and  normalisation 

methods  is  conducted  on  the  basis  of  formalised  metrics  and  encompasses  naïve 

tokenisation  (by  spaces),  regular -expression -based  tokenisation,  library 

implementations  (nltk,  spacy,  razdel),  stemming  (PorterStemmer, 

SnowballStemmer)  and  lemmatisation  (pymorphy3,  spacy  for  Russian).  Each 

method  is  assessed  against  criteria  such  as  the  size  of  the  resulting  vocabulary 

(number  of  unique  tokens),  the  out -of -vocabulary  (OOV)  rate  on  a held -out 

sample,  time  complexity  of  processing,  and  semantic  consistency  is  gauged 

indirectly:  for  example,  by  the  method’s  ability  to  conflate  different  word  forms 

of  a single  lexeme.  It  should  be  noted  that  the  resulting  text  representations  will 

subsequently  (in  later  practical  sessions)  be  employed  for  building  embeddings 

and  training  classification  models;  hence,  the  correctness  of  normalisation  is 

critically  important  already  at  this  stage. 

Task  6.  Training  and  comparison  of  three  subword  tokenisation  algorithms 

— Byte  Pair  Encoding  (BPE),  WordPiece  and  Unigram  Language  Model  — are 

carried  out  within  a controlled  experiment  with  fixed  parameters:  vocabulary  size 

is  varied  (8 000,  16 000,  32 000  tokens),  the  minimum  frequency  threshold  is  set 

at  two  occurrences,  and  the  toolset  employed  comprises  the  tokenizers  (Hugging 

Face)  or  sentencepiece  libraries.  The  metrics  analysed  include  mean  token  length 

(in  characters),  word  fragmentation  coefficient  (number  of  tokens  per  source 

word),  degree  of  text  compression  (in  characters  and  tokens),  and,  crucially,  the 

possibility  of  complete  reconstruction  of  the  input  text  from  the  token  sequence, 

which  confirms  the  correctness  and  reversibility  of  the  transformation. 

Task  7.  Linguistic  and  statistical  analysis  of  the  corpus,  with  an  emphasis 

on  its  lexical  structure,  includes  plotting  the  empirical  Zipf’s  law  curve  (the 

dependence  of  the  logarithm  of  word  frequency  on  its  logarithmic  rank)  with  a

subsequent  estimate  of  the  deviation  from  the  theoretical  distribution, 

visualisation  of  word  clouds  before  and  after  normalisation,  construction  of  heat 

maps  of  the  frequencies  of  the  most  heavily  fragmented  words  in  subword 

models,  as  well  as  the  creation  of  interactive  diagrams  of  token  distribution  by 

frequency  and  length,  which  allows  the  differences  between  processing  methods 

to  be  appreciated  intuitively. 

Task  8.  Training  of  subword  models  and  construction  of  vocabularies  are 

carried  out  exclusively  on  the  designated  training  portion  of  the  corpus.  All 11 

metrics  (OOV  rate,  fragmentation)  are  evaluated  on  a held -out  sample  that  was 

not  used  during  the  training  of  the  tokeniser,  in  order  to  avoid  an  overly  optimistic 

estimate  of  its  generalisation  ability. 

Task  9.  Development  of  a unified  text  preprocessor  in  the  form  of  a class 

with  methods  .fit()  and  .transform(),  capable  of  preserving  internal  state 

(vocabularies,  tokeniser  parameters)  and  supporting  serialisation.  Such  an 

approach  guarantees  reproducibility  and  ease  of  reuse  across  diverse  text 

processing  scenarios. 

Task  10.  Creation  of  an  automated  reproducibility  tester  includes  pinning 

the  versions  of  all  dependencies  in  a requirements.txt  or  environment.yml  file, 

developing  a suite  of  unit  tests  using  pytest  that  verify  the  idempotency  of  the 

cleaning  module,  the  correctness  and  reversibility  of  transformations  (e.g.,  the 

ability  of  the  BPE  decoder  to  reconstruct  the  original  text),  the  absence  of  data 

leakage  and  the  determinism  of  the  preprocessor  under  a fixed  seed,  as  well  as 

writing  a script  for  the  automatic  execution  of  all  tests  and  generation  of  a pass/fail 

report,  which  ensures  the  long -term  reliability  and  maintainability  of  the  code. 

Task  11.  Development  of  an  interactive  web  tool  for  the  visual  and 

functional  analysis  of  textual  data  is  aimed  at  creating  a convenient  interface  for 

exploring  and  comparing  processing  methods.  The  application  must  support 

uploading  a user  text  file  (in  CSV  or  JSONL  format)  or  selecting  from  built -in 

examples,  allow  the  choice  of  tokenisation  method  and  its  parameters  (e.g., 

vocabulary  size  for  BPE),  automatically  plot  distributions  of  token  lengths, 

frequency  spectra  and  OOV  rates,  visualise  subword  decomposition  (for  instance, 

the  segmentation  of  a word  such  as  “недопустимость ” into  fragments),  and 

generate  an  analytical  report  in  HTML  or  PDF  format.  Recommended 

technologies  are  Streamlit  in  combination  with  Plotly  or  Bokeh  for  interactive 

visualisation. 

Task  12.  Ensuring  the  openness  and  full  reproducibility  of  the  results 

completes  the  project  and  transforms  it  into  a scientific -engineering  artefact.  This 

encompasses  publishing  the  corpus,  the  preprocessor  source  code,  the  auto -tester, 

the  web  application  and  the  analytical  report  in  a public  repository  (GitHub  or 

GitLab);  compiling  a detailed  meta -description  containing  the  corpus 

characteristics  (volume,  sources,  linguistic  composition),  training  parameters 

(vocabulary  size,  frequency  threshold),  key  metrics  (OOV,  fragmentation, 

processing  time),  limitations,  licensing  terms  and  step -by -step  reproduction 

instructions;  deploying  the  web  application  on  an  openly  accessible  platform 12 

(e.g.,  Hugging  Face  Spaces);  and  adopting  open  licences:  MIT  or  Apache  2.0  for 

code,  and  Creative  Commons  Attribution  4.0  (CC  BY  4.0)  for  data. 

4. Additional Research Tasks 

1.  Analysis  of  the  influence  of  stop  words.  Investigate  how  the  removal  of 

stop  words  before  and  after  normalisation  affects  the  final  vocabulary  and 

frequency  characteristics  of  the  corpus. 

2.  Comparison  with  unsupervised  segmentation.  Train  a simple  model  for 

word  boundary  detection  (for  instance,  based  on  BPE  without  merge 

operations)  on  raw  text  without  spaces,  and  compare  the  result  with 

linguistic  tokenisers.

2.  Theoretical  Prerequisites 

Text  vectorisation  — the  transformation  of  unstructured  textual  data  into 

numerical  vector  representations  — is  a key  stage  of  the  NLP  pipeline.  The 

quality  of  the  resulting  vectors  directly  affects  the  effectiveness  of  solving  tasks 

such  as  classification,  clustering,  semantic  search  and  others.  A vector 

representation  is  not  merely  a convenient  format  for  computation;  it  is  a

hypothesis  about  which  properties  of  the  text  carry  meaning.  Two  documents 

represented  as  similar  vectors  will  be  treated  as  similar  by  any  downstream 

algorithm,  irrespective  of  whether  that  similarity  is  semantic,  stylistic,  or  merely 

an  artefact  of  the  chosen  vectorisation  method.  Hence,  the  choice  of  vectorisation 

strategy  is  as  consequential  as  the  choice  of  model  architecture. 16 

Contemporary  theory  and  practice  distinguish  the  following  classes  of 

vectorisation  methods: 

1.  Statistical  methods  — based  on  the  frequency  characteristics  of  words  and 

documents: 

− One -Hot  Encoding  — binary  encoding  of  words; 

− Bag  of  Words  (BoW)  — representation  of  documents  as  frequency 

vectors; 

− TF -IDF  — accounting  for  term  weight  adjusted  for  its  rarity  in  the 

corpus. 

2.  Dimensionality  reduction  methods  — enable  latent  semantic  structures  to 

be  revealed: 

− LSA  (Latent  Semantic  Analysis)  based  on  Singular  Value 

Decomposition  (SVD); 

− UMAP/t -SNE  — non -linear  methods  for  visualising 

high -dimensional  data. 

3.  Distributed  representations  (embeddings)  — trainable  dense  vectors 

encoding  semantic  and  syntactic  properties: 

− At  the  word  level:  Word2Vec  (CBOW/Skip -gram),  GloVe,  FastText 

(with  subword  composition); 

− At  the  document  level:  Doc2Vec  (PV -DM/PV -DBOW). 

These  approaches  differ  in  their  capacity  to  generalise,  robustness  to 

sparsity,  computational  complexity  and  requirements  for  the  volume  of  the 

training  corpus.  Whereas  statistical  methods  are  transparent  but  suffer  from  the 

"curse  of  dimensionality"  and  a lack  of  semantic  depth,  embeddings  offer 

compactness  and  a rich  semantic  structure,  yet  demand  considerable 

computational  resources  and  careful  hyperparameter  tuning.  Dimensionality 

reduction  methods  occupy  an  intermediate  position:  they  mitigate  the  sparsity 

problem  of  statistical  representations  and  can  uncover  latent  thematic  groupings, 

but  the  resulting  dimensions  are  often  difficult  to  interpret,  and  the  projection  is 

inherently  lossy.  A separate  consideration  is  the  distinction  between  sparse  and 

dense  representations.  Sparse  vectors  (BoW,  TF -IDF)  are  interpretable  at  the 

feature  level  — one  can  directly  inspect  which  words  contributed  to  a

classification  decision  — whereas  dense  embeddings  distribute  meaning  across 

all  dimensions,  making  individual  features  uninterpretable  while  capturing 

higher -order  semantic  relationships.  An  appreciation  of  the  trade -offs  between 

these  approaches  constitutes  the  basis  for  a considered  choice  of  vectorisation 

method  in  practical  tasks. 17 

3.  Procedure  for  Executing  the  Work 

The  work  is  performed  in  several  successive  stages,  each  directed  at 

attaining  specific  educational  and  research  outcomes. 

Task  1.  Use  the  corpus  of  Russian -language  news  texts  created  during 

Practical  Work  No. 1  as  input  data.  The  corpus  must  contain  no  fewer  than  100 –

150 000  words  and  be  presented  in  JSONL  format  with  the  fields:  headline,  body 

text,  publication  date,  URL  and  category  (if  available).  All  texts  must  be 

preliminarily  cleaned,  tokenised  and  normalised  (with  the  application  of 

lemmatisation  or  stemming  and  stop -word  filtering).  The  inclusion  of  materials 

in  the  languages  of  the  peoples  of  the  Russian  Federation  (Tatar,  Bashkir  and 

others)  is  permitted,  which  broadens  the  linguistic  diversity  of  the  corpus  and 

allows  the  robustness  of  vectorisation  methods  to  multilingualism  to  be  evaluated. 

Task  2.  Develop  the  software  module  classical_vectorizers.py,  which 

implements  statistical  methods  for  transforming  text  into  vector  space.  The 

module  must  support  the  generation  of  representations  based  on  One -Hot 

Encoding,  Bag  of  Words  and  TF -IDF,  as  well  as  work  with  n-grams  (uni -, bi -,

trigrams).  Use  of  the  scikit -learn  library  (in  particular,  CountVectorizer  and 

TfidfVectorizer)  is  mandatory.  Particular  attention  is  paid  to  controlling  the 

dimensionality  and  sparsity  of  the  resulting  matrices,  as  well  as  memory 

efficiency  when  handling  large  corpora. 

Task  3.  Implement  a module  for  revealing  latent  topics  and  semantic 

relations  in  the  corpus  by  means  of  dimensionality  reduction  methods. 

TruncatedSVD  is  applied  (within  LSA)  with  control  of  the  explained  variance 

ratio  (e.g.,  80 –95%),  together  with  the  non -linear  visualisation  methods  UMAP 

and  t-SNE.  The  aim  of  this  stage  is  not  only  to  compress  the  representation  but 

also  to  interpret  the  principal  components,  identifying  dominant  topics  and 

clusters  of  documents.  The  dependence  of  the  quality  of  thematic  separation  on 

the  number  of  components  and  the  chosen  method  is  evaluated. 

Task  4.  Carry  out  an  empirical  comparison  of  statistical  approaches  (One -

Hot,  BoW,  TF -IDF,  n-gram  models)  according  to  formalised  metrics:  

> −

dimensionality  of  the  vector  space;  

> −

degree of sparsity;  

> −

semantic  consistency  (measured  via  cosine  similarity  between  documents 

of  the  same  category);  

> −

processing  time  and  RAM  consumption. 18 

Results  are  compiled  into  a single  CSV  file  (vectorization_metrics.csv)  and 

accompanied  by  a qualitative  interpretation  that  reveals  the  trade -offs  between 

expressiveness  and  computational  efficiency. 

Task  5.  Train  and  compare  modern  embedding  models  on  the  prepared 

corpus.  Both  word -level  models  (Word2Vec  in  CBOW  and  Skip -gram  modes, 

FastText,  GloVe)  and  the  document -level  model  Doc2Vec  (PV -DM  and  PV -

DBOW)  are  investigated.  The  experiment  is  conducted  under  controlled 

conditions:  dimensionality  (100,  200,  300),  context  window  size  (5 –10)  and 

minimum  word  frequency  (5 –10)  are  fixed.  The  toolset  employed  comprises  the 

gensim,  fasttext  and  glove -python  libraries.  Evaluation  includes  vocabulary 

coverage,  quality  of  semantic  analogies,  correlation  with  reference  semantic 

scales,  and  clustering  effectiveness  (measured  by  the  ARI  — Adjusted  Rand 

Index). 

Task  6.  Investigate  the  deep  semantic  structure  of  the  trained  embeddings.

> −

identification  and  quantitative  assessment  of  semantic  axes  (gender, 

sentiment,  social  status)  and  measurement  of  biases;  

> −

analysis  of  the  top -10  nearest  neighbours  and  expert  evaluation  of  their

2.  Theoretical  Prerequisites 

Text  clustering  is  one  of  the  fundamental  unsupervised  learning  tasks  in 

NLP  and  serves  as  the  basis  for  a wide  range  of  applications:  automatic  thematic 

segmentation,  exploratory  data  analysis,  construction  of  recommender  systems 

and  anomaly  detection.  Unlike  classification,  clustering  does  not  employ  labels 

and  enables  the  discovery  of  natural  groupings  in  semantic  space,  reflecting  the 

internal  structure  of  the  data.  However,  the  absence  of  labels  also  means  that  every 

clustering  solution  is,  to  some  degree,  a construct  imposed  by  the  analyst.  The 

same  document  collection,  when  viewed  through  the  lens  of  different  feature 

spaces  or  clustering  algorithms,  may  yield  entirely  different  groupings  — each 

mathematically  valid,  yet  not  all  equally  useful.  This  places  a special  burden  on 

the  evaluation  strategy:  one  must  demonstrate  not  merely  that  a clustering  exists, 

but  that  it  is  stable,  interpretable,  and  aligned  with  some  external  criterion  of 

quality. 

Depending  on  the  problem  formulation  and  data  structure,  the  following 

principal  types  of  algorithms  are  distinguished: 

1.  Centroid -based methods: 

✓ k-means  — minimises  the  within -cluster  sum  of  squared  distances; 

requires  the  number  of  clusters  *k*  to  be  specified  a priori,  is 

sensitive  to  outliers  and  assumes  a spherical  cluster  shape; 

✓ k-medoids  (PAM)  — more  robust  to  outliers,  as  it  selects  actual  data 

points  as  centroids,  but  is  computationally  more  expensive. 

2.  Hierarchical methods: 

✓ Agglomerative  Clustering  builds  a dendrogram  by  successively 

merging  the  nearest  clusters.  It  permits  *k*  to  be  chosen  post  hoc 

and  supports  various  linkage  criteria:  ward  (variance  minimisation), 

average,  complete,  single.  It  is  particularly  useful  for  analysing  the 

hierarchical  thematic  structure  of  news  streams. 

3.  Density -based methods: 25 

✓ DBSCAN  groups  points  lying  in  dense  regions  of  space  and 

automatically  identifies  noise.  It  does  not  require  *k*  to  be  specified, 

but  is  sensitive  to  the  parameters  ε and  min_samples ;

✓ HDBSCAN  generalises  DBSCAN,  performs  better  with  clusters  of 

varying  density  and  automatically  determines  the  number  of  clusters, 

making  it  especially  suitable  for  heterogeneous  corpora. 

4.  Probabilistic methods: 

✓ Gaussian  Mixture  Models  (GMM)  assume  that  the  data  are 

generated  by  a mixture  of  Gaussians.  They  are  appropriate  for 

overlapping  clusters  and  allow  estimation  of  the  probability  of 

belonging  to  each  cluster  — an  important  property  for  tasks  of  fuzzy 

thematic  segmentation. 

5.  Modern and scalable methods: 

✓ Spectral  Clustering  employs  the  eigenvectors  of  the  similarity 

matrix;  it  is  effective  for  non -linearly  separable  clusters  but  requires 

O(n²)  memory; 

✓ BIRCH  is  optimised  for  large  datasets  by  constructing  a CF  tree  and 

performing  single -pass  processing,  which  makes  it  suitable  for  near -

real -time  scenarios. 

Clustering  quality  depends  directly  on  three  key  components:  

> −

the  correctness  and  volume  of  the  corpus  — clusters  reflect  only  what  is 

encoded  in  the  data;  

> −

the  method  of  transforming  text  into  feature  space  — it  is  here  that  the 

“language”  of  the  analysis  is  formed;  

> −

the  strategy  of  evaluation  and  interpretation  — without  rigorous  metric  and 

linguistic  assessment,  clustering  risks  becoming  “digital  astrology.” 

A critical  consideration  in  the  choice  of  vectorisation  method  for  clustering 

is  the  notion  of  distance.  In  sparse,  high -dimensional  spaces  created  by  TF -IDF, 

Euclidean  distance  is  often  uninformative  — all  documents  tend  to  become 

approximately  equidistant  from  one  another,  a phenomenon  related  to  the  curse 

of  dimensionality.  In  such  spaces,  cosine  similarity  is  generally  preferred,  as  it 

captures  the  angle  between  vectors  rather  than  their  magnitude,  aligning  better 

with  the  intuition  that  documents  treating  the  same  topics  should  be  similar 

irrespective  of  their  length.  In  dense  embedding  spaces,  by  contrast,  Euclidean 

distance  may  recover  meaningful  geometry,  but  this  depends  on  how  the 

embedding  model  was  trained.  The  absence  of  a universally  optimal  distance 26 

metric  means  that  the  choice  must  be  justified  empirically  for  each  combination 

of  tokeniser,  vectoriser,  and  clustering  algorithm. 

The  principal  instruments  for  such  empirical  justification  are  internal  and 

external  cluster  quality  metrics.  Internal  metrics  — including  the  Silhouette 

Score,  the  Calinski –Harabasz  Index,  and  the  Davies –Bouldin  Index  — evaluate 

the  coherence  and  separation  of  clusters  using  only  the  data  and  the  computed 

labels,  without  reference  to  any  external  ground  truth.  They  are  essential  when  no 

labelled  corpus  exists,  but  they  are  sensitive  to  the  choice  of  distance  metric  and 

may  favour  algorithms  that  make  similar  geometric  assumptions.  External  metrics 

— such  as  the  Adjusted  Rand  Index  (ARI)  and  Normalized  Mutual  Information 

(NMI)  — require  an  approximate  gold -standard  labelling  (e.g.,  the  topic  category 

of  a news  article)  and  measure  the  degree  to  which  the  discovered  clusters  recover 

that  labelling.  A rigorous  evaluation  protocol  combines  both  types  of  metrics: 

internal  metrics  to  guide  hyperparameter  selection,  and  external  metrics  to  assess 

alignment  with  a meaningful  external  criterion. 

A particularly  important  role  is  played  by  the  text  transformation  pipeline, 

which  includes:  

> −

Tokenisation:  from  simple  splitting  by  whitespace  to  subword  models 

(BPE,  WordPiece,  Unigram)  trained  on  one’s  own  corpus  (Practical  Work 

No. 1).  These  methods  are  especially  important  for  morphologically  rich 

languages  such  as  Russian,  where  a single  lexeme  may  generate  dozens  of 

word  forms.  

> −

Vectorisation: 

✓ Classical  statistical  methods  — Bag -of -Words  and  TF -IDF  (with  1–

3 n-grams)  create  sparse  yet  interpretable  spaces; 

✓ Static  embeddings  trained  on  one’s  own  corpus  (Practical  Work 

No. 2)  — Word2Vec,  FastText,  GloVe  — form  dense  vectors  that 

capture  semantic  relations; 

✓ External  pre -trained  embeddings  (https://fasttext.cc/docs/en/crawl -

vectors.html ),  GloVe  from  Stanford  — provide  universal  but 

potentially  biased  representations. 

Each  “tokeniser  + vectoriser”  combination  forms  a unique  feature  space, 

and  clustering  quality  may  depend  substantially  on  this  choice.  It  is  for  this  reason 

that  a systematic  comparative  analysis  of  all  components  is  not  an  optional 

exercise  but  a methodological  necessity. 

3.  Procedure  for  Executing  the  Work 27 

The  work  is  performed  in  several  successive  stages,  each  directed  at 

attaining  specific  educational  and  research  outcomes. 

Task  1.  Prepare  an  unlabelled  corpus  on  the  basis  of  data  from  Practical 

Work  No. 1.  Sources  may  include  nationwide  Russian  news  portals  (TASS,  RIA 

Novosti,  Lenta.ru , Meduza,  Kommersant),  as  well  as  regional  and  national -

language  resources  (Tatar,  Bashkir  and  others).  The  corpus  volume  must  be  no 

fewer  than  5 000  documents.  Each  document  contains  the  fields:  title , text , date ,

url , category . The  category  field  is  not  used  during  training  (the  task  is  entirely 

unsupervised)  but  may  serve  for  external  evaluation.  Data  are  stored  in  JSONL 

format.  Texts  are  preliminarily  cleaned,  normalised  and  tokenised  in  accordance 

with  the  requirements  of  Practical  Work  No. 1. 

Task  2.  Generate  all  possible  combinations  of  tokenisers  and  vectorisers. 

Tokenisers  used:  naïve  (by  spaces),  lemmatisation  (pymorphy3),  subword  models 

(BPE,  WordPiece,  Unigram  — trained  on  the  corpus).  Vectorisers  used:  classical 

(BoW,  TF -IDF  with  1–3 n-grams,  L2  normalisation),  own  embeddings 

(Word2Vec,  FastText,  GloVe  — trained  on  the  corpus),  external  embeddings 

(cc.ru.300.vec,  GloVe  Stanford).  For  embeddings,  the  document  vector  is  formed 

by  averaging  token  vectors  followed  by  L2  normalisation.  Metadata  for  each 

combination  are  saved  in  JSON  format. 

Task  3.  Develop  the  configurable  software  module 

clustering_algorithms.py,  providing  a unified  interface  for  applying  and 

comparing  the  algorithms:  KMeans,  KMedoids,  AgglomerativeClustering, 

DBSCAN,  HDBSCAN,  GaussianMixture,  SpectralClustering,  Birch.  The 

module  must  support  both  sparse  (for  TF -IDF)  and  dense  (for  embeddings) 

matrices,  as  well  as  automatic  hyperparameter  selection  (e.g.,  silhouette -based 

choice  of  *k*  for  k-means,  grid  search  for  ε and  min_samples  in  DBSCAN). 

Task  4.  Conduct  a rigorous  evaluation  of  cluster  solutions  for  each 

“tokenisation  + vectorisation”  combination.  Internal  metrics  are  employed: 

Silhouette  Score  (primary),  Calinski –Harabasz  Index,  Davies –Bouldin  Index. 

Where  approximate  labelling  exists  (the  category  field),  external  metrics  are 

applied:  Adjusted  Rand  Index  (ARI),  Normalized  Mutual  Information  (NMI).  For 

each  pipeline,  optimal  hyperparameters  are  tuned,  visualisations  are  generated 

(elbow  method,  silhouette  plots,  dendrograms),  and  results  are  stored  in  a

structured  format. 

Task  5.  Empirically  assess  the  influence  of  the  choice  of  tokeniser  and 

vectoriser  on  clustering  quality.  Results  are  presented  as  a summary  table  of  all 

combinations  (tokeniser  × vectoriser  × clustering  algorithm)  with  metrics 28 

indicated.  Additionally,  quality  heatmaps  across  pipelines  and  stability  boxplots 

(under  bootstrap  analysis)  are  constructed,  and  conclusions  are  formulated 

regarding  optimal  configurations  for  different  types  of  corpora  (news,  social 

media,  multilingual). 

Task  6.  Ensure  understanding  of  the  content  of  each  cluster.  For  each 

cluster,  the  top -10  keywords  are  computed  by  TF -IDF  within  the  subcorpus,  and 

the  most  frequent  uni -, bi - and  trigrams  are  analysed.  On  the  basis  of  lexical 

composition,  a semantic  label  is  assigned  (e.g.,  “sport”,  “politics”,  “economics”, 

“healthcare”),  thereby  turning  abstract  labels  into  meaningful  thematic  groups. 

Task  7.  Assess  the  reliability  and  performance  of  the  methods.  A bootstrap 

analysis  of  cluster  stability  is  conducted  (repeated  clustering  on  random 

subsamples),  and  execution  time  and  memory  consumption  are  measured  for 

different  data  volumes  (1K,  5K,  10K  documents).  Based  on  the  results, 

recommendations  are  formulated  for  pipeline  selection  depending  on  available 

resources  and  analysis  goals  (research  vs.  production  scenario). 

Task  8.  Create  an  interactive  web  application  using  Streamlit  or  Gradio, 

enabling  the  user  to:  select  a pipeline  and  clustering  algorithm;  visualise  a 2D 

projection  (UMAP/t -SNE)  with  interactive  highlighting  of  clusters;  view 

keywords  and  sample  documents  for  each  cluster;  compare  two  configurations 

side  by  side;  and  search  for  nearest  neighbours  for  a user -supplied  text. 

Task  9.  Ensure  openness  and  real -time  accessibility.  All  code  is  published 

on  GitHub/GitLab  under  the  MIT  licence.  The  web  application  is  deployed  on 

Hugging  Face  Spaces,  which  guarantees  support  for  Streamlit/Gradio,  use  of  a

custom  Dockerfile,  real -time  inference  mode  and  integration  with  the  Model  Hub. 

Cluster  labels  are  saved  for  each  configuration,  and  Model/Embedding  Cards  are 

created  with  a description,  metrics,  licence  and  code  example. 

Task  10.  Implement  a common  class  with  methods  .fit()  and  .predict(), 

supporting  serialisation  (via  pickle  or  joblib)  and  compatibility  with  the  scikit -

learn  API.  This  approach  facilitates  integration  of  clustering  into  end -to -end  NLP 

pipelines  and  ensures  reproducibility  upon  repeated  execution. 

Task  11.  Develop  a suite  of  unit  tests  using  pytest  that  verify:  

> −

determinism  of  results  under  a fixed  seed;  

> −

correctness  of  saving  and  loading  cluster  labels;  

> −

stability  of  metrics  upon  repeated  execution;  

> −

absence  of  information  leakage  between  folds  (under  bootstrap). 

Tests  are  accompanied  by  the  pinning  of  dependency  versions  in 

requirements.txt  and  a script  for  automated  execution  and  report  generation. 29 

Task  12.  Create  a detailed  meta -description  for  the  entire  artefact:  corpus 

characteristics  (volume,  sources,  languages,  category  balance);  parameters  of 

each  pipeline  (tokeniser,  vectoriser,  algorithm,  hyperparameters);  key  metrics 

(Silhouette,  ARI,  training  time);  limitations  (e.g.,  sensitivity  of  DBSCAN  to 

density);  recommendations  for  use;  and  step -by -step  reproduction  instructions 

(reproduce.md).  This  transforms  the  project  into  a full -fledged  scientific -

engineering  artefact.

7.  Conclusion 

This  work  is  not  merely  a grouping  of  documents  by  similarity.  It  is  an 

investigation  of  the  internal  semantic  topography  of  language,  encoded  in  vector 

spaces.  Here  the  understanding  is  formed  that  clustering  is  not  an  algorithm,  but 

a dialogue  between  the  data,  the  method  and  the  interpreter. 

In  the  course  of  the  work,  you  learn  to  ask  the  key  questions  that  determine 

the  scientific  and  practical  value  of  cluster  analysis:  

> −

“Do  the  clusters  reflect  real  thematic  or  semantic  groups?”  — a question  of 

solution  validity.  

> −

“How  stable  is  this  solution  upon  repeated  runs  or  changes  in  the  data?”  —

a question  of  robustness.  

> −

“Can  one  explain  why  document  A ended  up  in  cluster  X rather  than  Y?”  —

a question  of  interpretability.  

> −

“Is  reproducibility  guaranteed  — from  the  first  token  to  the  final  label?”  —

a question  of  transparency  and  scientific  integrity. 

The  answers  to  these  questions  constitute  the  difference  between 

“clustering”  and  meaningful  thematic  analysis.  It  is  precisely  this  kind  of 

systematic,  critically  minded  and  technically  rigorous  work  with  texts  that 

underpins  modern  systems  for  media  monitoring,  public  opinion  analysis, 

automatic  moderation  and  thematic  archiving. 32

2.  Theoretical  Prerequisites 

Text  classification  is  one  of  the  fundamental  machine  learning  tasks  in  the 

field  of  NLP  and  serves  as  the  basis  for  a broad  spectrum  of  applications: 

sentiment  analysis,  topic  categorisation,  spam  filtering,  genre  identification  and 

others.  Depending  on  the  problem  formulation,  three  main  types  are 

distinguished: 

1.  Binary  classification  — choosing  between  two  mutually  exclusive  labels 

(e.g.,  “positive”  / “negative”  review). 

2.  Multi -class  classification  — selecting  one  label  from  a finite  set  (e.g., 

“politics”,  “economics”,  “sport”,  “culture”). 

3.  Multi -label  classification  — assigning  several  labels  simultaneously  to  a

document,  which  is  especially  relevant  for  news  texts  covering  multiple 

topics. 

In  contemporary  practice,  classical  machine  learning  methods  that  operate 

on  a fixed  feature  space  are  widely  employed  to  solve  these  tasks: 

1.  Linear models: 

− Logistic  regression  — a probabilistic  model  with  L1/L2 

regularisation  capability,  affording  high  interpretability  and 

robustness  to  overfitting  in  sparse  spaces; 

− Support  Vector  Machines  (SVM)  — a powerful  tool  for  linear 

classification  in  sparse  spaces,  particularly  effective  when  using  TF -

IDF. 

2.  Decision -tree -based  ensemble  methods: 

− Random  Forest  — an  ensemble  robust  to  noise  and  outliers,  not 

requiring  feature  scaling; 

− Gradient  boosting  (CatBoost,  XGBoost)  — high -performance 

algorithms  delivering  competitive  quality  even  under  complex  non -

linear  dependencies. 35 

The  quality  of  these  models  depends  directly  on  three  key  components:  

> −

the  correctness  of  labelling  and  class  balance  — imbalance  may  lead  to 

inflated  accuracy  and  reduced  sensitivity  to  minority  classes;  

> −

the  method  of  transforming  text  into  feature  space  — from  the  choice  of 

tokeniser  to  the  type  of  vectorisation;  

> −

the  strategy  for  hyperparameter  tuning  and  estimation  of  generalisation 

ability  — without  stratified  cross -validation,  the  estimate  risks  being 

optimistic. 

These  three  components  are  not  independent.  A carefully  tuned  model 

evaluated  on  poorly  split  data  will  yield  misleading  conclusions;  a perfectly 

balanced  dataset  fed  through  an  inappropriate  vectorisation  pipeline  will 

underperform.  It  is  the  simultaneous  attention  to  all  three  — labelling, 

representation,  and  evaluation  — that  distinguishes  a methodologically  sound 

classification  experiment  from  a superficial  one. 

Unlike  regression  tasks,  classification  does  not  employ  "residuals"; 

however,  error  analysis  is  critically  important.  A single  aggregate  metric  such  as 

accuracy  or  F1  conceals  as  much  as  it  reveals,  particularly  under  class  imbalance, 

where  a model  may  achieve  high  accuracy  simply  by  predicting  the  majority 

class.  A rigorous  evaluation  therefore  examines  not  only  overall  performance  but 

also  per -class  metrics,  confusion  matrices  that  expose  systematically  confused 

category  pairs,  and  the  calibration  of  predicted  probabilities.  The  identification  of 

systematic  biases,  assessment  of  the  calibration  of  predicted  probabilities  (via 

reliability  diagrams  and  Expected  Calibration  Error),  as  well  as  interpretation  of 

the  causes  of  incorrect  predictions  through  techniques  such  as  SHAP,  LIME,  and 

feature  weight  analysis  are  essential.  Only  such  a comprehensive  approach 

permits  the  transition  from  a "working  model"  to  a reliable  and  trusted  system. 

3.  Procedure  for  Executing  the  Work 

The  work  is  performed  in  several  successive  stages,  each  directed  at 

attaining  specific  educational  and  research  outcomes. 

Task  1.  Prepare  a labelled  corpus  on  the  basis  of  data  from  Practical  Work 

No. 1.  Sources  may  include  nationwide  Russian  news  portals  (TASS,  RIA 

Novosti,  Lenta.ru , Meduza,  Kommersant),  as  well  as  regional  and  national -

language  resources  (Tatar,  Bashkir  and  others).  Three types of labelling are 

required:  

> −

Binary: sentiment (“ positive” / “negative”);  

> −

Multi -class:  topic  (“politics”,  “economics”,  “sport”,  “culture”); 36  

> −

Multi -label:  list  of  topics  per  document. 

The  minimum  volume  is  no  fewer  than  10 000  labelled  documents  per  task 

type.  Each  document  contains  the  fields:  title , text , sentiment , category ,

categories . Data  are  stored  in  JSONL  format.  The  split  into  train/validation/test 

is  performed  in  a ratio  of  70/15/15  while  preserving  stratification  with  respect  to 

the  target  variable.  Texts  are  preliminarily  cleaned  and  normalised  in  accordance 

with  the  requirements  of  Practical  Work  No. 1. 

Task  2.  Create  several  independent  feature  spaces.  Tokenisers  used:  naïve 

(by  spaces),  lemmatisation  (pymorphy3),  subword  models  (BPE,  WordPiece, 

Unigram  — trained  in  Work  No. 1).  Vectorisers  used:  TF -IDF  (1 –3 n-grams),  own 

embeddings  (Word2Vec,  FastText,  GloVe  — Work  No. 2),  external  pre -trained 

embeddings  (e.g.,  cc.ru.300.vec  (https://fasttext.cc/docs/en/crawl -vectors.html ), 

GloVe  from  Stanford).  For  embeddings,  the  document  vector  is  formed  by 

averaging  token  vectors.  Metadata  for  each  configuration  are  saved  in  JSON 

format. 

Task  3.  Develop  the  configurable  software  module  classical_classifiers.py, 

providing  a unified  interface  for  training  and  applying  the  models: 

LogisticRegression,  LinearSVC,  RandomForest,  XGBoost,  CatBoost.  The 

module  must  support  both  sparse  (for  TF -IDF)  and  dense  (for  embeddings) 

matrices,  as  well  as  documentation  of  all  parameters  and  methods. 

Task  4.  Conduct  rigorous  model  tuning  using  stratified  5-fold  cross -

validation.  Target  metrics:  F1  (macro)  — for  binary  and  multi -class,  F1  (samples) 

— for  multi -label.  Hyperparameter  search:  Grid  Search  (for  linear  models), 

Random  Search  or  Optuna  (for  boosting).  For  SVM:  when  working  with  sparse 

TF -IDF  representations,  only  the  linear  kernel  (LinearSVC)  is  used;  with  dense 

embeddings,  exploration  of  the  linear,  rbf  and  poly  kernels  is  permitted.  Results 

are  accompanied  by  visualisations:  hyperparameter  heatmaps,  learning  curves, 

convergence  plots.  All  configurations  and  metrics  are  saved  in  JSON. 

Task  5.  Empirically  assess  the  effectiveness  of  models  and  pipelines. 

Metrics  used:  Accuracy,  Precision,  Recall,  F1,  ROC -AUC,  PR -AUC,  Hamming 

Loss  (for  multi -label).  Results  are  presented  in  the  form  of  a summary  table 

(classification_metrics.csv),  ROC/PR  curves  and  boxplots  across  cross -validation 

folds. 

Task  6.  Ensure  model  robustness  under  uneven  label  distribution. 

Strategies  applied:  class_weight='balanced'  (built  into  most  models),  and,  where 

necessary,  SMOTE  (only  on  training  folds,  without  application  to  validation/test). 37 

Quality  assessment  is  conducted  using  PR -AUC  and  F1,  as  more  appropriate 

metrics  under  conditions  of  imbalance. 

Task  7.  Investigate  the  causes  of  incorrect  predictions  and  the  quality  of 

probabilities.  Reliability  diagrams  are  plotted,  and  the  Expected  Calibration  Error 

(ECE)  is  computed.  The  confusion  matrix  is  analysed  to  identify  “problematic” 

class  pairs.  Documents  with  high  confidence  yet  erroneous  predictions  are 

selected.  Interpretation  is  performed  via  SHAP,  LIME  and  feature  weight  analysis 

(for  linear  models).  Token  contributions  are  visualised  using  waterfall/force  plots.

integration  into  production  pipelines. 

Task  11.  Develop  a suite  of  unit  tests  using  pytest  that  verify:

determinism  under  a fixed  seed;

absence  of  data  leakage  between  train  and  test;

stability  of  metrics  upon  repeated  execution. 

Tests  are  accompanied  by  requirements.txt  and  a script  for  automated

2.  Theoretical  Prerequisites 

Automated  Machine  Learning  (AutoML)  aims  to  minimise  manual 

intervention  in  the  process  of  building  ML  models,  including  the  choice  of 

algorithms,  data  preprocessing,  hyperparameter  tuning,  ensembling  and  feature 

selection.  In  text  classification  tasks,  modern  AutoML  systems  exhibit 

fundamentally  different  levels  of  support  for  unstructured  text,  which  directly 

affects  their  applicability  to  morphologically  rich  languages  such  as  Russian. 

Three  principal  approaches  are  distinguished: 

1.  End -to -end  AutoML  with  native  text  support  — systems  that  accept  raw 

text  without  manual  vectorisation  and  automatically  apply  transformers, 

CNNs  or  static  embeddings.  Example:  AutoGluon  (TextPredictor)  —

employs  pre -trained  language  models  (e.g.,  RuBERT)  and  automatically 

adapts  them  to  the  data  via  fine -tuning  or  feature  extraction,  delivering  high 

quality  without  user  intervention. 

2.  AutoML  on  top  of  feature  representations  — frameworks  that  require 

preliminary  transformation  of  text  into  numerical  features  (e.g.,  TF -IDF, 

embeddings),  yet  fully  automate  model  selection  and  tuning.  Examples: 

FLAML,  H2O  AutoML  — effective  under  resource  constraints,  but 

sensitive  to  the  quality  of  the  input  representation. 

3.  Hybrid  approaches  — a combination  of  automatic  optimisation  and  manual 

control  of  key  stages  (e.g.,  choosing  the  tokeniser  from  Work  No. 1, 

substituting  one’s  own  embeddings),  enabling  a balance  to  be  struck 

between  quality,  speed  and  interpretability. 

The  quality  of  AutoML  solutions  depends  on  four  key  factors:  

> −

the  volume  and  quality  of  labelling  — AutoML  does  not  substitute  for  data;  

> −

the  language  support  of  the  framework  — many  systems  are  optimised  for 

English  and  may  ignore  Russian  morphology;  

> −

the  text  preprocessing  strategy  — even  in  end -to -end  systems,  preliminary 

cleaning  can  substantially  affect  the  result;  

> −

the  allocated  computational  budget  — quality  grows  with  time,  but  with 

saturation,  necessitating  a considered  choice  of  the  “quality –speed”  trade -

off. 44 

These  benefits,  however,  are  accompanied  by  notable  limitations.  AutoML 

systems  tend  to  produce  complex  ensembles  whose  decision  logic  is  difficult  to 

inspect,  reducing  interpretability  relative  to  hand -tuned  single  models.  The 

automatic  search  procedure,  if  left  unconstrained,  may  over -optimise  to  the 

peculiarities  of  the  validation  set,  yielding  models  that  fail  to  generalise. 

Furthermore,  the  computational  cost  of  the  search  itself  can  be  considerable,  and 

the  environmental  footprint  of  an  extensive  AutoML  run  may  outweigh  the 

marginal  gain  in  accuracy  over  a well -tuned  baseline.  For  these  reasons,  AutoML 

is  best  understood  not  as  a replacement  for  expert  judgement,  but  as  an 

accelerator:  it  rapidly  establishes  a competitive  baseline,  freeing  the  practitioner 

to  focus  on  domain -specific  considerations  — interpretability,  fairness,  and 

deployment  constraints  — that  no  automated  system  can  address. 

An  appreciation  of  these  trade -offs  forms  the  basis  for  the  rational  choice 

of  an  AutoML  tool  in  practical  tasks.

2.  Theoretical  Prerequisites 

Text  classification  using  deep  learning  has  passed  through  several 

evolutionary  stages,  each  addressing  the  fundamental  limitations  of  the  preceding 

generation.  Depending  on  the  problem  formulation,  three  main  types  of  text 

classification  are  distinguished: 

1.  Binary  classification  — choosing  between  two  mutually  exclusive  labels 

(e.g.,  “positive”  / “negative”  review). 

2.  Multi -class  classification  — selecting  one  label  from  a finite  set  (e.g., 

“politics”,  “economics”,  “sport”,  “culture”). 

3.  Multi -label  classification  — assigning  several  labels  simultaneously  to  a

document,  which  is  especially  relevant  for  news  texts  covering  multiple 

topics. 

To  solve  these  tasks  within  the  framework  of  deep  learning,  architectures 

differing  in  their  capacity  to  model  sequence,  context  and  semantic  dependencies 

are  applied.  Three key directions are distinguished: 

1.  Basic neural network models: 

− Multilayer  Perceptron  (MLP)  — operates  on  fixed  vector 

representations  of  text  (e.g.,  averaged  embeddings)  and  serves  as  a

baseline  for  comparison; 

− Convolutional  Neural  Networks  (CNN)  — apply  1D  convolutions  to 

identify  local  semantic  patterns  (e.g.,  key  phrases);  they  are  effective 

under  limited  computational  resources  and  with  short  texts. 

2.  Recurrent architectures and attention: 52 

− RNN,  LSTM,  GRU  — model  the  sequential  nature  of  text, 

addressing  the  long -range  dependency  problem  by  means  of  gates  in 

LSTM/GRU; 

− Bidirectional  (BiLSTM/BiGRU)  and  stacked  RNNs  — extract 

context  in  both  directions  and  at  several  levels  of  abstraction,  which 

is  especially  important  for  languages  with  free  word  order; 

− Encoder -decoder  with  an  attention  mechanism  — allows  the  model 

to  focus  dynamically  on  relevant  parts  of  the  text  when  computing 

the  final  representation,  significantly  enhancing  both  quality  and 

interpretability. 

3.  Transformer models: 

− The  abandonment  of  recurrence  in  favour  of  self -attention  enables 

parallel  processing  and  the  modelling  of  global  dependencies, 

making  transformers  the  dominant  architecture  in  NLP; 

− For  Russian,  the  most  effective  are:  RuBERT  (DeepPavlov), 

ruRoBERTa  (Sber  AI),  DistilRuBERT,  rubert -tiny,  mDeBERTa -v3 -

base  — all  pre -trained  on  large  Russian -language  corpora  and 

supporting  fine -tuning; 

− All  models  are  applied  via  fine -tuning,  where  the  pre -trained  weights 

are  adapted  to  the  specific  classification  task  by  adding  a

classification  head  configured  for  the  corresponding  task  type 

(binary,  multi -class  or  multi -label). 

Each  successive  architectural  generation  has  brought  not  only  higher 

accuracy  but  also  a different  kind  of  relationship  between  the  researcher  and  the 

model.  An  MLP  operating  on  averaged  embeddings  is  straightforward  to  train  and 

its  decision  surface  can  be  inspected  through  feature  weights,  but  it  discards  all 

word -order  information.  An  LSTM  with  attention  retains  order  and  offers  a

glimpse,  via  attention  weights,  into  which  parts  of  the  input  influenced  the 

prediction.  A transformer  gives  the  richest  representations  and  the  most 

interpretable  attention  patterns,  yet  it  also  demands  the  most  computational 

resources  and  the  greatest  care  to  avoid  overfitting.  Choosing  an  architecture  is 

therefore  a decision  about  what  trade -off  one  is  willing  to  accept:  simplicity 

against  expressiveness,  transparency  against  performance,  computational  cost 

against  marginal  gain. 

The  quality  of  these  models  depends  directly  on:  

> −

the  correctness  of  labelling  and  class  balance  — imbalance  is  especially 

critical  for  deep  models,  which  are  prone  to  overfitting; 53  

> −

the  preprocessing  strategy  — for  transformers,  lemmatisation  or  stemming 

is  not  recommended,  as  the  model  is  optimised  for  the  original  word  forms;  

> −

the  choice  of  loss  function,  regularisation  strategy  and  evaluation  metrics 

— simple  accuracy  is  often  misleading  under  conditions  of  imbalance. 

Unlike  classical  methods,  deep  models  require  visual  analysis  of  internal 

mechanisms:  neuron  activations,  attention  maps,  embedding  projections. 

Contextual  error  analysis  is  also  critically  important,  since  the  errors  of  deep 

models  are  often  systematic  but  semantically  subtle  in  nature  (e.g.,  confusion 

between  “sanctions”  and  “support”  in  political  texts).  For  precisely  this  reason, 

interpretability  is  not  an  option  but  an  obligatory  part  of  modern  NLP  research.

2.  Theoretical  Prerequisites 

Modern  NLP  systems  are  rarely  confined  to  a single  task.  In  real -world 

scenarios  — from  news  analysis  to  processing  customer  enquiries  — a

combination  of  methods  for  extracting,  structuring  and  generating  information  is 

required.  The  effectiveness  of  such  systems  depends  not  only  on  the  quality  of 

individual  components,  but  also  on  their  compatibility,  robustness  to  errors  and 

interpretability. 

Within  the  framework  of  the  present  work,  four  fundamental  directions  are 

distinguished,  each  possessing  its  own  methodological  and  architectural 

specificity: 

1.  Information  extraction: 

– NER  (Named  Entity  Recognition)  — a sequence  labelling  task:  each  token  is 

assigned  a tag  (PER,  ORG,  LOC,  etc.). 

– Key  phrases  — may  be  extracted  statistically  (TF -IDF,  RAKE),  via  ranking 

(YAKE),  or  with  the  help  of  transformers  (KeyBERT). 61 

– Classical  approaches:  CRF  (Conditional  Random  Fields)  atop  handcrafted 

features. 

– Deep  models:  BiLSTM -CRF  — the  gold  standard  prior  to  the  transformer  era. 

– Modern  solutions:  token  classification  heads  atop  RuBERT,  ruRoBERTa, 

trained  on  RuNNE  or  custom  annotations. 

2.  Text  generation: 

– Extractive  summarisation  — selection  of  the  most  relevant  sentences  (often  via 

TextRank,  BERTScore  or  trained  rankers). 

– Abstractive  summarisation  — generation  of  new  formulations.  Requires 

encoder -decoder  models:  BART,  T5,  mT5. 

– Paraphrasing  — a special  case  of  generation,  often  addressed  by  fine -tuning  T5 

with  the  prefix  “paraphrase:  ”. 

– Contemporary  Russian -language  models:  ruT5,  flan -t5 -base -ru,  mT5 -large  —

all  support  zero -shot  and  few -shot  configuration. 

3.  Semantic  analysis: 

– Representation  of  texts  as  dense  vectors  (sentence  embeddings)  via  SBERT, 

ruSBERT,  LaBSE. 

– Comparison  via  cosine  distance. 

– Applications:  duplicate  detection,  semantic  search,  clustering,  recommender 

systems. 

– Calibration  of  similarity  is  critically  important:  not  all  pairs  with  high  cosine 

similarity  are  semantically  equivalent. 

4.  Hybrid  architectures: 

– Pipelines  where  the  output  of  one  model  (e.g.,  NER)  becomes  the  input  of 

another  (e.g.,  a summariser  augmented  with  named  entities). 

– Use  of  a single  transformer  with  multiple  heads  (multi -task  learning)  — an 

advanced  yet  effective  approach  that  reduces  overall  complexity  and  error 

propagation. 

– Strict  coordination  of  data  formats,  tokeniser  compatibility  and  management  of 

inference  latencies  is  required. 

The  practical  work  is  structured  so  that  these  four  directions  are  not  studied 

in  isolation.  The  student  first  implements  and  evaluates  each  component 

independently,  then  combines  them  into  an  end -to -end  pipeline.  This  progression 

— from  isolated  evaluation  to  integrated  system  — mirrors  the  trajectory  of  a

real -world  NLP  project  and  cultivates  the  ability  to  diagnose  whether  an  error 

originates  in  extraction,  generation,  retrieval,  or  the  interfaces  between  them. 62 

The  quality  of  a solution  depends  not  only  on  the  model,  but  also  on  the 

correct  formulation  of  the  task,  the  choice  of  metric  and  the  analysis  of  error 

specificity  — for  instance,  hallucinations  in  generation  (introduction  of  facts 

absent  from  the  source)  or  omissions  in  NER  (especially  for  rare  or  ambiguous 

entities,  such  as  "Sber"  as  ORG  vs.  LOC).  For  precisely  this  reason, 

interpretability  and  error  analysis  are  not  an  option  but  an  obligatory  part  of 

modern  NLP  research.

2.  Theoretical  Prerequisites 

Modern  NLP  systems  are  increasingly  built  not  from  scratch  but  as 

assemblies  of  ready -made,  standardised  components  accessible  through  open 

platforms.  Hugging  Face  offers  precisely  such  a development  model  — modular, 

reproducible  and  user -oriented.  Its  central  idea  is  a unified  API  for  any  NLP  task, 

which  removes  barriers  between  tasks  and  enables  the  straightforward 

construction  of  hybrid  pipelines.  The  effectiveness  of  such  systems  depends  not 70 

only  on  the  quality  of  individual  components,  but  also  on  their  compatibility, 

robustness  to  errors  and  interpretability. 

Within  the  framework  of  the  present  work,  four  fundamental  directions  are 

distinguished,  uniting  the  11  official  Hugging  Face  tasks  by  methodological  and 

architectural  affinity: 

1.  Information extraction: 

− Text  Classification  — assigning  a document  to  one  or  more 

categories  (news,  sport,  economics). 

− NER  (Named  Entity  Recognition)  — a sequence  labelling  task:  each 

token  is  assigned  a tag  (PER,  ORG,  LOC,  etc.). 

− Feature  Extraction  — obtaining  dense  vector  representations  of  texts 

(sentence  embeddings)  via  SBERT,  ruSBERT,  E5. 

− Classical  approaches:  CRF,  TF -IDF  + RAKE  (as  baselines  for 

comparison). 

− Deep  models:  BiLSTM -CRF  — the  gold  standard  prior  to  the 

transformer  era. 

− Modern  solutions:  token  classification  heads  atop  RuBERT, 

ruRoBERTa;  feature  extraction  via  multilingual -E5. 

2.  Text generation: 

− Summarisation  — condensing  text  to  its  essence  (extractive  and 

abstractive). 

− Translation  — converting  text  from  one  language  to  another. 

− Text  Generation  — creating  new  text  based  on  a prompt  or  context. 

− Fill -Mask  — restoring  missing  context  ([MASK]). 

− Contemporary  Russian -language  models:  ruT5,  flan -t5 -base -ru, 

mT5 -large,  Helsinki -NLP  — all  support  zero -shot  and  few -shot 

configuration. 

3.  Semantic analysis: 

− Sentence  Similarity  — computing  cosine  distance  between 

embeddings  of  two  sentences. 

− Text  Ranking  — assessing  the  relevance  of  documents  to  a query 

(query/document  encoding). 

− Zero -shot  classification  — classification  into  arbitrary  labels  without 

training,  via  NLI  models. 

− Question  Answering  — extracting  a precise  answer  from  a given 

context. 71 

− Calibration  is  critically  important:  not  all  pairs  with  high  similarity 

are  semantically  equivalent;  not  all  answers  in  QA  are  factually 

correct. 

4.  Hybrid architectures: 

− Pipelines  where  the  output  of  one  task  (e.g.,  an  embedding  from 

Feature  Extraction)  becomes  the  input  of  another  (ranking  or 

clustering). 

− Using  a unified  platform  (pipeline  API)  lowers  the  barriers  between 

tasks  and  simplifies  composition. 

− Strict  coordination  of  data  formats,  tokeniser  compatibility  and 

management  of  inference  latencies  is  required. 

What  distinguishes  the  Hugging  Face  ecosystem  from  a mere  collection  of 

pre -trained  models  is  the  standardisation  it  imposes  across  all  these  tasks.  The 

same  pipeline  API  — with  methods  such  as  load,  predict,  and  save  — serves  text 

classification,  NER,  summarisation,  and  translation  alike.  For  the  practitioner, 

this  means  that  switching  from  one  task  to  another  does  not  require  learning  a

new  library  or  data  format;  it  requires  only  selecting  a different  model  and 

interpreting  a different  output.  This  standardisation  is  pedagogically  valuable 

because  it  allows  the  student  to  focus  on  the  conceptual  differences  between  tasks 

— what  makes  generation  different  from  extraction,  what  makes  zero -shot 

classification  fragile  — rather  than  on  the  mechanical  details  of  loading  and 

inference.  The  present  work  is  structured  to  exploit  this  property:  by 

implementing  all  eleven  tasks  within  a single  interface,  the  student  directly 

experiences  both  the  power  and  the  limits  of  a unified  API. 

The  quality  of  a solution  depends  not  only  on  the  model,  but  also  on  the 

correct  formulation  of  the  task,  the  choice  of  metric  and  the  analysis  of  error 

specificity  — for  instance,  hallucinations  in  generation  (introduction  of  facts 

absent  from  the  source),  instability  of  zero -shot  labels  under  synonymous 

substitution  of  classes,  or  omissions  in  NER  for  rare  entities  ("Tatneft"  as  ORG). 

For  precisely  this  reason,  interpretability  and  error  analysis  are  not  an  option  but 

an  obligatory  part  of  modern  NLP  research. 

3.  Procedure  for  Executing  the  Work 

The  work  is  performed  in  several  successive  stages,  each  directed  at 

attaining  specific  educational  and  research  outcomes. 

Task  1.  A unified  experimental  corpus  is  prepared  using  data  derived  from 

Practical  Work  No. 1.  The  data  sources  comprise  nationwide  Russian  news 72 

portals —TASS,  RIA  Novosti,  Lenta.ru , Meduza,  and  Kommersant —as  well  as 

regional  and  national -language  resources.  The  corpus  is  assembled  to  a volume 

of  no  fewer  than  10,000  documents,  each  represented  in  JSONL  format  and 

containing  the  fields:  title,  body  text,  URL,  and  date.  Additionally,  a synthetic 

dataset  is  constructed  for  the  task  of  Table  Question  Answering,  consisting  of 

triples  comprising  a table,  context,  and  question.  All  textual  materials  are 

preserved  in  their  original  form,  without  the  application  of  lemmatisation  or 

stemming. 

Task  2.  Several  independent  yet  mutually  compatible  data  representations 

are  created,  governed  by  the  requirements  of  the  downstream  task.  For  the 

majority  of  transformer -based  tasks,  tokenisation  is  performed  via 

AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained.  For  statistical  methods —specifically  RAKE  and 

YAKE —raw  text  subjected  to  minimal  cleaning  is  employed.  All  representations 

are  serialised  together  with  their  associated  metadata  in  JSON  format. 

Task  3.  A configurable  software  module,  hf_pipelines.py,  is  developed  to 

provide  a unified  interface  spanning  all  eleven  tasks.  The following components 

are supported:  

> −

Classification:  cointegrated/rubert -tiny2,  DeepPavlov/rubert -base -cased;  

> −

Named  Entity  Recognition  (NER):  DeepPavlov/rubert -base -cased -ner, 

mdeberta -v3 -base;  

> −

Question  Answering  (QA):  deepset/roberta -base -squad2,  AlexKay/xlm -

roberta -large -qa -multilingual;  

> −

Zero -shot  Classification:  facebook/bart -large -mnli, 

MoritzLaurer/mDeBERTa -v3 -base -xnli -multilingual;  

> −

Translation:  Helsinki -NLP/opus -mt -ru -en,  facebook/nllb -200;  

> −

Summarisation:  IlyaGusev/rut5_base_sum_gen,  cointegrated/ruT5 -base;  

> −

Feature  Extraction:  intfloat/multilingual -e5 -large,  sentence -

transformers/paraphrase -multilingual -MiniLM;  

> −

Text  Generation:  sberbank -ai/ruGPT -3.5 -13B  (accessed  via  the  Inference 

API);  

> −

Fill -Mask:  DeepPavlov/rubert -base -cased;  

> −

Semantic  Textual  Similarity:  cointegrated/ruRoBERTa -large -sts;  

> −

Ranking:  intfloat/multilingual -e5 -large  (employed  for  query/document 

encoding). 

The  module  provides  GPU  acceleration,  serialisation  of  outputs,  thorough 

parameter  documentation,  and  full  reproducibility. 73 

Task  4.  A rigorous  evaluation  procedure  is  conducted,  incorporating 

visualisation  of  the  evaluation  process.  A dedicated  hold -out  validation  set, 

comprising  15%  of  the  data,  is  employed  for  monitoring  purposes.  The target 

metrics are as follows:  

> −

Classification  and  NER:  macro -averaged  F1  score;  

> −

Question  Answering:  Exact  Match  and  F1  score;  

> −

Summarisation:  ROUGE -1,  ROUGE -2,  and  ROUGE -L;  

> −

Translation: BLEU;  

> −

Zero -shot  Classification:  accuracy  on  a manually  curated  golden  subset;  

> −

Semantic  tasks:  Spearman's  rank  correlation  coefficient  and  accuracy@k. 

Diagnostic  plots  are  produced,  encompassing  metric  curves,  error 

distributions,  and  hyperparameter  heatmaps.  All  configurations  and  metric  values 

are  persisted  in  both  JSON  format  and  the  Hugging  Face -native  format. 

Task  5.  An  empirical  assessment  of  the  effectiveness  of  the  various 

architectures  and  pipelines  is  performed.  The  results  are  presented  in  the  form  of 

a summary  table  (hf_metrics.csv),  box  plots  illustrating  variability  across  cross -

validation  folds,  UMAP  and  t-SNE  projections  of  embedding  spaces,  and 

illustrative  examples  of  generated  text  and  NER  annotation  markup. 

Task  6.  Model  robustness  is  evaluated  under  challenging  conditions, 

specifically:  the  presence  of  rare  and  out -of -vocabulary  entities  in  NER,  long -

form  documents  in  summarisation,  ambiguous  phrasing  in  question  answering, 

and  domain  shift  in  zero -shot  classification.  The  following  techniques  are  applied 

to  mitigate  these  challenges:  hallucination  filtering  by  means  of  natural  language 

inference  models,  calibration  of  similarity  scores  via  isotonic  regression,  and

2.  Theoretical  Prerequisites 

Modern  LLMs  have  fundamentally  altered  the  approach  to  NLP:  instead  of 

narrowly  specialised  models,  a unified  generative  architecture,  adapted  to  the 

task,  is  increasingly  employed.  The  choice  of  model,  however,  is  not  only  a

question  of  quality  but  also  a question  of  scientific  ethics,  engineering  robustness 

and  legal  security. 

1.  Classification  of  LLMs  by  accessibility: 

− Open -weight  models  (Llama  3,  Mistral,  Qwen,  DeepSeek,  Phi -3, 

ruT5)  — weights  are  open;  local  execution,  modification  and 

publication  are  permitted.  This  is  the  only  class  that  ensures 

reproducibility,  interpretability  and  control  over  inference. 

− Commercial  API -only  models  (GigaChat,  YandexGPT)  — closed, 

accessible  only  through  cloud  services,  irreproducible,  opaque  and 

not  recommended  as  the  foundation  of  a scientific  or  engineering 

project. 

2.  Efficient fine -tuning: 

− LoRA  (Low -Rank  Adaptation)  — trains  low -rank  matrices  instead 

of  full  weights,  reducing  memory  consumption  and  training  time  by 

orders  of  magnitude. 81 

− QLoRA  — combines  4-bit  quantisation  with  LoRA,  enabling  fine -

tuning  of  7B  models  on  GPUs  with  16 –24  GB  of  memory  (e.g.,  RTX 

3090,  4090).  This  makes  fine -tuning  accessible  in  academic  and 

start -up  environments. 

Full  fine -tuning  has  traditionally  been  regarded  as  the  gold  standard  for 

adapting  language  models,  since  it  updates  all  model  parameters  and  yields 

maximum  quality.  For  large  models  (≥3B  parameters),  however,  it  requires 

hundreds  of  gigabytes  of  GPU  memory  and  becomes  technically  and 

economically  infeasible  in  educational  settings  and  the  majority  of  industrial 

projects.  It  is  for  this  reason  that  parameter -efficient  adaptation  methods  — LoRA 

and  QLoRA  — have  superseded  full  fine -tuning,  preserving  95 –99%  of  the 

quality  while  dramatically  reducing  resource  requirements. 

3.  RAG architecture: 

− Divides  the  task  into  a retriever  (search  for  relevant  fragments)  and 

a generator  (formulation  of  an  answer  based  on  these  fragments). 

− Permits  knowledge  to  be  updated  without  retraining,  reduces 

hallucinations  and  makes  the  system  interpretable  (by  displaying 

sources). 

− The  quality  of  the  system  is  determined  primarily  by  the  retriever, 

not  the  generator:  even  a powerful  LLM  will  produce  a false  answer 

if  it  receives  incorrect  context. 

The  retriever  itself  can  be  implemented  using  sparse  methods  (BM25), 

dense  methods  (embedding -based  similarity  search  with  models  such  as 

multilingual -e5),  or  hybrid  combinations  of  both.  The  choice  of  retriever,  together 

with  the  chunking  strategy  that  determines  how  documents  are  segmented  before 

indexing,  is  often  more  consequential  for  end -to -end  performance  than  the  choice 

of  the  generator  model.  Retrieval  quality  is  measured  using  metrics  such  as 

recall@k  and  Mean  Reciprocal  Rank  (MRR),  which  capture  the  system's  ability 

to  place  relevant  documents  among  the  top  retrieved  results.  A RAG  pipeline  is 

therefore  best  understood  as  an  information  retrieval  problem  with  a language 

model  interface,  not  merely  as  a generative  model  with  added  context. 

4.  Ethical and methodological imperatives: 

− Reproducibility  — the  foundation  of  the  scientific  method.  Using 

closed  APIs  violates  it. 

− Licensing  purity  — publication  of  a project  is  possible  only  when 

models  with  permissive  licences  (Apache  2.0,  MIT)  are  used. 82 

− Responsibility  — the  developer  is  obliged  to  clearly  indicate 

sources,  limitations  and  the  degree  of  trust  in  predictions. 

It  is  critically  important  to  understand:  the  best  model  is  not  the  largest,  but 

the  one  that  is  open,  adapted  to  the  domain,  honestly  evaluated  and  furnished  with 

metadata. 

3.  Procedure  for  Executing  the  Work 

The  work  is  performed  in  several  successive  stages,  each  directed  at 

attaining  specific  educational  and  research  outcomes. 

Task  1.  The  domain  knowledge  base  is  constructed  using  data  derived  from 

Practical  Work  No. 1.  The  data  sources  comprise  nationwide  Russian  news 

portals:  TASS,  RIA  Novosti,  Lenta.ru , Meduza,  and  Kommersant.  The  corpus  is 

assembled  to  a volume  of  no  fewer  than  10,000  documents,  each  represented  in 

JSONL  format  and  containing  the  following  fields:  title,  body  text,  source  URL, 

date  of  publication,  and  language  code.  All  textual  materials  are  preserved  in  their 

original  form  without  modification. 

Task  2.  Semantically  meaningful  chunks  are  generated  for  the  retrieval 

module.  The  source  documents  are  segmented  into  fragments  spanning  300 –500 

tokens  whilst  preserving  sentence  boundaries,  achieved  by  means  of  the  nltk  or 

spaCy  libraries.  Each  fragment  is  augmented  with  the  following  metadata:  source 

identifier,  title,  date,  URL,  and  language  code.  The  resulting  collection  is 

subjected  to  filtering:  fragments  containing  fewer  than  50  words  are  discarded,  as 

are  those  exhibiting  a high  level  of  noise,  including  advertising  blocks  and 

boilerplate  phrasing.  The  final  collection  is  serialised  in  JSONL  format. 

Task  3.  A software  module,  llm_hub.py,  is  developed  to  provide  a unified 

interface  for  the  execution  of  open -weight  large  language  models.  Support  is 

implemented  for  the  following  architectures:  Llama -3-8B -Instruct,  Mistral -7B -

Instruct,  Qwen1.5 -7B -Chat,  DeepSeek -7B -Base,  Phi -3-mini -4k -instruct,  and 

ruT5 -base.  Two  operational  modalities  are  provided:  zero -shot  inference  utilising 

the  transformers  and  bitsandbytes  libraries,  and  CPU -bound  execution  employing 

the  GGUF  format  and  the  llama.cpp  framework.  A strict  prohibition  on  hard 

dependency  upon  the  GigaChat  API  is  enforced.  GPU  acceleration,  result 

serialisation,  and  reproducibility  are  assured. 

Task  4.  Comparative  fine -tuning  is  conducted  for  the  task  of  headline 

generation,  employing  two  distinct  paradigms —full  fine -tuning  and  QLoRA —in 

order  to  evaluate  the  quality -versus -resource  trade -off.  The  dataset  consists  of 

(body  text,  headline)  pairs  extracted  from  the  news  corpus.  For  full  fine -tuning, 83 

the  models  ruT5 -base  or  Phi -3-mini -4k -instruct  are  employed,  where  technically 

feasible;  for  QLoRA,  the  models  ruT5 -base,  Qwen1.5 -0.5B,  or  Mistral -7B -

Instruct  are  utilised.  The  QLoRA  configuration  specifies  4-bit  quantisation,  a rank 

of  r=8,  a scaling  factor  of  alpha=16,  and  the  training  of  attention  layers.  ROUGE -

L serves  as  the  uniform  evaluation  metric  across  all  experiments.  The  following 

aspects  are  subjected  to  comparative  analysis:  training  duration,  peak  GPU 

memory  utilisation,  convergence  rate,  and  robustness  to  overfitting.  All  artefacts 

are  preserved:  full  model  checkpoints  (for  full  fine -tuning),  LoRA  adapters  (for 

QLoRA),  training  logs,  hyperparameter  settings,  and  reproduction  scripts. 

Task  5.  A Retrieval -Augmented  Generation  (RAG)  system  is  constructed. 

The  retriever  component  is  built  upon  the  FAISS  library,  employing  the 

intfloat /multilingual -e5 -large  embedding  model —or,  alternatively,  rubert -tiny2 

where  inference  latency  is  prioritised.  The  generator  component  is  instantiated 

with  an  arbitrary  open -weight  large  language  model.  The  prompt  template 

supplied  to  the  generator  is  formulated  as  follows:  "Use  only  information  from 

the  context.  If  the  information  is  absent,  answer:  'I  do  not  know.'"  The  system 

outputs  comprise  the  generated  answer  accompanied  by  a list  of  relevant  chunks 

together  with  their  associated  metadata. 

Task  6.  A comprehensive  quality  assessment  of  the  resulting  system  is 

undertaken.  The  set  of  automatic  evaluation  metrics  encompasses  ROUGE -L and 

BERTScore.  Factual  accuracy  is  assessed  through  manual  annotation  of  50 

responses  on  a five -point  Likert  scale.  The  hallucination  rate  is  computed,  defined 

as  the  proportion  of  statements  lacking  corroboration  within  the  retrieved  chunks. 

A comparative  evaluation  is  performed  between  the  RAG  configuration  and  a

standalone  LLM  baseline.  The  influence  of  the  choice  of  retriever  (E5 -based 

dense  retrieval  versus  BM25  sparse  retrieval)  and  generator  model  size  (Llama  3

versus  Phi -3)  upon  overall  performance  is  examined. 

Task  7.  An  investigation  into  the  underlying  causes  of  errors  and  their 

interpretation  is  carried  out.  Hallucination  matrices  are  constructed, 

disaggregated  by  error  type:  factual  inaccuracies,  date  distortion,  and  named 

entity  errors.  Retriever  failures,  whereby  an  incorrect  context  leads  to  a false 

answer,  are  analysed.  Visualisation  techniques  are  applied:  UMAP  projections  of

2.  Theoretical  Prerequisites 

General  benchmarks  measure  the  broad  cognitive  abilities  of  LLMs,  yet  do 

not  reflect  their  suitability  for  practical  application  in  professional  or  educational 

contexts.  Specialised  benchmarks  fill  this  gap  by  focusing  on  domain  competence 

rather  than  general  erudition. 

The  need  for  domain  evaluations  is  driven  by  three  key  factors.  First, 

formal  accuracy:  adherence  to  orthographic,  mathematical  or  legal  norms,  where 

an  error  in  a single  digit  or  an  incorrect  reference  to  an  article  of  law  renders  the 

answer  completely  invalid,  even  if  the  model’s  general  reasoning  appears 

plausible.  Second,  structural  compliance:  many  professional  answers  must 

contain  strictly  defined  elements  — for  instance,  the  chain  “thesis  → argument 

→ example”  in  an  examination  essay  — which  is  not  captured  by  general  metrics 

oriented  towards  semantic  similarity.  Third,  contextual  adequacy:  models  pre -

trained  on  international  corpora  often  ignore  local  realities,  whether  the  specifics 

of  Russian  legislation,  the  content  of  school  curricula  or  the  morphology  of  the 

Tatar  language,  rendering  their  conclusions  formally  correct  but  practically 

useless. 

The  architecture  of  a specialised  benchmark  is  built  around  several 

interconnected  components.  Its  foundation  is  a corpus  of  tasks  furnished  with  a

multi -level  taxonomy  (subject  → type  → difficulty),  which  permits  results  to  be 

analysed  at  any  granularity.  Each  task  is  accompanied  by  a reference  answer;  for 

problems  that  admit  variability,  alternative  formulations  or  ranges  of  permissible 

values  (e.g.,  ±1  for  a numerical  answer  in  mathematics)  are  recorded.  The 90 

evaluation  criteria  are  formalised  either  as  machine -interpretable  rules  or  as 

annotated  templates  describing  the  obligatory  and  optional  elements  of  an  answer. 

These  criteria  drive  the  evaluation  pipeline  — an  automated  system  that  compares 

the  model’s  answer  with  the  reference  and  computes  a set  of  metrics  adapted  to 

the  task  type:  from  simple  accuracy  for  multiple -choice  items  to  coverage, 

factuality  and  structural  compliance  for  extended  answers. 

The  construction  of  such  a benchmark  raises  a methodological  challenge 

that  does  not  arise  in  general -purpose  evaluation:  the  scoring  of  extended,  free -

text  answers.  A short  answer  can  be  marked  correct  or  incorrect  by  direct 

comparison  with  the  reference;  an  essay  or  a diagnostic  justification  cannot.  The 

present  work  therefore  distinguishes  between  two  evaluation  regimes.  Short 

answers  are  assessed  through  normalised  exact  match  or  numerical  proximity. 

Extended  answers  are  assessed  through  a combination  of  three  methods:  rule -

based  checks  that  verify  the  presence  of  obligatory  structural  elements;  model -

based  comparison  using  BERTScore  against  the  reference;  and  a fine -tuned 

classifier  trained  to  predict  whether  individual  scoring  criteria  are  satisfied.  Each 

method  has  different  strengths  and  failure  modes,  and  they  are  designed  to  be 

used  in  combination,  not  in  isolation.  The  student's  task  includes  not  only 

implementing  these  evaluators  but  also  analysing  where  they  agree,  where  they 

diverge,  and  what  those  divergences  reveal  about  the  limits  of  automatic 

evaluation. 

The  range  of  potential  subject  domains  for  applying  such  an  approach  is 

exceedingly  broad.  In  education,  these  are  OGE  and  EGE  tasks  with  clear 

assessment  criteria  developed  by  FIPI.  In  medicine,  questions  on  clinical 

protocols  and  diagnostics,  where  the  cost  of  error  is  especially  high.  In  law,  tasks 

testing  knowledge  of  the  Civil  Code  of  the  Russian  Federation  with  the  mandatory 

requirement  to  cite  specific  articles.  For  low -resource  languages,  linguistic  tasks 

in  Tatar  or  Bashkir,  enabling  an  assessment  of  the  extent  to  which  a model 

accounts  for  morphological  specificity.  It  should  be  stressed  that  the  OGE  in  the 

present  work  serves  exclusively  as  a methodological  example,  demonstrating  the 

principles  of  constructing  a benchmark  in  a domain  with  formal  rules.  The 

developed  approach  is  fully  transferable  to  any  area  possessing  clear  assessment 

criteria. 

3.  Procedure  for  Executing  the  Work 

The  work  is  performed  in  several  successive  stages,  each  directed  at 

attaining  specific  educational  and  research  outcomes. 91 

Task  1.  A corpus  of  domain -specific  assessment  items  is  constructed  on  the 

basis  of  open -access  sources.  For  the  subject  area  of  Basic  State  Examination 

(OGE)  preparation,  the  sources  comprise  publicly  released  demonstration 

versions  and  the  official  item  bank  maintained  by  the  Federal  Institute  of 

Pedagogical  Measurements  (FIPI);  for  jurisprudence,  the  Civil  Code  of  the 

Russian  Federation  is  employed;  for  low -resource  languages,  relevant  textbooks 

and  national  corpora  are  utilised.  The  corpus  is  assembled  to  a volume  of  no  fewer 

than  300  items,  each  represented  in  JSONL  format  and  containing  the  following 

fields:  item  identifier,  subject,  item  type,  difficulty  level,  prompt,  reference 

answer,  and  scoring  criteria. 

Task  2.  A hierarchical  taxonomy  is  developed  according  to  the  schema: 

subject  → sub -subject  → item  type  → difficulty  level.  For  extended -response 

items,  key  elements  are  annotated,  specifying  the  required  components —for 

instance,  that  a response  must  contain  a definition,  an  illustrative  example,  and  a

concluding  inference.  All  metadata  are  persisted  in  a taxonomy.json  file. 

Task  3.  A software  module,  domain_evaluator.py,  is  implemented, 

providing  a unified  evaluation  interface.  For  short -answer  items,  the  module 

performs  normalisation  followed  by  either  exact  string  matching  or  numerical 

proximity  assessment  with  a tolerance  of  ±1.  For  extended -response  items,  a

tripartite  evaluation  strategy  is  applied:  a rule -based  component  employing 

regular  expressions,  named  entity  recognition,  and  detection  of  the  presence  or 

absence  of  key  phrases;  a model -based  component  computing  BERTScore  against 

the  reference  answer;  and  a classifier  component,  consisting  of  a fine -tuned 

RuBERT  model  derived  from  Practical  Work  No. 4,  which  assesses  the  response 

against  individual  scoring  criteria.  The  output  is  a structured  JSON  object 

containing  the  assigned  score,  a list  of  matched  elements,  and  any  detected 

hallucinations. 

Task  4.  Systematic  testing  of  open -weight  large  language  models  is 

conducted.  The  evaluated  architectures  comprise:  Llama -3-8B -Instruct,  Mistral -

7B -Instruct,  Qwen1.5 -7B -Chat,  DeepSeek -7B -Base,  and  ruT5 -base.  Three 

operational  modalities  are  examined:  zero -shot  inference,  few -shot  inference  with 

two  to  three  exemplars,  and  retrieval -augmented  generation  incorporating  the 

domain  knowledge  base  constructed  in  Practical  Work  No. 8.  A strict  prohibition 

on  dependency  upon  the  GigaChat  API  within  the  primary  pipeline  is  enforced. 

Task  5.  A comparative  analysis  of  answer  quality  is  performed.  The 

employed  metrics  comprise:  accuracy  for  short -answer  items;  BERTScore 

combined  with  coverage  for  extended -response  items;  the  hallucination  rate;  and 92 

structural  compliance,  defined  as  the  percentage  of  scoring  criteria  satisfied. 

Results  are  presented  in  the  form  of  a benchmark_results.csv  summary  table, 

accompanied  by  subdomain -level  plots  and  box  plots  disaggregated  by 

operational  modality. 

Task  6.  An  analysis  of  interpretability  and  error  patterns  is  undertaken. 

Confusion  matrices  are  constructed,  stratified  by  item  type.  Inventories  of 

hallucinated  content  are  compiled,  each  entry  accompanied  by  an  indication  of  its 

putative  source.  UMAP  projections  of  model -generated  responses  against 

reference  answers  are  plotted.  A qualitative  investigation  is  conducted  to 

determine  why  a given  model  failed  to  satisfy  a particular  scoring  criterion —for 

instance,  omission  of  a required  illustrative  example. 

Task  7.  The  gap  between  performance  on  general -purpose  and  domain -

specific  benchmarks  is  investigated.  Results  obtained  on  the  present  benchmark 

are  compared  against  the  published  MMLU  score  of  the  same  model,  where 

available.  Models  exhibiting  strong  performance  on  general  benchmarks  yet 

underperforming  on  the  domain -specific  OGE  items  are  identified.  A justification 

is  provided  for  observed  cases  in  which  retrieval -augmented  generation  or  few -

shot  prompting  yields  a performance  gain  within  the  narrow  domain. 

Task  8.  An  interactive  web  application  is  created  using  the  Gradio  library. 

The  interface  provides  for  selection  of  the  domain,  item  type,  model,  and 

operational  modality.  The  output  display  comprises  the  generated  answer,  a

criterion -level  evaluation,  a list  of  retrieved  sources  (for  the  RAG  modality),  and 

a side -by -side  comparison  with  the  reference  answer.  The  system  generates  an 

HTML  or  PDF  report  containing  metadata  and  hyperlinks  to  the  sources. 

Task  9.  The  openness  and  deployment  of  the  project  are  ensured.  The 

benchmark  dataset  is  published  on  Hugging  Face  Datasets  in  a format  compatible 

with  the  lm -eval  evaluation  harness.  The  source  code  is  published  on  GitHub  or 

GitLab  under  the  MIT  licence,  organised  into  the  directory  structure:  corpus/, 

evaluator/,  scripts/,  and  a Dockerfile.  The  application  is  deployed  on  Hugging 

Face  Spaces.  A note  is  included  specifying  that  the  OGE  subject  area  serves  as  a

methodological  exemplar  and  that  the  approach  is  transferable  to  any  domain. 

Task  10.  A unified  class,  DomainBenchmark,  is  implemented,  exposing  the

and  is  engineered  for  suitability  within  both  research  and  production  contexts. 

Task  11.  A suite  of  unit  tests  is  developed  using  the  pytest  framework. 

Specific  library  versions  are  pinned:  transformers==4.38.0  and  lm -eval==0.4.0. 93

2.  Theoretical  Prerequisites 

The  evolution  of  transfer  learning  in  natural  language  processing  represents 

a transition  from  static  embedding  representations  to  dynamic,  contextualised 

vector  models  produced  by  the  transformer  architecture.  Whereas  early 

approaches  such  as  Word2Vec  or  GloVe  fixed  a single  vector  for  each  word 

regardless  of  its  context,  modern  pre -trained  models  (BERT,  RoBERTa,  T5, 

BART)  function  as  universal  "language  priors."  They  capture  deep  syntactic  and 

semantic  regularities  of  language,  which  enables  them  to  be  effectively  adapted 

to  specific  applied  tasks  even  under  conditions  of  acute  scarcity  of  labelled  data. 

Depending  on  the  chosen  strategy  for  adapting  a pre -trained  model  to  a

target  task,  three  main  directions  can  be  distinguished.  The  first  direction,  full 

transfer  or  Full  Fine -Tuning,  involves  updating  all  parameters  of  the  model  during 

training  on  the  target  dataset.  This  method  potentially  yields  the  highest  task 

performance;  however,  it  is  associated  with  high  demands  on  computational 

resources  and  carries  a significant  risk  of  overfitting,  especially  when  working 

with  small  samples.  Its  successful  application  requires  careful  hyperparameter 

tuning,  including  a regularisation  strategy  and  a learning  rate  schedule. 99 

The  second  direction,  parameter -efficient  transfer  learning  (PEFT),  seeks 

to  overcome  the  limitations  of  full  transfer.  Instead  of  updating  hundreds  of 

millions  of  parameters,  PEFT  methods  modify  only  a small  fraction  of  them.  Key 

representatives  of  this  family  are  Adapters  — compact  bottleneck  layers  inserted 

between  the  main  blocks  of  the  transformer  — and  LoRA  (Low -Rank  Adaptation) 

— a method  that  approximates  updates  to  the  attention  weight  matrices  through 

the  product  of  low -rank  matrices.  Beyond  these  two,  the  PEFT  landscape  includes 

prompt  tuning,  where  trainable  virtual  tokens  are  prepended  to  the  input  rather 

than  modifying  the  model  itself;  prefix  tuning,  which  learns  a continuous  task -

specific  prefix  for  each  transformer  layer;  and  IA³,  which  scales  key,  value,  and 

feed -forward  activations  by  learned  vectors.  These  methods  differ  in  the  number 

of  additional  parameters  they  introduce,  the  granularity  at  which  they  intervene, 

and  the  extent  to  which  they  remain  compatible  with  the  original  model's  forward 

pass.  What  unites  them  is  the  same  core  principle:  the  pre -trained  backbone  is 

frozen,  and  adaptation  is  confined  to  a small,  modular,  and  easily  shareable 

component.  These  techniques  make  it  possible  to  train  less  than  one  percent  of 

the  total  number  of  model  parameters,  while  preserving  over  ninety -five  percent 

of  the  quality  achieved  with  full  fine -tuning.  Such  an  approach  proves  ideal  for 

resource -constrained  scenarios,  for  deployment  on  edge  devices  and  in  multi -task 

systems  where  multiple  specialised  adapters  must  be  stored  for  a single  base 

model  backbone. 

The  third  direction,  feature  extraction,  entails  using  the  pre -trained  encoder 

as  a frozen,  immutable  module  for  obtaining  vector  representations  of  text.  In  this 

case,  only  the  classification  head  situated  atop  the  encoder  is  trained.  This 

approach  is  the  fastest  and  most  robust  to  overfitting,  which  makes  it  valuable  for 

ultra -low -resource  scenarios.  Its  flexibility  and  upper  quality  bound,  however,  are 

usually  lower  than  those  of  methods  that  permit  adaptation  of  internal 

representations.  Feature  extraction  is  often  employed  as  a reliable  baseline  for 

comparison  with  more  complex  strategies. 

The  quality  of  knowledge  transfer  is  determined  by  a number  of 

interrelated  factors.  The  semantic  proximity  of  the  domain  on  which  the  model 

was  pre -trained  to  the  target  subject  area  is  of  fundamental  importance.  No  less 

important  is  architectural  compatibility:  encoder -only  models  (BERT)  are 

naturally  suited  to  understanding  and  classification  tasks,  whereas  encoder -

decoder  models  (T5,  BART)  are  the  standard  for  generative  tasks  such  as 

summarisation.  A critical  role  is  played  by  the  search  for  a balance  between 

generalisation  and  specialisation,  which  in  practice  is  expressed  in  tuning  the 100 

strength  of  regularisation  and  the  learning  rate  decay  schedule.  Finally,  for 

practical  applications,  especially  in  tasks  with  imbalanced  classes  or  in  safety -

critical  systems,  probability  calibration  — that  is,  the  correspondence  of  the 

model's  predicted  confidence  to  the  actual  frequency  of  correct  answers  — is 

important.  To  understand  what  information  the  model  retains  and  what  it  loses 

during  the  adaptation  process,  analysis  of  internal  representations  is  employed. 

The  tools  for  this  purpose  are  probing  methods  (training  linear  classifiers  on 

hidden  states),  visualisation  of  attention  maps  and  projections  of  embedding 

spaces,  which  is  directly  connected  with  model  interpretability  and  the 

justification  of  trust  in  its  decisions. 

3.  Procedure  for  Executing  the  Work 

The  work  is  performed  in  several  successive  stages,  each  directed  at 

attaining  specific  educational  and  research  outcomes. 

Task  1.  Three  annotated  subsets  are  prepared  using  data  derived  from 

Practical  Work  No. 1.  The  data  sources  comprise  TASS,  RIA  Novosti,  Lenta.ru ,

Meduza,  Kommersant,  and  belgech.ru . The  following  specifications  apply:  the 

Sentiment -1K  subset  consists  of  up  to  1,000  documents  labelled  with  binary 

sentiment  polarity;  the  RuNER -5K  subset  provides  named  entity  annotation  for 

the  categories  PER,  ORG,  and  LOC,  comprising  no  fewer  than  5,000  tokens  in 

CoNLL  format;  the  NewsSumm -3K  subset  contains  no  fewer  than  3,000 

(headline,  body  text)  pairs  intended  for  abstractive  summarisation.  All  subsets  are 

serialised  in  JSONL  format  with  the  fields  text,  sentiment,  ner_tags,  and 

summary.  A stratified  split  of  70/15/15  is  applied,  with  stratification  preserved 

where  applicable.  The  mutual  exclusivity  of  documents  across  the  three  subsets 

is  strictly  guaranteed. 

Task  2.  Input  representations  are  constructed,  and  pretrained  donor  models 

are  selected.  The  candidate  architectures  comprise:  DeepPavlov/rubert -base -

cased,  sberbank -ai/ruRoberta -large,  cointegrated/rubert -tiny2, 

microsoft/mdeberta -v3 -base,  IlyaGusev/ruRoBERTa -finance,  and  google/mt5 -

base.  Tokenisation  is  performed  via  AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained  for  encoder -

only  architectures,  whilst  a separate  dedicated  tokenizer  is  employed  for 

sequence -to -sequence  models.  Metadata  pertaining  to  the  tokenizer  configuration 

and  the  resulting  sequence  lengths  are  preserved. 

Task  3.  A software  module,  transfer_adaptors.py,  is  developed,  providing 

a unified  TransferAdaptor  interface  that  encapsulates  the  following  transfer 

learning  strategies:  feature  extraction,  full  fine -tuning,  layer -wise  learning  rate 101 

scheduling,  adapter  modules,  and  low -rank  adaptation  (LoRA).  The  module 

supports  three  downstream  task  types:  text  classification,  named  entity 

recognition,  and  sequence -to -sequence  generation.  GPU  acceleration  is  provided 

through  PyTorch  in  conjunction  with  the  Accelerate  library.  The  codebase  is  fully 

type -annotated  and  comprehensively  documented. 

Task  4.  A rigorous  training  procedure  with  continuous  monitoring  is 

conducted.  A dedicated  validation  split,  comprising  15%  of  the  data,  is  employed 

to  govern  early  stopping.  The  evaluation  metrics  are  macro -averaged  F1  score  for 

classification,  entity -level  F1  score  for  named  entity  recognition,  and  ROUGE -L

for  summarisation.  The  training  process  is  visualised  by  means  of  learning  curves, 

gradient  norm  plots,  and  loss  trajectories.  All  artefacts —including  configuration 

files,  model  weights,  and  training  logs —are  persisted  in  both  JSON  format  and 

the  native  Hugging  Face  format. 

Task  5.  An  empirical  assessment  of  the  effectiveness  of  the  various  transfer 

learning  strategies  is  performed,  encompassing  the  following  dimensions:  model 

quality,  quantified  by  F1  and  ROUGE  scores;  computational  efficiency,  measured 

in  terms  of  training  duration,  inference  latency,  the  proportion  of  trainable 

parameters,  and  peak  GPU  memory  consumption;  and  result  stability,  evaluated 

through  the  variance  of  quality  metrics  observed  across  distinct  random  seed 

initialisations.  Results  are  presented  in  the  form  of  a summary  table 

(transfer_comparison.csv),  box  plots  illustrating  the  distribution  of  metric  values, 

and  radar  charts  mapping  the  three -way  trade -off  among  quality,  resource 

consumption,  and  speed. 

Task  6.  The  relationship  between  downstream  task  performance  and 

training  set  size  is  investigated.  Subsets  of  the  following  sizes  are  drawn:  100, 

300,  1,000,  3,000,  and  10,000  instances.  The  RuBERT  model  is  trained  on  each 

subset  using  every  available  transfer  learning  strategy.  Learning  curves  are  plotted 

with  confidence  intervals,  and  the  point  of  saturation —beyond  which  additional 

data  yields  diminishing  returns —is  identified  for  each  configuration. 

Task  7.  The  robustness  and  reliability  of  model  predictions  are  ensured 

through  systematic  evaluation  under  perturbation.  A paraphrase  attack  is 

conducted,  wherein  3–5 reformulations  of  each  input  are  generated  via  a T5 -based 

paraphraser.  Model  calibration  is  assessed  using  reliability  diagrams,  the  expected 

calibration  error  (ECE),  and  the  application  of  Platt  scaling  and  isotonic 

regression.  Supplementary  calibration  metrics,  specifically  the  Brier  score  and 

negative  log -likelihood  (NLL),  are  reported. 102 

Task  8.  An  investigation  into  the  internal  mechanisms  underpinning 

transfer  learning  is  undertaken.  Linear  probing  is  performed  on  the  hidden 

representations  at  each  layer,  both  before  and  after  adaptation.  Dimensionality 

reduction  techniques —UMAP  and  t-SNE —are  applied  to  the  [CLS]  embeddings 

and  to  token -level  representations.  Attention  patterns  are  visualised  using  the 

bertviz  toolkit.  An  analysis  of  catastrophic  forgetting  is  conducted,  examining  the 

extent  to  which  the  model  retains  performance  on  the  original  pretraining 

objective  after  adaptation. 

Task  9.  The  openness  and  deployment  of  the  project  are  ensured.  The 

source  code  is  published  on  GitHub  or  GitLab  under  the  MIT  licence.  The 

annotated  subsets  are  released  on  Hugging  Face  Datasets.  All  adapted  models  are 

uploaded  to  the  Hugging  Face  Hub,  each  accompanied  by  a Model  Card 

specifying  the  model  description,  hyperparameter  settings,  evaluation  metrics, 

applicable  licence,  and  a representative  code  usage  example.  A demonstration 

application  is  deployed  on  Hugging  Face  Spaces  via  Gradio,  providing  for  the 

selection  of  task,  model,  and  transfer  strategy,  alongside  side -by -side  model 

comparison  and  attention  map  visualisation.

public  methods  .fit(),  .predict_proba(),  and  .explain().  The  class  is  designed  to  be

environment.  This  architectural  approach  facilitates  seamless  integration  into 

MLOps  infrastructure.

2.  Theoretical  Prerequisites 

RLHF  emerged  as  a response  to  the  fundamental  limitation  of  supervised 

fine -tuning  (SFT):  in  generation  tasks,  no  single  “correct”  answer  exists.  Instead, 

quality  is  determined  by  a multidimensional,  subjective  and  contextual 

assessment.  RLHF  overcomes  this  uncertainty  by  replacing  pointwise  labelling 

with  relative  preferences  that  better  reflect  the  complexity  of  human  judgement. 

Architecture of the RLHF pipeline 

1.  Supervised  Fine -Tuning  (SFT)  — the  base  adaptation  of  a pre -trained 

language  model  (e.g.,  mT5,  ruGPT)  to  the  format  of  the  target  task  (QA, 

dialogue,  summarisation). 

2.  Reward  Modeling  — training  a function  𝑟 𝜃 (𝑥 , 𝑦 ) that  predicts  how  well 

an  answer  𝑦  to  a prompt  𝑥  conforms  to  human  preferences.  The Bradley –

Terry model is commonly employed: 

𝑃 (𝑦 1 > 𝑦 2 ∣ 𝑥 ) = 𝜎 (𝑟 𝜃 (𝑥 , 𝑦 1) − 𝑟 𝜃 (𝑥 , 𝑦 2))

3.  Reinforcement  Learning  — further  training  of  the  SFT  model  𝜋 𝜙  using 

the  PPO  algorithm,  where  the  reward  is  replaced  by  𝑟 𝜃 , and  regularisation 

is  provided  via  KL  divergence  to  the  reference  model  𝜋 ref :

ℒRL  = 𝔼 [𝑟 𝜃 (𝑥 , 𝑦 ) − 𝛽  ⋅ KL (𝜋 𝜙 (𝑦  ∣ 𝑥 ) ∥ 𝜋 ref (𝑦  ∣ 𝑥 ))]

The  KL  penalty  term  is  not  a mere  technical  detail;  it  is  essential  for 

preventing  a phenomenon  known  as  distributional  shift.  As  the  policy  𝜋 𝜙  is 

updated,  it  begins  to  generate  texts  that  may  lie  outside  the  distribution  on  which 109 

the  reward  model  was  trained.  In  such  regions,  the  reward  model's  scores  become 

unreliable,  and  the  policy  can  learn  to  exploit  spurious  patterns  that  yield  high 

rewards  but  low  actual  quality.  The  KL  term  anchors  the  policy  to  the  reference 

model,  ensuring  that  it  does  not  drift  too  far  into  regions  where  the  reward  signal 

is  meaningless. 

Modern alternatives: 

− DPO  (Rafailov  et  al.,  2023)  demonstrates  that  preference  optimisation  can 

be  reduced  to  a classification  task  without  explicit  RL: 

ℒDPO  = −log  𝜎 (𝛽 log  𝜋 𝜙 (𝑦 1 ∣ 𝑥 )

𝜋 ref (𝑦 1 ∣ 𝑥 ) − 𝛽 log  𝜋 𝜙 (𝑦 2 ∣ 𝑥 )

𝜋 ref (𝑦 2 ∣ 𝑥 ))

DPO  reparameterises  the  Bradley –Terry  preference  model  directly  in  terms  of 

the  policy,  eliminating  the  need  for  a separate  reward  model  and  the  associated 

instability  of  RL  training.  This  makes  it  substantially  simpler  to  implement, 

though  it  also  means  that  the  reward  signal  cannot  be  inspected  or  audited 

independently  of  the  policy. 

- IPO  and  SLiC  improve  stability  with  weak  or  noisy  preferences. 

Critical challenges:  

> −

Annotator  bias:  cultural,  linguistic  and  professional  differences  distort 

preferences.  

> −

Over -optimisation:  the  model  learns  to  “deceive”  the  RM,  generating 

formally  correct  but  vacuous  or  sycophantic  answers.  

> −

Alignment  vs.  competence:  an  excessive  emphasis  on  safety  may  suppress 

useful  but  controversial  utterances.  

> −

Absence  of  ground  truth:  the  quality  of  RLHF  is  assessed  only  through 

proxies,  which  requires  a multi -method  approach. 

3.  Procedure  for  Executing  the  Work 

The  work  is  performed  in  several  successive  stages,  each  directed  at 

attaining  specific  educational  and  research  outcomes. 

Task  1.  A multitask  dataset  of  prompts  and  model -generated  responses  is 

constructed  in  both  Russian  and  Tatar.  The  data  sources  comprise  TASS,  RIA 

Novosti,  Meduza,  belgech.ru , and  the  Tatar  Electronic  Library.  The  tasks  covered 

are  question  answering,  dialogue,  and  summarisation,  with  1,000  prompts 

collected  per  task.  For  each  prompt,  six  candidate  responses  are  generated  using 

a supervised  fine -tuned  model  under  varying  decoding  parameters,  specifically 

temperature  and  top -p sampling.  The  total  volume  of  the  dataset  amounts  to  no 

fewer  than  18,000  prompt –response  pairs,  serialised  in  JSONL  format. 110 

Task  2.  A preference  dataset  is  compiled  and  verified,  comprising  no  fewer 

than  7,000  ranked  response  pairs,  of  which  1,000  are  in  Tatar.  Each  pair  is 

assessed  according  to  three  criteria:  helpfulness  (H),  truthfulness  (T),  and  safety 

(S).  An  annotation  protocol  is  established,  stipulating  a minimum  of  five 

independent  assessments  per  pair.  Inter -annotator  agreement,  measured  by 

Krippendorff's  alpha,  is  required  to  meet  or  exceed  a threshold  of  0.65.  The 

dataset  is  stored  in  the  Hugging  Face  PreferenceDataset  format. 

Task  3.  A software  module,  rlhf_core.py,  is  implemented,  providing 

support  for  the  following  reinforcement  learning  from  human  feedback 

algorithms:  proximal  policy  optimisation  (PPO),  using  either  the  TRL  library  or 

a custom  implementation;  direct  preference  optimisation  (DPO)  and  identity 

preference  optimisation  (IPO),  via  TRL  or  Axolotl;  and  contrastive  evaluation 

method  (CEM),  employing  beam  search  with  retraining.  A multi -head  reward 

model  is  implemented,  producing  three  scalar  outputs  corresponding  to 

helpfulness,  truthfulness,  and  safety.  The  supported  base  architectures  are  ru -t5 -

base,  ruGPT -3.5 -1.7B,  and  rubert -base -cased.  A unified  YAML  configuration  file 

governs  all  experiments,  and  logging  is  handled  via  Weights  & Biases  or  MLflow. 

Task  4.  Training  stability  is  systematically  ensured.  For  reward  model 

training,  validation  is  performed  against  the  area  under  the  ROC  curve  and 

Spearman's  rank  correlation  coefficient.  During  PPO,  the  Kullback –Leibler 

divergence  from  the  reference  policy  is  constrained  to  a maximum  value  of  0.02, 

with  a hard  limit  of  200  training  steps,  and  policy  entropy  is  monitored 

continuously.  For  DPO,  the  regularisation  parameter  β is  selected  through  a

dedicated  validation  procedure.  All  artefacts —model  checkpoints,  training  logs, 

and  UMAP  projections  of  hidden  state  representations —are  preserved  at  regular 

intervals. 

Task  5.  A multifaceted  evaluation  is  conducted.  The  automatic  evaluation 

suite  comprises  BERTScore,  MAUVE,  Self -BLEU,  and  FactScore.  Expert -based 

evaluation  is  performed  on  a sample  of  100  response  pairs,  assessed  by  three 

independent  evaluators,  yielding  no  fewer  than  200  individual  judgments  within 

an  A/B  testing  framework.  Behavioural  analysis  is  undertaken,  quantifying  the 

frequency  of  trigger  phrases,  response  length,  the  measure  of  textual  lexical 

diversity  (MTLD),  and  a systematic  categorisation  of  failure  modes. 

Task  6.  Risks  are  identified  and  thoroughly  documented.  A cultural  bias 

audit  is  performed,  probing  for  gender  and  nationality  stereotypes.  An  avoidance 

behaviour  test  is  administered,  assessing  the  model's  propensity  to  evade  topics 

relating  to  politics  and  religion.  Reward  over -optimisation  is  analysed  by 111 

examining  the  correlation  between  reward  model  scores  and  expert  human 

judgments.  The  findings  are  compiled  into  a formal  Ethics  Appendix. 

Task  7.  Behavioural  shifts  induced  by  the  alignment  procedure  are 

analysed.  N-gram  frequency  distributions  are  compared  before  and  after 

alignment.  Activation  patterns  are  examined  using  the  Captum  library.  Qualitative 

case  studies  are  conducted,  with  particular  attention  devoted  to  phenomena  such 

as  the  increased  avoidance  of  direct  answers  and  the  proliferation  of  generic, 

vacuous  phrasing. 

Task  8.  An  online  RLHF  platform  is  developed  using  the  Gradio  library. 

The  platform  enables  blind  A/B  comparison  between  two  models,  with  evaluation 

performed  along  the  three  predefined  criteria  (helpfulness,  truthfulness,  and 

safety)  plus  the  provision  of  a free -text  comment.  All  user  judgments  are 

automatically  persisted  to  a Hugging  Face  Dataset.  A trigger  mechanism  for 

subsequent  retraining  of  the  reward  model  is  implemented,  enabling  iterative 

improvement  from  freshly  collected  preference  data.

7.  Conclusion 

This  work  is  not  merely  “running  DPO  on  Colab.”  It  is  a deep  immersion 

into  the  philosophical,  technical  and  ethical  foundations  of  modern  AI  alignment, 

where  every  decision  — from  the  formulation  of  criteria  to  the  choice  of  β in  DPO 

— carries  moral  and  methodological  weight. 

In  the  course  of  the  work,  you  learn  to  ask  the  key  questions  that  determine 

the  maturity  of  AI  systems:  

> −

“Whose  values  does  the  model  embed  — and  whose  does  it  ignore?”  — a

question  of  cultural  and  linguistic  pluralism.  

> −

“Is  the  model  becoming  safer  — or  simply  more  covert  in  its  errors?”  — a

question  of  over -optimisation.  

> −

“Can  the  RM  be  trusted  — or  does  it  capture  only  surface  patterns?”  — a

question  of  the  depth  of  alignment.  

> −

“Is  reproducibility  guaranteed  — from  the  instructions  to  the  annotator  to 

the  final  A/B  test?”  — a question  of  scientific  integrity. 

The  answers  to  these  questions  constitute  the  difference  between  a model 

that  “pleases”  and  a system  that  serves  society  honestly,  transparently  and 

responsibly. 

Having  completed  this  work,  you  will  have  gained  not  simply  an  RLHF 

model,  but  a methodological  and  ethical  compass  for  designing,  evaluating  and 

deploying  any  aligned  AI  systems  — from  educational  chatbots  to  medical 

assistants,  capable  of  operating  not  only  intelligently,  but  also  fairly,  transparently 

and  with  respect  for  cultural  diversity. References
