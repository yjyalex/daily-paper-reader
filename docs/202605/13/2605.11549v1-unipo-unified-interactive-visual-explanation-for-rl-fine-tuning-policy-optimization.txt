Figure 1: UNIPO unifies the visual explanation of policy optimization algorithms for RL fine-tuning through three coordinated views that progressively reveal how algorithmic design shapes training. (A) Training Explorer summarizes the full training run with a multi-metric radial plot. (B) Step Inspector visualizes token-level objectives for a selected step’s prompt-responses set, revealing which tokens are reinforced or suppressed in the next gradient update. ABSTRACT 

Reinforcement learning has emerged as a dominant technique for fine-tuning the behavior of large language models, with policy op-timization (PO) algorithms such as GRPO, DAPO, and Dr. GRPO emerging in rapid succession to advance state-of-the-art reason-ing and alignment performance. However, the modular differ-ences between these algorithms, including targeted improvements to clipping, advantage estimation, and reward aggregation, are in-troduced across separate papers with inconsistent notation, mak-ing them difficult to compare and intimidating to the non-expert community. We present UNIPO, the first interactive visualization tool that exposes the token-level training dynamics of RL fine-tuning algorithms through a unified design. UNIPO connects three complementary views, a high-level training overview, a step-level prompt and response inspector, and a side-by-side algorithm com-parison, allowing learners to observe how individual design deci-sions propagate through training. Through two usage scenarios, we demonstrate how UNIPO supports both classroom instruction for non-experts and algorithm selection for AI practitioners. Our tool is open-source and publicly available at https://poloclub. github.io/unipo/ 

Index Terms: Interactive Visualization, Reinforcement Learning, Fine-tuning, Large-language Models.

1 INTRODUCTION 

Reinforcement Learning fine-tuning has emerged as a dominant technique for aligning pre-trained large language models with task-specific goals, including chain-of-thought reasoning, math solving, and persona matching [5, 2, 20, 19]. Building on policy optimiza-tion (PO) algorithms like REINFORCE and PPO, derivatives such as GRPO, DAPO, and Dr. GRPO have recently emerged in rapid succession. Each of these new algorithms makes targeted improve-ments to the shared PO framework, demonstrating the rapid evolu-tion of this algorithm family. Despite their widespread adoption in production-grade LLMs [8, 34], the internal mechanisms driving these algorithms’ performance remain difficult to build intuition for, particularly for learners and AI practitioners new to RL fine-tuning. Reconciling the differences between these approaches re-quires deep mathematical expertise and careful comparison across a growing body of work. Enabling better understanding of these algorithms is critical to expanding RL fine-tuning’s accessibility to expert and non-expert audiences. Existing resources, including books [27, 14], blog posts [15], and video tutorials [12, 11] are non-interactive, making it dif-ficult to observe how each algorithm’s modifications shape training behavior. Monitoring tools used by AI practitioners, like Weights & Biases [3], present a large amount of training metrics that make it difficult to isolate how individual algorithmic mechanisms con-tribute to the training behavior. Comparative blog posts [36] and benchmarking studies [17] provide direct algorithmic comparisons but cannot communicate how modular design decisions propagate through training at the token level. An interactive visualization is necessary to support hands-on exploration of training dynamics and side-by-side algorithmic comparison, particularly for non-experts who lack the background to reconstruct algorithm behavior from

2 RELATED WORK 

Policy Optimization for LLM Fine-Tuning. Reinforcement Learning has become a primary technique in LLM fine-tuning, both for aligning models with human preferences (RLHF [20]) and for improving reasoning through verifiable rewards [16]. Within RL, policy optimization (PO), which directly optimizes the model from reward signals, has become the dominant family of algorithms for LLM fine-tuning [30]. Inspired by the REINFORCE algorithm [32], Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) [21] served as the dom-inant fine-tuning approach for LLM alignment by introducing a learned critic to evaluate the policy at each step, at the cost of train-ing a second model alongside the policy. GRPO [22] eliminates this internal critic by computing a group-relative advantage from G

parallel responses. This enabled DeepSeek-R1 [8] to match larger models, establishing GRPO as a dominant RL fine-tuning algo-rithm. DAPO [34] refines GRPO with per-token importance ratios, asymmetric clipping, and the removal of the KL penalty, reaching state-of-the-art performance in half of the training steps of GRPO. The Dr. GRPO [18] algorithm preserves the core structure of GRPO while normalizing the response length to stabilize performance on longer responses. Each of these algorithms refines its predecessor but is introduced in its own paper with distinct notation, making direct comparison difficult [36, 29]. 

Visualization Tools for ML Explainability. A growing body of vi-sualization research has created interactive visualization tools that ensure foundational machine learning models remain approach-able to non-experts [24, 9, 25]. The CNN Explainer [31] estab-lishes a progressive design pattern, allowing users to explore model mechanisms at varying depths through explicit visualization of all model inputs, intermediate steps, and outputs. The Transformer Explainer [4] extends this approach with in-browser inference, ex-posing attention and token probabilities. Other tools focus on in-specting specific components of trained models, such as attention patterns [28, 33] and multimodal representations [1]. These tools focus on exploring inference-time behavior, with limited capacity to convey the training dynamics that allow these models to effectively learn. Practitioner tools such as Weights & Biases or MLflow [35] visualize these training dynamics, but abstract away algorithmic mechanisms in favor of convergence metrics, requiring model im-plementation and GPU access – both out of reach for non-experts. To our knowledge, no existing tool exposes the hierarchical break-down of how these RL fine-tuning algorithms derive their model updates through a unified token-level visualization, nor contextual-izes each algorithm’s contribution through the evolution of the RL fine-tuning algorithm family. 

3 DESIGN GOALS 

Through reviewing related works [9, 31, 4] and challenges of com-municating RL fine-tuning algorithms to both non-expert and expert audiences, we establish three primary design goals: 

G1. High-level overview of RL fine-tuning training dynam-ics. A single RL fine-tuning run produces thousands of train-ing steps across multiple metrics, including reward, loss, and KL divergence. Non-expert users need an approachable en-try point to orient themselves to these high-level training dy-namics before engaging with token-level mechanics [23, 31]. Monitoring tools like Weights & Biases [3] that track these metrics throughout model training are intimidating to a non-expert audience, and abstract away the underlying mathemat-ics from AI practitioners [9]. UNIPO addresses both au-diences through a radial training overview that orients non-experts to the training run while offering practitioners a navi-gable entry point into specific training steps. 

G2. Expose the token-level computations that connect response groups to gradient updates. The path from a sampled re-sponse group to a gradient update passes through token-level importance ratios and advantages that are obscured in static-text resources. Without observing how these values arise from real training runs, users cannot build an intuition for why spe-cific “correct” responses are reinforced and others are sup-pressed [24]. Non-experts need straightforward explanations of these computations, while AI practitioners need to be able to connect token-level behavior back to the mathematical no-tation of each algorithm’s objective function [7]. UNIPO bridges this gap by surfacing the token-level importance ra-tios and advantages that drive each gradient update, anchoring the corresponding mathematical notation to the concrete val-ues produced during training. 

G3. Contextualize each algorithm within the evolution of the RL fine-tuning family. State-of-the-art RL fine-tuning algo-rithms have evolved from targeted improvements to their pre-decessor’s weaknesses: GRPO removes PPO’s critic model, DAPO introduces dynamic sampling and asymmetric clip-ping, and Dr. GRPO refines normalization for long responses. Without seeing how each algorithm builds on its predecessors, users cannot contextualize the family’s development [13]. Ex-isting static-text resources require users to manually recon-cile notation across literature, a task that is time-consuming for experts [10] and intimidating for newcomers. UNIPO po-sitions each algorithm within the family’s progression, sup-porting cross-algorithm comparison that renders any two al-gorithms’ objective functions side-by-side. 

4 SYSTEM DESIGN AND IMPLEMENTATION 

To operationalize the three design goals from § 3, UNIPO utilizes three distinct connected views: Training Explorer (§ 4.1), Step In-spector (§ 4.2), and Algorithm Explainer (§ 4.3). algorithm’s objective function in the Algorithm Explainer . This lay-ered design approach allows non-experts to build intuition with the model training dynamics before approaching the underlying math-ematics, while AI practitioners can quickly move to comparing the objective functions at the appropriate mathematical depth. This initial release focuses on five representative PO meth-ods: REINFORCE, PPO, GRPO, DAPO, and Dr. GRPO. Using OpenRLHF, we fine-tune Llama-3.2-1B-Instruct with each of these five algorithms on a math reasoning task from the MATH dataset (∼1,000 steps per run). The frontend is built with Svelte and D3, loading a unified JSON training schema to visualize training dy-namics directly in the browser. 

4.1 Training Explorer 

The Training Explorer provides users with a high-level overview of the training dynamics across all steps (each step corresponds to one gradient update) of the training run ( G1 , Fig. 1A). Training metrics are visualized through a radial plot, where each concentric ring represents a metric (e.g., reward, policy loss, KL divergence, clipping behavior) corresponding to the training step’s angular posi-tion. We use a radial layout to present the entire training trajectory as a single, unified structure, enabling spatial alignment of mul-tiple metrics and facilitating pattern comparison across steps [6]. To maintain readability at scale, we apply LTTB (Largest-Triangle-Three-Buckets) downsampling [26] to reduce visual clutter while preserving the overall shape and key features of the data. A fish-eye interaction locally expands dense regions of the radial plot, en-abling users to inspect and select individual steps while preserving the global trajectory. By allowing side-by-side comparison of up to four of these metrics, users can view the full training dynamics for each of these algorithms. 

4.2 Step Inspector 

The Step Inspector presents the prompt-response group at a se-lected training step, along with the step-level aggregated objective and its underlying token-level contributions, including rewards and gradients for each response. Responses are visualized as token se-quences, with token-level objective values encoded as a color over-lay on the text. Lower values are shown in pink, indicating that the model will decrease the probability of those tokens in the next update, while higher values are shown in green, indicating the op-posite (Fig. 1B). While response-level rewards provide a high-level signal of pref-erence, gradient updates in RL fine-tuning are computed at the token level through importance ratios and advantages. By expos-ing token-level objective values, the Step Inspector makes explicit how each token contributes to the final gradient update (Fig. 1B and Fig. 2A), directly linking sampled responses to parameter up-dates ( G2 ). Users can select individual tokens to open the Algo-rithm Explainer (§ 4.3), which renders the algorithm’s objective function with the numerical values used to compute the selected token’s gradient. This interaction bridges abstract formulations with implementation-level quantities, letting non-experts build in-tuition about token-level behavior while allowing practitioners to trace how each value maps to the algorithm’s objective function. 

4.3 Algorithm Explainer 

The Algorithm Explainer uses a modular representation that enables cross-algorithm comparison and contextualizes the active algorithm within the evolution of the RL fine-tuning family ( G3 ). We decom-pose each step-level objective into aggregation terms, per-token ob-jective terms, and additional constraints , and further break down each per-token objective into the optimization target , such as the importance ratio, and the optimization strength , such as the advan-tage (Fig. 1C). Because these components recur across RL fine-tuning algorithms, this shared structure enables direct comparison across algorithms while preserving their specific differences. The view operationalizes this modular representation by present-ing the active algorithm’s objective function at multiple levels of complexity. Users can click each component to open an explana-tion card that connects abstract objective terms with the concrete computations performed at a training step. The Token-level Objec-tive Card expands the per-token objective into computations such as the importance ratio and advantage. The Aggregation Card traces how these token-level values are pooled across response groups and how they shape the resulting token-level gradients. Hovering over individual terms reveals tooltip explanations that make the mathe-matical notation accessible to non-expert learners. 

Comparison mode enables cross-algorithm comparison at the objective-function level, since direct step-by-step alignment is im-practical when each approach samples prompts in a different or-der. This design makes the evolutionary relationships between al-gorithms visible without overwhelming non-expert users, remain-ing accessible to AI practitioners exploring algorithmic differences. When users select a comparison algorithm, the two algorithms’ for-mulas are rendered vertically within each card, with color coding to mark removed, added, or modified components (Fig. 3). For ex-ample, users can compare the differences between the aggregation approaches of DAPO and Dr. GRPO to observe that Dr. GRPO removes DAPO’s response length bias (Fig. 3B). The same JSON schema drives this comparison: components with matching user-defined IDs align their formula and text, while unmatched IDs are highlighted as added or removed. 

5 USAGE SCENARIO 

We present two UNIPO usage scenarios demonstrating its support for pedagogy and research: (1) an instructor and students explore a counterintuitive training step to build intuition for GRPO’s group-relative reward dynamics (§ 5.1); (2) an AI researcher compares DAPO and Dr. GRPO objectives to design a hybrid algorithm tai-lored to her training data (§ 5.2). 

5.1 Instructor Teaching GRPO via Training Behavior 

Professor Quinn is updating his graduate reinforcement learning course to showcase modern applications of RL to LLM fine-tuning. From prior semesters teaching RL fundamentals, he knows that stu-dents struggle to build intuition from mathematical notation alone, particularly for newer algorithms where the optimization behavior is not apparent from the objective function alone. Searching for an alternative to static equations on lecture slides, he discovers UNIPO and decides to use it in his next class, focusing on GRPO to help students build intuition for how the algorithm determines which to-kens are reinforced during training. Figure 3: Algorithm Explainer ’s Comparison mode renders two al-gorithms side-by-side with color-coded differences, revealing evolu-tionary relationships across policy optimization methods. (A) GRPO vs. DAPO surfaces DAPO’s added Dynamic Sampling constraint, with tooltip explaining it in plain language for non-experts. (B) DAPO vs. Dr. GRPO contrasts aggregation strategies, annotating DAPO’s cross-group length bias and Dr. GRPO’s bias-free normalization. 

Surveying training dynamics with the Training Explorer. During his next lecture, Quinn opens UNIPO and selects the GRPO algorithm from the navigation bar, loading the Training Explorer view (Fig. 1A) for the MATH reasoning task. From the metric se-lector, Quinn enables three rings on the ra-dial plot across the 1,249 steps in the GRPO training logs. In the Training Explorer view for this model, re-sponse length (outermost ring) grows steadily as the model pro-duces longer reasoning chains, policy clip ratio (middle ring) rises as the policy drifts, and reward (inner ring) shows a weak signal in the first 150 training steps, with high step-to-step variance after-ward. He uses this view to teach a key idea: meaningful training signal in GRPO accumulates through distributional shifts that the radial layout makes apparent, even if individual training steps ex-hibit the step-to-step variance typical of policy gradient methods. 

Investigating a counterintuitive training step with Step Inspec-tor. Quinn clicks on step 242 in the radial plot, opening the Step Inspector view (Fig. 2A), which displays the step’s prompts and their response groups. He directs the class’s attention to Prompt 2: “Two numbers have a difference of 3 and a sum of 31. What is the larger of the two numbers?” Because of the simplicity of this problem, all sampled responses have arrived at the correct an-swer of 17. He asks the class to predict the token gradients of this response group, with most students predicting a strong positive gra-dient because of their intuition that high “correctness” should lead to a strong gradient signal. Quinn directs their attention to the re-sponse tokens themselves, which appear in white rather than the green reinforcement coloring that students expected (Fig. 2A). The 

Step-level Objective at the bottom of the panel reads −0.323 , but none of this update is coming from prompt 2’s tokens. The class is confused: every response is correct, reflecting a reward of 1 .00, but the model does not learn from this prompt at all. 

Diagnosing the zero-gradient moment with the Algorithm Ex-plainer. To explain what the students just observed, Quinn opens the Algorithm Explainer view by clicking on one of the white tokens to reveal the per-token Advantage and Importance Ratio (Fig. 2B). For prompt 2, every response carries a non-zero Impor-tance Ratio, but is scaled against an Advantage of 0 .000. Returning to the Step Inspector view, Quinn explains that the step-level objec-tive of −0.323 is driven by contributions from other prompts within the step, where the LLM produced a diverse group of responses. This moment reframes the students’ intuition on the GRPO algo-rithm: correct responses are not automatically reinforced, and only responses correct relative to the group provide useful updates to the policy. 

5.2 Researcher Comparing Algorithms for Training Run 

Riley is an AI researcher deciding between using DAPO and Dr. GRPO for training a reasoning model on olympiad-level mathemat-ics. She has implemented GRPO using OpenRLHF before and has monitored training runs via Weights & Biases [3], but each tech-nique frames its modifications relative to GRPO with its own no-tation, making direct DAPO vs. Dr. GRPO comparison difficult. She discovers UNIPO and decides to use its Algorithm Explainer to compare these methods at the objective-function level before com-mitting to a scaled training run. 

Comparing objective functions side-by-side. Riley opens UNIPO, selects DAPO from the algorithm navigation bar, and nav-igates to the Algorithm Explainer view. She clicks Dr. GRPO into the comparison slot, rendering both objective functions side-by-side with color-coded modifications (Fig. 3B). Curious about how each algorithm handles aggregation, she expands the aggregation portion of the objective function and sees that Dr. GRPO replaces DAPO’s implicit token weight with a constant max token length. 

Using UNIPO to determine task-specific suitability. Riley hovers over DAPO’s dynamic sampling constraint and reads that DAPO skips response groups where every response is correct or wrong. She connects this with her knowledge of the training data: as the model improves on easier olympiad problems, those prompts will contribute no gradient signal during model training. Returning to the aggregation panel, Riley reads that DAPO’s implicit token weight introduces a cross-group length bias (Fig. 3B), where a long response in a group of short responses will dominate the group’s gradient. Dr. GRPO’s constant max token length removes this bias, much better suited to the long chain-of-thought solutions in typical olympiad problems. 

Integrating a new algorithm into UNIPO. Riley concludes that neither algorithm will fully address her needs: she would like to train a model that has DAPO’s sampling efficiency and Dr. GRPO’s robust normalization scheme. Since dynamic sampling is a filter-ing step before the gradient update, she hypothesizes that dynamic sampling could be incorporated into Dr. GRPO’s aggregation. Ri-ley decides to prototype this hybrid approach: an insight enabled by UNIPO’s side-by-side objective comparison. Since UNIPO is open-source, she plans to contribute her hybrid algorithm and train-ing logs back to the project. 

6 CONCLUSION 

We introduce UNIPO, the first interactive web-based tool for the RL fine-tuning algorithm family that unifies the explanation of five RL fine-tuning algorithms through three coordinated views. UNIPO connects the high-level training dynamics of these algorithms to the token-level objective computation, making the evolutionary rela-tionships between these algorithms visible without requiring users to manually reconcile the differences. We open-source UNIPO and hope it serves as an extensible platform for the community to contribute new algorithms as the RL fine-tuning landscape rapidly evolves. REFERENCES
