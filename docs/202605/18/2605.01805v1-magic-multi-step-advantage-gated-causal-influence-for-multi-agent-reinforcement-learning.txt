## Abstract 

A key challenge in multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) lies in designing learning signals that effectively promote coordination among agents. Designing such signals necessitates the ability to quantify the true, long-term causal influence between agents. To address this, we introduce Multi-step Advantage-Gated Inter-ventional Causal MARL (MAGIC), a framework that extracts multi-step causal influences between agents and selectively converts them into intrinsic rewards. MAGIC uses causal intervention with conditional mutual information to quantify long-horizon agent influence, and introduces an advantage-based gating mechanism to ensure exploration is directed toward beneficial, goal-aligned behaviors. Experi-ments across multiple standard MARL benchmarks and task families, including MPE and SMAC/SMACv2, demonstrate that MAGIC outperforms state-of-the-art methods by a significant margin, achieving an improvement of at least 10.1% in the main evaluation metric. 

## 1 Introduction 

1.1 Motivation 

In recent years, multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) has been applied to traffic signal control Wu et al. [2020], cooperative autonomous driving Kiran et al. [2022], and robotic control Orr and Dutta [2023]. A common approach is for each agent to learn its own policy while treating others as part of the environment. As agents update their policies during training, the environment becomes non-stationary from each agent’s perspective, hindering stable and high-quality collaboration. To mitigate this non-stationarity and better capture collaborative structure in value estimation, centralized training with decentralized execution (CTDE) has become one of the mainstream frameworks in MARL, with representative methods including MADDPG Lowe et al. [2017] and COMA Foerster et al. [2018] for policy gradient, and MAAC Iqbal and Sha [2019] and QMIX Rashid et al. [2018] for value decomposition. During training, CTDE leverages global information to partially reduce non-stationarity; however, at execution, agents act independently based on local observations, and collaboration emerges passively through task rewards, lacking explicit signals to directly guide or promote coordinated behavior. To address these challenges, recent work has focused on designing more targeted coordination signals. One approach involves using single-step causal influence to build intrinsic rewards. Methods like SCIC [Du et al., 2024] and SI [Jaques et al., 2019] apply causal interventions or counterfactual reasoning to measure how an agent’s actions affect immediate actions or states of others, and use this influence as an auxiliary reward to reduce non-stationarity and encourage coordination. Another approach derives intrinsic rewards from statistical dependencies, such as correlation or mutual information (MI). For example, PMIC [Li et al., 2022] maximizes MI between the global

However, these methods face two key limitations. First, they rely on one-step dynamics, which fail to capture delayed cooperative effects that may only emerge after multiple steps. Second, when influence or MI is used directly as a proxy for coordination, the learner may reinforce behaviors that are highly influential but not beneficial to, or even potentially harmful to, team performance. This highlights the need for a coordination signal that reflects long-term effects on collective outcomes and intrinsically aligns with task success. To clarify these issues, consider a cooperative predator-prey scenario with two predators, a and b,jointly chasing a single prey. In Figure 1(a), agent b follows a smooth interception path behind the prey, while agent a gives up the shortest chase and moves sideways to block an escape corridor. This action effectively herds the prey toward b. Although this “give-way” action barely affects b’s next state and may worsen a’s immediate progress, it increases the probability of capture and improves long-term team performance over several steps. This improvement may not be reflected in single-step influence signals. In Figure 1(b), agent a instead cuts across b’s path to get closer to the prey, forcing 

b to turn sharply away from its original interception route. This substantial change in b’s next state would be rated highly by influence- or MI-based scores. However, such coordination actually allows the prey to escape more easily and ultimately reduces the team’s return. Intrinsic rewards that depend only on influence strength or on simple correlation may thus favor harmful, high-influence actions over beneficial herding actions. An effective coordination signal should instead reflect the long-term impact of an agent’s actions on team return. It should differentiate between helpful and harmful contributions over a longer horizon. 

1.2 Our Contribution 

To address the above challenges, we propose Multi-step Advantage-Gated Interventional Causal MARL (MAGIC), which explicitly models long-horizon causal influences among agents and aligns these influences with task returns, thereby providing more targeted learning signals for cooperative MARL. Concretely, MAGIC augments a standard CTDE backbone with two modules: a multi-step interven-tional causal influence module and an advantage-gating module. The multi-step module operates on a learned forward dynamics model. Starting from the current state, it performs short multi-step prediction rollouts for each candidate action and tracks how that action affects teammates’ local states over subsequent time steps, thereby making long-horizon influence explicit. To keep computational cost manageable, we train a single shared forward model from collected experience and conduct all rollouts within this model rather than in the real environment, so that the additional computation at each environment step scales linearly with the rollout horizon. To focus on causal effects rather than merely correlational ones, we intervene on the chosen action while keeping the state and other agents’ behaviors fixed. We then compare the conditional mutual information of the predicted trajectories resulting from different interventions. We interpret this quantity as a multi-step interventional causal 2influence signal. The advantage-gating module then leverages the task advantage for each agent, provided by the CTDE value function, to adjust this influence signal. Specifically, it suppresses actions with strong negative advantages and instead focuses on actions with positive advantages that genuinely promote coordination. On the theoretical side, we analyze both the multi-step interventional influence module and the advantage-gating module. For the former, we establish basic properties, consistency results, and error bounds for the multi-step causal influence estimator. These factors together characterize how the rollout horizon and the forward model’s accuracy affect the quality of the resulting causal signal. For the latter, we formalize advantage gating as a bounded reward-shaping term and show that, with suitable shaping weights, it neither alters the optimal joint policy of the original task nor increases the variance of the intrinsic reward. This indicates that the combination of multi-step causal modeling and advantage gating is beneficial in preserving the learning objective and stabilizing the training process. Finally, we evaluate MAGIC on standard MARL benchmarks, including cooperative navigation, cooperative predator–prey, and cooperative–competitive settings with adversarial agents. In all cases, MAGIC is applied only to the cooperative team and consistently improves its team return over the CTDE backbone and state-of-the-art influence-based and mutual-information-based intrinsic reward baselines.

## 2 Related Work 

In cooperative MARL, additional learning signals are often introduced to improve exploration, coordination, and credit assignment under sparse or delayed team rewards. Our work is most related to two lines of research. The first line uses inter-agent influence as an intrinsic reward. The second line studies how auxiliary rewards or coordination signals can be made consistent with task performance. We also compare with a recent parameter-sharing method because it improves coordination through a different mechanism. 

2.1 Influence-Based Intrinsic Rewards in MARL 

Intrinsic rewards have been widely used to encourage exploration in reinforcement learning, with common examples including empowerment, surprise, information gain, and learning progress [Mo-hamed and Rezende, 2015, Blaes et al., 2019, Houthooft et al., 2016]. In MARL, this idea has been extended to social influence. Early methods such as EITI [Wang et al., 2020], SI [Jaques et al., 2019], VM3-AC, and SI-MOA [Kim et al., 2020, Jaques et al., 2019] use mutual information or action influence to encourage agents to affect each other during learning. These methods show that inter-agent influence can provide useful coordination signals, but the influence is mainly measured through correlation, immediate action response, or trajectory-level dependence. More recent methods move closer to causal or task-aware influence. SCIC [Du et al., 2024] constructs a causal graph under CTDE and estimates single-step interventional conditional mutual information as an intrinsic reward. It is the closest influence-based baseline to MAGIC because both methods use intervention to measure causal influence between agents. The key difference is that SCIC focuses on one-step influence, while MAGIC estimates how one agent’s current action changes its teammates’ future states over multiple rollout steps. This allows MAGIC to capture delayed coordination effects that may not appear in the immediate transition.

## 3 Method 

MAGIC adds an auxiliary reward signal to a standard CTDE learner during policy training. This signal is intended to encourage actions that help teammates in later coordination. In many cooperative tasks, the effect of a current action may become clear only after several future interactions. Therefore, the auxiliary signal should satisfy two requirements. First, it should look beyond the next state, because a useful cooperative action may affect teammates more clearly after several interaction steps. Second, it should be aligned with task return, because changing teammates’ future states is not always beneficial for the team. MAGIC therefore combines two core components. The multi-step interventional causal influence estimator measures how one agent’s current action changes teammates’ future states over multiple horizons. The advantage-gated reward construction uses task advantage to control how strongly this estimated influence affects policy learning. Together, these two components convert multi-step causal influence into a task-aligned intrinsic reward. 43.1 Overview 

Figure 2 shows the training flow of MAGIC. The CTDE backbone collects trajectories and updates policies with a shaped training reward. The MAGIC module uses the collected transitions to learn a centralized forward model and an ICMI critic. For a selected agent and time step, the forward model generates intervention rollouts by changing that agent’s action inside the learned dynamics model. The ICMI critic then estimates how strongly this intervention changes teammates’ future states under the same scenario context. The raw influence score is agent-specific, but it is not yet a reward. An action can strongly affect teammates while still reducing team return. MAGIC therefore uses an extrinsic team advantage as a task-value signal to modulate the influence score before it enters policy learning. This construction preserves the causal-credit role of the influence estimator while using the advantage gate to control whether the estimated influence should be reinforced. The resulting intrinsic reward is added to the extrinsic team reward during training only.

Using raw influence directly as an intrinsic reward would therefore encourage any action that changes teammates, including harmful ones. ## 4 Experiments 

We evaluate MAGIC from three perspectives. We first test whether the proposed intrinsic signal improves learning on standard continuous-control coordination tasks. We then evaluate the same design on discrete-action micromanagement tasks with stronger partial observability and credit-assignment pressure. Finally, we analyze which components drive the gains and when the learned-dynamics causal score remains reliable. 

4.1 Experimental Protocol Benchmarks. We use two groups of environments. The first group contains three continuous-control tasks from the Multi-Agent Particle Environment (MPE): Predator Prey , Cooperative Nav-igation , and Cooperative Competitive . These tasks test cooperative pursuit, landmark coverage, and mixed cooperative-competitive interaction. The second group contains SMAC and SMACv2 micromanagement tasks, where agents must coordinate under partial observability, discrete actions, and delayed team rewards. This second group is used to evaluate whether the proposed signal remains useful in more structured and harder coordination settings. 

Baselines and backbones. We choose baselines according to the standard evaluation protocol of each benchmark group. On MPE, all methods are implemented on the same MADDPG-based CTDE backbone. This gives a controlled comparison with prior intrinsic-reward methods for continuous-control particle environments, including SI [Jaques et al., 2019], PMIC [Li et al., 2022], and SCIC [Du et al., 2024]. MADDPG serves as the plain CTDE backbone without intrinsic rewards. To examine whether MAGIC remains effective beyond the MADDPG-based MPE setting, we evaluate the same intrinsic module on SMAC and SMACv2 using a MAPPO-based CTDE protocol implemented in PyMARL2. This design tests the flexibility of the proposed coordination signal across both backbone families and task types. We include MAPPO as the plain policy-gradient backbone, QMIX as a representative value-decomposition baseline, PMIC and SCIC as recent influence- or credit-assignment baselines, and GradPS [Qin et al., 2025] as a recent coordination-oriented parameter-sharing baseline. Full implementation details for all baselines are provided in Appendix C. 

Evaluation protocol. All experiments are run with five random seeds under matched training budgets, observation settings, and evaluation procedures within each benchmark group. 4.4 Reliability of Multi-Step Causal Influence 

Since MAGIC estimates multi-step causal influence through learned-dynamics rollouts, its reliability depends on more than final task performance. A useful diagnostic should distinguish three quantities. The first is rollout error, which measures how accurately the forward model predicts future states. The second is score separability, which measures whether the ICMI critic can still distinguish matched action-outcome pairs from mismatched pairs under the same context. The third is downstream performance. This distinction is important because the intrinsic reward does not require exact long-horizon state reconstruction. It requires the learned model and critic to preserve enough relative separation between different interventions. We therefore report both prediction error and critic separation. In-MSE denotes rollout mean-squared error under policy actions, while Int-MSE denotes rollout mean-squared error under intervention rollouts, where the selected agent is intervened on and the other agents follow their learned policies. 

Sep. AUC is the ROC AUC for classifying matched versus mismatched intervention tuples using the horizon-aware ICMI critic. ## 5 Conclusion 

We propose MAGIC, a multi-step advantage-gated causal influence framework for cooperative MARL. MAGIC estimates how one agent’s action affects teammates over future steps and converts this influence into intrinsic reward only when it is supported by task advantage. This design separates influence measurement from task-value alignment, reducing the risk of rewarding harmful high-influence behaviors. Theoretically, we characterize the multi-step causal estimator and analyze the advantage gate as a task-aligned shaping mechanism. Empirically, MAGIC achieves the best average performance among strong baselines, including SI, PMIC, SCIC, GradPS, MAPPO, and QMIX, across MPE, SMAC, and SMACv2. Additional component and reliability analyses show that the gains come from both multi-step influence and advantage gating, and that the causal score remains useful when learned rollouts preserve intervention separability.

C.1 Multi-Agent Environment Configuration and Rewards Environment suite. All experiments are conducted on the Multi-Agent Particle Environment (MPE) benchmark suite [Lowe et al., 2017], instantiated via the PettingZoo implementation [Terry et al., 2021]. Agents move in a two-dimensional continuous plane with double-integrator dynamics and are subject to simple collision and boundary constraints. Unless otherwise stated, each task involves exactly five agents under a unified setting and is run for a maximum of T = 25 environment steps per episode. We use three representative tasks: 

Cooperative Navigation (C OOP . N AVI ) is a fully cooperative task where agents must jointly cover a set of landmarks while avoiding collisions. All agents share the same team reward and differ only in their initial positions and local observations. 

Predator Prey is a cooperative pursuit–evasion task in which multiple predators collaborate to capture a single prey under partial observability. Only predator policies are trained; the prey follows a fixed heuristic policy. The task is sensitive to coordinated tracking, role specialization, and collision avoidance among predators. 

Cooperative Competitive is a mixed cooperative–competitive scenario where a subset of agents must cooperate to occupy or defend a target region while adversarial agents attempt to interfere. Cooperative agents share a team reward, whereas adversaries receive an opposing signal, leading to coexisting cooperation and competition. A high-level summary is given in Table 4.

C.2 Network Architectures and Hyperparameters 

Within each benchmark group, methods are compared under matched backbone and training proto-cols. For MPE, all intrinsic-reward methods use the same MADDPG-based CTDE backbone, with MADDPG serving as the plain backbone. For SMAC and SMACv2, MAGIC and the intrinsic-reward baselines are evaluated under a shared MAPPO-based CTDE protocol, while QMIX is included as a representative value-decomposition baseline. Thus, architecture and optimization details should be interpreted within each benchmark group rather than as a single architecture shared by every method across all environments. 

MPE network architectures. For the MADDPG-based MPE experiments, actor, critic, value, forward-model, and ICMI networks are implemented as feed-forward multilayer perceptrons with ReLU activations. Actors use two hidden layers of size (128 , 128) and a final tanh squashing layer for bounded continuous actions. The centralized critic and centralized value network use two hidden layers of size (256 , 256) . The forward model is a two-layer MLP with hidden sizes (256 , 256) that maps the current centralized state and joint action to the next centralized state. The ICMI critic is also a two-layer MLP with hidden sizes (256 , 256) and estimates the NWJ lower bound used for interventional conditional mutual information. 

SMAC and SMACv2 architectures. For SMAC and SMACv2, we use the shared MAPPO-based PyMARL2 protocol described in Appendix C.5. MAGIC reuses the same intrinsic-reward design, including the forward model, horizon-aware ICMI critic, and advantage gate, while the policy and value updates follow the MAPPO backbone. 

Common hyperparameters. Table 5 lists the main hyperparameters used for the MADDPG-based MPE experiments. The SMAC and SMACv2 protocol is reported separately in Appendix C.5. 29 The shaping weight α is held fixed across tasks for each method and is chosen within the theoretically safe range implied by the reward-shaping analysis in Appendix A to avoid altering the optimal joint policy of the underlying CTDE game. 

Advantage gate instantiation. In all experiments, the gating function g(·) in Eq. (7) is instantiated as a sigmoid of the per-agent advantage:

C.5 SMAC and SMACv2 Protocol 

For the SMAC and SMACv2 evaluations, we use a unified PyMARL2-based protocol with StarCraft II version 4.10.0. MAGIC, SCIC, PMIC, and GradPS are evaluated on the same MAPPO-based CTDE backbone whenever applicable, and MAPPO is included as the plain policy-gradient backbone. QMIX is included as a representative value-decomposition baseline. All methods use identical observation spaces, action masking, and a 10 million environment-step budget without map-specific tuning. The shared MAPPO protocol uses Adam with learning rate 5 × 10 −4, 8 parallel workers, 3200 

collected transitions per update cycle, 5 PPO epochs, a PPO clipping ratio of 0.2, and GAE parameter 

λ = 0 .95 . Evaluation is performed every 10 ,000 environment steps using 32 deterministic test episodes. We report means and standard deviations over five random seeds. 

C.6 Compute Resources and Runtime 

MAGIC adds computation only during training. At execution time, action selection uses the same decentralized policies as the backbone and does not require forward-model rollouts or ICMI esti-mation. In our wall-clock measurements under identical setups, MAGIC increases training time by about 10% relative to SCIC. For reference, SCIC is about 15% slower than MADDPG and about 25% 

slower than MAPPO in our implementation. The added cost is mainly due to the moderate-horizon learned-dynamics rollouts and ICMI critic updates.
