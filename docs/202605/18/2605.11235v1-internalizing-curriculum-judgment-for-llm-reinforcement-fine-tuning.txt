Abstract 

In LLM Reinforcement Fine-Tuning (RFT), curriculum learning drives both effi-ciency and performance. Yet, current methods externalize curriculum judgment via handcrafted heuristics or auxiliary models, risking misalignment with the policy’s training dynamics. In this paper, we introduce METIS (METacognitive Internal-ized Self-judgment), a novel framework that internalizes curriculum judgment as a native capability. Leveraging a critical observation that within-prompt reward vari-ance effectively gauges prompt informativeness, METIS predicts this metric based on recent training outcomes as lightweight in-context learning examples. This intrinsic self-judgment then dynamically dictates the training allocation. Moreover, METIS closes the loop between judgment and optimization by jointly optimizing the standard RFT rewards and a self-judgment reward. This allows the policy to learn what to learn next, as a form of metacognition. Across extensive discrete and continuous RFT benchmarks from mathematical reasoning, code generation, to agentic function-calling, METIS consistently delivers superior performance while accelerating convergence by up to 67%. By bypassing handcrafted heuristics and auxiliary models, our work establishes a simple, closed-loop, and highly efficient curriculum internalization paradigm for LLM reinforcement fine-tuning. 

1 Introduction 

Effective learning depends not only on how a learner updates from feedback, but also on what it chooses to practice. Problems already mastered offer little information, while problems far beyond current ability often provide little actionable signal. The most useful practice lies near the boundary of current competence, echoing zone of proximal development in education [ 1]. Because this frontier shifts as learning progresses, a capable learner should therefore not only learn to solve problems, but also monitor their own learning state and decide which problems to learn next. This suggests a form of metacognition: using self-assessment to guide future learning effectively [2]. Such a principle has recently gained increasing traction in reinforcement fine-tuning (RFT) for large language models (LLMs) [ 3–5], which is operationalized as curriculum learning . Because rollouts are expensive in LLM RFT, the curriculum is not merely a data reordering: it determines how training compute is allocated and therefore directly affects both efficiency and final performance. Generally, a good curriculum prioritizes prompts that are informative for the current policy, rather than prompts that are uniformly easy, uniformly impossible, or selected by a static notion of difficulty.

While existing curricula rely on external schedules, heuristics, or auxiliary models, METIS internalizes curriculum judgment via in-context self-assessment, closing the loop by jointly optimizing standard RFT and self-judgment rewards. A key signal of such prompt informativeness arises naturally in group-relative RFT [ 4 , 6, 7], where the rewards are averaged from multiple rollouts of the same prompt: when all rollouts receive the same reward, the prompt provides little relative signal, since it is either consistently solved or failed; when rollouts produce diverse outcomes, the prompt exposes meaningful behavioral variation and induces more informative updates. Therefore, within-prompt reward variance provides a simple yet general proxy for identifying prompts at the frontier of policy competence [8–10]. In this paper, we formalize and extend such a proxy to handle both discrete and continuous rewards in LLM RFT. Moreover, such prompt informativeness is inherently dynamic. As the policy improves, previously insurmountable prompts may become informative, while once-useful prompts eventually become saturated. Existing curricula address this through various forms of adaptation (see Fig. 1): some rely on predefined coarse prompt groups based on domains or data sources [ 11 , 12 ], others apply hand-coded heuristics over rollout statistics or offline difficulty labels [ 8, 13 –16 ], and the rest train a separate model to score such informativeness [ 9, 10 ]. Crucially, across all these paradigms, curriculum judgment remains external to the policy. But because the competence frontier moves with the policy as it learns, accurate judgment requires tracking the policy’s current state; thus, we argue that the policy itself is the entity most directly aware of that state. As the LLM has strong inference and reasoning capabilities, it could be elicited to read its own rollout outcomes and predict which prompts will be informative for it next. However, current methods neglect such metacognitive potential, treating the policy as a passive recipient rather than an active architect of its own curricula. This motivates curriculum internalization . A capable learner does not merely follow a sequence of externally assigned exercises. It learns from its own attempts, forms an assessment of its current competence, and uses that assessment to decide what to practice next. We argue that an LLM trained with RFT should acquire an analogous capability. During training, rollout outcomes provide direct evidence about the policy’s current learning state. Internalizing curriculum judgment exposes the policy to this evidence directly and trains it to map recent prompt-outcome patterns to future prompt informativeness. The goal is therefore not only to select better prompts for the model, but to make the ability to judge useful prompts part of the model’s learned competence. This reframes curriculum learning from selecting data to training the model to decide what it should learn from next. To this end, we introduce METIS ( MET acognitive Internalized Self-judgment), a novel framework that internalizes curriculum judgment as a native policy capability. METIS asks the policy to self-judge candidate prompts by predicting their expected within-prompt reward variance as a measure of prompt effectiveness based on lightweight in-context examples constructed from recent training outcomes. The predicted scores then determine how the rollout budget is allocated. After rollout, the obtained data can be utilized for two coupled learning signals. The original task rewards improve the policy’s problem-solving behavior, while we introduce a judgment reward that calibrates the policy’s understanding of which prompts are informative. Together, METIS does not merely use the policy to solve tasks; it also learns what to learn next, closing the loop between judgment and optimization. Our main contributions are as follows: 1) We formulate curriculum learning for LLM RFT as a policy-dependent competence-frontier tracking problem, with within-prompt reward variance as a general signal of informativeness across both discrete and continuous rewards; 2) We propose METIS, the first LLM RFT framework to internalize curriculum judgment, while jointly optimizing 2task rewards and a self-judgment reward, transforming curriculum into a learned capability of the policy rather than an external selection mechanism; 3) On mathematical reasoning, code generation, and agentic function-calling benchmarks across discrete, continuous, and multi-turn rewards, METIS demonstrates improved final performance, yielding up to 67% wall-clock reduction with only around 3.9% per-step overhead, providing a metacognitive framework for efficient LLM RFT. 

2 Related Work 

Reinforcement fine-tuning (RFT). RFT methods vary in reward source and optimization objective. Standard RLHF aligns LLMs with human preference via PPO-style optimization [ 3 , 17 , 18 ], while critic-free, REINFORCE-style alternatives like RLOO, ReMax, and REINFORCE++ simplify the pipeline [ 19 – 21 ]. Reasoning-oriented fine-tuning increasingly relies on verifiable rewards [ 4, 5], where group-relative methods such as GRPO, DAPO, and GSPO estimate advantages from multiple rollouts of the same prompt [ 4, 6, 7], and process-level supervision provides a complementary direction [ 22 , 23 ]. Orthogonal to the specific update algorithm, METIS innovates the curriculum learning, emphasizing which prompts the policy trains on first, rather than how RFT is updated. 

Curriculum learning for RFT. We group existing methods by how curriculum judgment is produced, as illustrated in Figure 1. Category-level schedulers, such as DUMP [ 11 ] and SEC [ 12 ], adapt sampling over data sources or prompt categories, but remain coarse-grained and external to the policy. Prompt-level heuristic methods, including ADCL [ 13 ], SPEED-RL [ 24 ], DAPO Dynamic Sampling [ 6 ], VCRL [ 8], and GRESO [ 16 ], select or filter prompts using difficulty estimates, reward dynamics, or rollout statistics, but their judgments are still externally specified. Auxiliary-selector methods, such as PCL [ 10 ] and attention-based predictors [ 9], make curriculum selection learnable, but place the learned judgment in a separate model. In contrast, METIS internalizes curriculum judgment within the policy itself, enabling prompt-level pre-rollout self-judgment that is refined online from realized rollout outcomes without handcrafted heuristics or an auxiliary selector. 

In-context learning and model self-assessment. LLMs can adapt to new tasks via in-context learning (ICL) [ 25 – 30 ], and can express calibrated uncertainty about their outputs [ 31 – 36 ] during inference. METIS unites and extends these two capabilities for curriculum self-judgment in RFT. 

3 Problem Formulation 

We consider reinforcement fine-tuning (RFT) of a language-model policy πθ over a prompt pool P.For each prompt x ∈ P , the policy samples a rollout y ∼ πθ (· | x) and receives a bounded scalar reward r(x, y ) ∈ [0 , 1] . This formulation covers both binary verifiable rewards ( r ∈ { 0, 1}), common in mathematical reasoning, and continuous rewards such as partial test-case credit in code generation or trajectory-level scoring in agentic tasks. Notably, METIS operates uniformly across both regimes, whereas pass-rate heuristics used in prior work [6, 8–13] are restricted to the binary case.

The top-vθ set therefore traces a non-stationary competence frontier that moves with training, and a useful curriculum is one that tracks this frontier as it moves. Existing methods approximate Γ⋆ with a selector external to the policy: a fixed difficulty schedule [ 14 ,15 ], a hand-coded rule on post-rollout statistics [ 6, 8, 16 ], or an auxiliary selector model [ 9, 10 ]. In each case, what counts as informative is decided by a module the policy itself neither reads nor refines, leaving the external judgment liable to drift out of alignment with the policy’s evolving competence. METIS instead sets Γ = πθ : the policy itself decides the curriculum. At each step, it conditions on a

4.1 Self-Judgment via In-Context Learning 

The previous section reduced curriculum learning to estimating ˆvθ (x) ≈ vθ (x) for every candidate prompt x ∈ C t, with the selector implemented by the policy itself: Γ = πθ . The remaining design question is how the policy should produce this prediction. METIS addresses it through prompt-level 

in-context self-assessment: the policy reads a compact context built from the training history Ht and a candidate prompt, and emits a short structured judgment of the candidate’s expected informativeness. We note three properties that make ICL well-suited to curriculum self-judgment as follows: • Predictive. The policy emits ˆvθ (x) before any rollout, replacing the post-hoc filtering in prior variance-based methods [6, 11, 12] with a forward judgment over the candidate pool. • Adaptive. Conditioning on recent prompt–variance pairs lets the judgment track the moving competence frontier without retraining: prompts that have saturated or newly entered the informative band are reflected as soon as they appear in the conditioning context. • Efficient. Each judgment is a short structured response, in contrast to the n full rollouts that variance-filtering methods [ 8, 13 ] must generate per candidate just to compute the filter, typically more than an order of magnitude cheaper in our settings (cost analysis in Sec. 5). 4.3 Joint Optimization 

METIS optimizes two coupled token streams within the same policy: task-solution tokens (the rollouts yi) under the group-relative RFT objective Lpolicy of Eq. (2) , and self-judgment tokens under the calibration loss Ljudge . The combined objective is 

Ltotal = Lpolicy + λ Ljudge , (10) where λ controls the strength of the judgment update. Because both signals update the same policy, task rewards improve the model’s ability to solve prompts while calibration rewards improve its ability to anticipate which prompts will produce useful learning signal, so the induced curriculum evolves automatically with task ability, without any hand-tuned schedule, externally specified difficulty, or auxiliary selector. 5.2 Case Study: The Policy Tracks Frontier Competence via Its Own Curriculum

5.4 Compute Cost of Self-Judgment 

Fig. 5 reports per-step wall-clock overhead (left) and tokens-per-second-per-GPU throughput drop (right), both relative to No Curriculum. METIS’s only added cost over plain GRPO is a self-judgment forward pass on the m · B candidate prompts each step — a single short structured response per prompt, far cheaper than the n long task rollouts GRPO already produces, so the overhead stays marginal on both axes. The baselines each fail one axis: ADCL inflates wall-clock with extra rollouts that re-estimate prompt difficulty but never update the policy; PCL keeps wall-clock small but drops throughput substantially by training a separate value model alongside GRPO. SEC is the cheapest, where it only maintains coarse-grained EMA statistics over prompt categories; however, as we have seen, this efficiency is traded at the cost of accuracy. METIS is the only prompt-level method light on both axes, delivering effective curriculum essentially for free.

This shows that the variance-based curriculum of Sec. 3 remains effective even when reward arises from a stateful, multi-turn trajectory rather than a final-answer match. 96 Conclusions 

We presented METIS, a closed-loop framework in which the policy itself predicts prompt informa-tiveness via in-context self-judgment and is reinforced from realized rollout outcomes, learning both to solve prompts and to decide which prompts to learn from next. Across math, code, and agentic tasks, METIS delivers higher accuracy with up to 67% less wall-clock time at only 3.9% per-step overhead. These results indicate that prompt-level curriculum judgment can be reliably absorbed into the policy itself, removing the need for hand-tuned schedules or separately trained selectors in RFT. One potential limitation is that METIS relies on the base policy’s in-context learning capacity, which weaker backbones may not meet. While METIS produced visibly more stable training dynamics than baselines in our runs, we do not formally quantify training stability. Future work includes characterizing such stability systematically and deploying METIS on real-world LLM RFT systems. Our code, together with reproducibility instructions, will be made available on GitHub soon.

A.2 Dataset Pre-processing DAPO-Math-17k (math, training). We use the released DAPO-Math-17k collection of 17 .4kcompetition-style math problems [ 6 ] as is, without additional filtering. Each problem ships with a short reference final answer. The policy is instructed to produce a full chain-of-thought solution and a final-answer line; the reward is binary, equal to one when the extracted final answer exactly matches the reference under standard math normalization, and zero otherwise. 

CodeContests-Plus (code, training). We start from the public CodeContests-Plus dataset [ 44 ]and apply three filters that together leave 4,432 problems. First , we drop any problem with no Python program among its reference correct submissions, since we train a Python-only policy and the per-problem time limits are tuned for C++; problems that no Python program can solve within the allotted time only inject reward noise. Second , we drop problems with more than 25 test cases, since the long tail of test counts dominates rollout time without changing the per-problem ranking signal. Third , we drop problems whose statements reference figures (images or vector diagrams) that a text-only policy cannot read. The training reward is the fraction of test cases passed by the 15 model’s program when executed, continuous in [0 , 1] . The statement seen by the self-judgment predictor retains only the prose part of the description: we strip the formal Constraints, Input, Output, Examples, and Notes sections, as these are uninformative for variance prediction and would otherwise crowd the calibration context. 

BFCL v3 (agentic, training). We use all five multi-turn categories of the Berkeley Function Calling Leaderboard v3 [ 49 ] – Base, Missed-Function, Missed-Parameter, Long-Context, and Composite. Each task is a sequence of user turns interleaved with API calls against a stateful environment. We build a single system message that summarizes every available API in natural language, place the first user turn in the policy prompt, and feed the remaining user turns into the multi-turn interaction loop one at a time. The reference tool-call trajectory serves as ground truth, and the reward is computed against the actual sequence of tool calls executed by the model, yielding a continuous score in 

[0 , 1] . We split the resulting tasks 80% /20% into train and validation with per-category stratification, ensuring every category appears in both splits. 

Math evaluation suite. AIME 2024, AIME 2025, MATH-500, and Minerva Math share a single preparation pipeline. To keep offline numbers directly comparable to the training-time validation curves, we reuse the exact problem-wrapping instruction (think step by step and place the final answer in a boxed expression) together with the exact answer-extraction and Hendrycks-MATH normalization used during training. AIME 2024 contributes 30 problems and AIME 2025 contributes 

30 problems combining the two AIME 2025 olympiad sittings; MATH-500 contributes 500 problems whose released answers are already short strings. Minerva Math contributes 272 problems whose release contains full derivations rather than short answers; we recover each reference answer by taking the last boxed expression in the gold solution, with balanced-brace handling so that nested expressions (e.g. fractions) survive intact. 

Code evaluation suite. The code evaluation suite combines LiveCodeBench v6 [ 45 ] ( 1,055 prob-lems), HumanEval ( 164 problems), MBPP (full test split, 974 problems), and BigCodeBench (v0.1.4, 

1,140 problems). LiveCodeBench v6 is a competitive-programming benchmark with stdin/stdout test cases; we evaluate it under the same stdin/stdout prompt and execution-based scorer used during CodeContests-Plus training. The remaining three benchmarks are function-call-based. We evaluate HumanEval, MBPP, and BigCodeBench under benchmark-appropriate function-style prompts that ask the model to produce a complete Python function, matching the format each benchmark was originally designed for. Scoring on all four benchmarks is performed by executing the extracted code against the benchmark’s official tests in an isolated environment, with the score equal to the fraction of tests passed. 

A.3 Training Infrastructure 

All experiments are implemented on the VERL reinforcement-learning framework [ 52 ] with vLLM [ 53 ] as the policy rollout engine. For mathematical-reasoning training, rewards are cheap to compute (a verifier match against a short reference answer), so rollout, reward, and policy update run on a fully synchronous schedule. For code-generation and agentic function-calling training, rewards are dominated by external execution; we therefore run reward computation asynchronously, overlapping the scoring of completed rollouts with continued generation so that GPUs stay busy on the rollout engine while CPU workers handle the heavier execution side.

A.5 Baseline implementation details 

We compare METIS against three external-curriculum baselines: SEC, ADCL, and PCL. All three were originally proposed for binary-reward mathematical reasoning. We re-implement each on top of our shared GRPO trainer so that every method sees identical rollouts, optimizer, and hardware; the only differences are the curriculum mechanism itself and, for ADCL and PCL, the modifications described below that extend them to continuous-reward tasks. 

SEC. Self-Evolving Curriculum [ 12 ] treats curriculum learning as a non-stationary multi-armed bandit over coarse prompt categories. Our implementation closely follows the authors’ released code. We partition the training set into ten categories. At each training step, the sampler draws a category via Boltzmann softmax over per-category Q-values and then samples prompts uniformly within the chosen category until the batch is full. After the GRPO update, the Q-value of every category appearing in the batch is updated by an exponential moving average toward the mean per-token absolute advantage observed on its prompts, so categories that currently carry the largest gradient signal are progressively up-weighted. Because SEC consumes the GRPO advantage signal directly and never inspects the raw reward, the algorithm transfers to continuous-reward tasks (code generation, agentic function calling) without modification. 

ADCL. Adaptive Difficulty Curriculum Learning [ 13 ] is an offline-then-online easy-to-hard cur-riculum. Our implementation strictly follows the algorithm description in the original paper, using the curriculum-only variant. Before training, the base policy is rolled out on the entire training set to assign each prompt an initial difficulty score; the dataset is then sorted in ascending order by difficulty and partitioned into a fixed number of sequential chunks (eight in our runs). Training proceeds chunk by chunk; between chunks, the difficulty of the next chunk is re-estimated under the updated policy and the chunk is locally re-sorted, so the curriculum tracks the model’s evolving competence without re-sorting the whole dataset. In the binary-reward setting (mathematical reasoning), we keep the original definition of difficulty as one minus the empirical pass rate. For continuous-reward tasks (code generation, BFCL), pass rate is undefined since rewards are no longer 0/1; to extend ADCL to this regime, we rank prompts by their empirical reward variance instead: low-variance prompts are treated as easy (the policy’s behavior is already settled, either consistently succeeding or consistently failing), and high-variance prompts as hard (the behavior is still unstable). The chunk structure and between-chunk re-estimation are otherwise unchanged. 17 PCL. Prompt Curriculum Learning [ 10 ] replaces a hand-coded curriculum with a learned auxiliary value model. Our implementation strictly follows the algorithm description in the original paper. At each training step we sample a candidate pool several times larger than the batch, score every candidate with the value model, select the prompts whose predicted score best matches the desired target, run GRPO on the selected batch, and update the value model from the realized outcomes of that step. The value model is itself a language model: we initialize its backbone from the same pretrained model as the policy and attach a small scalar value head on top of the prompt’s final hidden state. It thus inherits the policy’s representation of natural-language problems while learning a separate readout for the curriculum signal. The value model and the policy do not share parameters during training. In the binary-reward setting we follow the original PCL: the value head regresses (under MSE) onto the empirical pass rate of each prompt, and selection picks the batch whose predicted pass rate is closest to a target of 0.5. For continuous-reward tasks, where pass rate is no longer defined, we adapt PCL to the informativeness signal used throughout this paper: the value head instead regresses onto the empirical reward variance of each prompt, and selection picks the prompts with the highest 

predicted variance. This keeps the auxiliary-selector design of PCL intact while making it apply uniformly to both binary and continuous rewards.

C Broader Impact 

METIS is a methodological improvement to reinforcement fine-tuning. Because its primary effect is to reduce the wall-clock and per-step cost of RFT (Sec. 5.4), its societal impact is shaped chiefly by who benefits from cheaper training rather than by any new model capability. 

Positive impact. Lowering the compute floor for reasoning, code, and agentic RFT broadens partic-ipation to academic and resource-constrained groups for whom replicating frontier RFT pipelines would otherwise be prohibitive, and reduces the aggregate energy and carbon footprint of model development at a given level of capability. 

Negative impact. The efficiency gain is intrinsically dual-use: METIS does not change what amodel can be trained to do, only how efficiently a given competence is reached, so it accelerates helpful and misuse-prone training pipelines alike. Our work does not itself contribute alignment or safety improvements, and downstream operators should treat the freed compute as an opportunity to strengthen evaluation and post-training safety work rather than only to scale capability. Concrete release-level safeguards are described in Appendix D. 25 D Safeguards 

METIS is a curriculum-learning method for reinforcement fine-tuning, not a foundation model or a dataset, and we release no new pre-trained checkpoints or scraped corpora; the artifacts shipped with the paper are limited to the training pipeline (MIT license) and reproducibility scripts. All datasets used (Tab. 7) are publicly released under permissive licenses with no personally identifying information, no scraped web text, and no demographic attributes, and the pre-trained backbones we fine-tune (Qwen3, Llama-3.1, DeepSeek-R1-Distill) are obtained from their official releases and inherit the safeguards put in place by their original authors; METIS does not weaken or remove these safeguards. As discussed in Appendix C, the efficiency gain from METIS is dual-use. We mitigate this by (i) restricting our reported experiments to public benchmarks under deterministic, machine-checkable rewards, (ii) documenting the full training pipeline (Appendix A) so that practitioners can layer standard safety post-training (RLHF, red-teaming, refusal training) on top of any METIS-fine-tuned model, and (iii) releasing the reference implementation as a small extension of VERL [ 52 ] rather than as model checkpoints, so that any deployment requires re-training and remains subject to the deployer’s own safety review.
