## Abstract 

On-Policy Self-Distillation (OPSD) has recently emerged as an alternative to Rein-forcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR), promising higher accuracy and shorter responses through token-level credit assignment from a self-teacher conditioned on privileged context. However, this promise does not carry over to thinking-enabled mathematical reasoning, where reported accuracy gains shrink and sometimes turn negative. We hypothesize that hindsight supervision can spec-ify better token-level alternatives in short thinking-disabled outputs, but in long thinking-enabled traces it more readily identifies redundancy than supplies better replacements. To test this, we applied OPSD separately to correct and incorrect rollout groups, so that compression and correction can be observed in isolation. Our results show that in thinking-enabled mathematical reasoning, OPSD behaves most reliably as a compression mechanism rather than a correction mechanism: training only on correct rollouts preserves accuracy while substantially shortening responses, whereas training only on incorrect rollouts damages accuracy. ## 1 Introduction 

On-Policy Self-Distillation (OPSD) has recently emerged as a promising alternative to Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) [ 1 –4]. Unlike RLVR, which reduces an entire rollout to a binary reward and broadcasts that single bit information across the generated tokens [ 5 – 7], OPSD can use richer textual feedback and convert it into token-level credit assignment. It does so through a self-teacher that shares the same parameters as the student model but is conditioned on additional privileged context, such as an expert demonstration, environment feedback, or the student’s own reflection. For each token in a student-generated rollout, the self-teacher provides a target distribution, which the student is trained to match. Thanks to these mechanisms, OPSD yields models with higher end-task accuracy and shorter response length. However, this is not the case in the setting of thinking-enabled mathematical reasoning. Here, the accuracy gains are often small and sometimes negative. Most of the gains reported in thinking-disabled regimes are lost once thinking is enabled. And on mathematical reasoning, with its longer reasoning traces, the remaining gains appear to shrink further than on tasks like knowledge, science, or tool-use. Together, these patterns suggest that something specific to thinking-enabled mathematical reasoning is limiting OPSD’s accuracy gains. We hypothesize that this is because, in thinking-disabled settings, the hindsight-conditioned self-teacher can directly specify the desired alternative tokens in the student’s short output, whereas in thinking-enabled settings, the student generates a sequence of intermediate reasoning steps for which it is difficult to pinpoint the correct alternative tokens. Furthermore, on complex mathematical reasoning tasks, it becomes increasingly more difficult to do so, as they require of even longer reasoning traces to be generated [ 8– 10 ]. Yet it remains capable of pointing out redundancies within the reasoning trace, since hindsight can reveal which steps were repeated or unnecessary without necessarily revealing which alternative reasoning steps would have repaired the solution . Therefore, it is our hypothesis that in thinking-enabled mathematical reasoning settings, OPSD manifests as a token-level repetition-penalty mechanism rather than an accuracy-improving correction mechanism. To test this, we applied OPSD separately to correct and incorrect rollout groups . If, in this setting, OPSD simply works as a repetition-penalty mechanism, OPSD training on correct rollouts will mainly compress already-correct reasoning traces, and OPSD training on incorrect rollouts will bring down accuracy. If it works as a correction mechanism, OPSD training on both groups of rollouts will show accuracy improvements. Our results show that in thinking-enabled mathematical reasoning, OPSD behaves most reliably as a compression mechanism rather than a correction mechanism . OPSD applied only to correct rollouts preserves accuracy while substantially reducing response length. Training only on incorrect rollouts damages accuracy, indicating that the hindsight-guided self-teacher does not reliably supply the missing reasoning steps needed to repair failed trajectories. Rather, its confident, less-exploratory signal is most useful when applied to already successful reasoning, where it removes redundancy and exploratory detours while preserving the underlying solution structure. We then test whether this compression-not-correction pattern can be explained by narrower imple-mentation factors. First, the self-teacher may lack a sufficient informational advantage, making accuracy improvement depend on richer privileged context. Second, the token-level signal may be too front-loaded, weakening over long reasoning traces because the privileged context appears only at the beginning. Third, reasoning correction may require many optimization steps before local token updates accumulate into global repair. Richer teacher contexts, mid-trace feedback reinjection, and longer training do not qualitatively change the outcome. These interventions move points along the accuracy-length tradeoff, but do not turn OPSD into a reliable failed-trajectory correction mechanism. In light of these findings, we propose a revised post-training pipeline for thinking-enabled mathemati-cal reasoning: SFT → RLVR → OPSD . SFT establishes format and instruction-following, RLVR expands the model’s reachable reasoning trajectories through outcome-based learning [ 6, 11 , 12 ], and OPSD finally serves as a compaction stage that shortens reasoning traces the model can al-ready execute. Figure 1 provides experimental evidence for this ordering. Therefore, in this setting, OPSD is better understood not as a replacement for RLVR, but as a post-RL mechanism for making already-learned reasoning cheaper to execute. Because inference cost scales with the number of generated tokens, this compaction directly reduces the serving cost of thinking-enabled reasoning models without giving up the capabilities RLVR delivered. 22 Background and Related Work 

2.1 On-Policy Self-Distillation 

On-Policy Self-Distillation builds on a long line of work in which a student is trained against soft targets from a teacher [ 13 , 14 ], with the special case where the teacher shares the student’s parameters and is conditioned on extra context not given to the student [ 15 ]. The student sees only the question and produces a rollout; the teacher sees the question plus a privileged context (a ground-truth answer, an expert demonstration, or a written reflection) and scores the student rollout token-by-token via reverse-KL. For a prompt x, let y denote the sampled response. OPSD draws y ∼ πS (· | x), then compares the student at each prefix y<t to a teacher that sees the same prefix plus privileged context c:

2.2 Related Work OPSD as a post-training mechanism. A growing body of work uses OPSD as a post-training mechanism, either as the main training objective [ 1– 4 , 16 ] or as a supplementary loss layered on top of another optimizer such as GRPO [ 17 –23 ]. However, most reported gains are measured with thinking turned off: prior work commonly uses instruct models or runs reasoning models with thinking disabled, even when evaluating on mathematical benchmarks [ 2, 16 – 23 ]. This distinction is consequential: in thinking-disabled regimes, teacher context can often specify the desired behavior as a single-step output that per-token KL can install directly as a pointwise mapping, whereas thinking-enabled mathematical reasoning requires a long sequence of intermediate steps, making it harder for a hindsight-conditioned self-teacher to identify locally better token-level alternatives that improve the final answer. Consistent with this distinction, OPSD-family methods report large accuracy gains in thinking-disabled settings but much smaller or mixed gains when evaluated with thinking enabled on mathematical reasoning (Table 1). Our work therefore asks whether OPSD in this regime corrects failed reasoning or instead compacts trajectories that are already viable. 3.1 Experimental Setup 

Our experiments are designed to isolate where the OPSD signal is applied, rather than changing the teacher, data, or evaluation protocol. Throughout the main comparison, we keep the teacher context fixed and vary only which rollouts contribute to training or which divergence is used. 

Teacher context. Our default privileged context is Reflection from GT , a student-generated reflection on the rollout produced after seeing the ground-truth answer. To construct it, the student first generates a rollout from the problem; then the same model receives the ground-truth answer and reflects on its attempt. During OPSD, the self-teacher is conditioned on the original problem and the generated reflection. Thus the privileged information enters through a student-generated reflection. We use 

Reflection from GT as the default context here because the main experiment is about rollout scope; we later vary the teacher context in Section 4. 

Models and data. We evaluate OPSD on three 7–8B thinking-enabled reasoning models that span the pre/post-RLVR axis. Qwen3-8B [ 30 ] and DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B [ 6 ] are two pre-RLVR base models; AceReason-Nemotron-7B [ 11 ] is a public RLVR checkpoint trained with GRPO [ 5]on top of R1-Distill-7B. This makes R1-Distill-7B the pre-RLVR initialization of AceReason-7B, giving a matched pre/post-RL pair on a single SFT base; we report Qwen3-8B and AceReason-7B in the main text and R1-Distill-7B in Appendix A. All OPSD runs use DAPO-Math-17K [ 7 ] as the on-policy training prompt distribution, and the paper’s claims are scoped throughout to thinking-enabled mathematical reasoning on these models and this distribution. 

Evaluation. We evaluate on MATH500 [ 31 ], AIME24, and AIME25, which together test mathemat-ical reasoning across a moderate-to-hard difficulty range. Each benchmark is scored using average@8 accuracy, sampled at temperature 0.6, and mean response length under a 30,000-token generation cap. We report ∆Accuracy in percentage points and ∆Length as a relative percentage change from each model’s own pre-OPSD baseline. In tables, Avg. ∆ (pp) always appears under the Accuracy header and averages accuracy changes across evaluation benchmarks, in percentage points, while Avg. ∆ (%) 

always appears under Length and averages relative length changes across evaluation benchmarks, in percent. 

Training protocol. Unless otherwise noted, all runs use AdamW with batch size 32, maximum training generation length 8,192, constant learning rate 1 × 10 −6, gradient clipping at 1.0, no warmup, and 100 optimization steps. The self-teacher is held frozen at the student’s initial weights throughout training, following [ 1, 32 ]. Student rollouts during training are sampled at temperature 0.7. Each 100-step run uses 4 × H100 GPUs for approximately 6 hours. For each sampled student rollout, we extract the final answer and verify it against the ground truth with a rule-based verifier that applies symbolic and numeric canonicalization. The resulting correctness label is used only for the outcome-filtered OPSD training settings below; standard All-rollout OPSD does not require it. 

3.2 Outcome-Filtered OPSD Training Reveals Compaction Rather Than Repair 

OPSD itself does not require rollout correctness labels. In the standard OPSD All-rollout setting, the only requirement for the reverse-KL distillation loss is privileged context for the self-teacher. We introduce outcome-filtered OPSD training as a diagnostic intervention: Correct-only and Incorrect-only use the same reverse-KL loss on correct and incorrect rollouts, respectively. If OPSD repairs failed trajectories, Incorrect-only should improve accuracy, since those are precisely the cases where correction is needed. Because rollout correctness flows into state selection but not update direction, it provides no direct channel for reinforcing the tokens that caused a correct answer. Its role is different: it diagnoses where the teacher-following compression is safe. 53.4 Correctness Determines Whether Compression Is Safe 

If correctness does not choose the token target, where does it matter? It determines the substrate on which the same hindsight-guided and confident self-teacher signal lands. On correct rollouts, it can remove redundant checks, over-verification, and self-sabotaging detours while preserving the solution structure. One plausible reading is the compounding-error account [ 4]: when per-token reasoning is approximately reliable, redundant tokens still carry small accumulating risk, so removing them can improve reliability without changing per-token competence. The same hindsight-guided signal is less safe on incorrect rollouts. Privileged context can concentrate the teacher-student gap at pivot tokens, where knowing the answer makes one path look obvious even though the unconditioned student may still need to explore [ 22 ]. When the rollout is wrong, those pivots are often precisely where exploration, checking, or recovery moves are needed. Compress-ing them can therefore make the failed path more confident and less exploratory, suppressing the reconsiderations that might have helped the student escape [32]. This mechanism mirrors the protocol contrast in Section 2.2: hindsight-guided supervision can help when privileged context largely specifies what the model should produce, but does not reliably install a new sequence of reasoning states that the student’s distribution does not already support [22, 32]. Compression is the primary observed operation; we do not observe a robust correction channel, not here and not even after varying KL direction, teacher contexts, signal reinjection, and training durations in Sections 3.5 and 4. 

3.5 Divergence Choice Does Not Restore Failed-Trajectory Repair 

A remaining possibility is that Incorrect-only fails because reverse-KL is mode-seeking. Reverse-KL pressures the student to keep probability mass only where the self-teacher agrees with the student’s current distribution, which may not force a failed trace toward teacher-supported alternatives [ 20 , 36 , 37 ]. We therefore include two divergence ablations in Table 2: All-rollout JSD , which replaces reverse-KL with a symmetric divergence on every rollout, and Split-direction , which keeps reverse-KL on correct rollouts but applies forward-KL to incorrect rollouts [21]. These variants change the accuracy-length tradeoff, but do not turn OPSD into a robust repair mechanism for failed trajectories. All-rollout JSD improves the AceReason average relative to 

All-rollout reverse-KL but still behaves primarily as a compressor, while Split-direction partially improves the Qwen3-8B tradeoff but does not transfer cleanly across benchmarks or models. Thus divergence choice is a useful ablation of the failed-rollout branch, but the main limitation is not reverse-KL alone: the self-teacher still supplies a hindsight-conditioned signal on the student’s own trajectory rather than a new sequence of reasoning states. 64 Richer Context, Signal Reinjection, and Prolonged Training 

The outcome-filtered training results leave three narrower explanations to rule out. First, OPSD may fail to correct because the self-teacher lacks sufficient privileged information. Second, the teacher signal may be too front-loaded, weakening before it reaches the mid-rollout reasoning steps where correction is needed. Third, correction may require many optimization steps before local token updates compound into sequence-level repair. We test all three and find that they move variants along the same accuracy-length tradeoff rather than turning OPSD into a reliable correction mechanism.

A third alternative explanation is that correction requires accumulation across many optimization steps before local token updates compound into sequence-level repair. OPSD updates the student one token at a time toward a context-conditioned teacher, and several hundred steps of this could in principle build up sequence-level repair that 100 steps cannot reach. ## 5 Where OPSD Belongs in the Training Pipeline 

Our results motivate SFT → RLVR → OPSD as the natural ordering in the setting we study. SFT establishes format and instruction-following from a fixed data or teacher distribution. RLVR is sparse, but because rollouts come from the current policy, successful updates feed back into the next sampling distribution and can expand the model’s reachable reasoning trajectories. OPSD then applies dense token-level pressure from a privileged self-teacher; in our experiments, that pressure is most useful for shortening and de-noising trajectories for problems the model can already solve, not for creating new ones. This suggests delaying OPSD until correct trajectories are sampled frequently enough that the self-teacher has enough viable trajectories to safely compress; before that, rollout compute is better spent on RLVR. OPSD is therefore best characterized here as a post-RL compaction stage: a low-budget update that removes redundant reasoning from already-supported solutions, but not a reliable substitute for RLVR when the missing behavior is a new reasoning state. 

## 6 Conclusion 

We investigated whether OPSD in thinking-enabled mathematical reasoning operates as a correction mechanism on failed trajectories or as a compaction mechanism on already-viable ones. Across Qwen3-8B and AceReason-Nemotron-7B, applying OPSD training to correct rollouts gave the safest accuracy-preserving compression, while applying it to incorrect rollouts damaged accuracy; richer teacher context, mid-trace reinjection, divergence changes, and longer training preserved the same compression-not-correction pattern. Our diagnosis is that a hindsight-guided self-teacher can help when privileged context largely specifies what the model should produce, but in our experiments is a weak correction source for long mathematical reasoning traces because it does not reliably provide the missing intermediate states needed for new solutions. Thus on these tasks OPSD remains useful in a different pipeline slot: RLVR establishes reachable reasoning capability, then a short post-RL OPSD stage makes that already-learned reasoning cheaper to execute, suggesting a revised pipeline. 9Acknowledgments and Disclosure of Funding

Reflection. 

Key insight: The problem hinges on recognizing that negative exponents in modular arithmetic correspond to modular inverses, which can be calculated using the Extended Euclidean Algorithm or trial multiplication, provided the base and modulus are coprime. What you did right: You correctly identified that $3^{-2}$ can be expressed as the square of $3^{-1}$, reducing the problem to computing a single inverse and squaring it, rather than finding the inverse of 9 directly, which simplifies the process. Efficient path: Identify the need for modular inverses, compute $a^{-1}$ via multiplication or algorithm, square it to get $a^{-2}$, sum the results, and reduce modulo $n$ using division or subtraction. 

D.4 Reflection from GT Problem. 

If the product $\left(4^5\right)\left(5^{13}\right)$ is written as an integer, how many digits are in this integer? 

Reflection. 

Memo: Key Insights and Efficient Path for Solving the Problem **Key Insight:** The critical insight was recognizing that $4^5$ can be rewritten as $2^{10}$, allowing the expression $2^{10} \cdot 5^{13}$ to be simplified by grouping $2^{10}$ and $5^{10}$ into $10^{10}$. D.5 Reflection from demo Problem. 

At the grocery store, I bought $4$ different items. I brought $3$ identical bags, and handed them to the cashier. How many ways are there for the cashier to put the items I bought in the $3$ identical bags, assuming he might leave some of the bags empty? 

Reflection. 

### Memo: Key Insights and Efficient Approach ---### **Key Insight** The problem involves **distributing distinct items into identical bags** with **allowed empty bags**, which is a classic case of **set partitioning**.
