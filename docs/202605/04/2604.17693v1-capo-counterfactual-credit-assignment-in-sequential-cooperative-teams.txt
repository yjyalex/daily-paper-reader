Abstract 

In cooperative teams where agents act in a fixed order and share a single team reward, it is hard to know how much each agent contributed, and harder still when agents are updated one at a time because data collected earlier no longer reflects the new policies. We introduce the Sequential Aristocrat Utility (SeqAU), the unique per-agent learning signal that maximizes the individual learnability of each agent‚Äôs action, extending the classical framework of Wolpert and Tumer [2002] to this sequential setting. From SeqAU we derive CAPO (Counterfactual Advan-tage Policy Optimization), a critic-free policy-gradient algorithm. CAPO fits a per-agent reward decomposition from group rewards and computes the per-agent advantage in closed form plus a handful of forward passes through the current policy, requiring no extra environment calls beyond the initial batch. We give ana-lytic bias and variance bounds and validate them on a controlled sequential bandit, where CAPO‚Äôs advantage over standard baselines grows with the team size. The framework is general; multi-LLM pipelines are a natural deployment target. 

1 Introduction 

Cooperative multi-agent systems arise in settings ranging from classical multi-agent reinforcement learning [Vlassis, 2007, Zhang et al., 2021] to modern pipelines composed of large language models and tool-using agents [Liu et al., 2025, Zhao et al., 2025, Chen et al., 2026]. A common chal-lenge in these systems is credit assignment: a team of agents receives only one team-level reward, and attributing this shared signal to individual agents is under-determined. Classical approaches handle this through centralized critics, whose input space grows exponentially in the team size [Fo-erster et al., 2018]; through factored value functions, which introduce modeling constraints [Sune-hag et al., 2018, Rashid et al., 2018, Wang et al., 2021, Peng et al., 2021]; or through cumulative importance-sampling corrections, whose variance grows exponentially in the number of agents al-ready updated [Kuba et al., 2022]. The sequential cooperative setting, in which agents act in a fixed order and are updated one at a time within a batch, brings a further difficulty: updating an upstream agent shifts the distribution over downstream agents‚Äô behavior, so rollouts collected under the pre-update policy are off-policy for subsequent updates within the same batch.

We extend the opacity-learnability framework of Wolpert and Tumer [2002] from simultaneous to sequential cooperative teams, and identify the unique prefix-dependent baseline that maximizes per-agent learnability; subtracting it from the team reward gives the Sequential Aristocrat Utility (SeqAU). Extension to multi-agent language-model pipelines [Liu et al., 2025, Chen et al., 2026, Zhao et al., 2025], where sequential execution is native, is left to follow-up work.

7 Related Work 

Cooperative MARL credit assignment. The opacity-learnability framing that motivates SeqAU originates with Wolpert and Tumer [2002]. COMA [Foerster et al., 2018] operationalized the AU counterfactual through a centralized critic at O(|A| K ) marginalization cost. Closer in spirit, Dr.Reinforce [Castellini et al., 2025] combines policy gradients with aristocrat-utility difference re-wards computed from the environment reward, but targets simultaneous-execution Dec-POMDPs and has no analog for the indirect effect that arises only under sequential execution. In the factored-policy limit, CAPO‚Äôs direct effect reduces to Dr.Reinforce‚Äôs aristocrat utility under additive re-wards. Additive or factored team-reward decomposition in cooperative multi-agent RL dates back to Guestrin et al. [2002] and Kok and Vlassis [2006]; the decomposed-critic family moves this prin-ciple from the reward to the value function, with DOP [Wang et al., 2021] assuming an additively factored Q and VDN [Sunehag et al., 2018], QMIX [Rashid et al., 2018], FACMAC [Peng et al., 2021], and DAE [Li et al., 2022] using related value-side factorizations. All assume simultaneous 

decentralized execution in Dec-POMDPs, with no mechanism for one agent‚Äôs action to enter an-other agent‚Äôs input, so the indirect effect that characterizes the sequential setting is identically zero by construction. CAPO inherits this additive-reward decomposition principle but applies it to the reward rather than the value function, identifying per-agent components by closed-form regression rather than by gradient-based critic learning. 

Sequential cooperative credit assignment. The sequential prior work is comparatively sparse. HAPPO [Kuba et al., 2022] performs sequential per-agent updates with a cumulative importance-sampling prefix, paying the exponential-in-k variance cost analyzed in Section 5; HA-GRPO is its critic-free GRPO variant. SeCA [Zang et al., 2023] imposes a virtual agent ordering and learns a counterfactual centralized Q-critic conditional on the prefix of preceding agents‚Äô actions, but the ordering is virtual (execution remains simultaneous and downstream agents do not actually condi-tion on upstream actions), and the centralized Q enumerates discrete actions, intractable for LLM token sequences. Seq-MADAC [Lu et al., 2025] uses a HAPPO-style sequential decomposition for hyperparameter configuration without a counterfactual baseline. Most relevant conceptually is Tri-antafyllou et al. [2025], who formalize the decomposition of the total counterfactual effect of an agent‚Äôs action in sequential multi-agent MDPs into an agent-specific effect (propagating through subsequent agents‚Äô actions, analogous to CAPO‚Äôs indirect effect) and a state-specific effect (prop-agating through state transitions). Their estimator is a Shapley-value post-hoc attribution; CAPO 10 turns the same direct-versus-indirect decomposition into a tractable policy-gradient estimator un-der the additive-reward assumption. Our work uses learnability maximization baseline which is in contrast with the optimal variance-reduction baseline [Kuba et al., 2021] for multi-agent settings. 

Critic-free policy gradients for multi-agent LLMs. A recent line adapts GRPO [DeepSeek-AI, 2025]‚Äôs critic-free within-group baseline to multi-agent LLM pipelines, with two families. Shared-baseline methods (MA-GRPO [Liu et al., 2025], AT-GRPO [Zhao et al., 2025], M-GRPO [Hong et al., 2025]) assign every agent the same trajectory or hierarchy-level advantage; HA-GRPO adds HAPPO‚Äôs cumulative importance-sampling prefix to correct sequential-update drift. Counterfactual methods estimate per-agent credit by intervening on upstream actions and observing downstream outputs. The two closest concurrent comparisons are C3 [Chen et al., 2026], which replays the downstream pipeline at alternative focal actions and reads actual environment rewards via a leave-one-out baseline (capturing the fixed-prefix counterfactual, but freezing downstream continuations to the rollout-collection distribution rather than the current joint policy, and paying one full re-ward evaluation per replay), and CCPO [Li et al., 2026b], which estimates counterfactual credit via topology-specific shortcuts (for example, ‚Äúremoving‚Äù the reasoner in a reasoner‚Äìsolver pipeline by resampling the solver from the empty prompt); these shortcuts are tightly tied to the pipeline struc-ture and do not extend to general sequential chains. Shapley-style alternatives such as SHARP [Li et al., 2026a] and Lazy Agents [Zhang et al., 2025] use multi-step causal attribution at correspond-ingly higher per-step cost; single-agent hindsight-credit work [Tan et al., 2026] is in the same wave but a different setting. CAPO‚Äôs distinguishing combination against this line is critic-free, reward-side (closed-form ridge regression rather than learned Q or environment replay), uses current-policy fictitious continuations (rather than frozen-prefix replay), and is generic across sequential chains (no topology assumption beyond fixed execution order). 

8 Conclusion 

We have presented CAPO, a critic-free per-agent policy gradient for cooperative multi-agent teams under sequential execution. On the theoretical side, we extended the opacity-learnability frame-work of Wolpert and Tumer [2002] to the sequential setting and identified the unique learnability-maximizing prefix-conditional baseline, which subtracted from the team reward gives the Sequen-tial Aristocrat Utility (SeqAU). On the algorithmic side, CAPO estimates the SeqAU advantage in closed form through an additive reward decomposition, an upstream-cancellation identity, and fic-titious sampling for the indirect effect. The resulting estimator admits analytic bias and variance bounds, which we validate on a controlled sequential-bandit testbed. Several directions remain open. The additivity assumption is exact when rewards separate across agents and gracefully lossy otherwise; heavily interaction-dependent rewards are a regime where a richer estimator may be preferable. Extension beyond one-step bandits to full sequential MDPs, and end-to-end evaluation on multi-LLM pipelines, are natural follow-ups.
