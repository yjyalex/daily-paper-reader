Abstract 

Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) has become a core post-training step for aligning large language models, yet the reward signal used in RLHF is only a learned proxy for true human utility. From an operations research perspective, this creates a decision problem under objective misspecification: the policy is optimized against an estimated reward, while deployment performance is determined by an unobserved objective. The resulting gap leads to reward over-optimization, or Goodharting, where proxy reward continues to improve even after true quality deteriorates. Existing mitigations address this problem through uncertainty penalties, pessimistic rewards, or conservative constraints, but they can be computationally burdensome and overly pessimistic. We propose Wasserstein distributionally robust regret optimization (DRRO) for RLHF. Instead of pessimizing worst-case value as in standard DRO, DRRO pessimizes worst-case regret relative to the best policy under the same plausible reward perturbation. We study the promptwise problem through a simplex allocation model and show that, under an ℓ1

ambiguity set, the inner worst-case regret admits an exact solution and the optimal policy has a water-filling structure. These results lead to a practical policy-gradient algorithm with a simple sampled-bonus interpretation and only minor changes to PPO/GRPO-style RLHF training. The framework also clarifies theoretically why DRRO is less pessimistic than DRO, and our experiments show that DRRO mitigates over-optimization more effectively than existing baselines while standard DRO is systematically over-pessimistic.

# 1 Introduction 

Large language models owe much of their practical usefulness not only to scale, but also to post-training . Pretraining creates a strong next-token predictor, but not necessarily a model that is helpful, safe, or responsive to human intent. Modern alignment pipelines therefore add supervised fine-tuning and then preference-based policy optimization, most prominently reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) [Christiano et al., 2017, Ziegler et al., 2019, Stiennon et al., 2020, Ouyang et al., 2022]. For readers outside the core RLHF literature, the basic pipeline is simple: prompts are sampled, the model generates candidate responses, humans express pairwise preferences over those responses, a reward model is fit to those preferences, and the language model is then optimized against the learned reward, often while being kept close to a reference policy. The leverage of this final stage is substantial. In the InstructGPT study, Ouyang et al. [2022] reported that a 1.3B aligned model was preferred by human raters to a 175B model without comparable RLHF, illustrating how much of a modern assistant’s behavior is determined by post-training rather than pretraining alone. That same pipeline also creates the problem studied in this paper. A reward model trained through a Bradley–Terry-style comparison objective [Bradley and Terry, 1952] is only a proxy for true human preference. It may rank responses well near the data distribution on which it was trained and extrapolate poorly once policy optimization begins pushing the model toward new regions of response space. In other words, the reward model is not merely noisy; it is an imperfect objective that is subsequently optimized hard. This is exactly the setting in which Goodhart’s law becomes operational. The empirical signature is now clear. In the controlled study of Gao et al. [2023], the proxy reward continued to improve as the policy moved farther from the initial model, while a stronger gold-standard reward first peaked and then deteriorated. This is not a cosmetic gap. It means that the very signal used for training can certify progress while actual quality is already declining. Hosking et al. [2024] sharpen the same point from another angle by showing that human-feedback scores themselves are not a gold standard and can be distorted by stylistic confounders such as assertiveness. Rafailov et al. [2024] show that analogous over-optimization appears even in direct alignment algorithms, and Karwowski et al. [2024] analyze the broader mechanism through the lens of Goodhart’s law in reinforcement learning. RLHF is a clean and consequential example of decision making under objective misspecification. The decision maker must choose a policy before the realized user preference is known, but the available objective is only an estimate. More importantly, the misspecification is decision-dependent: optimization changes the response distribution, which changes where the reward model is asked to extrapolate. This creates a robust decision problem with endogenous objective uncertainty. In the operations research 2Figure 1: Reward over-optimization in RLHF, adapted from Gao et al. [2023]. As the policy drifts farther from the initial model, the proxy reward can continue to rise even after the stronger evaluator has started to fall. literature, Ben-Tal et al. [2013] study robust solutions under uncertain probabilities, Wiesemann et al. [2014] develop a general framework for distributionally robust convex optimization, and Mohajerin Esfahani and Kuhn [2018] show how Wasserstein ambiguity sets lead to tractable, data-driven DRO. RLHF fits squarely into this line of thinking, but with an unusually transparent behavioral interpretation. Recent mitigation strategies already move in this robust direction. For example, Coste et al. [2024] and Eisenstein et al. [2024] use reward-model ensembles to hedge against reward hacking, Xiong et al. [2024] and Das et al. [2024] refresh the reward signal as the policy drifts, and Zhai et al. [2024], Xu et al. [2025], Yan et al. [2024], and Moskovitz et al. [2024] modify the policy objective through uncertainty penalties, pessimistic rewards, or support constraints. These ideas attack the right failure mode, but they also expose a second tension: robustness can itself become over-pessimistic. Xu et al. [2025] argue that KL-based protection can exclude policies that move far from the data distribution even when those policies perform well, Wolf et al. [2025] find that stability-oriented reinitialization reduces over-optimization but also limits optimization flexibility and yields diminishing gains, and Li et al. [2025] show that several divergence-based bonuses can bias exploration toward high-probability regions of the reference model and thereby reinforce conservative behavior. The challenge, then, is not merely to robustify RLHF, but to robustify it against the right benchmark. The most direct mathematical formalization of robustness would be standard distri-butionally robust optimization (DRO): maximize the worst-case value of a policy over a neighborhood of plausible true rewards around the proxy reward. But worst-case value is 3not the most compelling deployment benchmark. In practice, we do not ask whether a shipped model performs well relative to a fixed baseline of zero under an adversarially perturbed reward. We ask whether it performs close to the best policy that would have been chosen under the same realized true reward . That difference is not semantic. It is exactly where value-based robustness can become too pessimistic. When all candidate policies share the same reward ambiguity, comparing each one to an absolute worst-case baseline can penalize useful improvement more than necessary. This motivates our central idea: robustify regret , not value. We bring distributionally robust regret optimization (DRRO) to RLHF. DRRO compares a deployed policy to the best policy in the same plausible world, thereby asking for robustness without automatically giving up more upside than necessary. The benchmark is especially natural in RLHF because the decision is ex ante: one policy must be committed before the realized user preference is observed. This links RLHF directly to the recent ex-ante DRRO literature, including Agarwal and Zhang [2022], Cho and Yang [2024], Bitar [2024], and Fiechtner and Blanchet [2025]. At the same time, the problem is challenging because regret-robust optimization is usually harder than value-robust optimization, and broad classes of minmax-regret problems are NP-hard even in static settings [Averbakh, 2004, Averbakh and Lebedev, 2005]. Our contribution is to identify tractable structure for RLHF. For a fixed prompt, if one conceptually enumerates the finite set of possible complete responses, then the policy becomes a probability vector on a simplex and the promptwise problem becomes a portfolio-style allocation problem. Under an ℓ1 ambiguity set around the proxy reward, the inner worst-case regret has an exact solution, the adversary concentrates its budget on a single response, and the minimizer admits a water-filling interpretation with leftover mass assigned to the top response. That exact characterization, although derived in a stylized finite model, leads directly to a sampled robust bonus that can be inserted into PPO- or GRPO-style RLHF updates with only minor code changes. 

Contributions. The paper makes five contributions. (a) We formulate RLHF under reward misspecification as an ex-ante DRRO problem and show that Wasserstein ambiguity over reward functions reduces to deterministic ambiguity in their mean reward. (b) For the fixed-prompt simplex model, we characterize the inner adversary exactly and derive a closed-form water-filling solution for the promptwise DRRO policy under ℓ1

ambiguity. (c) We compare DRRO and DRO theoretically. When the proxy reward preserves the ranking of the true reward, we show that DRRO weakly dominates DRO under the 4true reward, and at the prompt-distribution level we derive regret certificates showing that DRRO pays only relative coverage while DRO pays absolute coverage. (d) We translate the exact promptwise theory into a trainable RLHF objective: a hard robust bonus, a softmax relaxation, a self-normalized importance-sampling estimator, and a dynamic KL-based ambiguity budget. (e) Empirically, we show that DRRO delivers the strongest mitigation of over-optimization, while standard DRO is measurably over-pessimistic. The rest of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 1.1 positions our contribution within the RLHF and robust optimization literatures. Section 2 introduces the RLHF notation and ex-ante DRRO formulation. Section 3 solves the fixed-prompt problem and interprets the resulting water-filling geometry. Section 4 develops the RLHF algorithmic surrogates. Section 5 then compares DRO and DRRO from rank-preserving and coverage-based perspectives. Section 6 records the final experimental design and tables. The key promptwise proofs appear in the main text, and the remaining proofs are deferred to the appendix. 

## 1.1 Literature Review 

RLHF and objective misspecification. RLHF emerged from the preference-learning framework of Christiano et al. [2017], who used human comparisons to train reward functions for reinforcement learning agents. Ziegler et al. [2019] and Stiennon et al. [2020] adapted that logic to language modeling and summarization, while Ouyang et al. [2022] turned it into the now-standard LLM pipeline of supervised fine-tuning, reward modeling, and reference-anchored policy optimization, often implemented with KL control. Direct preference methods such as DPO [Rafailov et al., 2023] eliminate the explicit reward-model optimization stage, but they do not eliminate the underlying problem of learning from a finite proxy for human preference. Among recent surveys, Kaufmann et al. [2025] provide a broad map of RLHF across domains, Chaudhari et al. [2024] focus on reward-model misspecification in LLM alignment, and Liu et al. [2026] emphasize preference learning, uncertainty quantification, and statistical inference. 

Over-optimization and mitigation strategies. The empirical evidence is now fairly systematic. Gao et al. [2023] document the canonical proxy-versus-gold divergence in RLHF; Rafailov et al. [2024] show that the same phenomenon persists in direct alignment algorithms; and Hosking et al. [2024] argue that human-feedback scores themselves can be misaligned with true quality because they over-reward stylistic features such as assertiveness. On the mitigation side, one family of papers tries to improve the reward model itself. Coste et al. [2024] and Eisenstein et al. [2024] study reward-model ensembles 5as a hedge against reward hacking; Miao et al. [2024] use an information-theoretic reward-model objective; Yang et al. [2024] regularize hidden states to improve generalization; Liu et al. [2025] target artifact robustness; and Bukharin et al. [2025] generate adversarial hard examples for reward-model training. A related line updates the reward model as the policy moves, including the iterative preference-learning method of Xiong et al. [2024], the active preference-optimization framework of Das et al. [2024], and the off-policy correction of Ackermann et al. [2025]. A second family leaves the reward model largely intact but modifies the policy objective. Zhai et al. [2024] penalize reward uncertainty estimated from LoRA ensembles, Xu et al. [2025] learn a pessimistic reward model, and Yan et al. [2024] optimize a reward-robust policy objective. A third family regularizes the policy toward regions where the reward model is thought to be reliable. Moskovitz et al. [2024] impose explicit constraints in RLHF, Dai et al. [2025] regularize toward behavior-supported regions, Rita et al. [2024] use demonstration-guided reinforcement learning, and Liu et al. [2024] show that SFT loss can play the role of an adversarial regularizer. These methods are important points of comparison for us because they reveal the central tension: protecting against reward misspecification is valuable, but methods built around pessimistic value or hard feasible-region restrictions can also suppress useful policy improvement. Xu et al. [2025] make this concern explicit by arguing that KL-based regularization can exclude high-performing policies with large KL divergence, and Li et al. [2025] show that divergence-based bonuses can reinforce conservative behavior rather than encourage exploration of uncertain but promising regions. 

DRO and DRRO in operations research. Standard DRO protects decisions against ambiguity in probabilities, distributions, and objective coefficients. Ben-Tal et al. [2013] study robust solutions under uncertain probabilities, Wiesemann et al. [2014] provide a general convex-optimization framework, Mohajerin Esfahani and Kuhn [2018] develop data-driven Wasserstein DRO, Blanchet and Murthy [2019] quantify distributional model risk, and Rahimian and Mehrotra [2019] survey the area. The concern that worst-case value may be overly conservative leads naturally to regret-based robustness. Averbakh [2004] and Averbakh and Lebedev [2005] develop classical minmax-regret models and complexity results; Chen and Xie [2021] study regret in the newsvendor problem; and Agarwal and Zhang [2022], Cho and Yang [2024], Bitar [2024], and Fiechtner and Blanchet [2025] develop distributionally robust regret formulations in modern statistical and learning settings. Our paper imports that line of work into RLHF and shows that the regret benchmark becomes especially natural once reward uncertainty, rather than transition uncertainty, is the main source of misspecification. 62 Model Setup and Problem Formulation 

This section turns the informal RLHF story from the introduction into a decision model. We first fix notation for prompts, responses, policies, proxy rewards, and latent true rewards, and then use that notation to formulate RLHF as an ex-ante robust optimization problem under reward misspecification. We begin with the objects that are already present in practical RLHF and make the proxy-versus-true reward gap explicit. Once that gap is written down carefully, the ex-ante DRRO and DRO formulations follow naturally, and the section closes by extracting the promptwise ambiguity set that will drive the analysis later on.

# 3 Closed-Form Promptwise Analysis 

We now isolate the core promptwise optimization problem and solve it exactly. This section is where the main theoretical intuition becomes visible: once a single prompt is fixed, RLHF under reward ambiguity reduces to a simplex decision problem with a remarkably explicit adversary and optimizer. Fixing a prompt first reveals where the adversary spends its budget. That inner structure then turns the outer policy problem into a water-filling allocation rule with a clear economic interpretation: protect the responses whose proxy rewards are high but whose current coverage is too low. Later sections will use exactly this geometry to compare DRRO with DRO and to build trainable RLHF surrogates.

## 3.3 Interpreting the Water-Filling Policy 

The water-filling theorem is useful not only because it is explicit, but also because it explains how the robustness budget changes the policy qualitatively. The policy does not flatten all responses equally. It first hedges against nearby competitors, then gradually widens support as the ambiguity budget grows, and it always keeps any residual probability mass on the top-ranked response. Figure 2 makes the mechanics of the optimizer visible. Small ambiguity budgets trust the proxy and therefore keep most probability on the best response, with only the nearest competitor receiving hedge mass. As δ increases, the threshold t⋆ falls and further responses enter the active support one by one. What matters is not their absolute rank alone, but whether their uncovered rewards still sit above the current water level.

# 4 Algorithm for RLHF 

The exact promptwise solution from Section 3 tells us what a regret-robust policy should look like once a prompt has been reduced to a finite simplex. RLHF training, however, never gets to move probability mass on that simplex directly. What it actually sees are sampled completions from a language model, and what it actually updates are model parameters. Those two viewpoints are close enough to guide the algorithm, but different enough to create real work. For a fixed prompt, the response space is astronomically large, so the adversarial coordinate from Proposition 3.1 is hidden from us and can only be glimpsed through a small sampled group. And even if the promptwise optimizer were available in closed form, it would still not produce a usable LLM update, because the trainable object is θ, not the full vector π(· | x). The way forward is therefore to rewrite the robust objective as a policy-gradient recipe, understand what bonus the exact theory implies, and then relax that bonus until it can be estimated reliably from grouped rollouts.

From a computational viewpoint, the additional burden is small. During autoregressive rollout, the model already returns the tokenwise conditional probabilities needed to form each completion probability ¯pk = ¯π(yk | x), so no extra model query is required; one only accumulates the emitted token probabilities along the sampled response. Once the proxy rewards are available, the hard-max variant adds a promptwise argmax over the sampled group and one scalar bonus, while the soft-max variant adds G scalar exponentials, one normalization, and the corresponding reshaped rewards. Relative to sequence generation, reward-model scoring, and the usual minibatch policy-gradient updates, these operations are negligible. # 5 Comparing DRO and DRRO 

The promptwise theory already suggests why DRRO should be less pessimistic than DRO: DRRO spreads probability only where doing so reduces plausible regret, while DRO spreads probability to guard absolute value against every perturbation. This section formalizes that difference from two complementary angles. We first study a promptwise ranking regime that mirrors how reward models are trained and evaluated in RLHF. We then give a distribution-level comparison based on integrated coverage, which captures how much reward-model extrapolation each robust objective demands across prompts. 

## 5.1 Rank-Preserving Proxy Rewards 

Pairwise reward-model training in RLHF is explicitly a ranking problem. The classical Bradley–Terry formulation and its descendants treat the goal as assigning larger scores to preferred responses than to rejected ones [Bradley and Terry, 1952, Christiano et al., 2017]. Modern reward-model pipelines for summarization and instruction following adopt exactly this view, training the proxy reward to preserve the preference ordering induced by human comparisons [Stiennon et al., 2020, Ouyang et al., 2022]. On the evaluation side, held-out pairwise accuracy and ranking correlation remain standard diagnostics of reward-model quality [Lambert et al., 2025], even though recent evidence shows that downstream RLHF performance depends on more than accuracy alone [Chen et al., 2024]. # 6 Experiments 

This section reports the empirical study of DRRO for RLHF, including the shared experimental setup, the main benchmark against competing mitigation strategies, the direct DRO-versus-DRRO comparison, and an internal ablation of the DRRO design choices. 

## 6.1 Experimental Design 

All experiments are implemented in a single VERL-based RLHF codebase with Ray workers and vLLM rollout, so that DRRO, DRO, PPO, GRPO, and the auxiliary robust baselines share the same data interface, policy backbone, prompt template, evaluation loop, and logging format. This design is important for fairness: in our repo, PPO is recovered by switching the advantage estimator to GAE, GRPO is the zero-robustness special case of the same training entrypoint, and DRO versus DRRO differ only in the robust bonus construction rather than in unrelated engineering choices. The codebase is 28 publicly available at https://github.com/aia00/DRRO .We use HuggingFaceH4/hh-rlhf only as a prompt distribution. From each example we extract the first human turn, denoted human_turn0 when available, and format prompts with the fixed template 

Human: {prompt}\nAssistant: .

For each run, the full prompt pool is randomly shuffled with seed 42, 512 prompts are held out for validation, and the remainder are used for online RL training. Thus, HH-RLHF is not used here as paired preference supervision for policy optimization; it is only the source of prompts on which the policy is rolled out and evaluated. Unless otherwise stated, all methods use the same model stack: (a) policy model: Qwen/Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct ;(b) proxy reward model: OpenAssistant/reward-model-deberta-v3-base ;(c) held-out evaluator: sileod/deberta-v3-large-tasksource-rlhf-reward-model .The policy is trained with LoRA adapters by default, using rank r = 8 , scaling α = 16 ,and dropout 0.05 . Our standard launcher configuration runs 300 policy-update steps with learning rate 10 −5, PPO clipping parameter 0.2, batch size 12 prompts per update, and 16 sampled completions per prompt, giving 192 sampled responses per training iteration. We cap prompts at 512 tokens and generated continuations at 128 new tokens, and we sample with temperature 1.0 and top-p = 0 .95 . Evaluation is performed every 5 updates, checkpoints are saved every 20 updates, and reward-model scoring uses batches of size 16. In the default multi-GPU configuration, three GPUs are allocated in total, with one reserved for reward-model inference and the remaining devices used by the actor, reference, and rollout workers. For DRRO-specific runs, the code supports both fixed and dynamic ambiguity schedules under a unified interface. The fixed-budget default is δ = 2 .5K, where K is the number of sampled responses per prompt, so the default fixed budget is δ = 40 when K = 16 . The soft-assignment surrogate uses temperature τ = 2 .0 by default. Dynamic-budget runs replace the fixed budget by 

δt = α · KL t,

where KL t is a smoothed sequence-level KL statistic computed over a sliding window of length 20, and the default online KL estimator in the code is the k3 variant. Standard DRO is implemented in the same script and reuses the same dynamic-budget interface, changing only the inner robust objective from regret-based to value-based pessimization. The core study in this paper compares five update rules under this shared rollout protocol: 29 (a) standard PPO; (b) standard GRPO; (c) DRO-style robust-value optimization with the same ambiguity schedule; (d) DRRO with the hard sampled bonus from (4.5); (e) DRRO with the soft bonus (4.12) and the dynamic budget (4.15). The five methods differ only in the update rule. Method (a) replaces GRPO with GAE and applies no robust correction. Method (b) keeps the grouped GRPO objective but sets the robustness budget to zero. Method (c) uses the same grouped rollout and ambiguity-budget interface as DRRO, but switches the robust objective to DRO, so the value-pessimistic correction is subtracted from the proxy reward. Method (d) uses DRRO with a fixed positive budget and the hard assignment rule in (4.5), assigning the full +δ correction only to the maximizing sample under the score ri − δp i. Method (e), our main variant, uses the soft surrogate in (4.12) together with the dynamic budget in (4.15) , thereby distributing the correction across sampled responses according to softened promptwise scores. The repository also contains additional mitigation baselines, including reward-ensemble methods, constraint-based RLHF, InfoRM-style proxy modeling, and BSPO-style behavior-supported regularization, all wired to the same prompt split and logging pipeline. These methods are useful for broader benchmarking, but the five methods above form the main controlled DRRO-versus-DRO study because they isolate the effect of robustifying value versus regret. Our primary metric is held-out gold reward under the stronger evaluator. We also record the proxy reward assigned by the training reward model and the sequence-level KL divergence from the frozen reference policy, since the central phenomenon of interest is precisely the divergence between proxy improvement and gold-quality deterioration as KL grows. The training loop writes log.csv files containing raw and step-0-normalized versions of these quantities, and the checkpoint-evaluation scripts produce matched 

eval_log.csv files for the over-optimization curves. 

## 6.2 Main Benchmark: State-of-the-Art Mitigation of Over-Optimization 

The main empirical conclusion is that DRRO provides the strongest mitigation of over-optimization in this setup. It not only reaches the best peak under the held-out evaluator, but also preserves that advantage later in training when proxy-greedy baselines begin to collapse. 30 Figure 3: Main benchmark across RLHF update rules and mitigation baselines. The dashed curve shows the proxy reward used for optimization, while solid curves report held-out gold reward against sequence-level KL from the initial policy. The dominant qualitative pattern is that proxy reward keeps improving even after several methods have already begun to lose gold reward, and that DRRO maintains the strongest gold trajectory over the widest useful KL range. The benchmark baselines fall into three broad families. The first family refines the reward proxy itself so that the policy has a harder signal to exploit. Ensemble-mean averages several proxy reward models to smooth out idiosyncratic reward-model errors [Coste et al., 2024, Eisenstein et al., 2024]. Ensemble-UWO uses the same ensemble but subtracts an uncertainty penalty based on ensemble disagreement, making high-variance responses less attractive [Coste et al., 2024, Eisenstein et al., 2024]. InfoRM retrains the proxy reward with an information-bottleneck objective so that the score depends less on spurious, easily hacked features [Miao et al., 2024]. The second family restricts policy optimization to regions where the reward signal is expected to be more reliable. BSPO estimates behavior support with a ScoreLM-style model and assigns pessimistic value to unsupported actions, thereby discouraging the policy from drifting too far from behavior-supported responses [Dai et al., 2025]. This places BSPO in the same broad class as constrained-RLHF methods that explicitly control the feasible policy region [Moskovitz et al., 2024]. The third family makes the reward used for optimization explicitly pessimistic. Empirically, this is exactly the behavior one would want from a regret-robust update rule: the optimizer remains willing to move toward high-value responses, but it is less willing to commit mass to responses whose apparent attractiveness is too dependent on reward-model misspecification. The practical takeaway from the figure is therefore stronger than “DRRO picks a better stopping point.” Rather, DRRO appears to improve the entire useful reward-versus-KL frontier, achieving a better balance between continued optimization and resistance to over-optimization throughout training.

## 6.4 Ablation Within DRRO 

The main internal ablation compares (a) fixed versus dynamic ambiguity budgets, and (b) hard winner-take-all bonuses versus the soft SNIS bonus. Figure 5 shows that both design choices matter, and that they matter in the expected direction. The fixed-budget hard-assignment variant is consistently the weakest curve: it improves at small KL, but it remains below the other three variants across almost the entire trajectory. Replacing hard assignment by the soft SNIS bonus already helps under a fixed budget, indicating that distributing the correction across plausible responses is more effective than committing the entire bonus to a single sampled winner. 34 The larger gain comes from switching from a fixed budget to the dynamic schedule. Both dynamic variants dominate their fixed-budget counterparts over a broad KL range, which suggests that tying the ambiguity level to the current policy drift is a better match to the onset of over-optimization than using a constant robustness coefficient. Among the four curves, dynamic-plus-soft is the strongest overall: it rises fastest after the initial shared regime, reaches the highest peak gold reward, and remains near the top even after the other variants begin to flatten. Dynamic-plus-hard is the next best variant and is noticeably more stable than either fixed-budget alternative, but it still underperforms dynamic-plus-soft in the high-performing region. The empirical conclusion of this ablation is therefore clear. Dynamic budgeting is the main driver of robustness, while soft assignment provides an additional improvement on top of that schedule. Together they yield the best reward-versus-KL tradeoff within the DRRO family, which is why the dynamic soft-assignment variant is used as our main DRRO configuration in the benchmark comparisons. 

# 7 Conclusion 

RLHF under reward misspecification is naturally a regret-robust decision problem. By importing DRRO into RLHF, we obtain a benchmark that asks the right deployment question: how far is the deployed policy from the best policy under a plausible true reward? For the fixed-prompt simplex model, this question is tractable and leads to an exact water-filling structure, a clean dominance result over DRO under rank-preserving truth, and a practical sampled robust bonus for policy optimization. At the distribution level, DRRO pays for relative coverage rather than absolute coverage, which formalizes why it is less pessimistic than DRO. The long-form empirical section is now structured to document the two central numerical claims of the project: state-of-the-art mitigation of over-optimization and clear evidence that DRO is too conservative in this setting.
