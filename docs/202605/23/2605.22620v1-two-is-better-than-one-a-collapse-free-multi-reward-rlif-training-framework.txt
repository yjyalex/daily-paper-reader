Abstract 

Reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR) has substantially improved the reasoning ability of LLMs, but often depends on external supervision from human annotations or gold-standard solutions. Reinforcement learning from inter-nal feedback (RLIF) has recently emerged as a scalable unsupervised alternative, using signals extracted from the model itself. However, existing RLIF methods typically rely on a single internal reward, which can lead to reward hacking, en-tropy collapse, and degraded reasoning structure. We propose a multi-reward RLIF framework that decomposes the training signal into two complementary com-ponents: an answer-level reward based on cluster voting and a completion-level reward based on token-wise self-certainty. To combine these signals robustly, we apply GDPO-based normalization to reduce reward-scale imbalance. We further introduce KL-Cov regularization, which targets low-entropy token distributions responsible for disproportionate entropy reduction, preserving exploration and preventing late-stage collapse. Across mathematical reasoning and code-generation benchmarks, our method improves stability and robustness over prior unsuper-vised RL approaches, while achieving performance close to supervised RLVR methods. These results show that complementary internal rewards, combined with targeted regularization, can support stable long-horizon reasoning without relying on external ground-truth supervision. 1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) have achieved striking success across domains, yet training them to reason through complex problems remains an open and central challenge in modern AI. Reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR) has emerged as a powerful paradigm, enabling models such as DeepSeek-R1 [DeepSeek-AI, 2025] and MiMo [Xiaomi LLM-Core Team, 2025] to attain strong mathematical reasoning through reward signals derived from ground-truth answer verification. However, RLVR critically depends on external supervision such as human-labeled reasoning traces, verified reference answers, or pre-trained reward models which limits scalability and practical applicability across domains where such supervision is unavailable, costly, or difficult to obtain. Reinforcement Learning with Internal Feedback (RLIF) offers an unsupervised alternative that eliminates the need for external supervision entirely. Instead of relying on ground-truth labels, RLIF methods derive reward signals solely from intrinsic model signals: either through consensus-based measures such as majority voting and semantic clustering [Zhang et al., 2025a], or through entropy minimization measures such as the self-certainty score [Zhao et al., 2025b]. These approaches have

demonstrated that meaningful reasoning improvements are achievable without labeled data, making them a promising path toward scaling LLM reasoning to arbitrary domains [Zuo et al., 2025, Zhao et al., 2025a, Prabhudesai et al., 2025]. Despite these promising results, existing RLIF methods exhibit recurring failure patterns, including reward hacking and model collapse [Agarwal et al., 2025, Prabhudesai et al., 2025]. Prior work has observed that models often achieve strong initial gains but gradually degrade after extended training. We argue that this decline stems from the structure of the reward signals themselves. For example, self-certainty rewards high-confidence token distributions, effectively using entropy minimization as a proxy for output quality. Majority voting, though consensus-based rather than explicitly entropy-driven, similarly favors outputs that the model already assigns high probability to, thereby indirectly reinforcing low-entropy behavior. Under both signals, the policy is incentivized to reduce token-level uncertainty, whether or not this reduction improves reasoning quality. This incentive creates two compounding failure modes. First, high-entropy transitional tokens, such as however , thus , because , and suppose , which often serve as structural anchors in chain-of-thought reasoning, are disproportionately penalized [Zhang et al., 2025b]. This can weaken logical coherence even when final-answer accuracy temporarily improves. Second, because shorter responses contain fewer uncertain tokens, the reward implicitly favors brevity over depth [He et al., 2026], encouraging terse, high-probability outputs rather than genuine improvements in reasoning quality. This pattern of early improvement followed by degradation is an empirical instance of Goodhart’s Law [Gao et al., 2022]: when a single proxy becomes the sole optimization target, the policy learns to exploit the proxy rather than improve the underlying capability. As optimization proceeds, the reward signal and true task performance can decouple, leading to overoptimization. This motivates a multi-reward RLIF objective that jointly optimizes structurally complementary signals rather than relying on a single proxy. Specifically, we propose a multi-reward RLIF framework that combines answer agreement and model confidence as intrinsic rewards. We then introduce a targeted regularization strategy to reduce collapse during optimization. To prevent either reward from dominating optimization, we normalize the advantages induced by each reward independently. We further introduce a targeted regularization strategy to reduce collapse during optimization. In particular, to mitigate entropy collapse while preserving exploration, we use KL-Cov regularization, inspired by Cui et al. [2025], which targets the small subset of tokens responsible for disproportionate entropy reduction while maintaining diversity in the remaining token distribution. Across five diverse mathematical reasoning benchmarks and two coding benchmarks, we show that our method outperforms several baselines, including single-reward RLIF frameworks such as self-certainty and majority voting Zhao et al. [2025b], Zuo et al. [2025] while maintaining stable gains under continued training. This stability is crucial for RLIF: as models scale and reasoning trajectories grow more complex, training must preserve exploration beyond early high-confidence behaviors rather than collapse into narrow reward-seeking patterns. Our contributions can be summarized as follows: • We propose a multi-reward RLIF framework with GDPO normalization that jointly optimizes answer-level consensus and completion-level self-certainty, mitigating the failure modes of single-proxy optimization. • We utilized KL-Cov regularization to the unsupervised multi-reward setting as a targeted policy constraint to high-covariance entropy-collapsing tokens while preserving exploration over the remaining token distribution. • Across five mathematical reasoning benchmarks and two coding benchmarks, we show that our method improves performance over single-reward RLIF baselines and remains stable under continued training. 

2 Related Work 

Reinforcement learning with internal feedback (RLIF). To eliminate external supervision, recent work has explored RLIF, where the model’s own predictions serve as the reward signal. INTU-ITOR [Zhao et al., 2025b] uses the average KL divergence between a uniform distribution and the model’s next-token distribution as a self-certainty score, rewarding more confident reasoning traces. EMPO [Zhang et al., 2025a] takes a complementary approach, employing entropy minimization by 2semantically clustering rollout answers and rewarding rollouts proportionally to their cluster size. TTRL [Zuo et al., 2025] shows that majority voting yields surprisingly effective rewards for test-time RL without ground-truth labels. Absolute Zero [Zhao et al., 2025a] and R-Zero [Huang et al., 2025] propose self-play frameworks where models generate their own training tasks, removing the need for curated datasets entirely. Zhou et al. [2025] extend RL-based reasoning to general domains without verifiers by maximizing the probability of generating reference answers. Co-rewarding [Zhang et al., 2025c] seeks complementary supervision from multiple views to stabilize self-supervised RL, addressing the training collapse that single-view signals cause. SSR-Zero [Yang et al., 2025] extends self-rewarding RL to machine translation, demonstrating that the paradigm generalizes beyond mathematical reasoning. While these methods advance unsupervised RL in various directions, they each rely on a single reward signal or a single feedback mechanism, making them susceptible to the failure modes described in Section 1. 

Entropy minimization, entropy collapse, and model collapse. Uncontrolled entropy minimiza-tion, rewarding more confident outputs to improve reasoning without any labels [Agarwal et al., 2025, Prabhudesai et al., 2025], is also the primary driver of collapse. Chen et al. [2026] formalize how clipping bias under spurious rewards reduces policy entropy, and Cui et al. [2025] show that 

∼95% of entropy reduction happens in the earliest training steps, with a tiny ∼0.02% fraction of high-covariance tokens with anomalously large centered cross-products between log-probability and advantage dominating the policy gradient and driving entropy to zero. The downstream pathology is well-documented: Shafayat et al. [2025] report complete model collapse in self-training (identical outputs regardless of input), Zhang et al. [2025b] show that all RLIF approaches exhibit a rise-then-fall trajectory attributable to over-optimization of a single internal signal, and He et al. [2026] confirm this pattern across intrinsic-reward methods at scale, with collapse timing set by model priors. 

Multi-objective RL and reward combination. Combining multiple reward signals in RL is challenging because naive summation leads to scale dominance, where the reward with larger variance overwhelms the gradient signal. GDPO [Liu et al., 2026] addresses this by normalizing each reward channel independently within groups before combination, ensuring that no single reward dominates purely due to scale differences. Our work builds on the GDPO normalization principle by applying it in the context: combining answer-level and completion-level rewards for unsupervised LLM training, paired with covariance-aware regularization to prevent entropy collapse. 

3 Method 

We present a multi-reward RLIF framework. The framework combines answer-level agreement, completion-level self-certainty, group-wise reward normalization, and covariance-aware regulariza-tion within a GRPO-style policy optimization objective. At a high level, for each prompt q, our method samples G completions from the rollout policy πθold .From each completion, we extract the final answer and cluster the resulting answers by equivalence. Each completion is assigned two rewards: an answer-agreement reward determined by the size of its answer cluster, and a completion-level confidence reward computed using self-certainty. We normalize each reward channel within the G-sample group and form a rollout-level advantage from their weighted sum. The policy is then optimized with a GRPO-style objective, with additional log-ratio regularization applied to high-covariance tokens. Figure 1 illustrates the overall training pipeline.

3.2 Answer-Level Reward via Cluster Voting 

The first reward channel assigns higher reward to completions whose final answers agree with many other sampled completions for the prompt. Given G rollouts for a prompt q, we extract the final answers a1, . . . , a G and group them into C clusters by exact equivalence on the normalized answer string. Rather than binary majority voting, which assigns reward only to the most popular answer, we assign each rollout g a reward proportional to the fraction with equivalent answers: 

Rcluster (ag ) = 

 {g′ ∈ { 1, . . . , G } : ag′ ≡ ag } / n parseable if ag is parseable ,

0 otherwise, (2) where nparseable is the number of rollouts (out of G) whose answer string parses successfully. For mathematical problems, the equivalence is measured with numerical consistency. This provides a denser signal than binary majority voting: minority clusters receive small but nonzero rewards rather than zero, and this type of clustering is shown to be robust to surface-level variation in equivalent answers [Zhang et al., 2025a, Kuhn et al., 2023]. In implementation, we additionally include a small auxiliary boxed-format reward (weight 0.1) alongside the cluster reward to gate non-parseable completions (reported in in Appendix F).

3.4 Group-Wise Multi-Reward Normalization 

Since the cluster and self-certainty rewards have different numerical scales, cluster voting produces values in [0 , 1] while self-certainty values depend on vocabulary size, naive combination can cause one signal to dominate the advantage purely because of scale and the performance degrades. We confirm this empirically in Appendix E: replacing per-channel z-scoring with a single combine-then-normalize step degrades downstream accuracy across different datasets (Figure 6), and a logprob-space gradient diagnostic (Figure 7) shows that the higher-variance self-certainty channel dominates the per-token gradient by roughly an order of magnitude whereas the cluster channel is effectively silenced. 3.5 KL-Cov: Covariance-Aware Policy Optimization 

We observe that two rewards provide complementary supervision, but each also induces a different degeneration mode when optimized alone. Cluster-only training drifts in answer space, with rising entropy and lengthening completions, while self-certainty collapses token-space entropy, producing peaked distributions and short ∼100 -token templates (Figure 3). Although GDPO normalization mitigates these instabilities by balancing the two reward channels, the resulting policy remains vulnerable to collapse. In our ablation, the GDPO-normalized combination still degenerates around step 240 when trained without additional exploration regularization (Figure 4). We therefore require a regularizer that targets the tokens responsible for collapse without unnecessarily constraining the rest of the policy. 4 Experiments & Results 

4.1 Experimental Setup Training details. We use Qwen2.5-1.5B-Base and Qwen2.5-3B-Base [Yang et al., 2024] as the base models for all experiments. We use the MATH training split of MATH-lighteval [Hendrycks et al., 2021], as the source of unlabeled training prompts. All models are trained with full finetuning using GRPO [Shao et al., 2024] as the base optimizer with a learning rate of 3 × 10 −6 and cosine scheduling. We use G = 7 rollouts per prompt with a maximum completion length of 3072 tokens. The GRPO KL penalty coefficient is set to γ = 0 .005 , following [Zhao et al., 2025b]. For our method, we set equal reward-channel weights α = β = 0 .5, use a top-2% covariance threshold k = 0 .02 ,and set βcov = 0 .05 . Training is conducted on a single node with 4 NVIDIA GH200 120GB GPUs. The effective batch size is 882 prompts per gradient update, corresponding to 6,174 rollouts per step. We train for 40 epochs ( 340 steps) using huggingface-trl framework [von Werra et al., 2020]. The full training, optimization, batch, hardware, and evaluation hyperparameters are reported in Appendix F and Table 6. 

Evaluation. We evaluate on seven benchmarks spanning in-distribution to out-of-distribution reasoning: GSM8K [Cobbe et al., 2021], MATH500 [Hendrycks et al., 2021], MMLU-Pro [Wang et al., 2024] (5-shot CoT), AIME 2024/2025, LiveCodeBench v6 [Jain et al., 2025] (LCB; pass@1, all-tests-must-pass), and CRUXEval-O [Gu et al., 2024] (output-prediction via ZeroEval [Lin, 2024] 

OEQA template). Math benchmarks are scored with math_verify using the training chat-style prompt; MMLU-Pro uses TIGER-Lab’s canonical 5-shot CoT completion-style prompt [Wang et al., 2024]. LCB evaluation follows the lighteval [Habib et al., 2023] pipeline verbatim. Among the 5 math benchmarks, the performance on AIME 2024/2025 can be considered as OOD generalization because AIME problems require novel multi-step olympiad-level reasoning with extremely low base-rate success which does not reflect in our training dataset. The two coding tasks are also considered as OOD generalization as the methods were not trained on coding datasets.

Training Dynamics: Model Collapse. In Figure 2, we illustrate model collapse by showing the full training dynamics, with checkpoints evaluated every 20 training steps. We report results on GSM8K (in-distribution arithmetic), MATH500 (in-distribution competition math), and MMLU-Pro (OOD multiple-choice reasoning), for both Qwen2.5-1.5B and Qwen2.5-3B. Across these settings, we observe two consistent patterns at both scales. First, single-reward INTUITOR exhibits model collapse due to reward-hacking (See Appendix G): self-certainty peaks early, around steps 40–60, and then declines monotonically, reaching near-zero math accuracy. Second, multi-reward + KL-Cov remains stable across the full training trajectory. It performs on par with supervised GRPO-GT, retaining 89 –93% of peak performance on 1.5B and 95 –99% on 3B at step 340. We also observe that multi-reward training alone delays collapse but does not fully prevent it, especially on 1.5B.

5 Discussion 

Figure 3 suggests that cluster voting and self-certainty expose complementary reward-hacking directions. The cluster-voting reward depends only on the extracted answer and is invariant to the token-level reasoning path. As a result, the policy can exploit this objective by drifting in reasoning space while concentrating final answers into a dominant cluster, producing long, high-entropy completions after collapse. Conversely, self-certainty acts directly on token probabilities but is invariant to answer correctness, allowing the policy to exploit the reward by becoming confidently deterministic, yielding short template-like outputs. The multi-reward objective partially mitigates these failures because each reward constrains a direction left under specified by the other. Cluster voting discourages arbitrary confident templates that do not produce consistent answers, while self-certainty constrains unconstrained token-level drift under cluster voting. Empirically, this widens the stable training window and yields more controlled entropy and length dynamics, avoiding both the entropy rebound of cluster-only and the premature entropy collapse of self-certainty-only training. Multi-objective reward design is motivated by the idea that individual signals capture distinct but incomplete aspects of desired behavior and can be additively combined [Zhao et al., 2025b]. Our work provides the first empirical demonstration of this effect in reasoning-training. However, though multi-reward training closes off single-reward loopholes ,complementarity alone is insufficient and KL-Cov remains necessary to stabilize the residual optimization dynamics. 

Limitations. Our framework introduces additional hyperparameters beyond standard GRPO, in-cluding the top-k covariance fraction βcov and the channel weights α and β. The KL-Cov sweep in Figure 4 suggests that performance is largely insensitive to βcov across a 200 × range. However, we do not conduct a systematic study of the relative weighting α/β and instead adopt the symmetric default α = β = 0 .5 throughout. In addition, the method relies on a sufficiently capable base model: the pretrained model’s initial reasoning ability plays an important role in its success. The proposed framework does not include safeguards for models with poor initial reasoning capability. 

6 Conclusion 

This paper presents a multi-reward framework with KL-Cov regularization for reinforcement learning from internal feedback (RLIF) mechanism in large language models. In response to the collapse observed under single-reward training, our approach combines two complementary intrinsic signals. Together, these rewards provide a richer training signal that mitigates the distinct degeneracies induced by either reward alone. KL-Cov further stabilizes optimization by targeting the small subset of tokens responsible for disproportionate entropy reduction, preventing late-stage collapse while preserving stable reasoning. 9Across mathematical reasoning and coding benchmarks, our method achieves performance close to supervised RLVR approaches such as GRPO, without requiring gold-standard solutions. These results highlight the potential of multi-reward RLIF as a scalable alternative to externally supervised reinforcement learning. More broadly, they suggest that future work can explore additional internal signals, potentially stronger than those studied here, to support stable long-horizon reasoning. Such stability is central to RLIF: as models scale and reasoning trajectories become more complex, training must preserve exploration beyond early high-confidence behaviors rather than collapse into narrow reward-seeking patterns.
