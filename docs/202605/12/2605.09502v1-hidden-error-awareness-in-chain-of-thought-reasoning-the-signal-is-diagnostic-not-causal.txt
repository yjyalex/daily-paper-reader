# Hidden Error Awareness in Chain-of-Thought Reasoning: The Signal Is Diagnostic, Not Causal 

Aojie Yuan 1 Zhiyuan Julian Su 1 Haiyue Zhang 1 Yi Nian 1 Yue Zhao 1

## Abstract 

Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting assumes that generated reasoning reflects a model’s internal computation. We show this assumption is wrong in a specific, measurable way: models inter-nally detect their own reasoning errors but out-wardly express confidence in them . A linear probe on hidden states predicts trace correctness with 0.95 AUROC—from the very first reasoning step 

(0.79)—while verbalized confidence for wrong traces is 4.55/5, nearly identical to correct ones (4.87/5). A text-surface classifier achieves only 0.59 on the same data, confirming a 0.20-point gap invisible in the generated text. This hidden error awareness holds across three model fami-lies (Qwen, Llama, Phi), 1.5B–72B parameters, and RL-trained reasoning models (DeepSeek-R1, 0.852 AUROC). The natural question is whether this signal can fix the errors it detects. It cannot. Four interventions—activation steering, probe-guided best-of-N, self-correction, and activation patching—all fail; patching destroys output co-herence entirely. The signal is diagnostic, not causal : a readout of computation quality, not a lever to redirect it. This delineates a boundary for mechanistic interpretability: error representations during reasoning are fundamentally different from the factual knowledge representations that prior work has successfully edited.

## 2. Related Work 

CoT faithfulness. Turpin et al. (2023) show that CoT ex-planations can be steered by irrelevant features, and Lanham et al. (2023) find that perturbing CoT steps often does not change the answer. These studies diagnose unfaithfulness via behavioral interventions. We provide a complemen-tary representational perspective: directly comparing what hidden states encode with what the text expresses, and quan-tifying the gap. 

Probing internal representations. Burns et al. (2023) discover “truth directions” via unsupervised CCS; Azaria & Mitchell (2023) train classifiers to detect falsehoods; Li et al. (2023) use probes for inference-time intervention. Concur-rent work by Zhang et al. (2025) probes hidden states in reasoning models for self-verification, demonstrating best-of-N selection. Our work reaches a fundamentally different conclusion: where they show the signal aids selection , we show it cannot improve the reasoning itself . We further establish a 0.20 AUROC gap between hidden-state and text-surface classifiers that verification-focused work does not measure. 

Self-correction and causal analysis. Huang et al. (2024) find that intrinsic self-correction remains limited. Our activa-tion patching experiments extend causal mediation analysis (Meng et al., 2022; Vig et al., 2020) from factual recall to multi-step reasoning, revealing that the causal structure of error representations differs qualitatively from that of factual associations.

## 4. The Signal Is Early and Hidden 

4.1. First-step prediction 

The first-step hidden state alone achieves 0.787 AUROC— 98% of full-trace performance (Table 4). For DS-R1-7B, first-step AUROC is 0.686, still well above chance. The model “knows” from Step 1 whether its reasoning will suc-ceed, yet proceeds to generate multiple incorrect steps. 

Two temporal regimes. Step-level probe scores (Figure 3) reveal distinct dynamics. Qwen2.5-3B shows front-loaded 

awareness: maximum separation at Step 1 (gap = 0.41), sug-gesting early commitment to a flawed trajectory. Qwen2.5-7B shows accumulating awareness: separation grows from 0.11 to 0.38 as error evidence builds. Neither pattern is con-sistent with the probe merely reading post-hoc text quality.

## 6. Does RL Training Close the Gap? 

DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B achieves 76% accuracy (vs. 46% for Llama-3.1-8B) but still exhibits 0.852 AUROC hidden error awareness (Table 1). Its first-step AUROC (0.686) is lower than Qwen-3B’s (0.787), consistent with reasoning models distributing computation across longer traces. The difficulty control effect is weaker ( p = 0 .447 , d =

−0.30 ), likely because 76% accuracy leaves too few mixed-outcome problems ( n=14). But the core finding stands: RL training does not close the diagnostic–causal gap .

transplanting hidden states from correct traces does not produce correct reasoning—it produces incoherent output. Reasoning quality emerges from distributed, multi-layer computation, not from a single editable feature. 

Contrast with factual editing. Meng et al. (2022) and Li et al. (2023) edit localized representations to reliably change outputs. Error signals during reasoning are different: they are epiphenomenal readouts of a distributed process. The tools that work for factual knowledge (ROME, ITI) should not be expected to work for reasoning quality. 

Non-monotonic scaling. The 7B/14B AUROC dip fol-lowed by recovery at 32B/72B suggests a capability– separability trade-off : intermediate models produce fewer but less systematic errors, creating harder linear separa-tion. Larger models develop more linearly structured error representations—the 72B achieves 0.977 AUROC despite lower raw accuracy. 

Implications. (1) CoT trace auditing is unreliable for safety monitoring: error signals are invisible in text. (2) Self-correction needs mechanistic grounding beyond surface prompting. (3) Process reward models (Lightman et al., 2024) may capture the same diagnostic signal but lack causal leverage. 

Limitations. We test up to 72B across three families plus one reasoning model, using MATH-500 as the primary benchmark. We do not test fine-tuning on probe signals or RL from probe feedback, which could potentially close the gap through training-time intervention. 

## 8. Conclusion 

LLMs encode error-predictive signals in their hidden states during CoT reasoning—signals that emerge early, are con-cealed from text, and persist across model families and scales. Four mechanistic interventions fail to leverage these signals, establishing that they are diagnostic rather than causal. This reveals a boundary: mechanistic interpretabil-ity can detect reasoning failures but cannot fix them with the tools that succeed for factual knowledge. Closing this gap likely requires training objectives that teach models to 

act on their internal error signals, not merely harbor them.

## F. Example Traces 

Two representative traces from Qwen2.5-3B on MATH-500, illustrating hidden error awareness. The correct trace receives a low probe score; the wrong trace receives a high probe score and high verbalized confidence (the unfaithful region). 

Correct trace — Probe score: 0.004, Confidence: 5/5                                                                                  

> Problem: One ticket to ashow costs $20 at full price. Susan buys 4tickets with a25% discount. Pam buys 5tickets with a30% discount. How many more dollars did Pam pay than Susan? Step 1: Susan’s discounted price =$20 ×0.75 =$15 Step 2: Susan’s total =4×$15 =$60 Step 3: Pam’s discounted price =$20 ×0.70 =$14 Step 4: Pam’s total =5×$14 =$70 Step 5: Difference =$70 −$60 =$10 ANSWER: $10 Correct (ref: 10)

Wrong trace — Probe score: 0.932, Confidence: 5/5                                                    

> Problem: Afarmer has 52 cows. Each cow gives 5 liters of milk per day. How many liters of milk does the farmer collect in aweek? Step 1: Daily milk =52 ×5=260 liters Step 2: Weekly milk =260 ×30 =7800 liters ANSWER: 7800 Wrong (ref: 1820, i.e. 260 ×7)

The wrong trace uses “30” instead of “7” for days in a week—a clear arithmetic error—yet the model expresses 5/5 verbalized confidence. The probe detects the error (score 0.932) from hidden states alone. No hedging, uncertainty markers, or surface-level cues distinguish this trace from a correct one. This is the defining pattern of hidden error awareness : the model knows it is wrong but does not say so.
