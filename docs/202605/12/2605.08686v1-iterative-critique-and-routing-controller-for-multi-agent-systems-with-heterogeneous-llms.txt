# Iterative Critique-and-Routing Controller for Multi-Agent Systems with Heterogeneous LLMs 

Wenzhi Fang 1, Liangqi Yuan 1, Guangchen Lan 1,Dong-Jun Han 2, Christopher G. Brinton 1

## Abstract 

Multi-agent large language model (LLM) systems often rely on a controller to coordinate a pool of heterogeneous models, yet existing controllers are typically limited to one-shot routing: they select a model once and return its output directly. Such routing-only designs provide no mechanism to critique intermediate drafts or support iterative refinement. To address this limitation, we propose a critique-and-routing controller that casts multi-agent coordination as a sequential decision problem. At each turn, the controller evaluates the current draft, decides whether to stop or continue, and, if needed, selects the next agent for further refinement. We formulate this process as a finite-horizon Markov Decision Process (MDP) with explicit agent-utilization constraints, design a composite reward for controller decisions across turns, and optimize the controller via policy gradients under a Lagrangian-relaxed objective. Extensive experiments across multiple heteroge-neous multi-agent systems and seven reasoning benchmarks show that our method consistently outperforms state-of-the-art baselines and substantially narrows the gap to the strongest agent, while using it for fewer than 25% of total calls. 

## 1 Introduction 

Multi-agent large language model (LLM) systems are becoming increasingly popular in practice [ 33 ,15 , 34 , 17 ]. A representative deployment pattern is an agent pool coordinated by a controller that selects among heterogeneous agents to balance quality and cost [ 4]. Most existing coordination mechanisms treat the controller primarily as a one-shot router, selecting a single model and returning the resulting response to the user [ 3, 19 ]. However, many queries, especially those requiring sophisticated reasoning, are hard to solve in one turn: an initial draft may be incomplete or incorrect, and feedback can enable substantial refinement in subsequent turns [ 21 ]. Yet one-shot routing-based coordination offers no such feedback loop: once an agent is selected, its response is returned without an opportunity for correction. To mitigate the limitations of one-shot routing, recent work has explored multi-turn coordination from different perspectives. Prior work [ 23 ] proposes a contextual-bandit framework where the router updates its selection strategy over rounds using user feedback. Router-R1 [ 36 ] trains an LLM router via reinforcement learning (RL) to perform multi-turn routing and aggregation, but primarily targets retrieval-style settings in which a query is decomposed into sub-queries and routed for information collection. Researchers in [ 11 ] study cost-controllable coordination by conditioning the controller on externally provided budget modes, enabling different routing behavior under different budgets. Despite this progress, these approaches largely emphasize routing and underexplore controller-to-agent interactive critique , as well as explicit workload balancing across heterogeneous agents. Based on these observations, we pose the following two questions:

Challenges. Answering these questions raises three key challenges. First, Joint routing and verification: Unlike one-shot routers that only select a model, the controller must simultaneously assess response draft quality, decide whether to stop or continue refining, and select the next agent when refinement is needed. These decisions are tightly coupled: the verification shapes the next generation, and the stopping decision depends on the controller’s assessment of the current draft.

Second, Trajectory-level credit assign-ment: in a multi-turn interaction process, routing and verifica-tion decisions made at different turns of the same trajectory can contribute unequally to the final answer. However, commonly used outcome-based rewards blur these differences, obscuring which turns are primarily responsible for success or failure. Third, 

Quality-utilization trade-off: optimizing only for answer quality encourages overuse of the strongest, often the most expensive, agent, undermining the purpose of maintaining a heterogeneous agent pool. A practical controller should therefore be able to improve response quality through multi-turn interaction while balancing utilization across agents. 

1.1 Contributions 

To address these challenges, we study an iterative critique-and-routing controller and develop an RL-based framework for multi-turn coordination, which formulates multi-agent routing as sequential interaction control. The controller serves as both critic/verifier and router: at each turn, it evaluates the draft response produced by the agent selected in the previous turn and decides whether to stop or invoke another agent for further refinement. We formalize this as a finite-horizon Markov Decision Process (MDP) with explicit agent utilization constraints. Our main contributions are as follows: • Sequential interaction formulation. We propose a multi-turn controller with dual roles as critic and router, and formulate multi-agent coordination as a finite-horizon MDP for cumulative reward maximization. In this formulation, each controller action jointly specifies an evaluation of the previous response, a stop/continue decision, and the next agent to invoke. To prevent over-reliance on the strongest agent, we further impose explicit agent-utilization constraints. • Reward and utilization-aware objective. We introduce a composite rule-based reward that evaluates both critique and routing decisions over trajectories. To facilitate optimization, we transform the constrained problem into an unconstrained one via Lagrangian relaxation, yielding a modified reward-maximization objective that can be optimized with standard policy gradient methods. • Empirical evaluation. Through extensive experiments on diverse heterogeneous multi-agent systems with different controllers, agents, and benchmarks, we demonstrate that our approach significantly outperforms baselines while maintaining comparable usage of the strongest agent. 

1.2 Related Work Multi-agent collaboration architectures. Prior work on multi-agent LLM systems can be broadly categorized into decentralized role-based collaboration and centralized controller-agent orchestration. In role-based systems, agents are assigned distinct roles and interact through predefined workflows [ 34 ,9, 25 ]. A closely related variant is cascaded specialization, in which different agents are invoked in a fixed sequence for generation, critique, and refinement, as in actor-critic style collaboration [ 5]2and generate-verify-refine pipelines [ 21 ]. Recent work also considers query decomposition and routing, where complex tasks are decomposed into subproblems that are assigned to specialized agents [ 29 , 14 , 26 ]. Another line of work studies centralized controller-agent systems with a star-topology architecture, where a single controller coordinates a heterogeneous pool of experts and decides which agent to invoke for each query [ 1, 3, 22 , 4, 11 ]. Our work focuses on this latter setting, and we discuss the most relevant controller design literature below. 

Centralized controller-agent orchestration. Within controller-agent systems, coordination strate-gies range from lightweight classifier-based routing to reasoning-driven decision-making. Early approaches formulate coordination as a one-shot model-selection problem, using classification [ 19 , 4]or representation-learning-based routers [ 20 , 2] to dispatch each query to a single agent. More recently, the authors in [ 23 ] cast multi-agent coordination as a contextual bandit problem, accounting for multi-turn user-agent interaction and enabling the response quality to improve over time from observed human feedback. Router-R1 [ 36 ] and CoRL [ 11 ] further adopt reasoning-capable LLMs as controllers and apply RL to cultivate reasoning-aware routing policies. Despite this progress, existing methods largely treat coordination as isolated routing decisions, rather than as a sequential interaction process. Moreover, they typically lack explicit utilization constraints, leaving balanced and efficient use of heterogeneous agents underexplored. 

## 2 Problem Background 

2.1 Controller Design for Multi-Agent Systems 

We consider a multi-agent LLM system with a heterogeneous agent pool M = {π1, π 2, . . . , π K } as shown in Figure 1, where agents differ in capability and cost. A controller coordinates this pool to respond to incoming queries. Unlike one-shot routing, where the controller selects a single model and directly returns its response without verification, we study a setting in which the controller coordinates the model pool over multiple turns by selecting an agent, evaluating/verifying the selected agent’s response, and deciding whether to continue or terminate the interaction. The problem is therefore not merely to identify the single best model for a query in one turn, which is often difficult in practice, but to learn a controller policy that maximizes final task performance over multi-turn interactions while satisfying system-level usage constraints across agents. The controller should allocate calls across heterogeneous agents so as to improve answer quality through sequential interaction, without collapsing to always invoking the strongest or most expensive model. 

2.2 Aren’t the Existing Solutions Good Enough? 

Although existing works have explored controller design in multi-agent systems from different perspectives, they still suffer from several limitations. Below, we discuss the two most relevant lines of work and describe their limitations in detail. 

Router-R1. Researchers in [ 36 ] introduce an RL-based controller for multi-agent LLM systems in information retrieval settings. Similar to Search-R1 [ 12 ], their approach decomposes a complex query into sub-queries and routes them to agents sequentially for information collection. However, this design is primarily tailored to retrieval-oriented scenarios, where queries are decomposable and the information returned by each agent is generally assumed to be correct and directly usable for subsequent reasoning, without explicit verification. Under such a design, incorrect or misleading intermediate outputs may propagate through the reasoning chain. In addition, Router-R1 is mainly optimized with outcome-level task rewards, which provide limited credit assignment in multi-turn trajectories: routing decisions made at different stages may contribute unequally to the final outcome, yet the reward signal does not clearly identify which intermediate actions should be rewarded. 

Cost-controllable routing. CoRL [ 11 ] studies routing from the perspective of cost control by inducing different controller behaviors under different inference budgets. To achieve this, they manually design separate prompt templates for low-, medium-, and high-budget modes, and the routing policy adapts accordingly. However, the budget mode must be explicitly specified at inference time, rather than inferred autonomously from the query and interaction history. Furthermore, their framework focuses on routing under externally imposed budget settings and does not model iterative controller-agent interaction with critique, verification, and stopping decisions. Their objective also does not explicitly encourage balanced utilization across heterogeneous agents. 33 RL-based Multi-Turn Coordination Methodology 

In this section, we propose an iterative critique-and-routing controller that models the agent coordi-nation as a sequential decision problem. We formalize this as a finite-horizon MDP [ 24 ] with explicit load-balancing constraints and optimize the controller using a policy-gradient algorithm. 

3.1 Multi-Turn Coordination for Multi-Agent LLM Systems 

Given a user query q, the controller sequentially selects agents from the pool M = {π1, π 2, . . . , π K }

and interacts with them to iteratively refine a response before producing the final output. The coordination process unfolds over multiple turns as follows. • Turn 1 (initial routing). For an incoming query q, the controller, denoted as πθ and parameterized by θ, selects an agent to produce the initial response: 

y1 = πθ (· | q, c), m1 = f (y1) −→ g1 = πm1 (· | q),

where c denotes system prompt used for the initial routing and f : Y → M is a parsing function that extracts the discrete routing decision from controller’s output text y.• Turn 2 (evaluation and re-routing). After observing agent response g1, the controller evaluates its quality. If the response is deemed insufficient, the controller re-routes the query to another agent and proceeds to the next turn: 

y2 = πθ (· | q, c′, g1), m2 = f (y2) −→ g2 = πm2 (· | q, g1, y2).

Here, y2 contains both a re-routing decision and an evaluation of g1, where the evaluation can be viewed as feedback that conditions the next generation. For turns t > 1, the prompt c′ differs from the first-turn prompt c by instructing the model to assess the draft response in addition to routing. • Turn t (general case). For subsequent turns, the controller repeats the same pattern: 

yt = πθ (· | q, c′, gt−1), mt = f (yt) −→ gt = πmt (· | q, gt−1, yt).

The interaction continues until the controller chooses to terminate or the turn limit T is reached. In summary, the controller serves a dual role: (i) router , selecting an agent from M and (ii) critic ,assessing response quality and generating feedback. We instantiate πθ as an LLM and design prompts 

c and c′, provided in Section B, to guide its behavior. To further strengthen these capabilities, we optimize the controller using RL, as detailed in the remainder of this section. 

3.2 Markov Decision Process Formulation 

We model the sequential interaction described in the previous subsection as a finite-horizon MDP defined by the tuple (S, A, T , R, T ). The state at turn t > 1 is st = ( q, c′, gt−1), comprising the user query, the system prompt, and the response generated in the previous turn. At the initial turn, 

s1 = ( q, c), where c is the initial system prompt. The action at turn t is the full controller output 

at := yt = πθ (· | st), from which we parse a routing decision mt = f (yt). Upon executing action at, the selected model mt produces a new response gt = πmt (· | q, gt−1, yt), and the state transitions to st+1 = ( q, c′, gt). The episode terminates when the controller deems the current response draft sufficient, or when the maximum number of turns T is reached. 

Reward. Following the seminal work [ 7], we adopt a rule-based action reward. Each action at := yt

jointly encodes routing and verification (i.e., evaluation). We define a binary verification reward 

Rv (st, at) and a binary routing reward Rr (st, at). The composite reward is: 

rt := R(st, at) = 0 .5 Rr (st, at) + 0 .5 Rv (st, at),Rr (st, at) = 1 (Cr (st, at)) , Rv (st, at) = 1 (Cv (st, at)) , (1) where the reward mixing coefficient is set to 0.5 to equally weight routing and verification. Cr (st, at)

and Cv (st, at) indicate whether the routing and verification decisions are correct, respectively. Notably, a routing decision is considered correct if the selected agent produces a correct solution to the problem. For boundary cases where one component is not applicable, we set the corresponding reward to zero (i.e., Rv (s1, a1) = 0 and Rr (sT , aT ) = 0 ). Similar to [ 11 , 36 ], our current reward formulation targets verifiable domains where the correctness of both decisions is well-defined. Extending the framework to non-verifiable domains would require learned or LLM-based judges. 4Training Objective. Datasets. Together, these benchmarks provide a comprehensive testbed for evaluating effectiveness and generalization of our method. 

Model Pools. We consider two heterogeneous multi-agent systems with different controllers and agent pools. In System 1, the controller is initialized with Qwen2.5-7B-Base, and the candidate agents are Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct, Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, and Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct [ 10 , 35 ]. In System 2, we use Qwen3-4B-Base as the controller, and the candidate agents are Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct, Ministral-3-8B-Instruct-2512, and Llama-3-2-1B-Instruct [ 10 , 35 ]. We also study additional controller choices; the corresponding results are reported in Appendix A.1.

4.1 Main Results 

In Table 1, we compare the accuracy of our method with several baselines across seven math benchmarks under two heterogeneous multi-agent systems. In System 1, where the controller is Qwen2.5-7B-Base and the agents have sizes 30B, 7B, and 1.5B, our method achieves an average accuracy of 80.5%. It outperforms the strongest routing baseline, Router-R1 (69.1%), by 11.4 percent-age points. The advantage is consistent across these benchmarks and is particularly pronounced on challenging benchmarks such as AGI-Eval-MATH and MinervaMath, highlighting the effectiveness of our approach in coordinating multiple agents for complex reasoning tasks. In System 2, with Qwen3-4B-Base as the controller and agents of sizes 30B, 8B, and 1B, our method further improves the average accuracy to 82.3%, surpassing Router-R1 (66.5%) by 15.8 percentage points. The strong results across both systems indicate that our method with explicit multi-turn interaction generalizes well across different controller capacities and agent compositions. ## 5 Conclusion and Limitations 

We studied controller design for multi-turn coordination in heterogeneous multi-agent LLM systems. We proposed an iterative critique-and-routing controller that evaluates intermediate drafts, decides whether to stop or continue, and selects the next agent for further refinement when needed. We formulated this process as a finite-horizon MDP with explicit agent-utilization constraints, designed a composite reward for decisions across turns, and optimized the controller via policy gradients under a Lagrangian-relaxed objective. Extensive experiments across multiple heterogeneous multi-agent systems and seven reasoning benchmarks demonstrate that our method consistently outperforms strong routing baselines and substantially narrows the gap to the strongest agent while using it for fewer than 25% of total calls. Overall, these results highlight the value of iterative refinement in multi-agent LLM systems. A limitation of our work is that experiments primarily focus on math reasoning benchmarks, where routing and verification rewards are relatively straightforward to define. In addition, the multi-turn design introduces extra inference latency and computational overhead compared to one-shot routing, reflecting a trade-off between performance and efficiency. 9References

## C Details on Datasets and Hyperparameters 

Details on Datasets. We fine-tune the controller on MATH-lighteval [ 8].
