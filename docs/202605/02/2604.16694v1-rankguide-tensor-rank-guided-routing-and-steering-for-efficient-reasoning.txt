## Abstract 

Large reasoning models (LRMs) enhance problem-solving capabilities by generating explicit multi-step chains of thought (CoT) reasoning; how-ever, they incur substantial inference latency and computational overhead. To mitigate this issue, recent works have explored model collaboration paradigms, where small reasoning models (SRMs) generate intermediate reasoning steps to achieve a better accuracy–latency trade-off. Despite recent progress, effectively and efficiently detecting and mitigating SRM failures in collaborative systems remains a key challenge. To address this is-sue, we analyze SRM inference in both the generated text and hidden-state spaces, and identify three types of failure modes: overconfidence , uncertainty ,and heavy revalidation . Building on these insights, we propose RankGuide ,a framework that improves the efficiency and effectiveness of SRM–LRM collaboration through tensor-rank-guided routing and steering. Specifically, RankGuide leverages a routing signal that incorporates tensor-rank signals 

derived from consecutive hidden states to detect when SRMs are likely to fail and selectively invoke LRMs. In addition, we introduce a tensor-rank-filtered steering vector extraction method to modulate the reasoning trajectory of SRMs, thereby improving their generation quality. By im-proving both routing and steering through tensor-rank signals, RankGuide enables SRM–LRM collaborative systems to achieve more efficient reason-ing with fewer steps and improved accuracy. Experiments on multiple reasoning benchmarks demonstrate the efficacy of RankGuide in reducing latency by up to 1.75 × compared to LRM, while maintaining competitive accuracy relative to prior methods. 

## 1 Introduction 

Large reasoning models (LRMs) (Guo et al., 2025; Yang et al., 2025) have recently demon-strated strong performance on complex problem-solving tasks by explicitly generating multi-step chains of thought (CoT) reasoning (Wei et al., 2022). By decomposing problems into intermediate steps, these models achieve significant gains in domains such as math-ematical reasoning (Hendrycks et al.; Zhang & Math-AI, 2024) and code generation (Jain et al., 2025). However, this improved capability comes at the cost of substantially increased inference latency and computational overhead, limiting their practicality in real-world deployment scenarios. To mitigate this issue, recent works (Shi et al., 2025; Pan et al., 2025; Fu et al., 2025; Zeng et al., 2026) have explored model collaboration paradigms, where small reasoning models (SRMs) generate intermediate reasoning steps while larger models are invoked selectively. These approaches offer a promising accuracy–latency trade-off by offloading simpler reasoning steps to lightweight models while preserving the capabilities of LRMs for more complex cases. Despite these advances, effectively and efficiently detecting and mitigating SRM failures 

remains a key challenge in collaborative systems. For instance, SpecReason (Pan et al., 2025) relies on LLM verifier feedback to decide whether to let larger models regenerate the 1

Recently, GlimpRouter (Zeng et al., 2026) triggers routing prior to each step based on entropy-derived uncertainty signals, thereby avoiding the need for LLM verifiers or rollback mechanisms. However, we observe that such approaches can fail when SRMs produce overconfident yet incorrect reasoning steps, resulting in missed routing opportunities and confident errors. Consequently, inaccurate failure detection allows errors to propagate through the reasoning chain, degrading final performance and diminishing efficiency gains. To better understand and address these limitations, we conduct a systematic analysis of SRM inference from the perspective of the generated text as well as the hidden-states. Our analysis reveals three characteristic failure modes: (a) overconfidence , where the model produces confident but incorrect reasoning; (b) uncertainty , where the model struggles to make progress; and (c) heavy revalidation , where the model repeatedly revisits prior steps without advancing the solution. These findings highlight the need for more reliable signals to detect diverse SRM failure modes and guide appropriate interventions during inference. Building on these insights, we propose RankGuide , a training-free serving framework that improves the efficiency and effectiveness of SRM–LRM collaboration. Our method leverages tensor-rank signals derived from consecutive hidden states to guide both routing and steering during LLM inference. These signals are used to detect likely SRM failures, enable selective LRM invocation, and filter the calibration set for high-quality steering vector extraction, ultimately leading to more efficient and reliable reasoning. Our contributions are summarized as follows: • We identify SRM failures as the primary bottleneck in collaborative reasoning systems, and provide a systematic analysis of SRM inference from both generated-text and hidden-state perspectives, revealing three key failure modes: overconfidence ,

uncertainty , and heavy revalidation .• We propose tensor-rank-guided routing, which employs a routing policy driven by a composite signal integrating token-level entropy and tensor-rank features derived from hidden states to identify potential SRM failures and selectively invoke LRMs, addressing both uncertainty and overconfident errors. • We introduce a tensor-rank-filtered steering vector extraction method that modu-lates the hidden-state trajectory of SRMs during inference using a steering vector computed from a tensor-rank-filtered calibration set, thereby reducing heavy revali-dation in SRM generation. Experimental results demonstrate that RankGuide can reduce latency and computational cost while maintaining competitive accuracy compared to prior methods. In specific, RankGuide can yield up to 1.75 × and 1.36 × latency benefit compared to LRM and SoTA collaborative inference framework, while maintaining or improving the accuracy.

Prior works primarily leverage tensor decompositions to approximate weight matrices or adapters (Tian et al., 2025; Li et al., 2026; Yang et al., 2024), thereby reducing memory and computational cost. 2.2 Model Collaboration 

Model collaboration (Wang et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2025) can improve inference efficiency by combining models of different capacities, where small reasoning models (SRMs) handle simple cases and large reasoning models (LRMs) are invoked for more complex reasoning. Recent works extend this paradigm to LLM reasoning by allowing SRMs to generate intermediate steps with selective verification or refinement. A central challenge is effective routing between SRMs and LRMs. Existing methods often rely on LLM verifier (Pan et al., 2025; Shi et al., 2025; Fu et al., 2025), which is costly and may lead to suboptimal decisions. To address this, GlimpRouter (Zeng et al., 2026) proposes an entropy-based confidence score that routes uncertain steps based on the SRM’s output distribution, achieving improved efficiency. However, relying solely on the SRM’s output confidence as the routing signal can be biased, as SRMs may be both highly confident and incorrect. To address this limitation, we propose RankGuide, which leverages tensor-rank signals from hidden states to provide a more reliable indicator of SRM reasoning quality for routing. 

2.3 Efficient Reasoning via Steering 

Beyond model collaboration, a prominent direction is latent-space steering of LRMs. In steering, hidden representations are modified to guide generation toward desired behaviors. Steering methods (Chen et al., 2025; Azizi et al., 2025; Xu et al., 2025) show that intervening in intermediate activations can improve reasoning quality or enforce concise generation patterns without retraining. While these approaches improve efficiency by enhancing reasoning through latent-space calibration, their performance gains are often limited, and they typically fall short of enabling SRMs to match the accuracy of LRMs.

To diagnose failure modes and redundancy in SRM generation, we use the delimiter “ \n\n” to segment model responses into individual reasoning steps, and study both per-step properties and inter-step correlations.

3.2 Understanding SRM Overconfidence under Latent Low-Rank Collapse 

Next, we analyze the quality of individual reasoning steps. Prior work, such as GlimpRouter 

Zeng et al. (2026), suggests that reasoning steps with high-entropy logits indicate model 

uncertainty and should be routed to stronger models for verification or refinement. However, our empirical findings reveal that low-entropy predictions are not necessarily reliable. Fig. 1 (middle-right) presents the entropy distribution of logits for reasoning steps grouped by their latent-rank structure. We define rank thresholds Tr1 = 8 and Tr2 = 60, and identify low-rank steps as those satisfying r1(Ht−W:t) < Tr1 ∨ r2(Ht−W:t) < Tr2 . Interestingly, we observe that low-rank steps exhibit substantially lower entropy (0.388 on average) than high-rank steps (0.983 on average), indicating higher confidence. These results indicate that collapsed reasoning trajectories often yield overconfident yet incorrect predictions, revealing that low entropy does not necessarily imply correctness. Consequently, routing strategies that rely solely on entropy may fail to identify such errors. Our findings therefore indicate that both high-entropy (uncertain) steps and low-entropy, low-rank (overconfident) steps should be considered as routing candidates in model collaboration systems .

## 4 RankGuide: Methodology 

In this section, we propose RankGuide , which leverages tensor-rank signals to address the SRM failure modes identified above. We first introduce rank-guided routing (§ 4.1), which adaptively switches between SRM and LRM generation based on rank and entropy signals, followed by rank-guided steering (§ 4.2), which modulates SRM hidden-state trajectories to suppress redundant validation. An overview of the framework is illustrated in Fig. 2. 

4.1 Rank-guided Routing Routing Signals. Before generating the next step, given the input prompt and previously generated outputs, the SRM performs a prefill pass to extract hidden states {Ht}Tt=1 from an intermediate layer, corresponding to reasoning-step boundaries, and collects the top-k out-put logits for next-token prediction. At each decoding step t, we compute two signals: (i) a tensor-rank measure R(Ht−W:t) (for t ≥ W), where Ht−W:t denotes the reshaped tensor con-structed from a sliding window of W consecutive reasoning steps, capturing dependencies across both the reasoning-step and hidden feature dimensions. Here, R(·) summarizes its TT ranks by incorporating both the reasoning-step rank ( r1) and a representative hidden-feature rank ( r2), as illustrated in § 3.1; (ii) the next-token prediction entropy H(pt) derived from the output distribution pt, corresponding to § 3.2. We introduce separate rank thresholds Tr1

and Tr2 , applied to the reasoning-step rank r1 and hidden-feature rank r2, respectively, along with an entropy threshold Te. As discussed in the motivation section, low-rank states indicate 5Preprint. Under review. representational collapse, while high entropy reflects model uncertainty. Accordingly, both high-entropy (uncertain) steps and low-entropy, low-rank (overconfident) steps are treated as routing candidates in our framework. 

Routing Policy. Based on the computed signals, we design a two-stage routing policy to determine whether to continue SRM generation or switch to the LRM. During calibration, we observe that when the SRM remains in a persistently low-rank regime across multiple steps, it often produces overconfident yet invalid reasoning and continues generating until reaching the maximum generation length without producing a valid answer. Motivated by this observation, we examine whether the low-rank feature persists over a sliding window of W reasoning steps. Specifically, if the tensor-rank measure satisfies 

R(Ht−i−W:t−i) < Tr, ∀i ∈ { 0, . . . , W − 1}, (4) we consider the reasoning trajectory to have fully collapsed and terminate SRM generation to produce the final answer. Otherwise, we further evaluate both rank and entropy signals at the current step. If 

R(Ht−W:t) < Tr ∨ H(pt) ≥ Te, (5) we route to the LRM for more reliable generation; otherwise, the SRM continues generation. This design enables the system to exploit confident and reliable SRM trajectories for ef-ficiency, while selectively invoking the LRM when overconfident or uncertainty failure patterns are detected.

5.2 Main Results RankGuide consistently achieves the highest accuracy with improved latency trade-offs. 

Figure 3 illustrates the accuracy–latency trade-offs of different methods under various threshold settings. RankGuide consistently outperforms existing routing approaches in terms of accuracy while maintaining lower latency than LRM. For example, on AIME-24 with DeepSeek-R1, RankGuide improves accuracy by 3.3% while reducing latency by 1.25 ×

compared to GlimpRouter, and achieves comparable accuracy to LRM with 1.41 × lower latency. Similarly, on AIME-25 with Qwen3, RankGuide improves accuracy by 3.3% while reducing latency by 1.13 × compared to GlimpRouter, and attains comparable accuracy to LRM with 1.59 × lower latency. In contrast, SpecReason incurs substantial latency overhead due to repeated verification and rollback, often resulting in higher latency than LRM while still achieving lower accuracy. GlimpRouter improves efficiency in some cases but lacks the ability to correct certain reasoning failures, leading to suboptimal accuracy. 

RankGuide reduces reasoning steps and re-validation redundancy in SRMs. Table 1 shows that these improvements are driven by more efficient reasoning behavior. RankGuide reduces the total number of steps by 19.3% on AIME24 and 16.2% on LiveCodeBench v6, while lowering the validation step ratio by up to 42.4% compared to SRM and up to 14.4% compared to previous routing methods. In contrast, GlimpRouter, which relies solely on uncertainty-based routing, fails to eliminate over-confident yet incorrect reasoning steps and thus maintains higher validation ratios and unnecessary reasoning steps. By explicitly suppressing redundant and over-confident reasoning steps, RankGuide produces shorter reasoning trajectories avoiding redundant validations, highlighting the importance of controlling both reasoning length and validation frequency for efficient inference.

## 6 Conclusion 

In this paper, we analyze failure modes of small reasoning models (SRMs) in collaborative systems, showing that uncertainty, overconfidence, and heavy revalidation lead to inefficient 9Preprint. Under review. and erroneous reasoning. We find that tensor-rank signals capture the intrinsic structure of reasoning and reveal failure patterns not reflected by entropy alone. Based on this insight, we propose RankGuide , a training-free framework that leverages tensor-rank signals to guide both routing and steering in SRM–LRM collaboration. By leveraging tensor-rank signals from consecutive hidden states, RankGuide adaptively routes unreliable reasoning steps to stronger models and steers SRM generation toward more efficient trajectories with fewer redundant steps. Experiments on multiple reasoning benchmarks demonstrate that RankGuide consistently achieves competitive accuracy with significant latency reductions compared to both LRM-only inference and prior routing methods.
