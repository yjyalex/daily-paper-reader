Abstract 

SWE-bench-style agentic reinforcement learning relies on expensive stateful tra-jectories, yet substantial compute is wasted on sampled rollout groups with skewed pass rates, where binary rewards provide a weak contrastive signal. We frame this inefficiency as a pass-rate control problem and show that a 50% pass rate is the most informative operating point: it maximizes reward entropy, the probability of surviving group filtering, RLOO advantage energy under GRPO, and success– failure contrastive structure. Guided by this principle, we propose Prefix Sampling 

(PS), which replays trajectory prefixes to steer skewed groups toward this regime: successful prefixes serve as head starts for mostly failing groups, while failing prefixes serve as handicaps for mostly passing groups. In stateful agent environ-ments, prefix states are reconstructed through replay while replayed tokens are excluded from the loss, restricting optimization to continuations generated by the current policy. On SWE-bench-style agentic RL, PS delivers end-to-end wall-clock speedups of 2.01 × on Qwen3-14B and 1.55 × on Qwen3-32B while preserving or improving final verified performance. For 14B, the SWE-bench Verified peak rises from the baseline peak of 0.273 to 0.295 under PS. Additional mathematical reasoning experiments on AIME 2025 show the same pass-rate control pattern and decompose the gains into replay, bidirectional coverage, and adaptive control. 

1 Introduction 

Reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR) is now widely used for post-training on tasks with clear external feedback, including software engineering and mathematical reasoning [ 1–3 ]. Within this paradigm, online policy-gradient methods such as Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) rely on rollout trajectories to produce contrastive learning signal [ 1]. As these tasks move toward agentic reinforcement learning, rollouts become longer, more interactive, and substantially more expensive to generate [ 3 , 4 ]. This raises a basic efficiency question: when rollouts dominate compute and wall-clock time, how much of that effort actually produces useful learning signal? 

Existing filtering heuristics show that not all rollouts are equally valuable, but the relationship among rollout pass rates, compute, and training benefit remains under-specified. To reduce compute spent on uninformative rollouts, many RLVR pipelines use group-level filtering [1 –3]. We use group filtering to denote the GRPO-style practice of discarding rollout groups whose binary rewards are all zero or all one. This operation is sometimes called rejection sampling in RLVR implementations, but differs from positive-only rejection sampling, which keeps only successful samples for supervised fine-tuning (SFT) or reinforcement. The need for such filtering is implicit in

DeepSeekMath/GRPO, since group-relative normalization gives no relative advantage signal when every reward in a group is identical [ 1]. DAPO later formalizes this exclusion as dynamic sampling, replacing all-zero/all-one groups until the batch is filled [ 2]. Beyond filtering, prior work injects guidance, reallocates rollout budget, mixes supervision, or calibrates hint strength to rescue difficult instances or preserve effective batches [ 5– 9]. These methods reshape pass rates implicitly. They rarely derive the target regime from binary-reward learning signal, and they do not directly control both too-hard and too-easy groups under fixed compute. We therefore formulate Rollout Pass-Rate Control (Rollout-PRC) for binary-reward RLVR tasks such as software engineering and mathematical reasoning [ 10 , 11 ]. For a task with multiple rollouts, the rollout pass rate is the fraction of successful rollouts in the group. Even after group filtering, many retained rollout groups in SWE-bench-style tasks remain highly skewed, such as 1/8 or 7/8, and provide weak contrast. Three perspectives—reward entropy, RLOO advantage energy under GRPO, and success–failure contrastive structure—all identify the same target: training is most informative when rollout pass rates are close to 50% [ 1, 12 , 13 ]. Rollout-PRC turns pass-rate management from an implicit byproduct of training into an explicit control objective. Guided by Rollout-PRC, we propose Prefix Sampling (PS), a bidirectional framework that routes skewed rollout groups toward the 50% regime through trajectory prefix replay. For mostly failing groups, it replays a successful prefix as a head start; for mostly passing groups, it replays a failing prefix as a handicap. Unlike hint-based approaches that rely on oracle solutions or reasoning traces from stronger models [ 5 , 6, 14 – 17 ], Prefix Sampling reuses trajectories produced by the current policy itself. In stateful agent environments, it reconstructs the prefix state through the existing rollout path and masks replayed tokens from the loss, so optimization is restricted to fresh continuations generated by the current policy. On SWE-bench-style agentic RL, it reaches the baseline’s best verified score in up to 1.76 × fewer evaluation steps and delivers 2.01 × and 1.55 × end-to-end speedups on Qwen3-14B [18] and Qwen3-32B [19] without sacrificing final performance. Our contributions are: • We identify Rollout Pass-Rate Control (Rollout-PRC) as a principled efficiency objective for binary-reward RL, and show that a 50% rollout pass rate maximizes reward entropy, RLOO advantage energy under GRPO, and success–failure contrastive structure. • We propose Prefix Sampling, a bidirectional pass-rate steering framework that replays trajectory prefixes to move skewed rollout groups toward this high-signal regime. The method works in stateful multi-turn agent environments because it reconstructs prefix state through the existing rollout path, applies optimization only to the continuation generated by the current policy, and adjusts prefix length for different pass-count buckets. • We demonstrate cross-scale gains on SWE-bench-style agentic RL and AIME 2025 reasoning [20 ], including up to 1.76 × fewer benchmark-evaluation steps, 2.01 × end-to-end speedup, and mechanistic and ablation evidence showing that the gains stem from steering rerollout pass rates toward the 50% target.

2.2 Prefix selection and adaptive prefix control 

Consider the concrete case of N = 8 rollouts grouped by pass count k. We discard degenerate groups (k ∈ { 0, 8}) and train on balanced groups ( k ∈ { 3, 4, 5}) as-is. Skewed groups are further split into 

hard , or too-hard , buckets ( k ∈ { 1, 2}), for which a successful trajectory is saved as a replay prefix, and easy , or too-easy , buckets ( k ∈ { 6, 7}), for which a failing trajectory is saved. This is the Prefix Selection stage in Figure 1. The Adaptive Prefix controller decides a single per-bucket scalar: the prefix ratio rb for each bucket 

b ∈ B = {1/8, 2/8, 6/8, 7/8}. We keep rb ∈ [0 .05 , 0.95] and, for a saved trajectory of length T , set the replay boundary to M = ⌊rb · T ⌋.For hard buckets ( k ∈ { 1, 2}), larger rb gives a longer successful head start and raises pass rate; for easy buckets ( k ∈ { 6, 7}), larger rb replays more of a failing trajectory and lowers pass rate. Both aim to move the rerollout pass rate toward p = 0 .5.Because model capability drifts during training, any fixed calibration can become stale. We therefore close the loop with a per-bucket feedback controller. For each bucket b, the trainer maintains an exponential moving average of the rerollout pass rate ˆpb, updated each time a replayed group from that bucket completes rerollout: 

ˆpb ← (1 − α)ˆ pb + α p (new ) 

> b

. (2) We use α = 0 .05 (half-life ≈ 13 .5 updates), a deadzone δ = 0 .03 around 0.5, an adjustment step 

η = 0 .05 , and a five-update cooldown after each adjustment. When ˆpb exits the deadzone, rb shifts by η in the direction that pulls the bucket back toward the target. The update direction reverses across the two sides: for hard buckets, rb decreases when ˆpb > 0.5 + δ and increases when ˆpb < 0.5 − δ;3for easy buckets, the rule is inverted. Thus each bucket has an independent closed-loop controller, with rb as the control knob and ˆpb as feedback. 

2.3 Prefix replay and prefix masking 

Once the controller settles on replay boundary M , Prefix Replay reconstructs the saved prefix state. In single-turn reasoning, this amounts to prepending tokens. For stateful coding agents, our imple-mentation feeds the saved prefix responses through the same action parser and environment executor used for ordinary model-generated responses. This replay-through-execution path reconstructs the code state, conversation history, tool outputs, and execution context at step M without introducing a separate sandbox-snapshot restore mechanism. From this state, the current policy generates a fresh continuation. However, if the loss were applied to the entire trajectory, replayed prefix tokens would inherit the advantage of the new rollout and contaminate credit assignment with off-policy actions. Prefix Masking prevents this. Let tcont denote the first token generated by the current policy after the replay boundary; the response mask is set to zero for all assistant tokens before tcont . Omitting clipping, normalization, and other implementation details, the masked GRPO policy-gradient term [ 1 , 21 ] can be written schematically as

Together, replay and masking decouple state manipulation from credit assignment: replay alters the distribution of starting states, while masking ensures that gradients originate only from actions sampled by the current policy after the replay boundary. After rerollout, non-degenerate rerollout groups are combined with ordinary balanced groups into the same Mixed Batch shown in Figure 1, then optimized by the same GRPO update. 

3 Experiments 

Our evaluation uses two complementary regimes. SWE-bench-style agentic RL [ 3 , 10 ] is the primary target: rollouts are long, stateful, and expensive, so both sample efficiency and wall-clock efficiency matter. Mathematical reasoning is a lighter single-turn setting that exposes per-task pass-count distributions and parent-to-rerollout transitions, and therefore lets us audit the mechanism more directly. 

3.1 Experimental recipe 

For SWE-bench, we train Qwen3-14B [ 18 ] and Qwen3-32B [ 19 ] in thinking mode on R2E-Gym-Subset [ 22 ] and evaluate on SWE-bench Verified [ 10 ]. Thinking mode provides the stronger long-horizon reasoning needed for high-difficulty software-engineering agents [ 23 ]. The baseline follows the DeepSWE GRPO++ recipe [ 3 ]: clip-high updates, KL-free policy optimization, no reward-standard-deviation normalization, length-normalized loss, leave-one-out advantage estimation, com-pact filtering, zero entropy bonus, and group filtering that discards 0/8 and 8/8 groups. Mathematical reasoning rollouts are single-turn, so the recipe needs no environment-state restoration. Our backbones are Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 [ 24 ] and Qwen3-8B [ 25 ], both run without thinking mode to model RL training on instruction-style policies [ 23 ]. We train on an AceReason-Math-Subset constructed locally from AceReason-Math [ 26 ]: we run Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 with 8 rollouts per problem, estimate each problem’s empirical pass rate, and exclude problems with pass rate at least 75%. This curation yields roughly 15,000 harder problems, and we evaluate the final models on AIME 2025 [ 20 ]. The baseline uses the same GRPO++ recipe but excludes components specific to agents. Both setups share several core configuration choices. We sample N = 8 rollouts per task. SWE-bench runs use max prompt length 4096 , while mathematical reasoning runs use max prompt length 2048 .The optimizer uses clip-high GRPO with upper clip bound 0.28 , actor learning rate 1×10 −6, and zero entropy bonus [ 1, 3, 13 , 21 ]. The batch size is 64 on SWE-bench and 128 on mathematical reasoning. Hardware is matched within each backbone: 4B uses 2 × 8 A800 GPUs, 8B uses 4 × 8

4A800 GPUs, and 14B/32B use 4 × 8 H200 GPUs. In the four main runs, Prefix Sampling uses the 4-bucket adaptive controller (PS-ada). Appendix C reports the run-level settings, shared GRPO++ backbone, and PS-ada controller parameters in Tables 5–8. 

Matched baseline. Our main comparison is against the matched DeepSWE/GRPO++ recipe because Prefix Sampling is intended as a drop-in controller with the same prompts, scalar rewards, 

N = 8 rollout budget, and deployment protocol. Prefix, hint, SFT-RL, and dynamic-allocation systems often add oracle/reference prefixes, teacher traces, SFT buffers, hint calibration, replacement rollouts, constructed prompts, or variable rollout budgets [ 2, 5 –8 , 14 –17 , 27 –29 ]; comparing to them directly would conflate pass-rate control with extra data, supervision, or compute. We therefore treat DAPO-style replacement sampling, DynaMO-style variable allocation, and oracle/hint-prefix methods as protocol-changing alternatives rather than matched fixed-budget baselines. Appendix B summarizes these resource/protocol differences. 

3.2 Evaluation metrics 

The headline outcome metric is task-level Pass@1, evaluated on SWE-bench Verified for agentic RL and AIME 2025 for mathematical reasoning [ 10 , 20 ]. All benchmark scores are averaged over 8 evaluation runs at each checkpoint. Efficiency is tracked by same-score convergence step, average wall-clock time per step, and valid rollout groups per batch, where a valid group is a non-degenerate group that survives group filtering. Outcome and efficiency metrics are reported across all four backbones where available. The mechanism analysis uses the 4B math run and reports distance to the 4/8 target, parent-to-rerollout pass-count transitions, bucket-level correction toward 0.5, and rollout-budget allocation across degenerate, skewed, and target-band outcomes.

5.2 From pass-rate control to trainable signal 

The pass-rate movement in Figure 4 matters because degenerate groups provide little binary contrast: 

0/8 has no successful trajectory, 8/8 has no failure, and both are typically removed before the policy update. By reducing extreme mass and increasing target-band mass, Prefix Sampling turns more of the same rollout budget into update-bearing groups that can carry reward entropy, RLOO advantage energy, and success–failure contrast. This keeps the causal claim narrow: Prefix Sampling does not balance every task or globally reshape the ordinary fresh-task distribution; it changes continuations for skewed partially successful groups, moving the controlled cohort toward the high-signal regime in Section 2.1. It also changes what the model practices: hard-side replay scaffolds missing steps, while easy-side replay creates self-correction. 6 Related Work 

Skewed pass rates are known to degrade binary-reward reinforcement learning efficiency, but existing methods usually mitigate them by reshaping the training distribution or adding scaffolding, rather than targeting a pass-rate regime explicitly [1, 2]. 

Implicitly reshaping pass rates via guidance. A first line rescues hard tasks by injecting partial supervision into failing rollout groups. PrefixRL and POPE condition on successful or oracle prefixes and optimize only the continuation [ 5, 6 ]. StepHint and hint-GRPO adapt hint granularity or hint usage for difficult samples [ 14 , 17 ]. ADHint adds difficulty-aware hint ratios and gradient modulation, while Self-Hinting uses model-generated hints to reduce external-supervisor dependence [ 15 , 16 ]. These methods change learner information through offline traces, oracle/reference solutions, teacher chains, or hints, and mostly rescue low-pass-rate tasks while leaving high-pass-rate tasks unaddressed. 

Implicitly reshaping pass rates via distribution. A second line preserves signal by reallocating compute, modifying batch construction, or mixing supervision into reinforcement learning. DAPO’s dynamic sampling filters all-fail/all-pass groups and samples replacements until the batch is filled, while DynaMO reallocates rollout budgets toward more informative prompts [ 2 , 7 ]. LUFFY uses off-policy guidance, SRFT and UFT mix supervised and reinforcement fine-tuning, and ReLIFT interleaves online fine-tuning for hard questions [ 8, 27 – 29 ]. Composition-RL constructs harder composite prompts from mastered ones and notes that always-solved prompts are wasteful [ 30 ]. Episode-level methods such as iGRPO, RLTF, and Experiential RL enrich interaction through self-conditioning, natural-language feedback, or reflection [ 31 –33 ]. These approaches are valuable but alter the resource or interaction protocol rather than steer retained skewed groups toward the 50% operating point. 

Explicitly targeting an informative regime. The closest conceptual line treats partial support as a mechanism for controlling effective task difficulty. SEELE uses item-response-style hint calibration to steer each instance toward a balanced success–failure regime [ 9 ]. This aligns with our motivation, but through hint calibration rather than our binary-reward criteria, and without bidirectional hard/easy control or replay-through-execution state reconstruction for agentic environments. Appendix B and Table 4 provide a detailed axis-by-axis comparison. 97 Conclusion 

Binary-reward RL is most informative near p = 0 .5: reward entropy, RLOO advantage energy, and success–failure contrast all peak there. Prefix Sampling uses replay-through-execution and prefix masking to steer skewed groups toward that regime. Across SWE-bench Verified and AIME 2025, it reaches baseline-level performance earlier, improves usable training signal, delivers 2.01 ×/1.55 ×

end-to-end speedups on 14B/32B, and ablations attribute the gains to replay, bidirectional coverage, and adaptive control.

A.1 Scope of Claims 

Our claims are scoped to binary-reward RLVR with grouped rollouts, and all main experiments use N = 8 rollouts per task. The largest-scale experiments are the intended stateful-agent setting on SWE-bench-style training, while the finest-grained mechanism audits use the 4B mathematical-reasoning run where pass counts, prefix metadata, and parent-to-rerollout transitions are easiest to inspect. The math results are scoped to the curated AceReason-Math-Subset described in Section 3.1; this shared subset supports the 4B/8B comparison, but is not a claim about unfiltered AceReason-Math. Finally, our main comparison is a matched GRPO++ baseline under the same data and rollout budget. Methods that use oracle hints, teacher traces, extra rollouts, or additional SFT buffers are discussed as related work and in Appendix B, but are not treated as directly comparable under the same fixed-resource protocol. 

A.2 Agentic Replay Assumptions and Costs 

Prefix Sampling assumes that replay prefixes can be saved. In agentic environments, prefix replay does not introduce a separate sandbox restoration mechanism: during prefix steps, saved model responses are fed through the same action parser and environment executor used for ordinary model-generated responses, so the environment state is reconstructed by replaying the prefix trajectory through the standard rollout path. We did not observe prefix-replay-specific restoration failures in our runs, but framework-specific nondeterministic tools remain implementation-dependent. The reported agentic step-time measurements are end-to-end measurements of this replay-through-execution training path. Prefix steps skip model generation and instead execute saved responses through the same environment path, so replay does not add a separate restore-latency component beyond the ordinary environment execution. Prefix replay also stores text prefixes rather than full sandbox snapshots. Table 3 gives a conservative upper-bound estimate for the prefix pool under our run configurations, using int32 token ids and the ratio cap of 0.95 . This state can be kept in CPU memory, so the additional GPU-memory footprint is negligible relative to model and rollout-system memory. The wall-clock gains should still be read as measurements for our implementation rather than a universal guarantee for all agent frameworks. The 50% target and the derivations in Appendix E apply to binary reward-side signal for grouped rollouts; they do not imply that every curriculum, every rollout count N , or every controller hyperparameter schedule is globally optimal. Our ablations compare fixed versus adaptive replay and hard-only versus bidirectional coverage, but they are not a full sensitivity sweep over ratio bounds, deadzone, step size, or non-50% targets. E Optimality of p = 0 .5 for Binary-Reward Learning Signal

E.5 Summary 

These derivations identify p = 0 .5 from several independent angles. It maximizes binary reward entropy, maximizes the probability that a group survives group filtering, maximizes mean-centered advantage variance and RLOO advantage energy, and maximizes both observed and expected success– failure contrastive pairs. Skewed partial groups such as 1/8 or 7/8 are not useless, but they provide much weaker binary learning signal than balanced groups. Prefix Sampling is designed around this fact: rather than merely drawing more rollouts, it changes the continuation state so that skewed but partially solved rollout groups move toward the high-signal region around 4/8. This is a statement about the binary reward-side signal available to a grouped policy-gradient update, not a theorem that every global curriculum should keep all tasks at 50% success or that trajectory diversity and policy-gradient geometry are irrelevant.

Each case is formatted as a compact audit panel followed by an evidence trail. G.1 Hard-side rescue: missing overlap correction Problem. Surface area of a 3 × 3 × 3 wooden cube after three centered 1 × 1 tunnels are drilled through opposite faces. 23 Gold answer. 72 .

Parent group. 2/8 correct; answer distribution 84:6 , 72:2 .

Prefix. Successful parent trajectory from the 2/8 bucket; prefix length 4644 .

Rerollout group. 4/8 correct; answer distribution 72:4 , 78:3 , 84:1 .

Evidence trail. The parent failures treat the three tunnels as independent and add all tunnel-wall area: 

Parent failure, pred. 84. “Remaining outer surface: 48 m2. Internal tunnel surfaces: 36 m2. Total surface area = 48 + 36 = 84 m2.” 

Parent success, pred. 72. “The holes intersect at the center of the cube ... we must be careful not to double-count the internal surface areas, or to subtract overlapping areas where the hole walls meet.” 

Replayed prefix state. “The three holes cross each other at the center. So, we must be careful not to double-count the internal surface areas ... because the holes intersect, internal surfaces from different holes may overlap or share area.” 

Rerollout partial failure, pred. 78. “The three tunnels contribute 36 m2 internally, but they intersect in three pairs ... remove 3 × 2 = 6 m2 from the 36 . So inner surface area = 36 − 6 = 30 ... total surface area = 48 + 30 = 78 .” 

Rerollout success, pred. 72. “Each tunnel has 4 walls ... in the central 1 × 1 × 1 cube ... subtract 

3 × 4 = 12 m2. So internal surface = 3 × 12 − 12 = 24 . Final surface area = 54 − 6 + 24 = 72 .” 

Takeaway. This case shows the hard-side role of Prefix Sampling. A rare policy-generated successful trajectory becomes an on-policy scaffold, and the fresh continuations produce contrast around the exact reasoning step that the parent group usually misses: whether the central overlap removes 0, 6,or 12 square meters of tunnel-wall area. 

G.2 Easy-side handicap: recovery from a common misconception Problem. Largest number of students, out of 50 , who can score strictly above the class mean. 

Gold answer. 49 .

Parent group. 7/8 correct; answer distribution 49:7 , 25:1 .

Prefix. Failing parent trajectory from the 7/8 bucket; prefix length 273 .

Rerollout group. 4/8 correct; answer distribution 25:4 , 49:4 .

Evidence trail. The parent group is mostly correct, but the single failure exposes the tempting misconception that “above average” means at most half the class: 

Parent success, pred. 49. “49 students score 100. 1 student scores 0. Total sum = 4900 . Mean 

= 98 . Now, the 49 students who got 100 are above 98, so they are above the mean.” 

Parent failure, pred. 25. “By definition, half of the scores are above the mean and half below ... it is impossible for more than half of the students to score above the mean, because the mean is the balance point of the data.” 

Replayed prefix state. “The mean is the sum of all scores divided by the number of students. By definition, half of the scores are above the mean and half below ... it is impossible for more than half of the students to score above the mean.” 

Rerollout failure, pred. 25. “You cannot have more than 25 students strictly above the mean ... the maximum number of students who can score strictly above the mean is 25.” 24 Rerollout success, pred. 49. “Actually, no. There is no rule that says only half the students can score above the mean ... 49 students scored 1, 1 student scored 0. Mean = 49 /50 = 0 .98 .Each of the 49 students scored 1 > 0.98 .” 

Takeaway. This case shows the easy-side role of Prefix Sampling. A mostly solved task would otherwise provide little new signal, because most parent rollouts already repeat the answer 49 . Re-playing the model’s own failing branch turns the task into a self-correction exercise: the continuation first inherits the plausible but false “half above the mean” heuristic, then explicitly rejects it in the highlighted sentence and constructs a counterexample. Thus the easy-side intervention does more than make an easy problem harder; it creates direct training signal for detecting and repairing a tempting wrong reasoning branch. 25
