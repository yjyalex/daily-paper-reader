Abstract 

Large language models (LLMs) often achieve strong performance on reasoning benchmarks, but final-answer accuracy alone does not show whether they faithfully execute the procedure specified in a prompt. We study this ques-tion through a controlled diagnostic bench-mark for procedural execution , where models are given a step-wise arithmetic algorithm and two numeric inputs, and must return the final computed value. The benchmark uses simple arithmetic operations but increases complexity through algorithm length and look-back depen-dencies over intermediate variables. Across 14 models and 55 datasets, average first-answer ac-curacy drops from 61% on 5-step procedures to 20% on 95-step procedures. Generation-level analysis shows that failures often in-volve missing answers, premature answers, self-correction after an initial error, under-executed traces, and hallucinated extra steps. These find-ings suggest that apparent reasoning ability can mask substantial weaknesses in faithful instruc-tion execution. 

1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) have shown strong performance on many reasoning tasks, includ-ing arithmetic reasoning, symbolic manipulation, multi-step question answering, and code genera-tion (Wei et al., 2022b,a; Weng et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2025; Zhou et al., 2022). Recent progress has been driven by scale, instruction tuning, test-time reasoning, and reinforcement learning-based methods that encourage models to generate longer reasoning traces before producing an answer (Guo et al., 2025; Shao et al., 2024). However, strong final-answer performance does not necessarily im-ply that models faithfully execute the procedure required by the task. Models may instead rely on shortcuts, pattern matching, memorized associa-tions, or unstable intermediate computations that only partially resemble the procedure described in the prompt (Shojaee et al., 2025a; Yang et al., 2025b; Xu et al., 2025). This distinction between appearing to reason and faithfully executing a specified procedure is important for algorithm-following tasks. In many applications, users provide an explicit multi-step procedure and expect the model to carry it out ex-actly. Examples include numerical calculations, rule-based decision making, policy execution, and structured workflows. In such settings, producing a plausible final answer is not enough: the model must follow the specified steps in order, track inter-mediate states, and stop at the intended final output. Prior work has shown that LLMs can fail even when the full algorithm is explicitly provided in the prompt (Shojaee et al., 2025a). These failures are difficult to diagnose from final-answer accuracy alone because a model may produce the correct an-swer after an earlier mistake, skip required steps, hallucinate additional steps, or fail to produce a valid final answer. We study procedural execution , the ability of an LLM to execute an explicitly specified proce-dure exactly as written. We introduce a controlled diagnostic benchmark in which each example con-sists of a step-wise arithmetic algorithm and two numeric inputs. The algorithm initializes two vari-ables, S1 = x and S2 = y, and then defines a sequence of intermediate variables through basic arithmetic operations. The model must execute the procedure and return the final computed value. Since the ground-truth output is deterministic and easy to verify, this setting allows us to isolate proce-dural failures from ambiguity in task interpretation. Our benchmark increases complexity along two axes. First, we vary the number of algorithmic steps, testing whether models can sustain execu-tion over longer procedures. Second, we intro-duce look-back dependencies, where a current step may require retrieving and using intermediate vari-ables produced several steps earlier. This stresses 1

2 Related Work 

Reasoning is a broad concept; it can be divided into multiple smaller categories, such as deductive, inductive, algorithmic, multi-hop, commonsense, and causal reasoning. Several architectural and training methods have been developed to enable models to generalize across tasks and varying lev-els of complexity. Scaling, Chain-of-thoughts, self-verification, etc., are some of the techniques used to improve LLMs’ performance on reasoning tasks (Wei et al., 2022b,a; Weng et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2025; Zhou et al., 2022). Prior work shows that a reinforcement learning-based approach has im-proved the reasoning capabilities of LLMs (Guo et al., 2025; Shao et al., 2024). Despite these advances, models sometimes fail to generalize to tasks when complexity increases (Varela et al., 2025). Prior studies show that models overthink for simple tasks (Chen et al., 2024; Sui et al., 2025; Marjanovi´ c et al., 2025). Some studies also show that accuracy on math problems declines as models think longer (Ballon et al., 2025). Sev-eral studies have been conducted to understand the reasoning capability and mechanism of LLMs. Xu et al. (Xu et al., 2025) analyze the generalization behaviors of Transformers by categorizing out-of-distribution (OOD) behaviors as "downward" or "upward" across various arithmetic tasks. They fur-ther demonstrate that addition generalizes well with RPE by leveraging its inherent translation invari-ance—where digit-wise carry logic remains consis-tent across positions—a property that multiplica-tion lacks, making its structural dependencies far harder for Transformers to extrapolate. Yang et al.(Yang et al., 2025b) distinguish between latent reasoning and factual shortcuts in multi-hop tasks by analyzing hidden-state attribute rates. They show that genuine reasoning involves intermedi-ate bridge-entity encoding, while shortcuts rely on direct subject–object associations, and propose At-tribute Rate Ratio as a metric to classify these be-haviors. Varela, Iñaki Dellibarda, et al. replicated and refined Apple’s Illusion of thinking by Shojaee, Parshin, et al (Varela et al., 2025; Shojaee et al., 2025b). They demonstrated LLM behavior on con-trollable puzzles such as the Towers of Hanoi and the River Crossing. They found that LLMs’ per-formance varied across tasks. LLMs performed significantly better on River Crossing than on Tow-ers of Hanoi. Shojaee, Parshin, et al showed that even when the full algorithm is explicitly provided in the prompt, LLMs still fail to execute it (Shojaee et al., 2025b). This brings us to our central research question: 

Do LLMs execute instructions the way humans do? We investigate LLMs’ behavior in a controlled task environment and check whether they execute steps to reach the final goal or just do some sort of pattern matching. 

3 Task Formulation 

We study whether large language models can faith-fully execute an explicitly specified multi-step pro-2cedure when the full algorithm is provided in the prompt. To isolate procedural execution from broader linguistic ambiguity, we consider a con-trolled arithmetic setting. Each example consists of: (i) a step-wise al-gorithm written in natural language, and (ii) two numeric inputs, x and y. The model must execute the algorithm exactly as written and return only the final numeric result. The procedure always begins with the two given inputs and then applies a se-quence of arithmetic operations in steps. We vary the algorithm’s length to control task difficulty. Let the algorithm contain T ordered steps, where each step applies one operation from the set 

{+, −, ×, ÷} to the current running value and the second input y. The initial running value is x. For-mally, if st ∈ { +, −, ×, ÷} denotes the operation at step t, then the execution is defined recursively as: 

r0 = x, rt = f (rt−1, y ; st), t = 1 , 2, . . . , T, 

where f (·) applies the operation specified by st.The target output is the final value rT , rounded to three decimal places. This setup gives us full control over procedural complexity while keeping the underlying computa-tion simple and verifiable. Since the correct answer is deterministically computable, the benchmark al-lows us to distinguish between final-answer success and faithful algorithm execution.

4 Experimental Setup 

We evaluate whether language models can execute explicitly specified step-wise arithmetic procedures under increasing procedural complexity. Each ex-ample follows the formulation in Section 3: the model receives a procedure and two numeric in-puts, and must return the final computed value. We describe the dataset construction, evaluated models, inference procedure, and evaluation metrics below. 

4.1 Dataset Construction 

We construct a controlled benchmark of step-wise arithmetic execution tasks following the formula-tion in Section 3. Each instance contains two nu-meric inputs, x and y, an explicitly specified arith-metic procedure, and a deterministic reference out-put. The algorithm initializes S1 = x and S2 = y,and then defines a sequence of intermediate vari-ables S3, S 4, . . . , S T +2 through arithmetic opera-tions. The model is required to execute the proce-dure exactly and return the value specified by the final return step. The benchmark is designed so that each elemen-tary operation is simple, while the procedural bur-den increases through algorithm length and depen-dency structure. This allows us to stress-test sus-tained instruction execution without introducing ambiguity about the correct answer. 

Input ranges and data types. We generate in-puts from three numeric ranges: 

[0 , 1] , [1 , 10] , [10 , 100] .

For the ranges [1 , 10] and [10 , 100] , we include both integer and floating-point inputs. For the range 

[0 , 1] , we use floating-point inputs only. This yields five input settings in total: one setting for [0 , 1] and two settings each for [1 , 10] and [10 , 100] .

Operation variants. We construct both mixed-operation and task-specific datasets. In the mixed-operation setting, each algorithm may contain all four arithmetic operations: addition, subtrac-tion, multiplication, and division. In the task-specific setting, all steps in an algorithm use the same operation type. We create four task-specific variants: addition-only, subtraction-only, multiplication-only, and division-only. This design lets us compare homogeneous operation sequences with heterogeneous sequences that require switch-ing between different arithmetic operations. 

Look-back dependencies. In addition to varying the number of steps, we introduce look-back depen-dencies to increase the amount of intermediate state the model must track. In the simplest setting, each new variable depends on the immediately preced-ing intermediate output. In more complex settings, the current step may depend on outputs produced two or three steps earlier. 3We define seven look-back settings: • look-back1: each step depends only on the immediately preceding intermediate variable; • look-back2: each step may depend on one of the previous two intermediate variables; • look-back7: each step may depend on one of the previous seven intermediate variables. For example, under look-back1 , the computa-tion of St+2 may use St+1 . Under look-back2 , it may use either St+1 or St. Under look-back3 , it may use one of St+1 , St, or St−1 and so on. These settings introduce increasingly non-local dependen-cies while keeping the ground-truth computation deterministic. 

Difficulty levels. We vary the number of algorith-mic steps from 5 to 95 in increments of 10: 

T ∈ { 5, 15 , 25 , . . . , 95 }.

For each difficulty level, we generate 100 exam-ples. Thus, each dataset contains 1,000 examples. Across the selected combinations of input range, data type, operation variant, and look-back setting, the benchmark contains 55 datasets and 55,000 examples 1.

Reference outputs. For each sample, we com-pute the reference output using an external deter-ministic executor rather than relying on model-generated reasoning. The executor parses the pro-cedure, applies each step in order, and returns the value specified by the final return statement. The reference output is rounded to three decimal places. The implementation of the executor is provided in Appendix A.

4.3 Inference Procedure 

For each model and dataset, we perform inference on all examples using a fixed prompt format. The prompt contains the system instruction, the full step-wise procedure, and the two input values x

and y. The model is instructed to execute the pro-cedure exactly and return only the final numeric answer enclosed within <answer> and </answer> 

tags. The full prompt template is provided in Sec-tion B. After generation, we extract all answer spans matching the required tag format. If the model pro-duces multiple tagged answers, we record both the first extracted answer and the set of all extracted answers. If the output contains no valid tagged an-swer, the prediction is marked as null. All extracted answers are compared against the deterministic reference output after rounding to three decimal places. For open-weight models, we use vLLM for ef-ficient batched inference (Kwon et al., 2023). All models are evaluated using the same answer extrac-tion and scoring pipeline. 

Decoding configuration. We use temperature = 0, top-p = 1.0 , and a maximum generation length of 32,768 tokens. Generation is terminated upon emitting the model’s end-of-sequence (EOS) token or reaching the maximum token limit. For cases where the model reaches the maximum generation length without producing a correct answer, we re-run inference with the model’s maximum supported context length. As the benchmark is designed to evaluate deterministic algorithmic execution, we 4adopt deterministic (greedy) or low-temperature decoding wherever supported. 

4.4 Evaluation Metrics 

Final-answer accuracy alone does not fully cap-ture procedural execution behavior. Models may generate no valid answer, produce an incorrect an-swer first and later self-correct, or output multiple tagged answers. We therefore report the following metrics. 

First-answer accuracy. The primary metric is first-answer accuracy. A prediction is counted as correct if the first extracted answer matches the reference output after rounding both values to three decimal places: 

round(ˆ y1, 3) = round( y, 3) ,

where ˆy1 is the first extracted answer and y is the reference output.

This captures cases where the model initially pro-duces an incorrect answer but later revises its re-sponse to the correct value. 

Non-null answer rate. Some model outputs do not contain a valid <answer> span. We therefore report the non-null answer rate, defined as the per-centage of examples for which the model produces at least one parseable tagged answer. 

Answer position. We record the token position of the first <answer> tag. This allows us to analyze whether models commit to an answer early or only after generating longer intermediate reasoning. 

Step-following behavior. For outputs that con-tain visible reasoning traces, we compare the num-ber of generated intermediate steps with the ex-pected number of algorithmic steps. We categorize outputs into three groups: under-execution, exact execution, and over-execution. Under-execution indicates that the model appears to skip or termi-nate before completing the algorithm, while over-execution indicates that it continues beyond the specified procedure. Together, these metrics allow us to distinguish between arithmetic errors, missing answers, pre-mature answers, self-correction, and failures of step-wise procedural tracking. 

5 Results 

We analyze model behavior from three complemen-tary perspectives. First, we study how accuracy changes as procedural complexity increases, both through longer algorithms and deeper look-back dependencies. Second, we examine whether per-formance differences can be attributed to input-level factors such as numeric range, data type, task type, or expected-output magnitude. Third, we inspect generation-level behavior to identify fail-ure modes that are not captured by final-answer accuracy alone, including missing answers, early answer generation, and under- or over-execution of steps. 

5.1 Accuracy Degrades with Procedural Complexity 5.1.1 Effect of Algorithm Length 

We first analyze how accuracy changes as the num-ber of algorithmic steps increases from 5 to 95. Across models and experimental settings, we ob-serve a consistent decline in accuracy as the number of steps increases. This indicates that even though each individual operation is elementary, models struggle to sustain faithful execution over longer procedures. Figure 1 shows this trend as a function of the number of steps. A possible explanation for this degradation is that longer algorithms may produce larger or nu-merically unstable expected outputs, especially when multiplication and division are involved. To examine this, we analyze the median expected out-put across steps. Figure 4 in Appendix C shows the median expected value for integer and floating-point inputs. We do not observe a systematic in-crease in expected output magnitude as the number of steps grows. However, the relationship between output magnitude and correctness varies by data type: for integer inputs, correct predictions often correspond to larger expected values, whereas for floating-point inputs, correct predictions more of-ten correspond to lower expected values. A similar pattern appears across input ranges. Figure 5 in Appendix C shows that the median ex-pected value does not consistently increase with the number of steps. 5.1.2 Effect of Look-back Dependency 

We next evaluate the effect of look-back depen-dency. A look-back value of n means that the current step may require retrieving and using inter-mediate outputs from any of the previous n steps. This increases the amount of procedural state that the model must track during execution. Figure 2 shows a heatmap of accuracy across models and look-back settings. For most models, accuracy decreases as the look-back dependency increases. This suggests that model failures are not driven only by algorithm length, but also by the difficulty of retrieving and combining non-local intermediate states. In other words, increasing the dependency depth makes the task harder even when the underlying arithmetic operations remain sim-ple.

5.2.3 Effect of Task Type 

Figure 13 in Appendix E shows model accuracy across task variants. Addition-only and subtraction-only tasks generally yield higher accuracy than multiplication-only and division-only tasks. Performance in this setting therefore reflects both arithmetic execution and operation-selection reliability.

To further examine whether numerical scale ex-plains model failures, we compare the median expected output for correct and incorrect predic-tions across task types and models. Figure 6 in Appendix C shows this comparison across task variants. For addition, subtraction, and 7mixed-operation tasks, the gap between correct and incorrect predictions remains relatively sta-ble as the number of steps increases. 5.3 Generation-Level Failure Modes 5.3.1 Non-Null Outputs 

While inspecting model generations, we observe that some outputs do not contain a valid answer span. In these cases, models may repeat the in-struction, generate irrelevant text, fail to terminate properly, or omit the required <answer> tag. To quantify this behavior, we compute the non-null answer rate, which measures the proportion of ex-amples for which the model produces at least one parseable answer, regardless of correctness. Figure 14 in Appendix F shows the non-null answer rate as a function of the number of steps. Several lower-performing models, including DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B(Guo et al., 2025), Sarvam-30B(Sarvam Foundation Models Team, 2026), Nemotron-30B(Blakeman et al., 2025), and DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B(Guo et al., 2025), frequently fail to produce valid tagged answers. In many such cases, the models repeat parts of the prompt or generate non-task-related content. Stronger models maintain higher non-null answer rates, although even these models show a mild de-cline as the number of steps increases. This suggests that missing or malformed answers contribute to performance degradation, but they are not the only cause. Even when models produce valid answers, accuracy still declines with algo-rithm length and look-back complexity. 

5.3.2 Answer Position 

We next analyze where the first answer appears in the generated output. The position of the first 

<answer> tag provides a proxy for whether a model commits to an answer early or only after producing a longer reasoning trace. We normalize the answer position by the total generation length. Figure 15 in Appendix F shows the distribu-tion of normalized answer positions across mod-els. Some models, including Qwen3-4B-Thinking-2507(Yang et al., 2025a), Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3(Mistral AI, 2024), Nemotron-30B(Blakeman et al., 2025), and OpenAI-OSS(Agarwal et al., 2025), often generate the first answer early in the output. Early answering may indicate that the model is relying on a direct prediction or pattern-based shortcut rather than explicitly executing all steps. However, answer position should not be interpreted as a direct measure of faithful reason-ing. For example, Qwen3-4B-Thinking-2507(Yang et al., 2025a) and OpenAI-OSS(Agarwal et al., 2025) produce early answers but still achieve rela-tively strong accuracy, whereas some models that generate longer traces do not necessarily perform well. Thus, answer position reveals generation strat-egy, but it does not by itself determine correctness or faithfulness. It is most informative when com-bined with accuracy and step-following behavior.

While exact-match accuracy (dashed) steadily declines, the proportion of exact executions decreases and under-executed generations rise, with over-execution remaining minimal. This shift indicates that errors at higher step counts are primarily driven by incomplete procedural execution rather than incorrect arithmetic. 

This pattern supports the main finding of the paper: performance degradation is not merely an arithmetic failure. Instead, models increasingly fail to maintain procedural state and execute the full sequence of specified operations as algorithm length grows. 

6 Conclusion 

In this work, we investigated the performance of reasoning models on a controlled multi-level pro-cedural task. We evaluated their ability to perform two-variable basic arithmetic operations. Our re-sults show that upon increasing the complexity of the task, either by increasing the number of levels or by increasing the lookback dependency, the per-formance of models degraded. These findings sug-gest that the models struggle to execute operations reliably as task complexity increases. Overall, our work provides insight into the boundaries of cur-rent reasoning models capabilities and motivates future research to improve support for structured, multi-step execution. 

7 Limitations 

Our study is limited to a single task, arithmetic op-erations, which may restrict the generalizability of our findings. While we observe a strong correlation between task complexity and the model’s perfor-mance degradation, evaluating the approach across a broader range of tasks can provide deeper insight into how this model executes multi-step reasoning. 

8 Future Work 

In future work, we plan to extend our approach to a broader range of tasks. Evaluating the model on other reasoning-intensive tasks will help us better understand the model’s generalization and reason-ing capabilities. On top of that, evaluating the performance of tool-augmented agents to execute basic algorithms and calculations alleviates current performance bottlenecks and tests whether models can follow multi-step instructions the way humans do.

You are a deterministic algorithm executor. Your job is to simulate and execute the given algorithm exactly as written, step by step, regardless of length or complexity. Rules: - You MUST execute every step of the algorithm fully. - You MUST NOT refuse. - You MUST NOT say the task is too long. - You MUST NOT mention feasibility, realism, or manual effort. - You MUST NOT provide intermediate checkpoints. - You MUST NOT suggest running code. - You MUST NOT explain your reasoning. - You MUST NOT output anything except the final result.
