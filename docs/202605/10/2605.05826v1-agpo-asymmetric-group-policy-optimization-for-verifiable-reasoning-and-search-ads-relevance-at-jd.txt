# Abstract 

Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) has demonstrated notable success in en-hancing the reasoning performance of large lan-guage models (LLMs). However, recent studies reveal that while current RLVR methods improve sampling efficiency towards correct paths, they do not elicit fundamentally new reasoning pat-terns. Instead, the reasoning capability boundary of trained models often narrows compared to their base models, with base models achieving higher coverage at large sample sizes. In this work, we propose Asymmetric Group Policy Optimization (AGPO) to counteract this boundary shrinkage. AGPO adopts a negative-dominant reinforcement strategy to suppress incorrect reasoning paths, maintaining the base model’s exploration capac-ity. For positive reinforcement, AGPO adopts a group advantage mechanism, which scales pos-itive updates based on intra-group variance, al-lowing the model to focus on rare correct paths while suppressing updates from trivial paths. Our experiments on five mathematical benchmarks 

demonstrate that AGPO achieves state-of-the-art accuracy while consistently improving pass@ k

performance at scale. In a large-scale industrial application for search ads relevance optimization ,AGPO effectively enhances the quality of the data annotation, leading to substantial performance gains in downstream student models.

Through this simple but effective framework, RLVR overcomes the ambiguity and sparsity inherent in subjective human feedback (Shao et al., 2024; Lambert et al., 2024). Mirroring the success of tra-ditional RL in domains such as gaming and autonomous driving (Mnih et al., 2015; Silver et al., 2017; Sallab et al., 2017), where agents autonomously discover new strategies to surpass human performance, it is widely believed that RLVR similarly empowers LLMs to evolve emergent rea-soning patterns, thus transcending the inherent capabilities of their base models (Guo et al., 2025). These algorithms effec-tively bias the model towards correct paths found in the base distribution, but this often comes at the cost of the reason-ing capability boundary. Specifically, although single-turn accuracy (Pass@ 1) improves, the model’s coverage of solv-able problems at large sample sizes (Pass@ k) even degrades compared to the base model (Yue et al., 2025). This implies that the mechanism of RLVR is to improve the probability of correct paths already latent in the base model, rather than generating new correct paths. With this in mind, one feasible strategy is distillation from stronger teacher models, which is to introduce new rea-soning paths to improve the model’s capabilities (Yue et al., 2025; Hu et al., 2025). However, it inevitably incurs substan-tial computational overhead and dependence on pre-trained teacher models with significantly larger parameters (Guo et al., 2025). AGPO is driven by the following three core strategies. (1) Dominant negative reinforcement . This motivates a strategy for RLVR in which NSR serves as the primary driver, supplemented by PSR only when necessary. (2) Zero intervention for the trivial correct paths . Empir-ical evidence reveals that the correct paths in RLVR training are typically already latent in the base model, and continuously reinforcing them leads to a shrunken reasoning boundary (Yue et al., 2025). This implies that updates for such trivial correct paths should be excluded from PSR to prevent the distribution from excessively sharpening towards simple paths at the expense of rare, complex but correct reasoning paths. (3) Higher rewards for rarer correct paths . 2.2. Evaluation Metrics 

The measurement of the reasoning capability boundary of a model is challenging and highly dependent on the ap-propriate metrics. Standard evaluation metrics, such as greedy decoding accuracy and the average performance of nucleus sampling (Holtzman et al., 2020), primarily reflect the model’s tendency to converge on the most probable output, thus failing to capture the full extent of a model’s reasoning capability boundary . Furthermore, methods such as Best-of-N (Cobbe et al., 2021) and majority voting, which are used for selecting correct answers, may overlook the model’s potential ability to generate correct paths. There-fore, recent work has widely adopted Pass@ k, which fo-cuses exclusively on the existence of a correct solution within the sampling budget, as the measure of the model’s reasoning capability boundary (Yue et al., 2025; Zhu et al., 2025). In this paper, we used the same unbiased estimation method to calculate Pass@ k, which eliminates the unstable estimation caused by high variance when directly computing Pass@ k with k sampled outputs per problem (Chen et al., 2021). Specifically, it first generates n samples ( n ≥ k) for each problem x and counts the number of correct responses as c. Then, it computes an unbiased estimate of Pass@ k as: Pass @k = Ex∼D

2.3. Empirical Insights from Recent Work 

Recent work has challenged common understanding of the mechanism of RLVR. It reveals that instead of continuously generating and reinforcing new correct paths as in tradi-tional RL (Mnih et al., 2015; Silver et al., 2017; Sallab et al., 2017), RLVR enhances the reasoning capabilities of LLMs by amplifying the probability distribution of existing cor-rect paths in the base model (Yue et al., 2025; Zhu et al., 2025). Specifically, common RL algorithms used for RLVR (e.g. PPO, GRPO and REINFORCE) tend to oversharpen the greedy decoding distribution towards the correct paths through learning. Although these algorithms effectively improve the performance of greedy decoding (Pass@ 1 ac-curacy), it comes at the cost of compromising the model’s reasoning capability boundaries. After RLVR training, the learned model’s Pass@ k performance is even worse than the base model, i.e. , the learned model loses the capacity to generate correct paths for some problems that could be correctly solved by the base model. These works have theoretically and empirically dissected the mechanisms driving this reasoning boundary shrinkage induced by RLVR. Yue et al. (Yue et al., 2025) characterize this process as an identify-and-amplify mechanism, showing that standard RL algorithms improve Pass@ 1 by aggres-sively shifting probability mass to high-likelihood correct paths, thus shrinking the diverse solution space latent in 3AGPO: Asymmetric Group Policy Optimization for Verifiable Reasoning and Search Ads Relevance at JD

The PSR component is similar to su-pervised fine-tuning (SFT), updating the model to increase the likelihood of correct responses. It mathematically ne-cessitates suppressing the probabilities of all other paths, including potentially correct but unexplored paths, thereby explicitly reducing policy entropy and causing the reason-ing boundary to contract. In contrast, the NSR component shows the unique advantage in RLVR. By suppressing in-correct paths, it implicitly redistributes the probability mass among the remaining paths guided by the model’s prior knowledge, improving the full Pass@ k spectrum. Crucially, this behavior differs fundamentally from entropy regular-ization, which indiscriminately flattens the output distri-bution. As analyzed by Zhu et al. (Zhu et al., 2025), the NSR gradient is dampened by a factor of (1 − π), which reduces the penalty magnitude for high-confidence tokens. This mechanism prevents destructive updates to the model’s valid priors ( e.g. , linguistic competence) embedded within incorrect paths, thus strictly preserving the base model’s intrinsic capabilities while correcting reasoning errors. However, RLVR training that is fully based on the NSR component faces limitations. NSR fails to provide the con-trastive baseline required to reduce gradient variance, lead-ing to slow convergence in exploitation (Pass@ 1) and po-tential training instability (Shao et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2025). Furthermore, it operates primarily as a pruning mechanism, which requires the base model to already have a strong prior to implicitly infer correct paths via elimination. In scenarios where the solution space is vast or the model’s initial capability is weak ( i.e. , cold start phase), pure NSR lacks the signal necessary to establish correct reasoning patterns (Zhu et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025). Therefore, since RLVR works by amplifying the probability of the correct paths rather than generating new correct paths, its core challenge lies in improving the efficiency of sampling the correct paths (Pass@ 1) while protecting the reasoning boundary (Pass@ k). Methodologically, this requires the RL algorithms to be well-designed and effectively balance the advantage magnitude of PSR and NSR. 

# 3. Methodology 

In this section, we propose Asymmetric Group Policy Op-timization (AGPO), a NSR-dominated dynamic advantage estimation method designed to address the trade-off be-tween sampling efficiency (Pass@ 1) and reasoning capabil-ity boundary preservation (Pass@ k) in RLVR. 

3.1. Motivation: The Dilemma of Unconstrained PSR 

Unconstrained amplification of the probability of sampled correct paths, typical in standard PSR, often causes the out-put distribution of the trained model to excessively sharpen towards frequently occurring correct paths. This bias is intrinsic to the on-policy sampling mechanism of RLVR, where frequently occurring correct paths are sampled dis-proportionately often, thereby accumulating the majority of positive reinforcement. In contrast, long-tail correct paths are sampled sparsely, receiving minimal positive reinforce-ment signals, which further suppresses their generation prob-ability in the optimized policy. To formalize this, given a specific prompt x, the prior correctness probability PΘ(x)

of the base model Θ is defined as 

PΘ(x) = Ey∼πΘ(·| x)[I(y ∈ Y +)] , (5) where πΘ represents the pre-trained base policy, Y+ de-notes the set of correct response sequences, and I(·) is the indicator function. The probability PΘ(x) quantifies the base model’s intrinsic capability on a specific instance x.We analyze the optimization dynamics across three distinct regimes of PΘ(x):• PΘ(x) → 0. The prompt exceeds the model’s current capabilities. In this regime, RLVR inherently struggles to generate fundamentally new correct paths without external reinforcement signal (Yue et al., 2025). • PΘ(x) → 1. The model has already mastered the problem latent in the prompt, with the output entropy typically being low. Continued reinforcement in this regime yields marginal improvement for the reasoning capability. Worse, it aggravates the collapse of the output distribution onto these low-entropy trivial paths, which limits exploration and degrades Pass@ k.• 0 < PΘ(x) < 1. This is the fundamental of RLVR. Although the correct paths already exist in the output distribution of the base model, the model cannot confi-dently output the correct path, with the output entropy typically being high. In this regime, RLVR utilizes contrastive signals to distinguish correct paths from 4AGPO: Asymmetric Group Policy Optimization for Verifiable Reasoning and Search Ads Relevance at JD 

incorrect ones, thus converting the model’s potential capacity into better performance. Under the on-policy sampling paradigm in RLVR, the re-ward accumulation for the correct paths is proportional to 

PΘ(x). Applying static positive rewards for any sampled correct path inevitably biases optimization towards already dominant modes, accelerating output distribution collapse. This drives a dynamic reinforcement strategy, where the advantage magnitude of the positive reward should be in-versely correlated with the sampling frequency, reducing the reward of trivial paths while amplifying the encouragement for rare, correct paths.

# 5. Application: Search Ads Relevance 

5.1. Search Ads Relevance Optimization 

In large-scale e-commerce search advertising systems , such as JD Ads Search (Fan et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2021), the relevance model serves as the critical gatekeeper that fil-ters out semantically mismatched advertisements before entering the ranking stage (Lu et al., 2020). This module op-erates downstream of candidate retrieval, ensuring that only semantically aligned advertisements proceed to the rank-ing stage. Formally, given a user query q and a candidate product p, the relevance model functions as a classifier to denote the relevance level. Due to strict latency constraints in online serving, the industry standard adopts a teacher-student distillation paradigm (Liang et al., 2023; Jiao et al., 2025). In this pipeline, a powerful LLM acts as the teacher to generate high-quality relevance labels, which are then used to train a lightweight student model ( e.g. , BERT-based encoder) for online inference. Therefore, the performance of the entire relevance module is upper-bounded by the la-beling accuracy of the LLM teacher. Optimizing the teacher model typically relies on human-annotated labels as ground truth. Since human annotations provide only the relevance label without explicit CoT reasoning, employing RL train-ing is often more effective than direct SFT (Jiao et al., 2025). At this point, optimizing the teacher model follows a stan-dard RLVR paradigm, typically employing methods such as PPO and GRPO. However, its training faces the same dilemma in RLVR, where the optimization objective often leads the policy to bias towards frequently occurring (q, p )

pairs and simple semantic cases, and the model’s ability to distinguish between long-tail (q, p ) pairs and complex cases decreases (Fan et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2021).

# F. Discussion 

We summarize the underlying mechanisms revealed by our study and discuss current limitations and future directions. 1. Insights into the RLVR Mechanism. Our research, together with existing research, reveals several important insights into RLVR (Yue et al., 2025; Zhu et al., 2025). First, the core function of RLVR is not the creation of fundamentally new reasoning capabilities, but rather the efficient filtering of reasoning paths. Second, prioritizing Negative Sample Reinforcement (NSR) provides a more stable and effective optimization pathway in RLVR. Third, and this is a key point emphasized in this work, we should prioritize reinforcing rare correct paths in RLVR. 2. The Constraint of Initial Capability (Cold-Start Problem). A significant limitation of RLVR methods is their heavy reliance on the base model’s initial exploration capacity. Our experiments on the AIME-2025 benchmark with the Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct model demonstrate that when the initial Pass@ 1 accuracy is near zero, the algorithm struggles to encounter sufficient positive reward signals to initiate the self-evolution process. 3. Instability in Long-Term Training. Although AGPO demonstrates superior entropy maintenance and short-term optimization efficiency, we observe that it faces stability challenges during extended training. Specifically, when the KL divergence penalty is minimized or removed to maximize performance, extensive training over hundreds of gradient steps can lead to a noticeable decrease in test set accuracy. This suggests that the implicit regularization provided by NSR-dominated strategies may be insufficient to prevent policy drift over long horizons. Future work should investigate more robust variance-reduction techniques or adaptive constraint factors to ensure that the model maintains its performance gains throughout the entire duration of large-scale reinforcement learning. 4. Potential Synergy with Scalable RL Framework. An interesting avenue for future research involves exploring the interplay between AGPO and emerging system-level optimizations, such as DAPO (Yu et al., 2025). While DAPO primarily focuses on enhancing training throughput and update dynamics through dynamic sampling and decoupled clipping, AGPO introduces a distinct approach to credit assignment via asymmetric advantage estimation. Given that these contributions appear to address different modules of the RL pipeline, it would be valuable to investigate whether AGPO’s advantage function could be integrated into frameworks like DAPO. We postulate that exploring such a synergy might offer a promising pathway for further scaling RLVR to larger models and more complex reasoning tasks, pending empirical validation. 23
