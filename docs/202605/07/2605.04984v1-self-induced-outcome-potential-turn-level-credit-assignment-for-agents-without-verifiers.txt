## Abstract 

Long-horizon LLM agents succeed or fail through intermediate information-gathering turns, yet training signals are usually observed only at the fi-nal answer, because process-level rewards require extensive high-quality human annotation. Existing turn-level shaping methods address this su-pervision mismatch by rewarding turns that increase the likelihood of a gold answer, but they require answer supervision or stable task-specific verifiers that are often unavailable in agentic settings. Conversely, recent label-free reinforcement learning (RL) methods extract useful self-signals from output distributions, but mostly at the answer or trajectory level and therefore cannot attribute value to intermediate turns. To address these issues, we propose Self-Induced Outcome Potential (SIOP), a framework that treats semantic clusters of final answers as latent future outcome states for potential-based turn-level credit assignment. For each query, we sample multiple rollouts, cluster their final answers into semantic outcome modes, and construct a reliability-aware target distribution over these latent states. We first formulate a distributional potential that rewards turns for increas-ing posterior support for reliable future outcome states, and then instantiate it with a tractable cluster-level approximation. Terminal outcome mass is added only at the final answer turn and propagated backward through turn-level advantages. The resulting objective generalizes information-potential shaping from gold-answer supervision to settings without task-specific gold verifiers while avoiding the broadcasted rollout-level advantages used by standard GRPO. We formalize the framework, establish its reduction to supervised turn-level shaping in the gold-answer limit, and show that SIOP consistently improves over verifier-free outcome-level baselines on seven search-augmented agentic reasoning benchmarks while approaching the average performance of a gold-supervised outcome baseline. Code is available at https://github.com/dl-m9/SIOP.git .

## 1 Introduction 

Recent reasoning models such as OpenAI o1 [ 1] and DeepSeek-R1 [ 2 ] have made a broader shift visible: large language models (LLMs) are increasingly trained to solve problems through extended reasoning rather than immediate answer generation. Agentic settings extend this paradigm from internal reasoning traces to external interaction. Instead of answering from a fixed prompt, an agent reasons over a partial state, retrieves evidence, calls tools, observes returned information, and decides whether further interaction is needed.

This turns model behavior from static generation into sequential decision making over reasoning, tool use, and external observations. The shift is visible across recent systems and training recipes. Agentic retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems surveyed by Li et al. [ 3] frame retrieval as adaptive interaction rather than static preprocessing. Kimi K2 [ 4 ] uses large-scale agentic data synthesis and joint reinforcement learning through real and synthetic environments. ReSearch [ 5] and Search-R1 [ 6] train search-augmented models to interleave reasoning and retrieval. ToolRL [ 7] and SimpleTIR [ 8] study tool-use RL and tool-integrated reasoning under multi-turn interaction. These works show that agentic RL has become a practical path for improving interactive reasoning. However, many of these training recipes still rely on outcome-level feedback: the policy receives rewards mainly from final-answer correctness, task success, or terminal score after the full trajectory is complete. Such outcome-only reinforcement learning is not well suited to interactive agents. In long trajectories, a single terminal reward cannot reliably identify whether a specific thought, tool call, or observation improved the agent’s future prospects. For example, ReasonRAG [ 9] and InForage [ 10 ] report that outcome-supervised agentic RL suffers from low exploration efficiency, gradient conflict, and sparse rewards. These studies indicate that terminal outcome supervision is not enough to guide the agent’s long-horizon behavior. To address this problem, one line of work proposes turn-level reward shaping. For example, methods such as IGPO [ 11 ], ∆Belief-RL [ 12 ], TIPS [ 13 ], and InfoPO [ 14 ] measure whether an interaction turn increases the model’s support for a desired target, such as the gold answer or the correct next action. These methods show that information-potential shaping can substantially improve multi-turn optimization. However, these methods inherit a strong assumption: a rewardable target must already be available. In many agentic tasks, there is no unique gold answer or stable task-specific verifier against which a turn-level potential can be computed. Another line of work removes this requirement by extracting training signals directly from model outputs. EMPO [ 15 ] clusters semantically equivalent responses and optimizes seman-tic entropy without external supervision, while TTRL [ 16 ] performs test-time reinforcement learning by selecting the majority answer from repeated samples as a self-generated pseudo-target. Related verifier-free RL methods, including RLPR [17], NOVER [18], and DRO [19], similarly show that model-internal confidence or answer distributions can support learning beyond tasks with explicit verifiers. Yet these methods remain mostly outcome-level: they tell the model which final responses appear more reliable, but not which intermediate turns actually moved the trajectory toward those outcomes. Building on these gaps, this paper studies how to lift unlabeled outcome-level reliability signals into turn-level credit assignment. Our key abstraction is to cluster sampled final answers into semantic outcome modes , where each mode is a query-specific group of answers that express the same underlying meaning, and then treat these modes as latent future states .Even without gold answers or stable task-specific verifiers, a batch of rollouts for the same query induces a structured distribution over these latent states. Some modes are frequent, consistent, evidence-supported, or otherwise more trustworthy than others, while others reflect low-consensus noise and should be down-weighted. We then define a potential function over this self-induced latent state space and reward each turn by how much it increases the model’s support for reliable future outcome states. The resulting self-induced outcome potential generalizes information-potential shaping from gold-answer supervision to verifier-free settings without task-specific gold verifiers. Concretely, we sample multiple full rollouts for each query, cluster their final answers into semantic outcome modes, and assign soft reliability weights to these modes. This yields a distributional target over reliable latent futures rather than a hard pseudo-label. Because estimating the full mode distribution at every turn is expensive, we instantiate this principle in practice with a multi-reference cluster-level approximation that conditions each rollout on its realized semantic outcome mode while scoring a small set of reference answers rather than a single prototype. Terminal outcome mass is added only at the final answer turn and propagated backward through turn-level advantages, so different turns of the same rollout 2receive different credit. This design preserves the key intuition behind potential-based shaping, namely rewarding the intermediate interactions that move the trajectory toward reliable future states, while avoiding the assumption that a gold answer is known during training. Our contributions are summarized as follows. 1. We formulate verifier-free turn-level credit assignment as potential-based learning over self-induced semantic outcome modes, where clustered final answers serve as latent future states rather than fixed gold targets or trajectory-level pseudo-labels. 2. We define a self-induced outcome potential (SIOP) over these latent states, calibrating empirical outcome modes by reliability signals and converting prefix-to-prefix changes in cluster support into turn-level process rewards. 3. We construct turn-specific advantages for long-horizon agents, assigning credit only to trainable tokens within each turn rather than broadcasting a trajectory-level score. 4. We provide theoretical characterizations showing how the proposed reward reduces to gold-answer shaping in the supervised limit and why it measures step-wise marginal support gain rather than broadcasting a trajectory score. We also present an empirical study on seven search-QA benchmarks showing that our method outperforms outcome-only verifier-free baselines and approaches a gold-supervised outcome RL baseline.

## 3 Methodology 

SIOP starts from a credit-assignment question: without a gold answer or task-specific verifier, what future state should an intermediate turn be rewarded for moving toward? We answer this by using semantic outcome modes as the future-state space for potential-based shaping. Figure 1 shows the resulting pipeline. 4.2 Comparative Results 

Table 1 reports EM and F1 on the seven-benchmark search-QA suite. On the 4B backbone, SIOP is the best verifier-free method on average, improving over EMPO by +0.7% EM and +1.8% F1 and over TTRL by +1.6% EM and +1.8% F1. The gains are clearest on multi-hop benchmarks where intermediate retrieval matters: on HotpotQA and 2Wiki, SIOP beats both verifier-free baselines and also exceeds the gold-supervised GRPO outcome baseline on both metrics, with the 2Wiki EM gap to EMPO reaching +6.6%. SIOP also closes most of the supervision gap to the gold-supervised IGPO turn-level baseline (-0.9% EM, -0.2% F1 on average) without using any gold labels at training time. On the 8B backbone, SIOP again achieves the strongest verifier-free average, reaching 0.388 EM and 0.480 F1. It matches the gold-supervised GRPO outcome baseline in average EM and is slightly higher in average F1, while still trailing on some individual tasks such as HotpotQA EM and MuSiQue EM. Against the gold-supervised IGPO turn-level baseline (0.401 EM / 0.493 F1), SIOP trails by 1.3% EM and 1.3% F1 on average, a small remaining supervision gap given that SIOP uses no gold labels at training time. The F1 advantage over GRPO plausibly stems from SIOP’s soft semantic-cluster supervision rewarding equivalent surface forms, while the dense per-turn signal compensates for GRPO’s sparse outcome reward at scale. These results suggest that the self-induced turn-level signal transfers to the larger backbone, although per-task variation remains.

## 5 Conclusion 

In this work, we have introduced verifier-free turn-level credit assignment for agentic reinforcement learning and instantiated it with Self-Induced Outcome Potential (SIOP). SIOP treats clustered final answers as latent future states and rewards each turn by the prefix-conditioned support gain it produces for the rollout’s realized mode, weighted by a reliability-calibrated belief over modes. On seven question-answering benchmarks with Qwen3-4B and Qwen3-8B backbones, SIOP outperforms outcome-only verifier-free baselines and approaches the average performance of a gold-supervised outcome baseline without using gold labels during training.

## A Related Work 

A.1 Outcome RL and Supervised Step-Level Credit Assignment 

Modern reasoning-model training typically starts from outcome-level RL with verifiable rewards. DeepSeekMath [ 29 ] introduced GRPO as a critic-free optimizer that compares sampled responses within each prompt group, and DeepSeek-R1 [ 2] showed that outcome rewards can elicit long chain-of-thought behavior at scale. DAPO [ 30 ] and GSPO [ 31 ] further improve this group-based paradigm by addressing sampling, clipping, loss normalization, and importance-weighting stability. This line clarifies how to optimize a policy when an outcome reward is already available. It does not by itself specify what the reward target should be without a task-specific verifier, or how that target should be assigned across interaction turns. The complementary literature studies whether intermediate reasoning steps should receive their own supervision. Lightman et al. [ 32 ] showed that process supervision can outperform outcome supervision for mathematical reasoning. Zhang et al. [ 33 ] generalized process reward models through entropy-regularized MDPs, and rStar-Math [ 34 ] uses MCTS-based self-evolved process preference models. Other methods refine the temporal granularity of RL itself: VinePPO [ 35 ] uses Monte Carlo rollouts to replace learned value functions for unbiased credit assignment, SPO [ 36 ] operates at a segment level between tokens and trajectories, TEMPO [ 37 ] derives nonparametric branch-gated corrections from prefix trees, and Zeng et al. [ 38 ] estimate turn-level advantages for multi-turn tool-use agents modeled as MDPs. These approaches show that finer credit is useful, but they still assume access to supervised step labels, verifiable outcomes, or an externally supplied task reward. For multi-turn agents, information-potential methods make the dependence on a target espe-cially explicit. IGPO [ 11 ] rewards a turn by the marginal increase in the policy’s probability 15 of the gold answer. ∆Belief-RL [12] uses belief updates for long-horizon credit assignment. TIPS [ 13 ] defines an information-potential reward for search-augmented LLMs, InfoPO [ 14 ] adapts the idea to user-centric settings, and InfoReasoner [ 39 ] optimizes retrieval-augmented reasoning with a synthetic semantic information-gain reward over retrieval trajectories. These methods establish that turn-level shaping is important for long-horizon optimization, but their potentials are still anchored to a gold answer, supervised belief state, externally specified utility, or retrieval-specific information objective. SIOP changes the role of the target: it constructs a query-specific target space from sampled final answers, treats semantic outcome modes as latent future states, and computes turn rewards as potential changes over this self-induced state space. The supervised gold-answer case is therefore a limiting case rather than the starting assumption (Proposition 2). 

A.2 Label-Free Reinforcement Learning from Output Distributions 

Label-free reasoning optimization tries to replace benchmark labels or task-specific verifiers with signals induced by the model’s own samples. One family relies on agreement structure across multiple outputs. Semantic entropy by Kuhn et al. [ 20 ] and Farquhar et al. [ 40 ] esti-mates uncertainty over meaning-equivalent answer clusters, while TTRL [ 16 ] uses majority behavior from repeated sampling as a self-generated label. EMPO [ 15 ] directly optimizes semantic clusters by minimizing semantic entropy, SEED-GRPO [ 41 ] uses semantic entropy to modulate policy updates on high-uncertainty prompts, and Agarwal et al. [ 42 ] show that entropy minimization alone can be surprisingly effective. Xu et al. [ 43 ] further study entropy scheduling from exploration to exploitation under noisy supervision. These meth-ods establish that output distributions contain usable training signal, but they also expose a failure mode: high agreement is not always correctness. RESTRAIN [ 44 ] addresses this issue by penalizing spurious consensus rather than trusting majority votes unconditionally. A second family uses internal model feedback, reference likelihood, or self-evaluation as the reward source. RLPR [ 17 ] uses token probabilities assigned to reference answers, NOVER [ 18 ] studies verifier-free incentive training from standard supervised data, DRO [19 ] uses reasoning-reflection signals, VeriFree [ 45 ] optimizes reference-answer likelihood without an external verifier, and Intuitor [ 46 ] replaces external rewards with self-certainty. Related self-improvement methods obtain training signal through self-rewarding or self-play: Self-Rewarding Language Models [ 47 ] use LLM-as-a-Judge rewards, SPIN [ 48 ] frames alignment as self-play, SPIRAL [ 49 ] and SPICE [ 50 ] extend self-play to multi-turn settings, and Absolute Zero [51] and R-Zero [52] study self-evolution from zero external data. The common limitation for our setting is not the absence of useful unlabeled signals, but their granularity. TTRL [ 16 ], EMPO [ 15 ], SEED-GRPO [ 41 ], RLPR [ 17 ], NOVER [ 18 ], VeriFree [ 45 ], and Intuitor [ 46 ] primarily attach rewards to prompts, final answers, or complete trajectories. They help decide which sampled outcomes or prompts are more reliable, but they do not identify which intermediate interaction turns moved the trajectory toward those outcomes. SIOP uses the same kind of unlabeled distributional structure for a different object: semantic outcome modes define a query-specific latent future-state space, and process rewards are computed as potential changes over that space. This turns outcome-level reliability into turn-level credit assignment rather than only outcome selection. 

A.3 Open-Ended Search and Tool-Using Agents

The agentic RAG survey by Li et al. [ 3] summarizes this shift from static retrieval to iterative information seeking. ReSearch [ 5 ] and Scent of Knowledge [ 10 ] further show that search behavior itself can be optimized rather than treated as a fixed preprocessing step. Reward design has therefore become central in tool-use and search-augmented RL. ToolRL [7 ] studies how reward granularity, scale, and temporal dynamics affect tool-use learning. ## C Training Algorithm 

Algorithm 2 summarizes the full training pipeline. Each iteration processes one batch of queries in four phases. The rollout and clustering phase samples K full rollouts per query, clusters their final answers into semantic outcome modes via the NLI judge, and computes the reliability-calibrated target distribution qθ (c | q) over those modes. The scoring phase runs a single forward pass on a frozen copy of the policy to obtain log pθ (y | τ≤t) for every 

(y, τ≤t) pair, aggregates them into the multi-reference cluster support ˜pθ (c | τ≤t), and turns consecutive differences into per-turn process rewards. The advantage construction phase adds terminal outcome mass at the final answer turn, pools the augmented rewards across the query group, computes the group-relative z-score, and propagates it backward through the discounted turn-level advantage. The policy update phase broadcasts the turn-level advantage onto the trainable tokens within each turn and applies the GRPO-style clipped objective. E.1 Compute Overhead 

We profile SIOP and GRPO under identical settings (Qwen3-4B, 8 ×A800-80GB, same re-triever, same agent loop, K=4 rollouts, 300 optimizer steps) and report in Table 6 the two phases where the per-step wall-clock time differs materially. In the factorized structure of Proposition 2, the target distribution qθ (c | q) is not a direct function of cluster frequency. It combines empirical mode mass m(c | q) with a reliability correction uθ (c, q) driven by evidence support. Proposition 3 makes this behavior explicit: a lower-frequency but evidence-consistent cluster can receive larger target mass whenever its reliability advantage is large enough to overcome the log frequency gap. Thus raw majority support alone is not enough to dominate the target distribution. The same separation carries into turn-level credit assignment. The outcome potential weights prefix support by the calibrated target mass of the rollout’s semantic outcome mode, so turns that increase support for reliable modes receive stronger credit than turns that only make a high-frequency mode more predictable. Cluster-level averaging across multiple rollouts and references further prevents a single wording choice from dominating the target. 24 G Limitations and Future Work 

Limitations. The experiments focus on search-augmented QA with Qwen3-4B and Qwen3-8B, a setting where semantic answer modes and retrieved evidence provide a controlled testbed for turn-level credit assignment. This scope keeps the evaluation focused, while leaving broader agentic environments to future study. Since SIOP uses generic semantic clustering and evidence-support models, its signal is most natural when final outcomes can be grouped by meaning and retrieved evidence can support or contrast those meanings. Settings with highly ambiguous outcomes, weak evidence, or fine-grained tool states may require adapting the outcome-state construction. 

Future work. Future work could extend semantic outcome potentials in several direc-tions. First, the same abstraction may apply beyond search-QA to agentic settings where trajectories end in semantically diverse outcomes, such as tool use, web navigation, and interactive code repair. Second, outcome states need not be limited to text-answer clusters. They could be defined over structured tool traces, multi-modal observations, or hierarchical semantic modes, allowing the reward to track richer forms of future progress. Third, the potential-based view opens the door to adaptive credit granularity, where the training signal is assigned not only at the turn level, but also to retrieved evidence, tool actions, or finer action spans when they are the main source of progress. 25
