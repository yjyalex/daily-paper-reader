## ABSTRACT 

Recent RL methods have substantially improved the reasoning abilities of LLMs. Existing reward designs mainly follow two paradigms: (1) Reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR) derives outcome signals from executable checks or ground-truth answers, but provides limited guidance for intermediate reasoning behaviors. (2) Rubrics-as-reward (RaR) goes beyond final-answer checking by using natural-language rubrics to assess reasoning quality and task compliance, but often requires instance-specific rubrics and substantial design effort. To address these issues, we introduce Metacognition-as-Reward (MaR ), a metacognition-inspired RL framework that guides LLM reasoning through two general process dimensions: i) metacognitive knowledge, which identifies task-relevant information without hand-crafted instance-specific rubrics, and ii) metacognitive regulation, which plans and adjusts the reasoning process to provide reward guidance beyond final-answer outcomes. MaR scaffolds model rollouts into explicit metacognitive components and optimizes them with a trajectory-level reward over task knowledge coverage, regulation fidelity, and final-answer correctness. In this way, MaR extends reward feedback to reasoning trajectories while grounding the reward signals in general metacognitive dimensions. Experiments on 22 benchmarks show that MaR 

consistently improves model performance, achieving up to a 7.7% gain over the base model and up to an 11.0% gain over vanilla DAPO. Notably, Qwen3.5-9B+ MaR narrows the gap to frontier models, surpassing GPT-OSS-120B on overall average and outperforming stronger models on several individual benchmarks. Process-level analysis further shows substantial improvements in reasoning process quality. MaR also generalizes to out-of-domain datasets, where MaR -trained models improve over their corresponding base models on average. Our code is available at https://github.com/OpenCausaLab/MaR. 

## 1 INTRODUCTION 

Large language models (LLMs) have shown strong capabilities in complex reasoning tasks, including mathematics, science, and coding (Luo et al., 2025; Dai et al., 2026; Shojaee et al., 2025; Zhuo et al., 2025). Recent reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR) further improves LLM reasoning by optimizing models with outcome signals derived from verifiable answers or executable checks (Shao et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2025; Lambert et al., 2025; Yu et al., 2025; Zhao et al., 2025b; 2026). However, such rewards leave the intermediate reasoning behaviors leading to the final answer weakly constrained (Lightman et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2026b). Rubrics-as-reward (RaR) extends this paradigm by using natural-language rubrics to evaluate broader response qualities (Zhou et al., 2025; Gunjal et al., 2026; Wei et al., 2026; Li et al., 2026). Yet these rubrics are often tied to instance-specific criteria, requiring substantial design effort and sometimes human involvement, while providing limited guidance on what general reasoning dimensions should be encouraged across tasks. This leaves a central question:

Can we define more general reward objectives for guiding LLM reasoning? 

Metacognition provides a natural basis for defining such objectives. In cognitive psychology, metacog-nition is commonly understood as “cognition about cognition”, encompassing knowledge of one’s own cognitive processes and the ability to monitor and regulate them (Flavell, 1979). It is commonly divided into metacognitive knowledge and metacognitive regulation (Schraw & Dennison, 1994). Metacognitive knowledge concerns awareness of task-relevant, available, and missing information, while metacognitive regulation concerns planning, monitoring, and adjusting the problem-solving process (Schraw, 1998). Rather than prescribing task-specific criteria, this distinction character-izes higher-level cognitive processes in human problem solving and provides general reasoning dimensions that can be evaluated across tasks. Based on this view, we propose Metacognition-as-Reward (MaR ), a metacognition-inspired RL framework that consists of three components. (1) Metacognition-inspired rollout generation : we scaf-fold model rollouts into an explicit structure that includes metacognitive knowledge, metacognitive regulation, optional lookback, and the final answer. Metacognitive knowledge guides the model to identify task-relevant information, while metacognitive regulation guides it to plan and track the reasoning process. When uncertainty arises, the model can use lookback to recover missing informa-tion before producing the answer. (2) Metacognitive monitoring reward : we define a trajectory-level reward over this structure that evaluates knowledge coverage, regulation fidelity, and final-answer correctness. This reward goes beyond task success by evaluating task-specific reasoning content through general metacognitive dimensions. (3) Policy optimization : we optimize the policy model using this reward, encouraging responses that are both task-correct and process-aligned. We conduct comprehensive experiments on 22 benchmarks spanning science, medicine, long-context reasoning, mathematical reasoning, and logical reasoning. MaR achieves up to a 7.7% improvement over the base model on the science benchmark, and Qwen3.5-9B+ MaR surpasses GPT-OSS-120B and Deepseek-V3.2 (685B) on selected medical benchmark. Compared with vanilla DAPO, MaR obtains up to an 11.0% gain on the rubric-based benchmark, suggesting that metacognitive supervision improves reasoning quality beyond final-answer optimization. Process-level analysis on long-context tasks further shows average improvements of 17.2%, 10.7%, and 9.8% in knowledge monitoring, regulation fidelity, and final-answer correctness, respectively. MaR also generalizes to out-of-domain (OOD) mathematical and logical reasoning benchmarks, where the 9B model achieves an average score of 75.1%, outperforming the base model by 2.4%. To summarize, our main contributions are as follows: • We formulate metacognition as a general principle for reward design, highlighting knowledge monitoring and reasoning regulation as reward dimensions beyond final-answer correctness. • We propose MaR , a metacognition-inspired RL framework that scaffolds model rollouts into metacognitive knowledge, metacognitive regulation, and final answer, and optimizes them with a trajectory-level reward over knowledge coverage, regulation fidelity, and answer correctness. • We validate MaR on 22 benchmarks across five domains. Extensive results show consistent perfor-mance gains, improved metacognitive process scores, and robust OOD generalization. 

## 2 RELATED WORK 

Metacognitive reasoning in LLMs. Recent work studying metacognition in LLMs can be broadly categorized into three perspectives: understanding whether such abilities exist, eliciting them through prompting, and improving them through training. For analysis, Ma et al. (2025) study how to measure LLM meta-cognition more reliably, while Ji-An et al. (2025) examine whether LLMs exhibit metacognitive monitoring and control over their internal activations. These studies suggest that LLMs show partial metacognitive abilities, but such abilities remain unstable. At inference time, Wang & Zhao (2024) introduce a structured self-evaluation procedure inspired by human introspective reasoning. Lee et al. (2025) combine pragmatic reasoning with reflective prompting for sarcasm detection, and Ta et al. Reward design for LLM reasoning. Recent RL methods for LLM reasoning largely rely on outcome rewards derived from verifiable answers or executable checks. Such rewards are effective in tasks where correctness can be automatically judged (Team et al., 2025; Zeng et al., 2025; Hu et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025; Zhao et al., 2025a; Wang et al., 2026b). Despite their effectiveness, verifiable outcome rewards provide limited guidance on intermediate reasoning behaviors and are less applicable to open-generation tasks, where correctness cannot always be reduced to a single verifiable answer. RaR extend reward design beyond verifiable outcomes by using natural-language criteria to evaluate broader response qualities. They can capture dimensions such as helpfulness, completeness, reasoning quality, and task-specific constraints (Liu et al., 2025b; Xie et al., 2025; Shen et al., 2026; Ye et al., 2025; Masters & Albrecht, 2026). However, these objectives are often derived from instance-specific criteria, and thus provide limited guidance on what general reward objectives should guide LLM reasoning across tasks. 

## 3 METHOD 

We instantiate this metacognitive view as a structured RL framework, as shown in Figure 1. The core idea is to make intermediate reasoning behaviors observable and rewardable during policy optimiza-tion. Specifically, each response is organized into metacognitive knowledge (MK), metacognitive regulation (MR), optional LOOKBACK , and a final answer. This structure reveals which task-relevant information the model identifies, how it plans and monitors its reasoning, and whether it recovers missing information under uncertainty. Metacognitive knowledge. The MK part asks the policy to explicitly identify the task-relevant knowledge needed for solving the problem, in-cluding key facts, definitions, constraints, rules, procedures, exceptions, etc. To make knowledge coverage easier to inspect and evaluate, we re-quire MK to be written as atomic indexed items, e.g., [mk1] , [mk2] , and so on. This design makes knowledge monitoring explicit before the model proceeds to reasoning and answer generation. 

Metacognitive regulation. After identifying the relevant knowledge, the policy specifies a [plan] 

that describes how it will solve the problem. The plan is written as short, executable steps, so that the subsequent reasoning has an explicit procedure to follow. This structure exposes the model’s regulation process and supports subsequent evaluation of regulation fidelity. 

LOOKBACK . This is a recovery step triggered when the policy becomes blocked, uncertain, or detects that some necessary knowledge was omitted from MK. It first states the missing information in 

[Seeking] , and then records the recalled or derived knowledge in [Found] . This step makes knowledge recovery explicit during reasoning, and allows the grader to assess whether the policy can correct an incomplete initial knowledge state. 3.3 METACOGNITIVE MONITORING REWARD 

Inspired by the monitoring-control framework (Nelson, 1990), we design a metacognitive monitoring reward ( MMR ) to evaluate whether πθ can: i) accurately identify task-relevant knowledge, ii) appro-priately regulate its subsequent reasoning, and iii) produce a correct final answer. For each rollout, 

MMR is computed from fixed dataset annotations and grader-estimated rollout signals. The fixed annotations provide the gold knowledge units for each instance, while an LLM grader πG estimates the rollout-dependent quantities required by the component rewards defined below. The full grader prompt and scoring protocol are provided in Figure 10. 4Knowledge monitoring reward ( KMR ). We evaluate whether the policy πθ identifies the task-relevant knowledge required for solving the problem from two aspects: (1) the initial coverage of gold knowledge units in MK, and (2) the recovery of initially missed knowledge through LOOKBACK .Let n denote the total number of gold knowledge units annotated for the instance. Given the policy rollout, the grader πG estimates k, the number of gold knowledge units correctly identified in the initial MK. It also estimates r, the number of gold knowledge units that are missed in the initial MK but successfully recovered through LOOKBACK . If LOOKBACK is not used, we set r = 0 . We define the knowledge monitoring reward as: 

KMR = k+rn . (3) Thus, KMR measures the proportion of required knowledge units covered either by the initial MK or by subsequent LOOKBACK .

Regulation monitoring reward ( RMR ). Beyond knowledge identification, we also monitor whether 

πθ follows its self-generated MR during subsequent reasoning. Concretely, we define the regulation monitoring reward as: 

RMR = a(1 − λs ), (4) where a ∈ [0 , 1] is estimated by the grader πG and measures the consistency between the policy’s actual reasoning process and its stated MR, s ∈ { 0, 1} is a grader-estimated shortcut indicator, and 

λ ∈ [0 , 1] is a shortcut penalty coefficient. A shortcut occurs when the policy reaches the final answer while bypassing its own declared regulatory process. Thus, a rewards faithful plan execution, while the multiplicative penalty discourages such shortcut trajectories. Since intermediate reasoning traces are not always faithfully translated into final answers (Lanham et al., 2023; Paul et al., 2024), this process-level consistency serves as a useful supervision signal beyond answer correctness alone. 

Correctness reward ( CR ). We define a task-level reward to evaluate whether the final answer ˆy

matches the ground truth y:

CR (ˆ y, y ) = I ˆy = y ∨ ˆy ≃ y, (5) where I(·) is the indicator function, and ≃ denotes semantic equivalence judged by the grader πG.Thus, CR = 1 if the answer either exactly matches the ground truth or is judged semantically equivalent to it, and CR = 0 otherwise. 

Final reward. We define the final reward as the sum of the three components: 

R = KMR + RMR + CR . (6) Here, KMR , RMR , and CR respectively capture πθ ’s MK, MR, and final answer correctness, with each term bounded in [0 , 1] .3.4 POLICY OPTIMIZATION

Effect of grader choice. Figure 7 analyzes the effect of grader choice. We compare three graders, including Qwen3.5-9B, GPT-OSS-120B, and Qwen3.5-397B, and report the average accuracy on science and medical benchmarks. The dashed lines denote the corresponding Qwen3.5-9B baseline without MaR . We find that: 

Stronger graders provide more effective metacognitive supervision. Among the three graders, Qwen3.5-397B achieves the best performance in both domains, reaching 67.6% on science and 67.7% on medical. Compared with the Qwen3.5-9B baseline, it improves the average accuracy by 3.8% and 1.7%, respectively. This suggests that stronger graders provide higher-quality process supervision, leading to more effective policy optimization. 

MaR remains effective even with a lightweight self-grader. Using Qwen3.5-9B as the grader still improves over the Qwen3.5-9B baseline, increasing science performance from 63.8% to 65.9% and medical performance from 66.0% to 66.6%. This indicates that MaR does not strictly rely on a very 9large external grader. Even when the policy is supervised by a grader of the same scale, metacognitive supervision still brings consistent gains, making MaR more practical for resource-constrained settings. 

Effect of reward component. Figure 8 studies the effect of each reward component by removing KMR, RMR, and CR from MaR respectively. We report the average accuracy on science and medical benchmarks. We observe that: 

All reward components contribute to MaR , with process-level supervision playing a particularly important role. Removing any component leads to performance degradation on both science and medical benchmarks, showing that KMR, RMR, and CR are all useful for effective optimization. On science benchmarks, removing CR, KMR, and RMR decreases the average accuracy from 67.6% to 66.5%, 66.0%, and 65.6%, respectively. On medical benchmarks, the corresponding scores drop from 67.7% to 66.8%, 66.2%, and 66.7%. Notably, removing KMR or RMR generally causes larger degradation than removing CR, suggesting that process-level supervision through knowledge monitoring and regulation fidelity plays a central role in the effectiveness of MaR .

## 5 CONCLUSION AND LIMITATION 

Conclusion. In this work, we introduce MaR , a metacognition-inspired RL framework that uses metacognitive knowledge and regulation as general reward dimensions for LLM reasoning. MaR 

structures rollouts into explicit metacognitive components and optimizes them with a trajectory-level reward over knowledge coverage, regulation fidelity, and answer correctness. By grounding reward design in general metacognitive dimensions, MaR extends reward feedback from final-answer outcomes to reasoning trajectories, while avoiding the need for hand-crafted instance-specific rubrics. Experiments on 22 benchmarks show consistent gains in task performance, reasoning process quality, and out-of-domain generalization. 

Limitation. Despite our best efforts to formulate MaR as a general metacognitive reward framework and evaluate it across a broad set of reasoning benchmarks, our work still has several limitations. First, although our evaluation covers multiple reasoning domains, the training data are mainly derived from science and medical tasks, and broader training domains may further improve generality. Second, our experiments focus on text-only reasoning, leaving multilingual, multimodal, and interactive agent settings for future exploration.

## C DETAILED TRAINING SETTINGS 

For RL training, we set both the maximum prompt length and response length to 4096 tokens, with an overlong buffer of 256 tokens enabled, no KL reward/loss. The lower and upper clip ratios are to 0.2 and 0.28, respectively. During rollout generation, we use temperature = 1 .0, top-p = 1 .0, and top-k = −1. The actor learning rate is 2 × 10 −6 with 10 warmup steps. We use a training batch size of 224, generate N = 8 rollouts per prompt, set the mini-batch size to 112, and train for 270 steps. All experiments are conducted with NVIDIA H200 GPUs, each with 141GB of memory.

## E BROADER IMPACTS 

This work may have positive societal impacts by improving the reliability of LLM reasoning through process-oriented reward design. By encouraging models to identify task-relevant knowledge and follow explicit reasoning plans, MaR may support more interpretable and controllable reasoning behavior. At the same time, stronger reasoning models and LLM-based reward systems may still produce incorrect outputs. Therefore, MaR should be viewed as a research framework rather than a deployment-ready safety mechanism, and practical use should involve careful validation. 25
