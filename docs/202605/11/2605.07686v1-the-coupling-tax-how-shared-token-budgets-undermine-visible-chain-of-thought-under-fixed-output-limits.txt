## Abstract 

Chain-of-thought reasoning is often treated as a monotone way to improve language-model accuracy by letting a model think longer. We identify a countervailing effect, the coupling tax : when reasoning traces and final answers share one output-token budget, long traces can crowd out the answer they are meant to support. Across GSM8K, MATH-500, and five BIG-Bench Hard tasks with Qwen3 models at three scales, non-thinking mode matches or outperforms thinking mode on GSM8K and MATH-500 at every budget up to 2048 tokens, while harder tasks shift the crossover to larger budgets. We derive a truncation-waste decomposition, Acc think (b) = 

αcFL(b) + αt(1 − FL(b)) , that predicts this crossover from chain-length and accuracy statistics and explains inverse scaling within the Qwen family. A DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B replication shows the same pattern under a different thinking interface. As a mitigation, split-budget generation 

decouples reasoning and answer budgets; on full MATH-500, IRIS reaches 74.0% accuracy, a strengthened extraction variant reaches 78.8%, and a fixed non-oracle SC+IRIS gate reaches 83.6%. The results show that test-time reasoning should be evaluated as a budget-allocation problem, not only as a question of whether longer traces are available. 

## 1 Introduction 

Chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning [Wei et al., 2022] has become the dominant paradigm for improving LLM performance on complex tasks, operationalized in systems like OpenAI o1 [OpenAI, 2024], QwQ [Qwen Team, 2024], and DeepSeek-R1 [Guo et al., 2025]. The implicit promise is simple: more thinking tokens, more accuracy. We study this promise under fixed output-token budgets, the serving knob that directly controls latency, cost, and throughput. The question is not whether cutting off a chain mid-sentence is harmful. It is whether a system should invoke visible CoT at all when the answer must fit inside a fixed cap and the model also exposes a native direct-answer mode. This choice must be made before the full chain length is known, and common deployment caps can fall below a task- and model-specific crossover. The promise breaks down under two measurable conditions: (i) reasoning chains typically exceed the budget, causing truncation waste; and (ii) the model has an architecturally distinct non-thinking mode. Because autoregressive decoding spends a forward step per generated token, simply raising the cap until every chain completes can multiply latency and throughput cost; the relevant question is therefore the accuracy–token tradeoff at the cap actually served.

The coupling tax and split-budget generation. The root cause is architectural: reasoning and the final answer are coupled in a single output stream. We call this the 

coupling tax . The fix follows naturally: decouple via split-budget generation . Generate a reasoning trace (budget Br ), then feed it—even if truncated—to a separate non-thinking pass (budget Ba).

We provide a claim-to-measurement account of when visible CoT should be disabled under a fixed cap and how much budget is needed before it becomes competitive: 1. Diagnostic Crossover Estimator (§4): Acc = αcFL + αt(1 −FL) turns measured chain-length and truncated-chain accuracy into a crossover estimate—absent from concurrent work. 2. ## 2 Background and Related Work 

Chain-of-Thought Reasoning. Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting [Wei et al., 2022] and its variants [Wang et al., 2023, Yao et al., 2023] have become the de facto approach for improving LLM reasoning. Recent work has moved beyond prompting to training models with explicit reasoning traces, yielding “thinking” LLMs such as DeepSeek-R1 [Guo et al., 2025], QwQ [Qwen Team, 2024], and Qwen3 [Yang et al., 2025]. These models produce a <think>...</think> block before the final answer. While effective at unconstrained budgets, we show that this thinking overhead imposes a severe penalty under realistic token constraints. 

Test-Time Compute Scaling. The scaling of inference-time computation has emerged as a complementary axis to model scaling [Snell et al., 2024]. Best-of-N sampling with learned verifiers [Cobbe et al., 2021], process reward models [Lightman et al., 2024, Wang et al., 2024], and iterative refinement [Madaan et al., 2023] all trade additional tokens for improved accuracy. Muennighoff et al. [2025] demonstrate that “budget forcing”—controlling the length of reasoning chains—can improve efficiency, but operate within the thinking paradigm. Our findings challenge a shared assumption of these approaches: that more reasoning tokens always help . At constrained budgets, the overhead of structured reasoning can actively hurt 

performance. 

Adaptive Computation and Early Exit. Adaptive computation [Graves, 2016, De-hghani et al., 2019] allows models to allocate variable compute per input. In the LLM setting, speculative decoding [Leviathan et al., 2023, Chen et al., 2023a] and early exit mechanisms [Schuster et al., 2022, Bae et al., 2023] reduce inference cost by terminating gen-eration early. Our work identifies a free confidence signal—natural stopping—that requires no additional training or auxiliary models, and differs from these approaches in routing 

between thinking and non-thinking modes rather than adjusting depth within a single forward pass. More broadly, LLM cascades [Chen et al., 2023b] route queries between models of different sizes to balance cost and quality. Mrsd applies the cascade principle within a single model by routing between reasoning modes rather than model sizes—a distinction enabled by the hybrid think /nothink architecture of models like Qwen3. The connection to cascade classifiers [Viola and Jones, 2001] is deliberate: we formalize Mrsd as a two-stage cascade with interpolation-dominance guarantees (Appendix). 

Budget-Aware Reasoning and Concurrent Work. A growing body of concurrent work addresses reasoning efficiency. AdaptThink [Zhang et al., 2025] teaches models to decide when to think; SelfBudgeter [Li et al., 2025] learns per-instance budgets. Most closely related, Ma et al. [2025] concurrently demonstrate that reasoning models can be effective without thinking, showing nothink outperforms thinking under matched token budgets—an empirical finding our work shares. Elastic Reasoning [Xu et al., 2025] concurrently proposes split-budget generation via a training-based approach (budget-constrained RL rollout). Our work differs from both in three ways: (i) we provide a diagnostic decomposition (Acc = αcFL + αt(1 −FL))that estimates the crossover budget from measured chain-length statistics—neither Ma et al. 3Table 1: The Thinking Tax: Non-thinking outperforms thinking at all matched budgets. ## 3 The Thinking Tax: An Empirical Study 

Before introducing our method, we present a systematic empirical study. All experiments use 

Qwen3-8B Yang et al. [2025] with native thinking mode, with Qwen3.5-9B and Qwen3.5-27B for cross-scale validation, evaluated on full GSM8K ( n= 1,319 ) unless noted. We control test-time compute via max_new_tokens , capping total output tokens; both modes operate under the same budget b. Non-thinking uses enable_thinking=False . Greedy decoding (τ = 0) is used throughout to isolate truncation from sampling variance. When thinking mode exhausts its budget, a multi-level heuristic extracts a numerical answer from the truncated output ( \boxed{} , final-answer markers, last-number fallback; see Appendix A)—this is 

favorable to thinking mode. 

3.1 Finding 1: Non-Thinking Beats Thinking at All Matched Budgets 

On the complete GSM8K test set, nothink@256 achieves 87.5% vs. only 18.0% for 

think@256 —a 69.5 pp gap. At 27B, the gap is even starker: nothink@512 = 95.5% vs. think@512 = 18.4% (+77.1 pp; Table ?? ). Non-thinking saturates at moderate budgets; thinking reaches parity only at b=2048 , using 744 vs. 153 average tokens. 

Failure-mode decomposition. The tax is mostly truncation, not weak reasoning con-ditional on finishing. At b=256 , 98.6% of thinking responses hit the cap, while the rare natural-stop samples are all correct. At b= 512 , natural-stop rate rises to 37.4% and those samples reach 99.0% accuracy, but the still-truncated majority reaches only 31.8%. 

3.2 Finding 2: Natural Stop Is a Free Confidence Oracle 

At b= 512 , 37.4% of samples terminate naturally (accuracy 99.0% ) while truncated samples reach only 31.8%, a 67.2 pp gap. This signal is binary, endogenous, and free—no logit access or calibration needed. DeepSeek-R1-8B shows similar natural-stop behavior. Token utilization analysis (Appendix F) reveals substantial waste at higher budgets, moti-vating adaptive allocation. 

3.3 Finding 4: The Thinking Tax Scales with Model Size 

At b= 512 , thinking-mode accuracy collapses with model size: 56.9% (8B), 15.5% (9B), 18.4% (27B) while non-thinking remains high: 93.1%, 93.2%, 95.5%. At b= 512 , the tax is 36.2 pp 4(8B), 77.7 pp (9B), 77.1 pp (27B)—a 2.1× observed ratio at this budget. The root cause is longer chains: the 27B natural-stop rate is just 0.7% at b= 512 (vs. 37.4% for 8B). At 

b= 1024 , 9B nothink remains 94.6% [93.3, 95.7] while think@1024 is 41.8%; even at b= 2048 , 9B thinking reaches only 66.8%. 

3.4 Finding 5: The Tax Generalizes Beyond Mathematics 

The same pattern extends to non-mathematical reasoning: on the five-task BBH suite, non-thinking has a +33.3 pp advantage at b= 256 , and the crossover occurs between 1024 and 2048 tokens. Per-task crossovers vary from ∼512 ( boolean_expressions ) to >2048 (causal_judgement ), consistent with the crossover diagnostic (Proposition 3). Full per-task results in Appendix U. 

Summary. Our findings show: (1) non-thinking dominates at all matched budgets ≤512; (2) natural stop predicts 99.0% accuracy; (3) the tax worsens with model scale; (4) the tax generalizes to non-mathematical reasoning (BBH); and (5) 31.8% of problems are beyond reach at any budget (Appendix G). These findings motivate Mrsd (§5). 

## 4 Diagnostic Analysis 

We organize the empirical findings into a quantitative diagnostic decomposition that expresses thinking-mode accuracy, characterizes the crossover budget, and accounts for inverse scaling through chain-length statistics. The goal is not to claim a new probability law; the value is that the identity exposes which measurable quantities determine the mode choice under a fixed cap and yields testable crossover predictions. We validate this use in Appendix R: 20 random 50-sample GSM8K pilots predict budget-sweep accuracy with 3.48 pp average RMSE, and a held-out BBH check has 0.8 pp error at b=2048 .

4.5 The Coupling Tax and Split-Budget Generation 

The coupling constraint |Z| + |A| ≤ b forces reasoning and answering to compete for the same budget. When αc ≈ ¯αnt , truncation waste accounts for essentially the entire tax. The decomposition identifies two levers: increase FL(b) (larger budgets) or increase αt by feeding truncated traces to a separate answer pass. This motivates split-budget generation : allocate separate budgets br (reasoning) and ba (answering) with no coupling. 

Definition 3 (Coupled vs. split generation) . Coupled: |Z| + |A| ≤ b. Split: reasoning br ,answering ba, separately budgeted.

## 5 An Exploratory Mitigation: Mrsd 

The decomposition in §4 reveals a structural cause for the thinking tax: reasoning and answering share a single output channel . This section develops split-budget generation 

and instantiates it as Mrsd (Multi-Round Self-Distillation), a training-free inference framework. Mrsd is deliberately simple: it demonstrates that the coupling tax is recoverable through inference-time restructuring alone, establishing a lower bound on what more complex approaches can achieve. 

5.1 The Coupling Problem 

In standard thinking mode, reasoning tokens Z and answer tokens A share one budget: 

|Z| + |A| ≤ b. (5) When L(q) > b , generation is truncated mid-chain before any answer tokens are produced, creating a zero-sum coupling. At b=256 on GSM8K, FL(256) < 0.02 —over 98% of chains are truncated. 65.2 Split-Budget Generation Key insight. Decouple reasoning from answering by allocating separate budgets: a reason-ing pass (budget Br , possibly truncated) and an answer pass (budget Ba) that reads the trace as context. The total cost is Br + Ba, but truncation no longer prevents a well-formed answer. A truncated chain Z1: Br is not worthless—it contains partial decomposition and intermediate computations. The accuracy gain (Proposition 6) is: 

∆ = (1 − FL(Br )) · (αextract (Br , B a) − αt(Br )) ≥ 0. (4) 

5.3 Mrsd: Multi-Round Self-Distillation 

Mrsd has three stages. First, a non-thinking probe with budget B1 resolves easy queries by natural stopping and returns immediately. Second, unresolved queries receive a thinking pass with budget Br ; if the chain completes, its answer is used directly, and if it truncates, the partial trace is passed to a separate non-thinking answer extractor with budget Ba.Third, up to K refinement rounds reuse the previous answer as a hint, stop on consecutive agreement, and otherwise fall back to a majority vote over extracted answers. This is the same algorithm evaluated below; full pseudocode is deferred to Appendix X. 

Stage 0: Difficulty triage. A non-thinking probe with budget B1 resolves easy queries via natural stopping ( ∼88.8% on GSM8K at B1=256 , accuracy 94.4%). 

Stage 1: Think-then-extract. For the ∼11.2% of hard queries, split-budget generation produces a reasoning trace (budget Br ), then feeds it to a non-thinking answer pass (budget 

Ba), eliminating the zero-sum coupling. 

Stages k ≥ 2: Iterative refinement. Subsequent rounds provide the previous answer as a hint; the model can verify, correct, or re-derive. Convergence (consecutive agreement) stops iteration; 98% of escalated queries converge within K=3 rounds on GSM8K. Token efficiency analysis, design choices, and the interpolation-dominance theorem (Theo-rem 1) are deferred to Appendix X.

6.4 Cross-Scale Validation (27B) 

At 27B, insufficient budgets still favor nothink because nearly all chains truncate, but adequate split budgets make decoupled extraction favorable. The 27B IRIS–TOWN gap averages +34.5 pp over three seeds, and a GSM8K check at b= 4096 still shows a residual coupling tax, consistent with the crossover exceeding 4096 tokens for larger models. Cross-hardware replication shows small numeric variation from bfloat16 nondeterminism, but the qualitative conclusions are invariant. 

## 7 Discussion and Conclusion 

Why the tax matters. The mechanism is truncation, but the magnitude is not a simple verbosity effect: at b= 256 , 98.6% of thinking responses produce no parseable answer, yielding a 69.5 pp loss. The 2.1× amplification from 8B to 9B/27B at b= 512 and the 99.0% natural-stop oracle further show that chain length, budget, and mode interact. 

Mitigation and scope. On GSM8K, full-set Mrsd improves over nothink@256 by +3.41 pp and over coupled Town by +4.93 pp; on matched MATH-500 samples, decoupling budgets recovers +25.4 pp. Overall, shared reasoning-and-answer budgets impose a task- and model-specific penalty below the crossover, while natural stop provides a free 99.0% PPV routing signal. Mrsd reaches 90.9% on full GSM8K and 74.0% on full MATH-500; IRIS+ and a fixed SC@5 gate reach 78.8% and 83.6% . The scope is fixed-budget structured reasoning; it can reduce wasted reasoning compute, but each task/model crossover should be measured before disabling CoT. 9References

A.1 Hardware and Software

Cross-hardware and seed disclosure. MATH-500 nothink@2048, think@2048, think@4096, self-consistency, IRIS, and TOWN results are from H800 hardware (seed = 42 ). MATH-500 nothink@512 and nothink@1024 are A100 reference rows (seed = 42 ); think@1024 is an A100 diagnostic row only (seed = 11 ). Cross-hardware replication shows ∼5 pp variation under bfloat16 nondeterminism. Single-mode baselines (nothink vs. think) are independent evaluations, not paired comparisons; all IRIS vs. TOWN comparisons (the primary mecha-nism test) are on the same H800 hardware and seed. Greedy decoding ( τ = 0) is used for all main experiments; no sampling or nucleus filtering is applied. A sampling robustness pilot (τ =0 .7) confirms the tax persists under stochastic decoding (Appendix D). 11 Answer extraction. Two extraction pipelines are used depending on the experiment script: (1) run_experiment.py applies a 32-token projection pass when thinking mode exhausts its budget, giving thinking mode a free continuation bonus; (2) run_nothink_baseline.py 

uses multi-level heuristic extraction ( \boxed{} , final-answer markers, last-number fallback) without projection. The main-text 8B GSM8K results ( n= 1,319 , seed = 42 ) use pipeline (2); all conclusions hold under both pipelines since the heuristic extractor is strictly less favorable to thinking mode than projection. 

A.2 Budget Control 

Token budgets are controlled via the max_new_tokens parameter in HuggingFace 

model.generate() , which caps the total number of newly generated tokens. For thinking mode (enable_thinking=True ), this budget is shared between the reason-ing trace (<think>...</think> ) and the final answer. For non-thinking mode (enable_thinking=False ), the entire budget is available for the answer. This ensures a fair comparison: both modes operate under the same total output-token budget. 

A.3 Answer Extraction 

We use a multi-level extraction pipeline: 1. Search for \boxed{...} patterns 2. Search for #### markers (GSM8K convention) 3. Search for “Final answer:” patterns 4. Fall back to the last number in the output When thinking mode exhausts its budget without producing a final answer, two extraction strategies are used depending on the experiment script (see “Answer extraction” para-graph above). The main 8B GSM8K results ( n= 1,319 , seed = 42 ) use heuristic extraction (pipeline 2, no projection). The heuristic extractor recovers answers from truncated outputs via last-number fallback; without any extraction support, think@512 accuracy drops to <6%, confirming that truncated chains rarely produce parseable answers. Because this recovery is favorable to thinking mode, reported thinking-mode accuracies are optimistic and the measured tax is conservative. 

Extraction heuristic analysis. Both extraction pipelines (heuristic and projection) give thinking mode answer-recovery support that non-thinking mode does not need, ensuring a fair-or-better comparison for thinking mode. To quantify: without any extraction heuristic (no last-number fallback, no projection), think@512 on GSM8K drops to <6%, since the vast majority of truncated chains produce no parseable answer. With heuristic extraction, accuracy recovers to 56.9%. Critically, this reduces the measured thinking tax: the “true” tax (without answer recovery heuristics) would be even larger, making our reported tax a conservative estimate for this evaluation protocol. 

A.4 Dataset Details GSM8K. We use the standard test split ( n= 1,319 problems) [Cobbe et al., 2021]. All main-text analyses use the complete set ( n= 1,319 ). A 200-sample subset (seed 42) used during development is reported in Table 4 for reference. 

MATH-500. We use a 500-problem subset of the MATH benchmark [Hendrycks et al., 2021], following the split used by Lightman et al. [2024]. We evaluate on the full 500-problem set ( n= 500 ) for all headline MATH-500 configurations; the nothink@512 row in Table 3 is explicitly marked as a pilot baseline. Early experiments used 40-sample subsets across data seeds {42, 404, 505, 606, 707} for rapid prototyping; all final results use the complete set. 

BIG-Bench Hard (BBH). To validate the thinking tax beyond mathe-matical reasoning, we evaluate on five subtasks from BIG-Bench Hard [Suz-gun et al., 2023]: boolean_expressions (n= 250 ), causal_judgement (n= 187 ), 

date_understanding (n= 250 ), logical_deduction_five_objects (n= 250 ), and 12 tracking_shuffled_objects_three_objects (n= 250 ), totaling n= 1,187 samples. Answer extraction uses exact match against the gold-standard option label.

Definition 5 (Truncation rate) . Given token budget b, the truncation rate is ρ(b) ≜

Assumption 2 (Binary outcome structure (restated)) . When the chain completes naturally (L ≤ b), the model produces a well-formed answer with accuracy αc(b) ≜ Pr (correct | L ≤ b) ∈

At b=512 on GSM8K ( n= 1,319 ), αc = 99 .0% among natural-stop samples vs. αt = 31 .8%

In practice, αt varies with b: at b=128 , nearly all truncated samples lack any answer ( αt ≈ 0); at b= 512 , answer-extraction heuristics and projection passes recover partial answers ( αt ≈ 31 .8% ). Critically, the qualitative findings are unchanged: both hardware configurations show (1) monotonic IRIS accuracy improvement with Bthink , (2) IRIS outperforming TOWN, and (3) rising natural stop rates. Nothink@1024 cross-run variance. W.4 Modal Specialization: Why Mode Matters More Than Budget

Our data falsify this: on Stage 3 samples (truncated thinking), IRIS uses 108% of TOWN’s tokens yet achieves +31.2 pp higher accuracy (68.8% vs. 37.5%). W.5 The Coupling Constraint and Decoupling Advantage 

Building on the modal specialization insight, we now formalize the structural advantage of decoupled generation. 

Setup. A reasoning model receives question q and generates output under a total token budget b. Let L(q) denote the natural chain length —the reasoning tokens if unconstrained. In coupled mode (standard CoT), the model produces a single stream S = Z ⊕ A where Z

is the reasoning trace and A is the answer, subject to |Z| + |A| ≤ b. Because generation is autoregressive and Z precedes A, when L(q) > b − | A|min the answer is truncated or absent entirely. In decoupled mode , reasoning and answering receive separate budgets (br , b a) with br +ba ≤

b: first generate reasoning Z′ = Zbr (truncated at br if L > b r ), then generate answer A′

from Z′ with budget ba.

Accuracy functions. Define three accuracy regimes: 

• αc: accuracy when reasoning completes (L ≤ br ); the model reaches its natural conclusion. 

• αt: accuracy in coupled mode when reasoning is truncated (L > b − | A|min ); the answer is squeezed out of the output stream. 

• αe(br , b a): accuracy in decoupled mode when reasoning is truncated ( L > b r ); a dedicated extraction pass with budget ba reads the partial trace. 

Proposition 10 (Coupling Impossibility) . Let b > 0 be a token budget. Suppose: (i) Pr( L > b − | A|min ) > 0 (some chains are truncated under coupled generation), (ii) αe(β, |A|min ) > α t where β = b − | A|min (dedicated extraction with the same effective reasoning budget outperforms truncation residual), and (iii) completed-chain accuracy under the decoupled answer pass is no worse than under the coupled stream. Then there exists a decoupled strategy using the same total budget b that strictly dominates 

coupled generation: 

Acc d(b) > Acc c(b). (11) 

Proof. The coupled accuracy decomposes as: 

Acc c(b) = FL(β) · αc + (1 − FL(β)) · αt, β ≜ b − | A|min . (12) Consider the decoupled strategy with br = β, ba = |A|min (identical effective reasoning budget as coupled mode, with the minimum answer allocation separated out). Its accuracy is: 

Acc d(β, |A|min ) = FL(β) · αc + (1 − FL(β)) · αe(β, |A|min ). (13) The first terms are identical up to condition (iii). The gap from truncated samples is: 

∆ = (1 − FL(β)) · (αe(β, |A|min ) − αt

). (14) By condition (i), 1 − FL(β) > 0. By condition (ii), αe − αt > 0. Therefore ∆ > 0, proving strict dominance. 32 Interpretation. This establishes that the coupling tax is not merely an empirical artifact but a structural consequence of serializing reasoning and answering in a single stream.

W.9 Two-Source Tax Decomposition 

The coupling tax admits a decomposition into two terms with distinct scaling behaviors. 

Proposition 13 (Two-Source Decomposition) . Assume αc(b) ≥ αt(b) (completed chains are more accurate than truncated ones). The coupling tax decomposes as Tax (b) = TL (b)+ RR (b),where: 

TL( b) ≜ (1 − FL(b)) · (αc(b) − αt(b)) ≥ 0, (20) 

RR( b) ≜ Acc nt (b) − αc(b). (21) 

TL is the truncation loss : accuracy destroyed by incomplete chains. RR is the marginal reason-ing gap : the difference between population-level non-thinking accuracy and the completed-chain thinking accuracy. As b → ∞ , FL(b) → 1 and TL → 0, so lim b→∞ Tax (b) = RR (∞)—a residual gap that persists if αc(∞) < Acc nt (∞).Proof. Remark 3 (Interpretation caveat) . RR compares Acc nt (measured over the full question distribution) with αc (measured on the subset {L ≤ b} whose chains complete—typically easier questions). Remark 4 (Regime classification) . The decomposition identifies two regimes: truncation-dominated (RR < 0, i.e., completed thinking outperforms population nothink—the case for 8B on GSM8K), and reasoning-neutral (RR ≈ 0, completed thinking and population nothink achieve similar accuracy—the case for 27B at b= 4096 ). If RR > 0 at any finite budget, a residual tax persists even as truncation vanishes; however, this has not been observed in our experiments. The crossover budget b∗ satisfies TL( b∗) = −RR( b∗).

Remark 5 (The “stuck reasoning” principle) . Proposition 14 formalizes an intuition: the longer a model has been reasoning without finishing, the more it benefits from switching to answer-extraction mode . Under DFR chain-length distributions, a chain that has consumed 

τ tokens without completing is less likely to complete in the next ba tokens than a chain at τ ′ < τ would be. This means the think-mode continuation becomes increasingly futile, while the truncated trace grows richer—making nothink extraction increasingly valuable. The DFR property is empirically plausible for reasoning chains: models that are “stuck” in elaborate reasoning paths tend to remain stuck, exhibiting worse-than-memoryless behavior. Formal validation of the DFR assumption (empirical hazard estimation with confidence bands) is an important direction for future work.
