Abstract 

Large reasoning models (LRMs) achieve strong performance via extended chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning, yet suffer from excessive token consumption and high inference latency. Existing reinforcement learning (RL) approaches for CoT compression rely on uniform, static length penalties that neglect model capability dynamics and problem-level difficulty variation. We propose ExpThink , an RL framework that addresses both dimensions through two complementary mecha-nisms. First, experience-guided reward shaping tracks the shortest correct solution found so far for each problem and applies a three-tier reward: full credit for con-cise correct responses, discounted credit for verbose correct ones, and zero for incorrect ones. The threshold tightens automatically with model improvement, forming a self-evolving curriculum that requires no manual scheduling. Sec-ond, difficulty-adaptive advantage replaces standard deviation normalization with correct-count normalization, yielding monotonically difficulty-scaled gradients that amplify learning on hard problems to preserve accuracy while suppressing gradients on easy ones to encourage brevity. Together, these mechanisms enforce an accuracy-first, compression-second training objective. Experiments on multiple mathematical reasoning benchmarks demonstrate that ExpThink reduces average response length by up to 77% while simultaneously improving accuracy, achiev-ing up to 3× higher accuracy-efficiency ratio (accuracy divided by average token count) than the vanilla baseline and outperforming existing RL-based compression methods on both metrics.

Inference-time strategies such as adaptive routing and early exits reduce output length but leave the model’s inherent verbosity unchanged [ 49 , 18 , 51 ]. Supervised fine-tuning on distilled concise traces is prone to distribution shift and depends heavily on demonstration quality [ 44 , 52 ]. Reinforcement learning (RL) offers a more principled path by jointly rewarding correctness and brevity, and methods such as L1, ThinkPrune, LC-R1, and Laser have demonstrated substantial token reduction with minimal accuracy loss [ 1, 8, 17 , 28 ]. Yet all existing RL approaches share a structural limitation (Figure 1): each epoch is treated as an independent episode, discarding all trajectory information after every update. This epoch-independent design neglects a basic fact: the policy’s capability is not stationary. As training progresses, problems that once required lengthy derivations become solvable in fewer steps, yet fixed length penalties assume a static compression goal throughout. The training process itself already produces exactly the signal needed: each time the model finds a short correct solution, it establishes a tighter per-problem compression bound, tracing a natural coarse-to-fine curriculum. Standard RL discards this signal after every update, never using it to recalibrate the reward. We propose ExpThink to exploit this signal. The framework maintains an experience buffer that records the shortest correct solution per problem across all past epochs, defining an adaptive reward threshold that tightens automatically as the policy improves, without any manual scheduling. Our key contributions are: • We identify a structural limitation shared by existing RL-based CoT compression methods: historical trajectory information accumulated during training is discarded after each update and never used to recalibrate the compression objective as the policy improves. • We propose ExpThink , an experience-driven RL framework with a self-evolving compres-sion curriculum, an experience-guided three-tier reward, and difficulty-adaptive advantage estimation that jointly optimize accuracy and brevity. • ExpThink reduces average response length by up to 77% while consistently improving accuracy across all model scales, with stable training dynamics, achieving up to 3× higher IPT than the vanilla baseline and outperforming all prior RL-based compression methods on both metrics.

3 ExpThink 

The key insight driving ExpThink is that standard RL training discards two valuable signals after every update: evidence that certain problems can be solved correctly, which establishes a per-problem achievability bound on response length, and evidence that the LRM already solves many problems consistently, which identifies where gradient budget is wasted on correctness and should shift to brevity. ExpThink captures both signals through a minimal modification to DAPO [ 48 ]: a running experience buffer that converts the first signal into an adaptive reward threshold, and a count-based advantage normalizer that converts the second into difficulty-weighted gradients. 3.1 Experience-Guided Reward Shaping 

A binary correctness reward treats every correct rollout identically and therefore gives the policy no reason to prefer shorter derivations over longer ones. Length penalties can address this, but the penalty is typically static: it is fixed ahead of time and remains unchanged as the LRM improves, even though the same problem might admit progressively shorter solutions over the course of training. We observe that every correct rollout the LRM produces is already direct evidence of an achievable length bound on that problem, and that these bounds monotonically tighten as the policy improves.

3.2 Difficulty-Adaptive Advantage 

Prior work has proposed reshaping the reward to reflect problem difficulty [ 28 , 27 ], yet intervening at the reward level is insufficient: the per-group standard deviation σ used in GRPO is a concave function of |C q |, peaking when half the rollouts are correct and vanishing at both extremes. Because |C q | counts exactly how many rollouts the policy solved correctly, gradient magnitude is now strictly decreasing in |C q |,preserving difficulty awareness all the way to the parameter update. This substitution also reveals a clean structural property: the advantage gap between a short-correct and a long-correct rollout in the same group equals (1 − rpen )/(|C q | + ϵ), which is the selective pressure the gradient exerts on length. On hard prompts this gap is a minor fraction of a large overall advantage, so the update drives correctness; on easy prompts the overall advantage shrinks and this gap becomes the dominant signal, so the update drives brevity. We term this the accuracy-first, compression-second learning dynamic: the LRM is pushed toward conciseness only where correctness is already reliable, with no manual scheduling required. In combination with the self-evolving threshold from Eq. (1) , the two mechanisms jointly induce an implicit curriculum along both the accuracy and length axes. 4 Experiments 

4.1 Setup Models. We evaluate ExpThink on DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B, DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B and Qwen3-8B [7, 40] as backbone models. 

Training. Training is conducted on the DeepScaleR dataset [ 31 ], which contains approximately 40K mathematical problems with calibrated difficulty levels. We set α = 0 .1 and rpen = 0 .5 as default; full hyperparameters are provided in Appendix B. 

Evaluation benchmarks. We evaluate on five standard mathematical reasoning benchmarks: AIME24 [ 6 ], AMC23 [ 33 ], MATH-500 [ 26 ], Minerva Math [ 20 ], and OlympiadBench [ 14 ]. To further assess out-of-domain generalization, we additionally evaluate on LiveCodeBench [ 19 ] for code reasoning, GPQA-Diamond [ 38 ] for graduate-level scientific question answering, and MMLU [ 15 ]for broad multitask language understanding. Detailed descriptions of all benchmarks are provided in Appendix C. 

Evaluation protocol. We report Pass@1 accuracy, average response token length, and Intelligence per Token (IPT), defined as accuracy divided by response length in kilotokens. To quantify efficiency gains, we additionally report ∆Acc and ∆Token as the average relative change (%) across all benchmarks with respect to the Vanilla baseline. Evaluation runs are replicated 16 times on AIME24, 8 times on AMC23, and once on the remaining benchmarks, which have larger test sets. Context length and sampling hyperparameters follow DeepSeek-R1 [7]. 

Baselines. We compare against five representative RL-based methods spanning aggressive and conservative length-control strategies: LC-R1 [ 5 ], Laser [ 28 ], AutoThink [ 41 ], AdaptThink [ 50 ], and JET [12]. Detailed descriptions of each baseline are provided in Appendix E.

(2) Larger models benefit more. IPT increases steadily with model size: from 7.23 to 23.29 on the 1.5B model, from 11.15 to 32.84 on 7B, and from 8.64 to 51.04 on Qwen3-8B. Methods with aggressive length penalties, such as LC-R1, compress responses substantially but hurt accuracy; conservative methods such as Laser and JET preserve accuracy but stall near ∼50% or less compression. 4.6 Generalization to out-of-distribution benchmarks. 

Table 3 complements the in-domain results in Table 1 by testing whether the efficiency gains on math transfer to other task types without any further fine-tuning. On the 1.5B model, ExpThink 

achieves the largest accuracy improvement and the greatest token reduction among all methods, with an IPT of 27.26 compared with 9.01 for the Vanilla baseline. 5 Conclusion 

We introduced ExpThink , a reinforcement learning framework that breaks the epoch-independence assumption of prior CoT compression methods. By maintaining a per-problem experience buffer of the shortest correct solution observed across training, ExpThink defines an adaptive reward threshold that tightens automatically as the policy improves, eliminating manual schedule design. Paired with count-based advantage normalization, this self-evolving curriculum allocates optimization pressure toward correctness on hard problems and toward brevity once correctness is reliable. Across three backbones, ExpThink consistently improves the accuracy–efficiency tradeoff, reducing token usage by up to 77% while simultaneously gaining accuracy, and the gains generalize to out-of-distribution benchmarks including GPQA-Diamond and LiveCodeBench. These results establish cross-epoch trajectory reuse as a lightweight and effective principle for training reasoning models with higher intelligence per token. 

Limitations. The training data and primary evaluation are limited to mathematical reasoning with verifiable answers, and transfer to interactive tool use or multimodal settings remains to be confirmed. 9The reward also depends on reliable answer verification, which may not hold for tasks with ambiguous or partially correct outputs. A controlled comparison against DAPO under matched compute, which would further disentangle the contributions of the experience buffer from those of DAPO-style training, is also left to future work. Finally, the tolerance margin α and discounted reward rpen that guard against over-compression are empirical choices; although rpen is ablated in Section 4.3, a systematic study of α sensitivity across diverse domains is left to future work. 

Future work. We plan to extend ExpThink to multimodal reasoning, where concise traces must preserve visual grounding. A further direction is to store full trajectories in the experience buffer and perform off-policy replay, enabling the curriculum to leverage not only length statistics but also the content of past successful solutions for richer reward shaping and diversity-aware exploration. 10 References

A.1 Experience-Guided Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards 

A growing body of work leverages historical rollout experience to improve GRPO training. On the experience-reuse side, LANPO [ 21 ] accumulates past rollouts into a dynamic pool for cross-sample abstraction, BAPO [ 42 ] replays difficulty-aware batches of previously failed problems, RPO [ 47 ]caches and reuses trajectory prefixes to cut rollout cost by up to 95%, and SOUP [ 46 ] splices off-policy prefixes with on-policy continuations via importance weighting. On the reward-shaping side, WS-GRPO[ 34 ] learns prefix-level continue/stop signals from outcome-only data, F-GRPO [ 37 ] applies focal-loss-inspired re-weighting to shift gradient mass toward rare-correct trajectories, XRPO [ 2]sharpens advantages via novelty-aware rollout allocation, HiLL [ 45 ] co-trains a hinter policy to recover learning signals on hard problems, and STEP [ 4] drives adaptive resampling from historical success rates. 

A.2 Reinforcement Learning Based Efficient Reasoning 

Reinforcement learning offers a more direct route by jointly rewarding correctness and brevity, allowing the model to discover shorter valid paths without annotated demonstrations. The dominant paradigm augments the reward with a length penalty, as in L1 [1], ConciseRL [8], ThinkPrune [17], and LC-R1 [ 5]. DLER [ 27 ] identifies that naive length penalties can suppress high-entropy explo-ration tokens critical for reasoning, proposing a recipe that recovers accuracy while reducing response length by over 70%. LEASH [ 24 ] introduces an adaptive Lagrangian penalty that dynamically adjusts the constraint coefficient to accelerate convergence toward a target length. GR3 [ 25 ] replaces additive length penalties with multiplicative group-relative reward rescaling, eliminating compensatory opti-mization shortcuts that arise in standard formulations. Dr. GRPO [ 29 ] corrects an optimization bias in GRPO that artificially inflates response length for incorrect outputs. Adaptive frameworks such as DAST and AutoThink [ 39 , 41 , 50 ] allocate inference budgets as a function of estimated problem difficulty, and Laser [ 28 ] combines difficulty-aware penalties with explicit exploration of incorrect trajectories. ProRL v2 [ 35 ] demonstrates that scheduled cosine length penalties within prolonged RL training enable sustained improvement over thousands of steps without the model collapsing to short contexts. Beyond scalar penalties, finer-grained formulations have emerged: SWAP [ 23 ] applies step-level penalties weighted by each step’s contribution to the correct answer; FGO [ 13 ] subdivides GRPO response groups by length and entropy to prevent representation collapse during compression; SCMA [ 3 ] uses a dual-agent RL loop that segments reasoning into chunks and scores each for redundancy; CEEH [ 32 ] selectively regularizes entropy based on estimated problem difficulty; and Li et al. [22] tie length penalties to whether the model has already demonstrated ability to solve a given problem with a shorter response, enabling cross-domain generalization. Despite their diversity, all these RL methods treat each training epoch independently: the reward at epoch e depends only on trajectories sampled during epoch e, leaving cross-epoch compression progress unused. 

B Training Hyperparameters 

We train all models on 8 × A800 80GB GPUs with a global batch size of 512 and a maximum sequence length of 16,384 tokens. To address memory constraints under long-context training, we employ Fully Sharded Data Parallel (FSDP) with CPU offloading. During optimization, we adopt the GRPO objective with β = 0 .001 and asymmetric clipping parameters εlow = 0 .2 and εhigh = 0 .28 . The learning rate is fixed at 1 × 10 −6. We use AdamW as the optimizer with default parameter settings. For response generation during training, we use temperature 

= 1 .0 and top-p = 1 .0 to encourage exploration; during validation, we use temperature = 0 .6 and top-p = 0 .95 . Training runs for 300 update steps. At each step, we sample G = 16 responses per prompt to construct response groups for GRPO-based advantage computation. Validation is conducted periodically during training, and checkpoints are saved every 10 steps for model selection and reproducibility. Notably, we do not incorporate KL divergence regularization into the reward function; the reward signal is computed independently of any KL penalty term. 16 C Datasets 

DeepScaleR [ 31 ]. DeepScaleR is a curated mathematical reasoning dataset comprising approxi-mately 40K problems, constructed by scaling up the difficulty distribution of existing open-source mathematical corpora. It is built upon a systematic difficulty-aware selection pipeline: problems are first scored by estimated difficulty, and then progressively sampled to emphasize challenging instances that demand multi-step derivations, algebraic manipulation, and advanced problem-solving strategies. DeepScaleR serves as the training corpus for ExpThink . Its main strengths lie in its carefully calibrated difficulty gradient, broad coverage of mathematical topics spanning algebra, geometry, number theory, and combinatorics. 

AIME24 [ 6]. AIME24 consists of the official problems from the 2024 American Invitational Mathematics Examination (AIME) I and II and has become a widely used benchmark for evaluating high-level mathematical reasoning. Its problems typically require sophisticated combinatorial analy-sis, geometric constructions, or number-theoretic reasoning, and each admits a unique three-digit integer answer. In public repositories, AIME24 usually appears as a 30-problem set designed to assess models under realistic contest conditions. 

AMC23 [ 33 ]. AMC23 is a benchmark derived from the 2023 American Mathematics Competition, primarily AMC 10 and AMC 12. The problems are reformatted for model evaluation and usually take the form of multiple-choice or integer-answer questions, covering core high-school contest topics such as algebra, geometry, probability, and number theory. The overall difficulty exceeds routine classroom exercises but remains below that of AIME and Olympiad-level problems. Compared with AIME24, AMC23 typically contains a larger number of instances and is therefore commonly used as a medium-difficulty benchmark. 

MATH-500 [ 16 ]. MATH-500 is a curated subset of 500 competition-level problems drawn from the MATH dataset. The original MATH dataset contains approximately 12,500 U.S. high-school competition problems, each accompanied by a full step-by-step solution, and spanning topics such as algebra, geometry, counting and probability, number theory, and precalculus. Difficulty levels follow the Art of Problem Solving (AoPS) scale from 1 to 5. Evaluation is conducted using an exact-match criterion on the final answer. 

Minerva Math [ 20 ]. Minerva Math is a collection of mathematical and quantitative reasoning problems assessing formal mathematical and scientific reasoning capabilities. In publicly released evaluations, roughly 272 problems are included, primarily sourced from MIT OpenCourseWare and covering advanced undergraduate- and graduate-level STEM topics such as solid-state chemistry, astronomy, differential equations, and special relativity. Compared with pure mathematics competition datasets, Minerva Math emphasizes cross-disciplinary quantitative problems. 

OlympiadBench [ 14 ]. OlympiadBench is a bilingual multimodal Olympiad-level science bench-mark designed to evaluate the reasoning and problem-solving capabilities of LLMs using extremely challenging mathematics and physics competition problems. The dataset aggregates 8,476 questions drawn from international and national mathematics Olympiads, physics contests, and the Chinese col-lege entrance examination, spanning both text-only and image-based multimodal formats. Empirical results indicate that even state-of-the-art systems achieve relatively low performance, highlighting the benchmark’s exceptional difficulty. 

MMLU [ 15 ]. MMLU (Massive Multitask Language Understanding) is a comprehensive benchmark spanning 57 diverse academic subjects, ranging from STEM disciplines to humanities and social sciences. Each problem is presented in a four-option multiple-choice format, and the questions are drawn from real-world examination materials at difficulty levels from elementary to advanced professional. MMLU is used in our work to assess out-of-distribution generalization of the efficiency learned by ExpThink .

LiveCodeBench [ 19 ]. LiveCodeBench is a holistic and contamination-free benchmark designed to evaluate large language models on a broad spectrum of code-related capabilities. It continuously collects problems from competitive programming platforms—LeetCode, AtCoder, and CodeForces— using only contests published after the training cutoffs of most evaluated models, thereby eliminating 17 data-leakage artifacts that plague static coding benchmarks such as HumanEval and MBPP. Each problem falls into one of four task categories: code generation, self-repair, code execution, and test-output prediction. LiveCodeBench is used in our work to evaluate whether the reasoning efficiency acquired through ExpThink generalizes to complex, out-of-distribution programming tasks. 

GPQA [ 38 ]. GPQA (Graduate-Level Google-Proof Q&A) is a rigorous multiple-choice benchmark comprising 448 questions across biology, physics, and chemistry, authored by domain specialists with doctoral-level expertise. The questions are deliberately crafted to resist web-search assistance: skilled non-experts who spent over 30 minutes searching the internet achieved only 34% accuracy, while in-domain PhD experts reached approximately 65%, underscoring the benchmark’s exceptional difficulty. GPQA is used in our work to probe whether ExpThink improves deep scientific reasoning in settings that demand genuine domain knowledge rather than surface-level pattern matching.

All baseline methods are grounded in reinforcement learning (RL) and address the problem of efficient reasoning from distinct perspectives. 

LC-R1 [ 5 ]. LC-R1 (Length-Compressed R1) addresses reasoning efficiency by augmenting the GRPO objective with two complementary reward signals. The first is an explicit length reward that penalizes the total token count of generated reasoning sequences, applying a global conciseness pressure. The second is a compression reward that specifically targets the elimination of redundant self-verification patterns—i.e., segments where the model unnecessarily rechecks already-established conclusions—without broadly suppressing productive reasoning steps. This targeted decomposition allows LC-R1 to achieve approximately 50% reduction in sequence length with only around 2% accuracy degradation. We use the publicly released checkpoints LCR1-1.5B and LCR1-7B. 

Laser [ 28 ]. Laser (Length-bAsed StEp rEward shaping) provides a unified framework for efficient reasoning through step-level length-based reward shaping, using a step function controlled by a target length as the primary reward signal. Its extended variant, LASER-D , incorporates two additional mechanisms: dynamic adaptation , where the reward specification evolves continuously as model behavior changes throughout training; and difficulty-awareness , which imposes heavier length penalties on easier queries to encourage fast responses for simple problems while preserving extended chain-of-thought for genuinely hard ones. This explicit difficulty-conditioned reward design allows the model to learn a problem-adaptive reasoning budget. We use the checkpoints Laser-DE-L4096-1.5B and Laser-DE-L4096-7B, both trained on the same dataset as ours. 

AutoThink [ 41 ]. AutoThink is motivated by the observation that inserting a simple ellipsis token into prompts can stochastically trigger either thinking or no-thinking mode in R1-style models, revealing a latent controllability in reasoning behavior. Building on this finding, AutoThink proposes a three-stage reinforcement learning curriculum with progressive reward shaping. Stage 1 employs batch-level reward balancing to maintain the coexistence of both thinking and no-thinking modes during early training; Stage 2 reinforces task accuracy within each mode independently; Stage 3 introduces a length-aware reward to suppress unnecessary computation and encourage direct responses for simpler queries. Trained on the same dataset as ours, we use the checkpoints Distill-R1-1.5B-AutoThink-Stage3 and Distill-R1-7B-AutoThink-Stage3. 

AdaptThink [ 50 ]. AdaptThink is grounded in the empirical finding that forcing reasoning models to always engage in extended chain-of-thought is suboptimal: for simpler problems, skipping CoT entirely ( NoThinking mode) can improve both speed and accuracy. AdaptThink leverages RL with two complementary components to exploit this observation: a constrained optimization objective that nudges the model toward NoThinking responses while preserving overall accuracy, and an 

importance-sampling scheme that balances the ratio of Thinking versus NoThinking training samples to enable stable exploration of both modes. Together, these enable the model to adaptively select whether to invoke full chain-of-thought reasoning or to respond directly, based on perceived problem difficulty. Trained on the same dataset as ours, we use the checkpoints AdaptThink-1.5B-delta0.05 and AdaptThink-7B-delta0.05. 

JET [ 12 ]. JET (Just-Enough Thinking) is motivated by Evidence Accumulation Models from cognitive science, which suggest that sufficient evidence for a decision is often accumulated early in a reasoning sequence, rendering subsequent steps redundant. JET operationalizes this insight through two mechanisms: trajectory truncation during rollout , which exposes the model to shorter, distributionally consistent reasoning paths during RL training to overcome the distributional barrier that prevents naive length rewards from generating compact rollouts; and a quality-controlled length reward , which incentivizes conciseness while explicitly preserving answer correctness. Rather than penalizing long outputs after generation, JET shapes the model’s internal stopping criterion so that it proactively terminates reasoning at the appropriate point. We use the checkpoints JET-1.5B and JET-7B. JET has been accepted at ICLR 2026. 19 F Supplementary Experiments
