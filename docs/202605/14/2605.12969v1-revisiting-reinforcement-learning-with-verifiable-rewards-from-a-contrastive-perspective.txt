## Abstract 

Reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR) has become a widely adopted paradigm for improving LLMs’ reasoning capabilities, and Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) is one of its most representative algorithms. In this paper, we first show that GRPO admits an equivalent discriminative reformulation as a weighted positive-negative score difference. Under this view, GRPO increases sequence-level scores of verified positive rollouts (responses judged correct) and decreases those of negative rollouts (responses judged incorrect), where the scores are averages of clipped token-level importance sampling ratios. This reformula-tion reveals two structural limitations of GRPO: likelihood-misaligned scoring ,where clipped ratio-based surrogate scores are optimized instead of generation likelihoods, and score-insensitive credit assignment , where rollout-level credit is assigned without accounting for relative score gaps between positive and negative rollouts in the same group. To address these limitations, we propose ConSPO, a framework for Con trastive Sequence-level Policy Optimization in RLVR. Con-SPO replaces GRPO’s clipped ratio-based scores with length-normalized sequence log-probabilities, aligning the optimized rollout scores with the likelihoods used in autoregressive generation. It then optimizes a group-wise InfoNCE-style objective that contrasts each positive rollout against negative distractors from the same group, enabling credit assignment to depend on their relative scores. This contrastive formulation amplifies updates for poorly separated positives while concentrating suppressive updates on high-scoring negatives. Moreover, ConSPO introduces a curriculum-scheduled margin, guiding optimization from coarse positive-negative ordering in early training toward stronger separation in later stages. Extensive evaluations across diverse backbone models, parameter scales, and training datasets show that ConSPO consistently outperforms several strong RLVR baselines on challenging mathematical reasoning benchmarks. Code will be released upon paper acceptance.

Recent works recalibrate importance sampling ratios via sequence-level geometric means [ 8 , 9] or incorporate token-level soft gating to mitigate policy entropy collapse [ 10 ]. Despite these advances, existing approaches mainly modify GRPO around its original objective, leaving its core optimization mechanism less explored. Recent studies have highlighted the potential role of discriminative learning principles [ 18 , 19 ], providing a useful departure point from the standard group-relative advantage formulation. In this paper, we make this connection explicit by rewriting GRPO as a weighted positive-negative discrimination objective (Proposition 1). For each question, GRPO increases sequence-level scores of verified positive rollouts and decreases those of verified negative rollouts. This reformulation reveals two limitations in the GRPO objective. First, GRPO suffers from likelihood-misaligned scoring : it optimizes a surrogate score that is not the likelihood used for generation. Its discriminative form separates positive from negative rollouts by averaging clipped importance sampling ratios, rather than by comparing their sequence likelihoods under the current policy. Thus, GRPO can enlarge the surrogate score gap without improving the likelihood ordering between positive and negative rollouts. This training-inference mismatch weakens the connection between objective improvement and generation quality, leading to suboptimal policy improvement [ 20 ]. Second, GRPO suffers from score-insensitive credit assignment : it assigns credit across rollouts through reward-based advantages, which under binary rewards are governed by the group pass rate, i.e., the fraction of positive rollouts in the group, without considering the within-group scores of positive and negative rollouts. As shown in Figure 1, the resulting gradient is insensitive to score gaps among rollouts. Within a group, positive rollouts share one coefficient and negative rollouts share another, regardless 2of whether a positive is barely separated from negatives or a hard negative already receives a high score; thus, the rollouts that most need corrective updates are not explicitly emphasized. Dynamic sampling mitigates this bias by retaining groups with intermediate pass rates [ 21 – 23 ], but it does not remove the underlying source in the GRPO objective. To address these limitations, we propose ConSPO, which revisits RLVR as Con trastive Sequence-level 

Policy Optimization. ConSPO first addresses likelihood-misaligned scoring by replacing the clipped importance sampling ratio scores in GRPO with length-normalized sequence log-probabilities. This score is the same quantity used to rank responses during autoregressive generation, thereby aligning the training objective with inference-time likelihoods. ConSPO then addresses score-insensitive credit assignment with a group-wise InfoNCE objective [ 24 ], where each verified positive rollout is contrasted against negative distractors generated for the same question. As shown in Figure 1, the softmax normalization over each positive rollout and its negative distractors makes the gradient depend on their relative scores within the group, rather than only on reward-derived group statistics. For a positive rollout, the update is amplified when its score remains close to the distractors and is smoothly attenuated once it becomes well separated. For negative rollouts, the total suppressive signal becomes stronger when positives are poorly separated from the distractor set, and this signal is distributed according to the negative scores, assigning larger updates to high-scoring incorrect rollouts. Furthermore, ConSPO introduces a margin into the contrastive objective to prevent premature gradient attenuation when positive rollouts only marginally outperform negative distractors. We schedule the margin in a curriculum manner [ 25 , 26 ], allowing optimization to begin with coarse positive-negative ordering and gradually shift toward stronger separation. Extensive evaluations across diverse backbone models, parameter scales, and training datasets show that ConSPO consistently improves performance on seven challenging mathematical reasoning benchmarks. Our main contributions are summarized as follows: • We establish an equivalent discriminative reformulation of GRPO, showing that it optimizes a weighted positive-negative score difference. This reformulation reveals two limitations of GRPO: 

likelihood-misaligned scoring and score-insensitive credit assignment across rollouts. • We propose ConSPO, which constructs a group-wise contrastive objective with likelihood-aligned sequence scores and a curriculum-scheduled margin, enabling contrast-sensitive credit assignment within rollout groups. • Extensive experiments across diverse settings show that ConSPO consistently outperforms several strong RLVR baselines on seven challenging reasoning benchmarks. 

## 2 Related Work 

Reinforcement learning with LLMs. GRPO [ 2] circumvents the reliance on a parameterized value model by estimating group-relative advantages directly from outcome-level rewards, maintaining robust performance across diverse reasoning tasks. Guided by this paradigm, subsequent efforts have advanced GRPO toward better performance, more stable training, and more efficient reasoning. Dr. GRPO [ 27 ] removes the standard deviation in advantage estimation to mitigate difficulty bias, and adjusts loss allocation to reduce length bias. DAPO [ 21 ] introduces dynamic sampling to reduce high-variance gradient updates, and employs length-based reward shaping for finer-grained length control. GMPO [8] addresses outlier effects in asynchronous training by replacing token-level clipping with sequence-level geometric-mean clipping. CISPO [ 28 ] introduces soft clipping to preserve learning signals discarded by hard clipping, while SAPO [ 10 ] applies asymmetric, temperature-controlled clipping for finer-grained and stable updates. HAPO [ 11 ] and ShorterBetter [ 12 ] promote concise reasoning by selecting shorter trajectories from either historical or current generations. While DisCO [ 29 ] reinterprets GRPO through a discriminative lens under binary rewards and explores different scoring functions, it does not address the score-insensitive credit assignment induced by GRPO’s objective. In contrast, ConSPO applies an InfoNCE-style contrastive objective to online RLVR, using likelihood-aligned sequence scores and a curriculum-scheduled margin to adapt credit assignment to within-group rollout scores. 

Contrastive Objectives for Policy Alignment. Contrastive learning trains models by distinguishing positive samples from negative distractors, with InfoNCE-style objectives serving as a standard formulation [ 24 ]. Foundational methods improve contrastive learning by enlarging the negative set, 3for example, through large-batch sampling in SimCLR [ 30 ] or momentum queues in MoCo [ 31 ]. Inspired by contrastive learning principles, recent policy alignment methods formulate preference optimization as a task of discriminating between preferred and dispreferred responses. For example, DPO [ 32 ] and CPO [ 33 ] optimize policy likelihoods over preference pairs without explicit reward modeling, while SimPO [ 20 ] further uses length-normalized sequence log-probabilities as an implicit ranking score. Unlike these offline preference optimization methods, ConSPO transfers the contrastive principle to online RLVR, where positive-negative comparisons are constructed from verifiable rewards on policy-generated rollouts rather than from static pairwise preference data.

Implications. Equation (7) and Lemma 1 formalize the two limitations of GRPO discussed above. First, Eq. (7) reveals likelihood-misaligned scoring : GRPO scores each rollout by averaging clipped token-level importance sampling ratios. Second, Lemma 1 reveals score-insensitive credit assignment : within a valid group, all positive rollouts receive the same coefficient and all negative rollouts receive another. These coefficients are determined by the group pass rate through pbpq (1 − bpq ), rather than by the score gaps between positive and negative rollouts. Therefore, GRPO assigns the same rollout-level credit to positives with different score gaps, and likewise to high-scoring and low-scoring negatives within the same group.

4.3 Scheduled Margin as a Separation Curriculum

(10) enables contrast-aware credit assignment, its gradients may attenuate prematurely when positive rollouts only marginally outperform their negative distractors. This issue becomes more pronounced in later training stages, where positive and negative scores may already be correctly ordered but still lack sufficient separation. To prevent premature gradient attenuation, ConSPO introduces a scheduled margin that lowers the effective score of each positive rollout in the contrastive objective. Benchmarks. We evaluate our models on seven benchmarks, including AIME 2024, AIME 2025, AIME 2026, HMMT 2025 [ 39 ], MATH500 [ 40 ], AMC, and OlympiadBench [ 41 ]. We report pass@1 on most benchmarks, and avg@32 on AIME, HMMT, and AMC to mitigate the variance introduced by the relatively small size of these datasets [42]. 5.2 Main Results Performance with reasoning models. As shown in Tables 1 and 2, ConSPO delivers consistent improvements on reasoning models. On DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B, ConSPO achieves the best average performance of 44.4, outperforming GRPO by 4.4 points and the strongest baseline by 1.6 points. Moreover, ConSPO achieves the highest performance across all seven benchmarks in this setting, indicating its generalization across different reasoning tasks. The same trend holds for larger reasoning models. On DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B and DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B, ConSPO improves over the strongest baseline by 2.1 and 1.9 points, respectively. These results suggest that the proposed contrastive objective remains effective across different parameter scales. 

Generalization across backbone models and training datasets. As shown in Tables 3 and 4, ConSPO generalizes well across both base and instruction-tuned models. On Qwen3-4B-Base, ConSPO achieves the best average performance of 34.0, outperforming the strongest baseline by 2.1 points. Notably, on the challenging HMMT25 benchmark, where the base model obtains only 0.9, ConSPO improves the score to 8.0, suggesting that our method can substantially enhance performance on difficult reasoning tasks where the initial model is weak. On Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct, whose base reasoning capability is substantially weaker, ConSPO still achieves the highest average performance of 29.2, improving over the strongest baseline by 0.6 points. Moreover, Table 5 shows that ConSPO remains effective when trained on the DAPO-Math-17k dataset, achieving an average performance of 44.1 and outperforming the strongest baseline by 1.9 points. These results demonstrate that the effectiveness of ConSPO is not tied to a specific backbone or training dataset.

## 6 Conclusion 

In this paper, we investigate the optimization structure of GRPO and establish an equivalent dis-criminative reformulation that characterizes it as a weighted positive-negative score difference. This reformulation reveals two structural limitations of GRPO: likelihood-misaligned scoring and score-insensitive credit assignment across rollouts. To address these limitations, we propose ConSPO, which optimizes a group-wise contrastive objective using likelihood-aligned sequence scores and a curriculum-scheduled margin to enable contrast-sensitive policy optimization. ## B Detailed Experimental Settings 

B.1 Benchmarks 

We evaluate the mathematical reasoning ability of the trained models on seven benchmarks that are commonly used in recent RLVR studies [35, 43, 7, 44]. • AIME24–26 — Problems from the 2024, 2025, and 2026 American Invitational Mathematics Examination. AIME consists of challenging high-school competition problems with integer-valued answers, requiring precise multi-step reasoning rather than multiple-choice selection. • HMMT25 — Problems from the February 2025 Harvard–MIT Mathematics Tournament. The benchmark covers competition-level problems in algebra and number theory, combinatorics, geom-etry, and team-based rounds. • MATH500 — A 500-problem evaluation subset of the MATH benchmark. It contains competition-style problems across seven subjects, including algebra, geometry, counting and probability, number theory, and precalculus. • AMC — Problems from the American Mathematics Competitions, covering a broad range of high-school mathematics topics. Although AMC is originally multiple-choice, the problems still require careful reasoning and symbolic manipulation. • OlympiadBench — An Olympiad-level benchmark containing challenging mathematics and physics problems. It evaluates advanced scientific reasoning on problems collected from Olympiad-style competitions and related examinations. Following prior work [ 42 ], we report avg@32 on AIME, HMMT, and AMC to reduce the variance caused by their relatively small test sizes, and report pass@1 on the remaining benchmarks. 

B.2 Implementation Details 

The training and evaluation configurations for the R1-Distill models are summarized in Tables 7 and 8, respectively. For the experiments on Qwen3-4B-Base and Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct, we keep the same settings as the R1-Distill models except for the maximum response length, which is set to 4096 during both training and evaluation. ## D Limitations 

Despite our best efforts, this study has several limitations. First, extending the empirical evaluation to larger-scale models, such as 32B and above, remains an important direction for future work. Second, ConSPO is a preliminary exploration of RLVR from a contrastive perspective, and broader choices of objective formulations and rollout scoring functions remain worth investigating. 

## E Broader Impacts 

The potential negative social impacts of our method align with those typically associated with general LLM reasoning technologies. We emphasize the importance of adhering to the principles of fair and safe deployment in LLM systems. 17
