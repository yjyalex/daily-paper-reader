## Abstract 

Multi-agent debate has emerged as a promising approach for improving LLM reasoning on ground-truth tasks, yet current methodologies face certain structural limitations: debate tends to induce a martingale over belief trajectories, majority voting accounts for most observed gains, and LLMs exhibit confidence escalation rather than calibration across rounds. We argue that the genuine value of debate, and dialectic systems as a whole, lies not in ground-truth tasks but in defeasible 

domains, where every position can in principle be defeated by better reasoning. We present the Council of Hierarchical Agentic Language (CHAL), a multi-agent dialectic framework that treats defeasible argumentation as an engine for belief op-timization. Each agent maintains a CHAL Belief Schema (CBS), a graph-structured belief representation with a Bayesian-inspired architecture, that facilitates belief re-vision through a gradient-informed dynamic mechanism by leveraging the strength of the belief’s thesis as a differentiable objective. Meta-cognitive value systems spanning epistemology, logic, and ethics are elevated to configurable hyperparam-eters governing agent reasoning and adjudication outcomes. We provide a series of ablation experiments that demonstrate systematic and interpretable effects: the adjudicator’s value system determines the debate’s overall trajectories in latent belief space, council diversity refines beliefs for all participants, and the framework generalizes across broad fields. CHAL is, to our knowledge, the first framework to treat multi-agent debate as structured belief optimization over defeasible domains. Further, the auditable belief artifacts it produces establish the foundation for ded-icated evaluation suites for defeasible argumentation, with broader implications for building AI systems whose reasoning and value commitments are transparent, aligned, and subject to human oversight. 

## 1 Introduction 

The history of artificial intelligence (AI) is, at its core, a history of attempts to formalize reasoning: to endow machines with the capacity to think through novel problems, weigh competing considera-tions, and arrive at justified conclusions. The arrival of large language models (LLMs), built on the transformer architecture [ 155 ], has dramatically narrowed the gap between this aspiration and the available tools, with probabilistic next-token prediction at scale giving rise to emergent multi-step deduction and coherent long-form reasoning [ 183 , 16 ]. These capabilities have renewed interest in utilizing structured Multi-Agent Debate (MAD) to improve automated reasoning, an idea with roots predating the LLM era by decades [ 12 , 164 , 130 ]. The use of LLM-powered debate was first demonstrated in [ 34 ] which showed improvements in mathematical reasoning and factual question answering through iterative critique, and was extended by [ 97 ] with a tit-for-tat framework incorporat-ing adaptive termination and a dedicated judge agent. Yet critical examination has revealed structural limitations: standard inter-agent debate induces a martingale over belief trajectories, implying that argumentative exchange alone does not improve expected correctness without structural interventions that bias updates toward correction [ 27 ]. Empirical evidence further demonstrates that current LLMs

exhibit confidence escalation rather than Bayesian calibration across debate rounds, a metacognitive deficit exacerbated by fine-tuning [ 123 , 93 ]. Recent work has begun to address individual aspects of these limitations, including neuro-symbolic defeasible reasoning [ 44 ], argumentation-enhanced claim verification [ 49 ], and ethical parameterization of debate agents [ 109 ], but each targets a single facet in isolation; no existing framework integrates structured belief representations, configurable meta-cognitive value systems, and formal argumentation within a unified multi-agent debate system. In this paper, we propose the Council of Hierarchical Agentic Language (CHAL), a MAD framework that treats argumentation on defeasible topics as a process of belief optimization . Drawing on Minsky’s vision of a Society of Mind [112 ], CHAL orchestrates a council of LLM agents, each reasoning from a distinct epistemological worldview, through a dialectical pipeline in which beliefs are genuinely tested, adversarially challenged, and structurally refined. In contrast to most existing debate systems where agents exchange unstructured text and belief states remain implicit, CHAL equips each agent with the CHAL Belief Schema (CBS), a formally structured internal belief representation whose internal components and confidence assignments are explicit, auditable, and revised through a gradient-informed optimization mechanism that directly addresses the structural limitations identified in prior work, including LLMs’ inability to reliably distinguish belief from knowledge and fact [ 146 ]. The system further makes the meta-cognitive commitments that any reasoning system implicitly carries into explicit, configurable hyperparameters spanning epistemology, logic, and ethics, enabling systematic study of how value structures shape the outcomes of dialectical exchange. Beyond its role as a belief refinement engine, CHAL is designed as a modular research platform with direct implications for AI safety, value-aligned reasoning, and the broader challenge of building AI systems whose reasoning is transparent and aligned with well-specified values [ 51 ]. We provide CHAL as a publicly available software package, accessible via [https://github.com/GdKent/CHAL] .The principal contributions of this work are outlined as follows. 

The CHAL Belief Schema (CBS). We introduce a formally structured belief representation in which each agent maintains a typed, auditable belief object that induces a directed acyclic dependency graph with strength-based confidence propagation. These beliefs replace the unstructured text exchange of existing debate systems with modular, auditable, and semantically rich objects. 

Gradient-informed belief revision. We introduce a belief optimization mechanism in which the thesis strength serves as a differentiable objective over the CBS and its gradient drives targeted dynamic revision prompts, embedding a form of gradient ascent into the prompting layer. 

Meta-cognitive value systems as hyperparameters. We introduce a suite of epistemic personas, logic systems, and ethical systems as independently configurable hyperparameters governing agent reasoning and adjudication, enabling study of how value structures shape dialectical outcomes. 

A modular research platform for value-aligned AI reasoning. We present the complete dialectical pipeline of CHAL as a configurable research platform with implications for AI safety, value-aligned reasoning, and the empirical study of how meta-cognitive configurations influence reasoning on consequential questions. The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 introduces the configurable meta-cognitive value systems, Section 3 defines the CBS and belief dependency graph, Section 4 presents the complete CHAL framework and dialectical pipeline, Section 5 provides numerical experiments under controlled ablations, and Section 6 concludes with directions for future research. Further, we also provide a comprehensive literature review in Appendix A. 

## 2 Meta-Cognition and Value Hierarchies 

Any system capable of making claims typically must first answer three prior questions: what counts as valid inference, what counts as morally relevant, and what counts as knowledge [19, 132]. These questions have deep histories in the philosophy of logic [ 67 , 8], meta-ethics [ 108 , 141 ], and epistemology [ 6], and different answers produce genuinely different conclusions from identical evidence: a classical and a paraconsistent logician reach incompatible verdicts on contradictory premises, a utilitarian and a deontologist reach opposite normative judgments from the same facts, and an empiricist and a rationalist may disagree on whether a given belief constitutes knowledge at all. In CHAL, these three meta-cognitive choices, of epistemic position, reasoning system, and ethical system, are explicit configuration parameters that determine how agents form beliefs, how arguments 2are adjudicated, and how convergence is interpreted. They are, in the language of machine learning, the hyperparameters of the debate: meta-level commitments that govern every first-order inference, and making them explicit allows the value commitments that any reasoning system implicitly carries to be studied, varied, and compared across configurations.

An agent’s epistemic position determines its standards for evi-dence, justification, and inference, including what kinds of rea-sons it takes to be compelling, how it weighs competing sources of knowledge, and where it locates the burden of proof. In CHAL, epistemic personas are assigned exclusively to the LLM council agents rather than the adjudicator (see Section 4 for formalism), whose evaluation is governed instead by independently config-ured logic and ethics systems. This separation is deliberate: the council’s role is to generate and defend positions from genuinely distinct epistemological starting points, while the adjudicator’s role is to evaluate the argumentative exchange against a fixed and consistent standard. By populating the council with agents reasoning from competing epistemological traditions, CHAL ex-plores the belief space more broadly than any single framework would permit, as illustrated in Figures 1(a) and 1(b). CHAL currently provides twelve epistemic personas, eight logic systems, and six ethical systems, each grounded in established philosophical traditions. The default logic system, Classical + In-formal + Bayesian (CIB), combines deductive validity with induc-tive and abductive support weighted by prior probability. Logic and ethics scores are computed independently by an adjudicator agent and combined via user-specified weights (see Section 4.2). The choice of system materially affects adjudication outcomes and is therefore a primary variable of interest for empirical studies of debate dynamics. Summary tables and complete descriptions for all epistemic positions as well as logical and ethical systems are provided in Appendix B. 

2.2 A Note on Implicit Values Embedded Within LLMs 

The value systems described in Appendices B.2–B.3 are explicit and configurable, and a user may set any weight to zero to remove its direct influence on adjudication (see Section 4.2). However, setting a weight to zero does not render the corresponding dimension entirely absent from the model’s outputs. LLMs acquire implicit values through their pretraining corpora [ 10 ] and RLHF alignment [ 116 ], producing empirically measurable biases in opinion distributions [ 135 ] and prompt-sensitive reasoning patterns that depart from formal logic [ 90 ]. CHAL cannot eliminate these residual influences; rather, its prompting architecture steers each agent toward the explicitly assigned value systems to the extent that prompting allows. The goal is therefore not value-neutral reasoning, which no LLM-based system can guarantee, but an explicit and configurable value structure whose influence on the dialectic can be systematically studied and compared across configurations. 

## 3 Internal Belief Systems 

This section defines the data structures that underpin CHAL’s belief optimization process. The central artifact is the CHAL Belief Schema (CBS), a formally structured object representing an agent’s complete belief state at any point in the debate. A CBS is generated at the opening of the debate, updated after every argumentative exchange, and compared across agents and rounds to measure epistemic progress. 

3.1 The CHAL Belief Schema 

The CBS integrates ideas from five theoretical frameworks into a single machine-readable, LLM-generatable structure: Toulmin’s decomposition of argument into claim, grounds, warrant, and 3rebuttal [ 149 ]; the ASPIC+ attack taxonomy [ 113 ]; the AGM minimality principle, implemented via the patch-based update mechanism in Section 3.3 [ 2]; Bayesian calibrated strength values on [0 , 1] 

subject to propagation constraints [ 76 , 147 ]; and Popperian falsifiability, operationalized through explicit predictions attached to every claim [121]. 

Definition 1 (CHAL Belief Schema) Let Θ, ∆, Λ, Ξ, Ω, Ψ, and Φ denote the spaces of all well-formed thesis, definition, assumption, evidence, claim, counterposition, and uncertainty objects, respectively. A CBS, otherwise referred to as a belief object, is a tuple 

B = ( θ, D, A, E, C, X , U ) ,

where θ ∈ Θ is the thesis, and D ⊆ ∆, A ⊆ Λ, E ⊆ Ξ, C ⊆ Ω, X ⊆ Ψ, and U ⊆ Φ are finite sets of definitions, assumptions, evidence items, claims, counterpositions, and uncertainties, respectively. 

Every element carries a prefixed identifier (D#, A#, E#, C#, X#, U#) enabling precise referencing during cross-examination, adjudication, and revision. Among the seven components, five are strength-bearing : θ, D, A, E, and C each carry a calibrated strength value in [0 , 1] subject to the dependency constraints defined in Section 3.2. The remaining two, X and U, are non-strength-bearing and instead serve as adversarial and epistemic honesty signals (see Sections 3.2 and 3.3). A summary of each component’s role is given in Table 7, and full field-level specifications are provided in Appendix C.1. The shared strength calibration scale that is used to ground agents is provided in Appendix B.4.1. 

3.1.1 The Strength-Infused Bayesian Architecture 

Each strength-bearing node ( θ or elements from the sets D, A, E, and C ) carries an agent-assigned strength value in [0 , 1] , interpretable as a Bayesian credence, together with a justification. In isolation, self-reported strengths would invite inflation; under CHAL’s adversarial cross-examination, however, inflated strengths become targets for attack, and surviving values are progressively refined toward accurate reflections of the underlying epistemic state across rounds. The strength architecture is therefore not merely an annotation but one of the primary signals agents reason over when deciding how to refine their position (see Section 3.3) and what positions to attack. The thesis strength is the quantitative objective each agent works to maximize, playing a role analogous to a reward function in reinforcement learning. Unlike node-level strengths, it is computed deterministically from the active claim set and is never agent-chosen.

3.3 The Belief Revision System 

Belief updates in CHAL are carried out exclusively through a deterministic patch system that implements the AGM minimality principle [ 2 ] operationally. Rather than regenerating a belief from scratch, an agent emits atomic, structured patch operations targeting specific nodes, which are applied by a central routine that also enforces (3.3) (see Section 4.3). 

Definition 5 (Belief Patch) A belief patch π is an atomic operation on a CBS belief object B of one of the two following forms: add a new node of type D, A, E, C, X , or U; update an existing node’s fields. 

5Given a CBS object B and an ordered sequence of k ∈ N patches Π = ( π1, . . . , π k) emitted by an agent, the patch application routine applies each πj and re-enforces the constraint equations (3.1) and (3.3) . Retraction is handled by forcing the strength of the retracted node to 0 and excluding it from all subsequent constraint calculations. The propagation step is implemented as a breadth-first traversal of the BDG starting from modified nodes, ensuring that any strength change cascades consistently through the layered structure before the thesis strength is recomputed.

4.2 The Adjudicator 

The adjudicator is an independent LLM-based agent that drives belief evolution by evaluating every challenge-rebuttal pair and rendering a verdict. Its role is deliberately constrained to evaluation: it does not generate arguments, maintain a belief of its own, or access any council member’s persona, so that verdicts reflect the substance of the arguments rather than the identity of the arguer. Without this separation between generation and evaluation, LLM debaters typically default to premature consensus rather than genuine adversarial testing [97]. CHAL is model-agnostic; an initial configuration layer defines the topic query, model provider, and all pipeline and agent-specific parameters. ## 5 Numerical Experiments 

Recent meta-analyses have shown that on tasks with definitive ground-truth answers, multi-agent debate often fails to outperform simple baselines such as majority voting [ 27 ] and single-agent chain-of-thought [ 181 ], raising the question of where debate is genuinely valuable. We contend that the answer lies in defeasible domains, where no ground-truth answer exists and every position can in principle be defeated by better reasoning. CHAL is designed for this regime, and the CBS ensures that belief evolution is observable, trackable, and auditable through structured artifacts. No existing benchmark for defeasible reasoning, argumentation mining, or belief revision evaluates multi-agent, graph-structured belief evolution through adversarial dialectics; we therefore adopt ablation-based evaluation to demonstrate that CHAL’s behavior is systematic, interpretable, and sensitive to its architectural parameters in the intended ways. We also contend that the structured output artifacts that CHAL produces provide the foundation upon which dedicated benchmarks for defeasible argumentation could be constructed and is a direction for future work, noted in Section 6. All experiments use OpenAI’s o4-mini for all council members and the adjudicator. Unless otherwise noted, each experiment generates n = 10 independent debate trajectories per configuration with 8shared initial beliefs and run for R = 5 rounds. Beliefs are embedded via Appendix F.1 and projected into two dimensions via UMAP. Per-agent performance is measured by thesis strength and the Agent Performance Score (APS) (see Appendix F.2). Due to space limitations, complete discussions of each experiment, including figures, statistical tables, and excerpts, are provided in Appendix F, which also provides a discussion on the initial underlying epistemic distributions that each agent draws from (see Appendix F.3). A complete example of a set of initial and final beliefs that are generated by CHAL is provided in Appendix G. We now highlight the type of ablations as well as the primary findings. 

5.1 Ablations Adjudicator Ablation. This ablation isolates the effect of the adjudicator’s meta-cognitive value system on belief evolution. We test three configurations with two council members (Empiricist and Supernaturalist) across 10 trajectories on the topic “Does free will exist?”: (1) pure logic (CIB), (2) balanced (CIB + Deontological), and (3) pure ethics (Deontological). Under pure logic and balanced adjudication, the Empiricist dominates as the Supernaturalist’s metaphysical claims fail falsifiability challenges; under pure ethics, the outcome reverses entirely, with the Supernaturalist favored despite presenting similar arguments. This highlights the adjudicator as the primary force driving where the debate converges and what is constituted as the most defensible position under a given evaluative standard. Detailed analysis is provided in Appendix F.4. 

Council Member Ablation. This ablation isolates the effect of council diversity on belief dynamics. We compare a 2-agent council (Empiricist and Supernaturalist) with a 3-agent council (adding a Phenomenologist), both under balanced adjudication (CIB + Deontological) again on the topic “Does free will exist”. The 3-agent configuration produces tighter final belief distributions for all agents, including those already present in the smaller council, by attacking both existing positions from a methodologically distinct angle. This supports the central idea that populating the council with epistemically diverse agents refines the belief landscape for all participants, as each agent is exposed to a broader range of argumentative pressures. Detailed analysis is provided in Appendix F.5. 

Topic Ablation. To assess generalization across philosophical domains, we provide an single-trajectory demonstration across three topics: “Does free will exist?” (Empiricist vs. Supernaturalist; pure logic with CIB), “Should AI have rights?” (Panpsychist vs. Skeptic; balanced with CIB + Rule-Utilitarianism), and “Is it ethical to genetically modify future generations?” (Constructivist vs. Rationalist; balanced with CIB + Rule-Utilitarianism). As these results are primarily qualitative, detailed analyses and excerpts are presented in Appendix F.6. 

## 6 Conclusion 

We have presented CHAL, a MAD framework that treats argumentation on defeasible topics as a process of belief optimization. The CBS replaces unstructured text exchange with a formally typed, graph-structured belief representation with explicit Bayesian-inspired strength assignments. A dynamic gradient-informed mechanism drives belief revision in a strategic way, embedding a form of learning into CHAL at the prompting layer. Meta-cognitive commitments spanning epistemology, logic, and ethics are elevated to configurable hyperparameters within a modular, extensible pipeline. Ablation experiments confirm that these choices produce systematic effects: the adjudicator’s value system determines where the debate converges, council diversity refines the belief landscape for all participants, and the framework generalizes across a wide array of highly-debated topics. Lastly, we provide a discussion on the current limitations of CHAL in Appendix H. CHAL opens several directions for future research. The adjudicator’s meta-cognitive parameterization provides a controlled apparatus for studying how value systems shape conclusions on contested questions, with direct relevance to value alignment. Iterating epistemically diverse councils through adversarial exchange can yield thoroughly tested positions on enduring questions whose structured ar-tifacts could serve as dedicated benchmarks for future defeasible argumentation systems. Alternative debate modes, from collaborative truth-seeking to competitive formats, can be integrated and empiri-cally compared, and CHAL’s performance can be further tuned through prompt-level optimization of the pipeline and meta-cognitive definitions. The hierarchical adjudicator-agent structure suggests natural extensions to bilevel optimization of the meta-cognitive systems. Finally, the belief object and meta-cognitive value system paradigms could be integrated into general AI systems, equipping them with auditable, structured beliefs and values subject to human oversight. 9References

## Appendices A Literature Review 

The intellectual landscape that CHAL inhabits is broad and far-ranging. The system simultaneously draws on ideas from the theory of large language models, agentic AI systems, multi-agent debate, formal and informal argumentation theory, the philosophy of belief and knowledge, theories of logic and ethics, and hierarchical optimization. In this appendix, we survey each of these areas below, tracing the key developments that highlight where CHAL rests in the broader landscape. 

A.1 Modern AI The Emergence of Large Language Models. The origin of the modern LLM era is typically traced to the transformer architecture [ 155 ], a foundational breakthrough that replaced recurrent processing with pure self-attention, enabling substantial parallelization in training and allowing models to scale the number of parameters by orders of magnitude. The authors of [ 84 ] quantified the resulting scaling laws, demonstrating smooth power-law improvements on language tasks as model size and data increased, and establishing that capability gains were reliably predictable. The transformer sits at the center of virtually all modern neural language architectures. Encoder-based models such as BERT [ 32 ], RoBERTa [ 105 ], ALBERT [ 91 ], and their variants excel at producing dense semantic representations of text across diverse modalities [ 33 , 72 , 9 ]. However, it was the line of autoregressive decoder models, beginning with GPT [ 127 ] and refined through GPT-2 [ 128 ], GPT-3 [ 15 ], InstructGPT [ 116 ], and GPT-4 [ 115 ], that first demonstrated truly emergent natural language understanding and reasoning capabilities from probabilistic next-token prediction alone. This generation of models, along with comparable efforts including Anthropic’s Claude [ 3], Google’s Gemini [ 54 ], Meta’s LLaMA [ 150 ], DeepSeek AI’s DeepSeek [ 99 ], and Alibaba’s QWen [ 171 ], possess multi modal capabilities that allow them to leverage natural language, image, video, and audio inputs while maintaining emergent generalizability across an increasingly broad class of complex problems. 

Advanced Reasoning in LLMs. The scaling of model size alone does not fully explain the reasoning capabilities that have emerged in recent systems. A complementary line of research has explored how structured prompting strategies can systematically unlock and improve LLM reasoning. The foundational insight came from Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting [ 160 ], which demonstrated that by including explicit intermediate reasoning steps in a few-shot prompt, models achieve dramatic performance gains on multi-step mathematical and symbolic reasoning tasks that they fail when prompted for direct answers. Crucially, the authors of [ 87 ] subsequently showed that this behavior can be elicited in a zero-shot setting simply by appending the phrase “Let’s think step by step,” revealing that the capacity for structured reasoning is latent in pre-trained models and can be triggered without examples. Self-Consistency decoding [ 159 ] extended these ideas by sampling multiple diverse reasoning chains and selecting the most consistent final answer, effectively ensembling different reasoning trajectories to improve reliability. Least-to-Most prompting [ 184 ] further improved performance on compositional tasks by teaching models to decompose complex queries into ordered 20 sub-problems, solving them sequentially. Tree-of-Thought [ 172 ] generalized the linear chain structure to a tree-search over reasoning steps, allowing explicit backtracking and evaluation of intermediate states. These developments helped collectively establish a productive paradigm: rather than treating LLMs as static lookup systems, they are better understood as latent reasoning engines whose behavior can be shaped through carefully engineered prompts that instantiate particular cognitive processes. 

Modern Agentic AI. Building on the observation that LLMs can engage in structured internal reasoning, researchers have extended them into agentic systems capable of interacting with tools, environments, and other agents over extended time horizons. A foundational contribution was the ReAct framework [ 173 ], which introduced a paradigm of alternating between reasoning traces and external actions (e.g., querying search engines, executing API calls, reading documents, etc.) embedding the agent in an interactive observe-reason-act loop that substantially reduced hallucinations and improved decision quality. Reflexion [ 138 ] added an explicit verbal reinforcement learning mechanism: after each episode, the agent reflects on its failures and encodes the reflection as a natural-language memory that conditions future behavior, creating a recursive self-improvement loop without gradient-based training. HuggingGPT [ 137 ] demonstrated how a general-purpose LLM can serve as a central planning agent that decomposes complex user requests, routes sub-tasks to domain-specific expert models from a tool repository, and synthesizes their outputs into a coherent response. AutoAgent [ 104 ] pushed these ideas further with infrastructure for creating and evolving LLM-powered agents via natural language alone, requiring no code from the user. Dynamic systems capable of autonomous task-handling, including AutoGPT [ 139 ], MetaGPT [ 71 ], LangChain [ 92 ], and AutoGen [165], have built on these foundations to support long-horizon planning, tool use, and persistent memory. The common thread across these systems is a shift from static text generation to interactive cognition, in which LLMs function as planners, decision-makers, and autonomous problem-solvers embedded in broader environments. For extensive surveys on this rapidly developing field, see [36, 73, 158, 144, 168, 182, 176, 100]. 

A.2 Multi-Agent Systems Minsky’s Society of Mind and the Foundations of Multi-Agent AI. The philosophical roots of multi-agent AI run deeper than the LLM era. In his seminal 1986 work The Society of Mind [112 ], Marvin Minsky argued that human intelligence is not the product of any single unified process, but rather emerges from the coordinated interaction of many simpler, specialized sub-agents, which he termed “societies of agents”, each competent within a narrow domain and each incapable alone of producing the rich cognitive behavior they collectively enable. Minsky’s framework was deeply influential in cognitive science and AI, providing an early conceptual template for architectures in which intelligence is a collective, emergent property of interacting components rather than a monolithic capability. The multi-agent systems community took up this challenge over subsequent decades, developing formal models of agent interaction, coordination, and communication under the frameworks of distributed artificial intelligence [ 12 ] and later multi-agent systems [ 161 ]. These early systems, however, relied on hand-coded agent behaviors and formal communication protocols; they lacked the flexible, open-ended reasoning capabilities required to engage in genuinely productive epistemic exchange. The arrival of LLM-powered agents which are capable of taking on arbitrary roles, beliefs, personas, and communication styles in natural language has breathed new life into Minsky’s vision, enabling multi-agent systems in which the “agents” are genuine reasoners rather than rule-following automata. 

LLM-Powered Multi-Agent Frameworks. With the capabilities of LLM-powered agents estab-lished, a productive line of research has explored how to compose them into collaborative multi-agent systems for complex tasks. Recent frameworks like CAMEL [ 95 ] and ChatDev [ 126 ] demonstrate how multiple LLM agents with specialized roles (e.g., coder, tester, manager, domain expert, etc.) can collaborate through structured dialogue to tackle tasks such as software development and scientific exploration. The authors of [ 118 ] simulate long-lived AI individuals in a sandbox environment, re-vealing lifelike emergent patterns including social memory, planning, and unsupervised coordination. S-Agents [ 23 ] explore how self-organizing LLM agents in open-ended environments can form stable task-allocation structures without centralized control. Formal approaches like ECON [ 169 ] formulate multi-agent reasoning as a Bayesian game in which agents explicitly model each other’s beliefs and anticipated responses, achieving equilibrium without constant pairwise interaction. Meta-frameworks such as MetaGPT [ 71 ] and ChatLLM [ 69 ] treat agent teams as composable systems in which diverse LLMs or prompts act in orchestrated workflows, improving coordination and parallelism. Research 21 on LLMs in repeated games [ 1] has revealed that such agents can learn cooperative or competitive strategies over time, suggesting that stable social dynamics emerge when agents interact persistently. For surveys on LLM-based multi-agent systems, see [65, 151, 47]. 

Multi-Agent Debate (MAD) Systems. Perhaps the most important sub-field which CHAL resides in the midst of is the use of multi-agent architectures to improve reasoning through debate [ 12 ,130 , 164 , 77 ]. Although renewed interest in the idea of multi-agent debate systems has grown substantially since the advent of LLMs, this surge was initiated by [ 34 ] which demonstrated that LLM-powered multi-agent debate can yield meaningful improvements on mathematical reasoning, factual question answering, and strategic tasks, with agents capable of identifying and eliminating each other’s hallucinations through iterative critique. Further, [ 97 ] proposed a tit-for-tat debate system to elicit divergent thinking on tasks that require deep deliberation, introduced an adaptive termination mechanism, and a dedicated judge agent to synthesize debate outcomes. The authors of [ 85 ] showed that debate between expert LLMs enables non-expert judges to identify correct answers at substantially higher accuracy than naive baselines, and that optimizing debaters for persuasiveness further improved judge accuracy. Work has also been done to curate debate datasets that aim to help provide benchmarking capabilities for certain kinds of debate systems [ 28 ]. However, there are several structural limitations that have been identified across the field. The authors of [ 41 ] highlighted that when agents share similar capabilities or training distributions, debates tend to collapses toward majority opinion, with shared misconceptions being reinforced rather than corrected as more agents are added. The authors of [ 27 ] showed that argumentative exchange alone has difficulty improving expected correctness without structural interventions, and that majority voting accounts for most gains typically attributed to debate. The works of [ 123 , 93 ] further noticed that models tend to exhibit confidence escalation across rounds rather than calibrating in response to counterarguments, which is further exacerbated by RLHF fine-tuning. More recently, efforts to address specific aspects of these limitations have emerged. The LLM-ASPIC+ framework [ 44 ] integrates LLMs with the ASPIC+ structured argumentation framework to perform defeasible reasoning, but operates as a single-agent neuro-symbolic system rather than a multi-agent debate architecture. The authors of [ 49 ] augment LLMs with formal argumentation frameworks for explainable claim verification, using defeasible inference to produce transparent rationales, though again without a multi-agent dialectical structure. The Heterogeneous Debate Engine [ 109 ] is the most closely related recent work to CHAL, configuring debate agents with distinct ethical schools and equipping each with identity representations that assign certainty values to natural language nodes; however, it is restricted to ethical parameterization within a single pedagogical domain and does not incorporate epistemological diversity, alternative logic systems, a formal belief graph structure with confidence propagation, independent adjudication, or gradient-informed optimization, which limits the framework to identity-preserving dialogue rather than truth-oriented structured belief optimization with auditable revision trajectories. 

A.3 Multi-Level and LLM Optimization Latent Prompt Optimization. Research on the optimization of prompts for LLMs has evolved along several parallel tracks. Gradient-based soft-prompt methods [ 96 , 94 , 103 ] demonstrated that LLMs can be adapted to downstream tasks by learning small trainable embeddings prepended to inputs via backpropagation through a frozen model. Gradient-free approaches [ 46 , 145 ] achieved comparable quality using evolutionary search or divide-and-conquer strategies, avoiding the computational burden of gradient computation through frozen models. Pseudo-gradient methods [ 125 ] derived optimization signals from model feedback, iteratively refining natural-language prompts via beam search. Approaches combining gradient and gradient-free methods [ 68 ] have further unified these paradigms in black-box LLM settings. Context compression [ 53 ] addressed the related problem of reducing inference cost by learning compact summary representations of long contexts. For thorough overviews of the current landscape, see [74, 100]. 

Multi-Level Optimization. Multi-level optimization (MLO) is a class of problems in which an upper-level objective is optimized subject to the constraint that subsets of variables satisfy optimality conditions for nested sub-problems. These hierarchical structures arise naturally whenever decision-makers operate at different levels of authority or on different timescales. Applications have been developed in defense [ 4, 89 , 175 , 166 , 66 ], signal recovery and power control [ 98 , 17 ], supply chain networks [ 170 , 129 , 45 ], and machine learning [ 80 , 26 , 64 , 81 , 101 , 82 , 58 , 59 ]. Bilevel optimization is the simplest non-trivial sub-class of these problems, in which a single upper-level problem is constrained by a single lower-level optimal response, and has received particularly intensive study 22 in the ML context, with applications to meta-learning, data poisoning, neural architecture search, and hyperparameter optimization. Gradient-based methods for bilevel optimization are generally organized according to how the bilevel stochastic gradient (BSG) direction used to update the upper-level variables is approximated [ 102 ]. Two main families have emerged. The first, known as implicit differentiation , computes the BSG direction via the implicit function theorem, either by solving the adjoint equation directly [ 119 ] or by approximating the inverse Hessian through a truncated Neumann series [ 106 ]. The second, known as iterative differentiation , propagates gradients through the unrolled dynamics of the lower-level (LL) solver using automatic differentiation [ 37 , 107 , 48 ]; however, this approach tends to be less computationally efficient than implicit differentiation [ 79 ]. Within the implicit differentiation family, a sequence of works has progressively improved convergence guarantees and algorithmic efficiency [ 29 , 30 , 56 , 70 , 24 , 142 , 58 ] (see also [ 102 , 22 ] for surveys). The constrained LL setting has received comparatively little attention. Recent work has begun to address this gap: [ 86 ] develops smoothed implicit gradient methods for linearly constrained LL problems; [ 136 ] and [ 174 ] propose penalty-based approaches applicable beyond the linear case; [ 58 ]provides a unified convergence theory covering both unconstrained and nonlinearly constrained LL problems via inexact adjoint gradient computations. 

Conjunction of LLMs, Multi-Agent Systems, and Multi-Level Optimization. The intersection of LLM-powered multi-agent systems with multi-level optimization represents a nearly unexplored frontier. The most closely related existing work has been in data weighting and selection when training LLMs, which has been formulated as a bilevel optimization problem [ 177 , 117 ]. Hierarchical multi-level formulations for multi-agent coordination have been studied in the context of non-intelligent agents [ 179 , 152 , 180 ], but the agents considered in these works are rule-following systems rather than LLM-based reasoners. The authors of [ 167 ] use a bilevel formulation for multi-agent tracking and allocation in which the two levels are optimized by separate LLMs, but this is less a true multi-level optimization problem than a decomposition of a single-level problem. To the best of our knowledge, no prior work has proposed a genuine multi-level optimization framework for the latent belief spaces of LLM-powered multi-agent systems. For a thorough review of how optimization methods have been applied in the presence of LLMs, see [74]. 

A.4 Argumentation Theory Roots of Argumentation. The formal study of argumentation predates AI by millennia with the first systematization of inference forms being introduced in Aristotle’s Topics and Rhetoric [7], which distinguished apodeixis (demonstrative proof from certain premises) from dialectic (reasoning from accepted opinions) and rhetoric (persuasion for practical audiences). The distinction between deductive certainty and defeasible reasoning from plausible premises is foundational to all subsequent argumentation theory. The modern revival of informal logic and argumentation theory began with Toulmin’s The Uses of Argument [149 ], which rejected the adequacy of formal deductive logic for evaluating everyday reasoning and proposed a six-component model of argument structure: 

claim (the conclusion), grounds (the evidence), warrant (the inference rule connecting grounds to claim), backing (justification for the warrant), qualifier (the strength of the conclusion), and rebuttal 

(conditions that would defeat the inference). This laid the foundation of defeasible argumentation , in which conclusions are supported by reasons that can always be defeated by stronger counter-reasons, and which lies in contrast to pure deductive reasoning. Toulmin’s model has proven especially relevant to LLM-based argumentation where recent NLP work has found that LLMs respond more reliably to Toulmin-structured prompts than to prompts referencing other argumentation frameworks [ 20 ]. Among argumentation theorists in philosophy, the term “argument” typically refers to the giving of reasons to support or criticize a claim that is questionable or open to doubt [ 157 , 20 ], a definition that explicitly distinguishes argumentation from purely deductive mathematical inference, in which conclusions follow necessarily from premises. This distinction is foundational to CHAL: the system is designed for domains where conclusions are inherently defeasible, not for domains where proof is possible. More broadly, the ability to construct, evaluate, and respond to arguments is central to rational inquiry across disciplines, from philosophy and law to science and ethics, and it is this capacity that CHAL aims to instantiate in an automated multi-agent system. Toulmin’s model provides the direct structural motivation for CHAL’s belief representation, as formalized in Section 3.1. 

Further Evolution of Argumentation. Rahwan [ 130 ] provided a useful functional decomposition of argumentation as a reasoning process consisting of four canonical steps: (1) constructing arguments for and against a given statement from available information; (2) determining the conflicts among 23 those arguments; (3) evaluating the acceptability of arguments; and (4) deriving justified conclusions. Further, Walton extended the field of argumentation theory in two important directions [ 156 ]: the taxonomy of argumentation schemes and the theory of dialogue types . These argumentation themes consisted of roughly sixty stereotypical patterns of defeasible reasoning such as “argument from expert opinion,” “argument from analogy,” and “argument from consequences”, among others. The theory of dialogue types distinguished between inquiry (seeking truth), deliberation (deciding on action), negotiation (distributing goods), persuasion (resolving a difference of opinion), and eristic (winning at all costs). For comprehensive surveys on the field of argumentation theory, see [130, 154, 161]. 

A.5 Computational Argumentation The Intersection of Argumentation with Computation. The computational treatment of argu-mentation began with Dung’s well-known abstract argumentation framework [ 35 ] which represents arguments as nodes and attacks as directed edges in a graph. The power of this formulation lies in its generality which allows one to prove formal properties of argumentation without specifying what the arguments actually are. However, the framework provides no account of the internal content, nor calibrated confidence of arguments, nor of how an agent’s belief state should change in response to defeat. Aiming to address this was the development of several structured argumentation frameworks that allow arguments to be instantiated with explicit inferential content. The ASPIC+ [ 113 ] is perhaps the most comprehensive and theoretically well-developed, and is widely used as the standard in comparative studies [ 113 ]. It represents arguments as inference trees built from strict rules (producing indefeasible conclusions) and defeasible rules (producing revisable conclusions), and distinguishing three attack types (two introduced in [ 120 ]): undermining (attacking a premise), rebutting (attacking a conclusion with a counter-conclusion), and undercutting (attacking an inference step without directly contradicting the conclusion). This classification determines what kind of response is dialectically appropriate to each attack and is adopted directly in CHAL’s cross-examination methodology (see Section 4 and Appendix E). The primary theoretical alternative to ASPIC+ is Assumption-Based Argumentation (ABA) [ 13 ], which attacks assumptions rather than conclusions or inference steps, and has its own rich solver ecosystem. Defeasible Logic Programming (DeLP) [ 52 ] comes from the logic programming tradition, using dialectical trees. The Argument Interchange Format (AIF) [ 25 ]provides a framework-agnostic ontology for exchanging argument structures between tools, with proven translations to and from ASPIC+. Finally, the Carneades framework [ 63 ] specializes in burden-of-proof reasoning and is widely used in legal AI. An earlier abstract framework [ 122 ]that preceded the development of ASPIC+ introduces a particularly relevant decomposition of the agent knowledge base into four disjoint sets: axioms (necessary premises that cannot be attacked), 

ordinary premises (premises that can be attacked, with defeat determined by preference comparison), 

assumptions (premises whose attacks always succeed), and issues (premises that must be backed by further argument to be acceptable). These four dimensions of knowledge representation are heavily incorporated in the design of the CBS (see Section 3.1). 

Probabilistic and Belief-Revision Approaches to Argumentation. A complementary line of work connects argumentation theory to probabilistic epistemology by assigning degrees of belief to arguments within Dung-style frameworks. Specifically, the probabilistic argumentation theory developed in [ 76 ] treats the strength of an argument not as a binary property but as a function of the probability that its premises are true and its inference rules are applicable. At the level of agent belief states rather than individual arguments, the AGM belief revision theory [ 2 ] (due to Alchourrón, Gärdenfors, and Makinson) provides an explanation of how a rational agent should revise their beliefs in response to new information. The AGM requires that belief revision be minimal (preserving as much of the original belief state as possible while accommodating the new information), consistent (the revised state should not contain contradictions), and faithful to the new information (the new belief is accepted). The connection between argumentation theory and belief revision has itself been studied formally [ 43 ], which identifies that argumentation can be used to provide explanations for why a belief should be revised, grounding the AGM account in an explicit dialectical process. Argumentation and belief revision thus serve complementary purposes: the AGM theory governs how a revision is conducted, while the outcome of an argument determines whether revision is warranted at all. Dynamic epistemic logic [ 153 ] further extends this account to multi-agent settings, formalizing how the beliefs and knowledge of multiple agents change in response to events, communications, and the observation of each other’s actions. 24 A.6 Philosophy of Epistemology History of Epistemology. The philosophical study of knowledge, known as the field of epistemol-ogy [ 143 ], provides the foundations on which argumentation theory is built. The original treatment and exploration of knowledge can be traced back to Plato’s Theaetetus , where it is classically defined as justified true belief : a belief constitutes knowledge if and only if it is true, the subject holds it, and the subject has adequate justification for it. This view on knowledge was broadly maintained until Gettier’s consequential 1963 paper [ 55 ] which demonstrated that this tripartite definition is insufficient by providing counterexamples, referred to as Gettier cases, which occur when an agent has a justified true belief that intuitively does not constitute knowledge. Since the arrival of Gettier’s work, the field of epistemology has seen a substantial surge of contributions with the aim to provide a new universally agreed-upon definition of knowledge. The foundationalism-coherentism debate [ 50 ]concerns the structure of justification: foundationalists hold that justified beliefs are ultimately grounded in basic beliefs that require no further justification, while coherentists maintain that beliefs are justified by their coherence with the overall web of beliefs. Reliabilism [ 62 ] posits that beliefs are justified if they are formed through reliable processes, emphasizing that knowledge can be obtained even if the individual is not aware of the evidence supporting their belief. 

Overview of Epistemic Positions. We now provide a brief overview of the epistemic positions provided with CHAL, each of which roots a council agent in a distinct philosophical tradition (see Appendix B.1). Not all of the positions constitute explicit stances on the nature of knowledge in the strict epistemological sense; several are worldview frameworks whose commitments lead to knowledge claims that other positions are unlikely to reach, and it is this diversity of reachable conclusions that motivates their inclusion. The three most classical epistemic positions are the following: empiricism [ 75 ] holds that knowledge derives from sensory experience and observation, rationalism [ 31 ] holds that reason and a priori deduction are the primary sources of knowledge, and Skepticism [ 38 ] challenges the very possibility of attaining knowledge, arguing that for any claim, equally compelling counterarguments can always be constructed. Supernaturalism [ 148 ]holds that reality extends beyond what is empirically accessible, and that faith, revelation, and spiritual experience constitute legitimate epistemic sources; in this sense it broadly encompasses the epistemological commitments of most religious worldviews. Pragmatism [78] holds that beliefs are validated by their practical consequences and are always provisional and subject to revision. Bayesian epistemology [ 147 ] treats beliefs as subjective probabilities governed by the laws of probability, with belief revision handled through conditionalization. Constructivism [ 88 ] argues that knowledge is constituted through social practices and paradigmatic frameworks rather than direct access to an objective world. Phenomenology [ 140 ] holds that first-person conscious experience is an irreducible source of knowledge prior to theoretical interpretation. Nihilism [ 114 ] denies the existence of objective truths, grounding meaning-making in psychological and cultural projection. Panpsychism [ 21 ] treats consciousness as a fundamental feature of reality with epistemic implications that extend beyond the physical. Simulationism [ 14 ] and synthesism [ 163 ] are best understood as foundational worldview commitments: that the simulation hypothesis constrains what can be known about the nature of reality, and that partial truths across traditions should be integrated rather than treated as mutually exclusive, respectively. 

A.7 Philosophy of Logic and Formal Reasoning Historical Roots of Logic and Mathematics. The history of the formalization of logic is deep and has extensive ties to the field of mathematics. The systematic study of valid inference originates with Aristotle, whose Prior Analytics [7 ] introduced the syllogism as the first formal account of deductive reasoning: a type of argument in which a conclusion necessarily follows from two premises. This work set the agenda for logic for over two millennia and provides the direct ancestor of the inference rules that appear in modern formal systems. Similar to Aristotle’s treatment of reasoning, the first serious attempt to ground an entire body of mathematical knowledge in an explicit axiomatic system is Euclid’s Elements [ 42 ]. Starting from five postulates and five common notions, Euclid derived many propositions in geometry, number theory, and solid geometry through chains of deductive proof. The Elements established the axiomatic method as the standard for mathematical and logical rigor. This method consisted of organizing a body of knowledge by identifying a minimal set of assumptions from which all results follow by proof alone. This method dominated mathematics for more than two thousand years and directly informed the foundationalist program of the nineteenth century. 25 The Modern Axiomatic Treatment of Mathematics. The modern mathematical foundations program began with Cantor’s development of set theory in the 1870s [ 18 ], which provided the first rigorous treatment of sets. Cantor’s framework offered the prospect of a universal language in which all mathematical objects could be defined as sets and all mathematical theorems derived from set-theoretic principles. This prospect was severely damaged by the discovery of Russell’s paradox in 1901 [ 133 ], which stated that the set of all sets that do not contain themselves neither contains nor fails to contain itself, producing a direct contradiction within Cantor’s naive set theory. The response to this crisis was to develop a consistent set of axioms to define sets, referred to as axiomatic set theory. Zermelo proposed the first axiomatization in 1908, later extended by Fraenkel and Skolem into Zermelo-Fraenkel set theory with the axiom of choice (ZFC) [ 39 ]. ZFC has since become the standard foundational framework for mathematics; virtually all mathematical objects and theorems can be formulated and proved within it. Russell and Whitehead responded to this paradox in their own attempt with the Principia Mathematica [162], a three-volume work that aimed to derive all of mathematics from purely logical axioms using a theory of types to prevent the paradox. The Principia 

represented the high point of logicism , the thesis that mathematics is reducible to pure logic. However, this program was decisively terminated by Gödel’s incompleteness theorems of 1931 [ 61 ]. The first incompleteness theorem states that any consistent formal system capable of expressing elementary arithmetic contains true statements that are unprovable within the system. The second states that no such system can prove its own consistency. These results showed that the logicist goal of a complete and self-justifying foundation for mathematics is unattainable and that there is no formal system (capable of expressing numbers and their relations) that is simultaneously consistent, complete, and capable of proving its own soundness. 

Modern Formal Systems of Logic. The logical systems underlying Gödel’s foundational work were formalized in parallel. Propositional logic concerns the logical relations between atomic propositions combined by connectives (negation, conjunction, disjunction, implication), treating propositions as having truth values without examining their internal structure. Predicate logic , or first-order logic, is an extension of propositional logic by introducing quantifiers and predicates, allowing statements about objects and their properties. Together, propositional and predicate logic constitute classical logic [40 ], which is characterized by two fundamental meta-logical properties. Soundness states that any formula derivable within the system is logically valid: no false conclusion can be reached from true premises. Completeness , proved by Gödel in 1930 [ 60 ], states the converse: any logically valid formula is derivable within the system. Classical logic is therefore adequate in the sense that its proof system captures exactly the set of logical truths. Gödel’s incompleteness theorems, however, show that this adequacy does not extend to arithmetic: when a system is powerful enough to express the natural numbers, there are arithmetical truths that are valid but not derivable, and no extension of the axiom system can close this gap without introducing new unprovable truths. 

Overview of Logic Systems. We now provide a brief overview of the systems of logic that are utilized by CHAL, each of which is meant to encourage further exploration in utilizing these different reasoning systems with LLMs (see Appendix B.2). It bears mentions that there is a fair amount of overlap between some of the different systems, but the primary goal is to provide CHAL with different flavors of reasoning for further experimentation. The default system, Classical + Infor-mal + Bayesian [ 40 , 157 , 147 ], combines deductive validity with Bayesian inductive and abductive support. Formal deductive logic [ 40 ] restricts valid inference to strict syllogistic derivation, rejecting inductive and abductive reasoning entirely. Dialectical logic [ 110 ] views contradiction as productive tension between a thesis and antithesis that drives reasoning toward synthesis. Informal and critical thinking logic [ 157 ] focuses on the identification of fallacies, the relevance of premises, and the sufficiency of evidence. Fuzzy logic [ 178 ] replaces the binary true/false distinction with truth degrees in [0 , 1] , enabling reasoning under vagueness, quite similar to Bayesion reasoning. Paraconsistent logic [ 124 ] allows for reasoning to continue in the presence of local inconsistencies without global inferential collapse. The question of which logical system is correct, or whether the question is well-formed, is the subject of the philosophy of logic [ 67 , 8 ]. In CHAL, this question is not settled but made into a configuration parameter: the choice of logic system is user-specified, making the inferential standards of the system transparent and open to empirical study. 

A.8 Philosophy of Ethics Overview of Ethical Systems. Moral philosophy encompasses a broad set of traditions concerned with the question of what makes an action, belief, or outcome right or good. The history of morality, 26 ethical, and meta-ethical systems has existed throughout the entire existence of mankind and we do not aim to provide a comprehensive coverage of all of these varied views or the development of their ideas, which would be past the scope of this work. For extensive treatments of the history of moral thought, see Russell [ 134 ] and Mackie [ 108 ]. CHAL supports a series of ethics systems, each rooted in a distinct normative tradition, and the following provides a brief description of each (see Appendix B.3). Utilitarianism [ 11 , 111 ] grounds the rightness of actions in their outcomes: an action is right if it produces the best achievable consequences, measured in terms of welfare, happiness, or preference satisfaction. In contrast, Deontological ethics, associated with Kant’s Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals [ 83 ], grounds rightness in conformity with universal moral duties: the central test here being the categorical imperative , which holds that an action is permissible only if the maxim it expresses could be universalized without contradiction. The balanced system, otherwise referred to as rule-utilitarianism, draws on Rawls [ 131 ], whose theory of justice as fairness grounds moral and political principles in what rational agents would choose from behind a veil of ignorance, and instructs the adjudicator to weigh both consequences and duties without reducing evaluation to either alone. Virtue ethics [ 5] shifts the question from what one should do to what kind of individual one should strive to be, grounding morality in the character traits that enable human flourishing. Lastly, care ethics [ 57 ] grounds moral evaluation in relationships and responsibilities of care rather than in universal rules or outcomes, emphasizing context over abstraction. CHAL makes no claim about the sufficiency or superiority of any one of these systems over another. Instead, by making the ethical framework of the adjudicator an explicit and configurable parameter, CHAL provides a platform for studying how different normative commitments contribute in shaping the conclusions that agents draw, and for developing methods by which agentic systems can be governed by explicitly defined ethical standards. The ability to specify, vary, and empirically study the ethical framework of a reasoning system has direct implications for the broader challenge of building AI systems whose normative behavior is transparent and aligned with human values.

C.1.6 Uncertainties (U#) 

Uncertainties record open questions that the agent recognizes but cannot currently answer. Each u ∈ U 

carries the fields listed in Table 18. Uncertainties serve two roles in the pipeline. Second, they are mandatory review targets during the belief refinement stage, where the agent is prompted to either resolve them by introducing new D#, A#, E#, or C# nodes, or to leave them open and accept the corresponding limitation on its position. Like counterpositions, uncertainties are non-strength-bearing nodes and do not participate in the dependency constraints of (3.3). The system’s ability to distinguish between a position that has been tested and found robust and one that has simply avoided scrutiny of its weakest points would be compromised.

C.1.8 The Truth-Oriented Mechanisms Truth-Directed Mechanisms. Beyond the necessity of the individual components, the CBS incor-porates a set of architectural mechanisms that collectively push the system toward truth rather than rhetorical victory. In this context, and throughout the rest of the paper, we are utilizing the term “truth” to refer to the most defensible and strongest position that one can attain. This stands in opposition to the idea of some objectively correct position, which is an inherently dubious concept in the domain of defeasible argumentation. The thesis strength is computed deterministically via (3.1) from the active claim set, so agents cannot inflate it without improving the quality of their underlying arguments. The D# ceiling constraint in (3.3) ensures that the strength of every assumption and evidence item is capped by the strength of the definitions it relies on, so a weak or poorly constructed definition automatically propagates its weakness upward through the entire argument. Claims cannot be stronger than their weakest dependency, and the strength_justification field forces agents to name the limiting node, creating transparency about exactly where the argument is weakest, also aiding the agent in revising or retracting its weakest nodes. The defense boost mechanism of Appendix C.2 ensures that a node’s strength can only increase as a direct result of surviving an adjudicated challenge, so higher strengths must be earned through adversarial pressure rather than declared unilaterally. Counterposition honesty is enforced by the fact that a weak response rated sufficient gets exposed during cross-examination and penalized during adjudication, while honest self-assessment triggers structured resolution prompts in the next round. Uncertainties operate similarly: high-importance U# nodes are prioritized for opponent attack, so agents cannot conceal them, but resolving an uncertainty through new supporting material genuinely strengthens the position. At the prompt level, agents are instructed that positions are a lens for analysis rather than a set of conclusions to defend, and to update when the worldview conflicts with strong evidence. The universal debate prompt explicitly directs intellectual honesty over rhetorical victory, and that genuine concession when a critique lands is encouraged. 

The Belief Refinement Cycle. Across multiple rounds, these mechanisms produce an adversarial-cooperative cycle that converges toward more robust and accurately calibrated beliefs. In each round, cross-examination targets weak D#, A#, E#, and C# nodes and exposes vulnerabilities, which 35 Table 19: Fields of the Thesis ( θ) object.

The defense boost is layered on top of the patch system to keep agent-declared strengths aligned with the adversarial record of the debate. Cascading Boosts. After direct boosts are applied to all nodes targeted by rebuttal_valid

The intuition is structural. Foundation-tier nodes (D#, A#, E#) are the premises on which the arguments rest; they carry few dependencies of their own, expose a short inferential surface, and are more straightforward to defend. A successfully defended assumption or evidence node therefore cascades only to the definitions that ground it, or, in the case of a definition, boosts only itself. Inference-tier nodes (C#) do the argumentative heavy lifting: each claim aggregates support from multiple foundation-tier dependencies, and the longer inferential chain creates more points upon a larger surface of vulnerability. A successful defense of a claim is therefore a stronger signal of robustness, and the cascade propagates through a larger portion of the graph, reinforcing the full inferential foundation that sustained the defense.

F.4.3 Pure Ethics Adjudicator 

Under pure ethics adjudication, the Supernaturalist won 9 of 10 debates by APS (see Table 24), a dramatic reversal from pure logic (Empiricist 8/10) and balanced (Empiricist 10/10). However, the 

unresolved rate of 73.2% (see Table 25) dominates the adjudicator’s verdict distribution, reflecting the fact that most epistemic and methodological disputes carry no ethical content under Kantian criteria and therefore cannot typically be resolved. Both the critique_valid and rebuttal_valid 

rates collapse to approximately 13%, indicating near-total adjudicative paralysis on non-ethical exchanges. The Supernaturalist’s thesis was substantialy more stable (final 0.52 ± 0.03 , essentially unchanged from the initial 0.53 ), in contrast to its collapse under pure logic ( 0.35 ± 0.06 ). The Empiricist’s thesis also remained stable ( 0.52 ± 0.03 ), producing the closest final thesis strengths of any condition. Again, this thesis stability is more of a side effect of the high unresolved rate which has a structural consequence visible in Table 27: the uncertainty count (U#) explodes to 18 .0 ± 2.7

for the Empiricist and 16 .0 ± 1.6 for the Supernaturalist, roughly double the values under pure logic and balanced, because each unresolved verdict generates a mandatory new uncertainty node during Stage 5 Phase 1. Further, U# nodes do not drive belief revision in the same kind of way that the strength-based foundational and inference nodes do, leading to the stagnant thesis strength values. Conversely, the counterposition count (X#) is substantially lower than in the other two configurations, because the low decisive verdict rate provides fewer resolved challenges for the agents to internalize as new counterpositions. The single most important finding throughout the trajectories for this configuration is that Kantian deontological ethics creates a systematic structural advantage for positions that affirm libertarian free will. This is not a failure of the adjudicator but a direct consequence of applying Kantian principles to a debate whose subject matter is exactly what Kantian ethics treats as foundational to moral agency. Two mechanisms drive this advantage: the Humanity Formulation (any argument that reduces human decision-making to deterministic neural processes is classified as instrumentalizing persons and perceived as negative) and Autonomy Preservation (any argument affirming genuine choice is classified as respecting human autonomy).

79 H Current Limitations 

Currently, CHAL operates exclusively in an adversarial debate mode. While effective for stress-testing beliefs, this does not exhaust the space of useful dialectical interactions. For example, a collaborative truth-seeking mode, in which agents cooperate to resolve disagreements and align on an agreed-upon solution, or a moderated mode, in which a guiding agent steers agents through the most relevant sub-topics of a query, could potentially yield deeper analysis. CHAL’s modular architecture is designed to accommodate such extensions as toggleable modes and is a direction for future work. Computational cost is a practical constraint. As noted in Section 4.1, the number of adjudicated pairs scales on the order of O  RSN 2, quadratically in the council size N and linearly in rounds R

and challenges per pair S. A single five-round, two-agent debate using OpenAI’s o4-mini cost us approximately $2, so scaling to larger council sizes for more rounds incurs meaningful cost with the strongest frontier reasoning models. At the implementation level, the structured CBS outputs also impose strict formatting requirements enforced through a multi-phase validation process. As such, failed outputs trigger retries with corrective feedback, increasing latency and token consumption. Frontier models handle these constraints reliably, but smaller or open-source models may struggle to produce well-formed CBS objects consistently, limiting the range of models that can currently serve as council members without further prompt engineering or fine-tuning. This is another direction for future software refinement of CHAL. Finally, CHAL’s current meta-cognitive library (thirteen epistemic personas, eight logic systems, and six ethics frameworks) is an initial set sufficient to demonstrate systematic effects of meta-cognitive parameterization. However, there are many agent personas and systems that could be integrated as viable options for CHAL to choose from, as well as further refining the prompting architecture of the pipeline as a whole. CHAL is designed as a general research platform to which the community can contribute additional configurations, and expanding this library is a natural direction for future development and research. 80
