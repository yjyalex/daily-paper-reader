Abstract 

Large Reasoning Models (LRMs) achieve strong performance on mathematical reasoning tasks but remain unreliable on challenging instances. Existing test-time scaling methods, such as repeated sampling, self-correction, and tree search, improve performance at the cost of increased computation, yet often exhibit dimin-ishing returns on hard problems. We observe that output disagreement is strongly correlated with instance difficulty and prediction correctness, providing a useful signal for guiding instance-level strategy selection at test time. Based on this insight, we propose a training-free framework that formulates test-time scaling as an instance-level routing problem, rather than allocating more computation within a single strategy, dynamically selecting among different scaling strategies based on output disagreement. The framework applies lightweight resolution for consistent cases, majority voting for moderate disagreement, and rewriting-based reformulation for highly ambiguous instances. Experiments on seven mathematical benchmarks and three models show that our method improves accuracy by 3%–7% while reducing sampling cost compared to existing approaches. 

1 Introduction 

Large Reasoning Models (LRMs) have achieved high performance on mathematical reasoning benchmarks such as Olympiad problems [ 35 , 26 , 10 ], yet struggle with challenging instances. A common direction is test-time scaling, which allocates additional computation at inference time to improve output quality. Representative approaches include repeated sampling [ 31 , 20 , 23 , 13 ], self-correction [ 27 , 7, 21 , 18 , 2], and tree search methods [ 36 , 37 , 4, 12 ]. While effective, these approaches often suffer from diminishing returns as computation increases [ 33 ], suggesting that computation is not effectively allocated across instances of varying difficulty. Most existing methods primarily focus on adaptively allocating computation within a single strategy, with limited attention to selecting among different strategies. Therefore, a key challenge is not only how to allocate computation, but also how to select appropriate inference strategies for different instances. Among various test-time scaling strategies, rewriting has been shown to be an effective approach for enhancing model performance without retraining, based on the observation that model outputs are highly sensitive to variations in problem formulation. Prior work has explored this idea from different perspectives. Zhou et al. [43] improves robustness via rewriting combined with a voting mechanism. Zhang et al. [41] applies rewriting as a preprocessing step before code generation. More recently, Lai et al. [19] further enforces consistency across prompt formulation, expression, and language style to stabilize model behavior.

Easy instances are defined as those correctly answered in a single sampling, while hard instances are those incorrectly answered in a single sampling. However, while rewriting is effective, it is not universally beneficial. It may alter the semantic meaning of the original instance or introduce incorrect assumptions during reformulation. As shown in Figure 1, rewriting degrades performance on easy instances, while it consistently improves accuracy on hard instances. In contrast, majority voting achieves strong performance on easy instances, but degrades on hard instances. We further observe a strong relationship between instance difficulty, output disagreement, and prediction correctness: hard instances tend to exhibit higher output disagreement, whereas easy instances usually produce more consistent results. Moreover, instances with severe disagreement under majority voting, characterized by a flat distribution over multiple answers, are highly correlated with incorrect predictions. This observation indicates that output disagreement reflects the model’s uncertainty under stochastic sampling, providing a natural signal for guiding strategy selection. In particular, when model outputs are highly consistent, instances are typically easier and majority voting is sufficient; whereas under high disagreement, instances are more likely to be challenging, making rewriting more effective. This reveals a clear performance trade-off between majority voting and rewriting-based strategies and motivates selecting appropriate inference strategies based on model disagreement. Based on this finding, we design a training-free framework without fine-tuning or external models. Rather than introducing new reasoning modules, our framework formulates test-time scaling as an instance-level decision problem, determining which strategies should be applied under different uncertainty conditions. The framework operates by routing instances to different strategies based on the degree of model disagreement, which we interpret as a proxy for uncertainty under stochastic sampling. In the first stage (Disagreement Filter) , we identify no-disagreement instances via a two-sampling consistency check. These instances are directly resolved without additional sampling or rewriting, avoiding unnecessary computation. In the second stage (Vote Resolve) , instances with minor disagreement are further sampled and resolved via majority voting over multiple generations, leveraging increased sampling to improve reliability. In the final stage (Rewrite & Rethink) ,instances with persistent disagreement are rewritten and re-sampled to escape inconsistent reasoning. This framework selects appropriate strategies for different uncertainty levels, improving efficiency by avoiding redundant computation on easy cases while focusing resources on ambiguous one. We conduct experiments on seven mathematical benchmarks and three LRMs. The results demonstrate that our method improves mathematical reasoning accuracy by 3%–7% absolute, with particularly strong gains on challenging benchmarks where the base models perform poorly. Moreover, our method achieves these improvements while using fewer samplings on the evaluated benchmarks. We further validate the generalization ability of our method on code generation tasks. Finally, we provide an analysis of each component, highlighting the effectiveness of the proposed design and the role of rewriting in improving robustness.

2.2 Rewriting 

Rewriting has been widely studied in both information systems and language model reasoning. In database systems, query rewriting is used to improve retrieval efficiency [ 25 ]. Recent work extends this idea to LLMs. RewriteLM [ 28 ] combines instruction tuning and reinforcement learning to optimize rewrite quality, while LLM-R2 [ 22 ] learns to distinguish semantically equivalent but more effective rewrites via contrastive training. Other studies explore prompt optimization and preference-based rewriting [17, 5]. In mathematical reasoning, Zhou et al. [43] demonstrate that surface form significantly affects model performance and propose SCoP, which applies paraphrasing with self-consistency. Zhang et al. [41] adopt a rewrite-then-execute strategy to improve robustness, while Lai et al. [19] enforce multi-dimensional consistency across prompts and expressions. These works highlight rewriting as an effective way to improve reasoning stability and generalization. However, these methods primarily focus on improving single-pass generation quality, and do not explicitly address uncertainty-aware computation allocation during inference. 

3 Methodology 

Our method is illustrated in Figure 2, and consists of three stages: Disagreement Filter , Vote Resolve , and Rewrite & Rethink . We employ two minimal disagreement detectors to partition samples into three categories based on the degree of divergence, namely No Disagreement Samples, Minor Disagreement Samples, and Severe Disagreement Samples. Majority voting is applied to Minor Disagreement Samples, while rewriting is used for Severe Disagreement Samples, and the corresponding results are updated in the global answer. Algorithm 1 provides a step-by-step procedure of how each sample is processed in our framework. Section 3.1 defines the minimal disagreement detector and the sample categorization. Section 3.2 describes the first two stages, including how samples are filtered and partitioned, as well as how iteration steps are determined. Section 3.3 details how final answers for Severe Disagreement Samples are obtained via rewriting.

To exploit this observation, we propose a minimal disagreement detector (MDD), which performs two samplings to obtain a1 and a2. If a1̸ = a2, the sample is identified as exhibiting disagreement; otherwise, it is considered consistent. Answer equivalence is determined using a normalized matching procedure that accounts for numerical, structural, and symbolic equivalence (see Appendix D). As shown in Figure 2, consistent samples are directly accepted without rewriting. This design is motivated by the observation that rewriting is beneficial for hard instances, while providing limited gains for easy ones (Figure 1). Therefore, MDD is designed to maintain high recall of correct predictions while providing a lightweight decision rule for choosing between majority voting and rewriting based on disagreement degree. Table 7 further shows that increasing the number of consistency checks reduces recall of correct instances, motivating the use of a two-sampling design for efficiency. We perform multiple independent evaluations of each sample using MDD. Let di denote the number of detected disagreements for the i-th sample Si. Each disagreement is computed independently within one stage from a single pairwise comparison, without comparing answers across different stages. Based on di, samples are categorized into three groups: 

No Disagreement Samples (NDS): di = 0 . No disagreement is observed, indicating stable model behavior; these samples are directly accepted without additional computation. 

Minor Disagreement Samples (MDS): di = 1 . A single disagreement is observed, indicating moderate uncertainty; additional sampling is required to improve reliability. 

Severe Disagreement Samples (SDS): di ≥ 2. Multiple disagreements indicate highly unstable predictions. The answer distribution is flat, making it difficult to obtain a reliable result by simply increasing the number of samplings. In such cases, sampling alone is insufficient, and rewriting is required to reformulate the input before re-reasoning. To better illustrate the relationship between disagreement level and correctness, we further quantify the relationship between disagreement level and correctness via P (correct | NDS/MDS/SDS ) in Table 8, showing a strong negative correlation between disagreement and accuracy. This suggests that disagreement is a reliable signal for distinguishing samples with different levels of difficulty.

As shown in Figure 2, during the first iteration (Disagreement Filter), the model performs two samplings for all samples and selects one of the sampling results to record as a provisional answer in the global answer, which maintains the current best answer for each sample and is updated via stage-wise overwriting. Simultaneously, the two sampling results are checked by the MDD. Samples with consistent answers are categorized as NDS, and their answers in the global answer remain unchanged. The remaining samples proceed to the second iteration. During the second iteration (Vote Resolve), the model performs two samplings for each remaining sample and continues to use the MDD to check consistency. Samples showing agreement in this round are categorized as MDS. For these MDS, majority voting is applied to all available sampling results (i.e., the two from the first iteration plus the two from the second, making four in total) to determine the final answer, which is then updated in the global answer. The remaining inconsistent samples are categorized as SDS. These samples proceed to the next stage (Rewrite & Rethink), where the final answer is determined via rewriting followed by re-reasoning. 

3.3 Rewriting And Rethinking Mechanism 

After the iterative filtering stage, the remaining samples are marked as SDS, indicating that the generated answers exhibit a high degree of uncertainty. For such samples, it is difficult to derive a reliable answer through majority voting. We therefore employ rewriting to determine the final answer. This method requires neither additional training nor the introduction of external models. Instead of generating additional reasoning paths, the model is prompted to reformulate the problem and reattempt solving it from an alternative formulation of the problem. We apply the rewriting prompt template shown in Appendix C to each SDS for a single rewrite, generating a rewritten set Srewrite . The rewriting step aims to reformulate the surface expression of the problem while preserving its underlying semantics and final answer. For each sample in Srewrite ,we perform a single sampling to obtain an answer arewrite , which is recorded in the global answer. By altering the surface form of the problem, this process encourages the model to reconsider the task from a different perspective, helping it escape previously incorrect reasoning trajectories. 4 Experiments 

4.1 Experimental Settings 

We evaluate our approach on seven widely used mathematical reasoning benchmarks, including GSM8K [ 8], Math500 [ 15 ], Gaokao2023en [ 40 ], Olympiadbench [ 14 ], AMC23, AIME24, and AIME25, using three base models (Qwen3-4B [ 30 ], Qwen3-8B, and DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B [9]). We use accuracy of the final answers as the evaluation metric. To better understand the performance of our method, we compare with four representative methods: majority voting, dynamic voting [ 33 ], Best-of-N with an outcome reward model, and SCoP [ 43 ]. The first three are abbreviated as Majority, DV, and BoN. For fair comparison, we define a sampling as any base model generation, including both reasoning and rewriting, and constrain all methods to six samplings per instance. Our method uses at most six samplings per instance (2 for stage-1, 2 for stage-2, 1 for rewriting, and 1 for re-reasoning). The Majority method samples six times per instance and selects the final answer through simple voting; the DV method allows up to six samplings per instance and selects the final answer through a confidence-based voting mechanism with a threshold of 0.7, selected based on a threshold sensitivity analysis (Table 10); BoN also samples six times per instance, then uses the Skywork-Reward-V2-Llama-3.1-8B-40M [ 24 ] model as an outcome reward model to score each answer and choose the one with the highest reward as the final answer; SCoP, on the other hand, applies one round of problem rewriting to all instances, followed by five rounds of sampling, and selects the final answer through simple voting. We use temperature 0.6, top-p 0.95, and top-k 20, and run three seeds (0, 42, 777). Experiments are conducted under 8K and 16K contexts (Table 1 and 5) on the Qwen2.5-Math 2 framework with 8 NVIDIA A100 GPUs. We further evaluate our method on a larger-scale model (Qwen3-32B) on a subset of benchmarks to verify scalability, with results shown in Table 11.

4.2 Main Results 

The main experimental results are shown in Table 1. Our method outperforms baselines on almost all benchmarks across three models, achieving the highest average accuracy. Through experiments, we find that significant improvements are achieved on all benchmarks except GSM8K, while on GSM8K, the performance changes of Qwen3-8B and Qwen3-4B are not as pronounced as those of DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B. This is because Qwen3-4B and Qwen3-8B already achieve high scores on GSM8K, with only about 5% of samples answered incorrectly, indicating that GSM8K contains only a small fraction of samples that cause the model to disagree. As shown in Table 6, for Qwen3-4B and Qwen3-8B on the GSM8K benchmark, the SDS after two rounds of iterative filtering account for only 1.5% to 2% of the total samples, making rewriting yield almost no improvement; in contrast, for DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B, SDS constitute 16% of the total samples, suggesting that the quantity of SDS influences the effectiveness of rewriting to some extent. In addition, we record the number of samplings used in the experiments. We observe that, across three models, our method requires significantly fewer samplings than other methods while achieving comparable or even higher accuracy. We select three benchmarks (Math500, Olympiad, and AIME24) for illustration and visualize the results in Figure 3. On Math500 and Olympiad, our method uses approximately 40% to 60% of the maximum sampling budget, and even on the more challenging AIME24, it uses only about 70% of the maximum sampling budget. We also show in Figure 4 the average output tokens and wall-clock time for each sample.

5 Analysis and Discussion 

5.1 Ablation Study 

To verify the contribution of each component in our framework, we conducted an ablation study on the Qwen3-8B. We sequentially present the experimental results of four settings: the model without any component (Baseline), the version with only the rewriting component (OnlyRewrite), the version with only the majority voting component (OnlyMajority), and the complete framework with all components integrated (Full Method). As shown in Table 3, when applying only the rewriting mechanism OnlyRewrite, the model shows a significant improvement over the baseline, especially on more challenging benchmarks such as Olympiad and AIME24. This indicates that rewriting is more effective on difficult problems that tend to cause disagreement. The OnlyMajority also improves over the baseline, but the improvement is less pronounced than that of OnlyRewrite, likely because majority voting struggles to effectively resolve highly ambiguous or difficult samples with severe disagreement, whereas rewriting demonstrates stronger efficacy on such samples. In contrast, for easier problems, majority voting can often achieve high accuracy without rewriting, highlighting the task-dependent nature of each component. When combining both the rewriting and majority voting mechanisms, the method achieves the best performance, highlighting the synergy between the two components: the rewriting module improves accuracy on challenging tasks, while the majority voting mechanism filters out noise generated from ambiguous samples. Together, they enable the framework to achieve comprehensive improvements in both accuracy and reasoning stability. To further evaluate the robustness of our method under different sampling budgets, we conduct additional experiments on Math500 with Qwen3-8B using 4 and 8 samplings, as shown in Table 4. To accommodate different budgets, we accordingly adjust the number of iterations in our framework. Specifically, under the 4-sampling setting, our method performs one iteration (two samplings) followed by a Rewrite & Rethink step, while under the 8-sampling setting, it performs three iterations (six samplings) followed by a Rewrite & Rethink step.

Ineffective Rewriting In another class of cases, rewriting offers no improvement. The errors in these situations primarily stem from: answer formats that do not conform to the required specifications

Therefore, even if rewriting provides a clearer version of the problem, the model is still likely to repeat the same formatting or arithmetic errors during its entire reasoning process. 

Harmful Rewriting In our experiments, we find that rewriting is not always effective and can even cause originally correct answers to become incorrect. We identify an important reason for this: the original question is already simple, but the rewritten version compresses the text excessively, removing or obscuring key conditions, which leads the model to reason incorrectly (over-compression error) .Therefore, avoiding rewriting short and clear questions reduces the negative impact of rewriting. These results indicate that our disagreement-based routing strategy more effectively identifies when rewriting should be applied, increasing effective rewrites while reducing harmful ones. 

6 Conclusion 

This work proposes a training-free framework that formulates test-time scaling as an instance-level strategy routing problem, using model disagreement as a lightweight signal to guide the selection of inference strategies. By dynamically routing instances to different strategies, the framework applies more appropriate strategies to uncertain samples while avoiding unnecessary or harmful processing on easier ones. We evaluate our method on seven mathematical reasoning benchmarks using three models. The results show that it consistently improves accuracy while requiring fewer sampling runs than existing methods. D Answer Equivalence Checking 

To determine whether two answers are equivalent, we adopt a hierarchical matching procedure that goes beyond exact string matching.
