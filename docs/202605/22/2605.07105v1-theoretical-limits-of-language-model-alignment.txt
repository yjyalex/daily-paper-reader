# Theoretical Limits of Language Model Alignment 

Lucas Monteiro Paes ‚ąó, Natalie Mackraz ‚ąó, Barry-John Theobald , Federico Danieli 

Apple Language model (LM) alignment improves model outputs to reflect human preferences while preserving the capabilities of the base model. The most common alignment approaches are (i) reinforcement learning, which maximizes the expected reward under a KL-divergence constraint, and (ii) best-of-N alignment, which selects the highest-reward output among 

N independent samples. Despite their widespread use, the fundamental limits of reward improvement under a KL budget remain poorly understood. We characterize the information-theoretic limits of KL-regularized alignment by deriving the maximum achievable expected reward gain for a fixed KL-divergence budget. Our first result provides a closed-form expression for the optimal reward improvement, governed by a Jeffreys divergence term rather than the ‚ąöKL used in prior analyses. We further reformulate this expression as a covariance under the base model, yielding a practical estimator that predicts achievable alignment gains from base model samples alone. We extend our analysis to the proxy reward setting, showing that the gap between ideal and proxy alignment ( reward hacking ) grows with the magnitude of reward error and when the KL penalty factor decreases. We then prove that reward ensembling mitigates reward hacking, providing a theoretical justification for this technique used in practice. Empirically, we compute the KL‚Äďreward Pareto frontier for two tasks for LMs, safety and summarization, and show that best-of-N closely approaches the theoretical limit, while PPO and GRPO remain substantially suboptimal. Our theoretical results shed light on several empirically observed phenomena in the alignment literature and suggest that algorithmic improvements are needed to achieve optimal alignment without high inference costs.

# 1 Introduction 

Large Language Models (LLMs) perform complex tasks on behalf of users: such as solving mathematical problems [44, 14], moderating online content [29, 32], writing code [11, 3], and shopping [53, 28]. While these LLMs solve multiple problems without fine-tuning, their output may be toxic, deceiving, or otherwise harmful [25, 35, 7]. To ensure that models perform in a safe manner and avoid producing harmful outputs, they usually undergo an alignment step to ensure their outputs follow human preferences that ideally reflect ethical values and social norms [33, 9, 10, 21]. Many approaches for aligning language models have been proposed including, (i) KL-regularized reinforcement learning [4, 43], (ii) direct preference optimization [37], (iii) best-of-N finetuning [46], among others [31, 55, 20, 49]. At their core, these methods aim to solve a KL-regularized reinforcement learning (RL) problem shown in (2.1). This approach begins by defining a reward function r which scores model outputs according to how well they align with user preferences. A base LLM ŌÄbase is then fine-tuned to maximize the expected reward while penalizing (proportionally to a parameter őĽ) the KL-divergence from the base, leading to the aligned model ŌÄr,őĽ [12, 5, 4, 43]. Despite the rapid proliferation of alignment algorithms, their theoretical limits remain largely unaddressed. Progress in alignment is typically measured by achieving higher expected reward for a fixed KL divergence budget [19, 37, 31, 20], where the KL divergence acts as a proxy for how much of the base model‚Äôs general capabilities we retain after alignment [34]. # 2 Background 

Language model. A large language model (LLM) is a conditional probability distribution ŌÄbase over sequences of text V. Given a user query x ‚ąą V , the model generates a response y according to said distribution, 

y ‚ąľ ŌÄbase (¬∑ | x). In practice, ŌÄbase is obtained through pretraining and supervised fine-tuning to follow user instructions [36, 45]. This training procedure yields a highly capable model often referred to as a base model ,but model responses may not align with human values and preferences, motivating the need for an alignment procedure to ensure safe model behavior [5, 35, 7, 4]. 

Reward models. Several alignment procedures rely on a reward model r : V √ó V ‚Üí R that assigns a scalar score r(x, y) measuring the quality of alignment of response y to query x. Ideally, the reward reflects user preferences: if a user prefers yw over yl in response to x, then the associated reward should also be larger. If a reward model perfectly reflects user preferences it is termed gold reward and we denote it by r‚ąó. Reward models are typically learned from preference data [33, 4, 37, 5]. However, due to annotation noise and the stochastic nature of training a reward model, the resulting model is an imperfect proxy for the true user preferences, often called a proxy reward [19], which we denote by ňúr. We analyze the difference in models aligned with a gold and a proxy reward in Sec. 4. 

KL-regularized alignment. At the heart of several alignment techniques [33, 37, 31] lies the KL-regularized reinforcement learning problem (2.1). Specifically, to nudge the base model ŌÄbase toward user preferences, these approaches seek an aligned policy over the vocabulary V that maximizes the expected reward while penalizing deviation from the base model ŌÄbase . Formally, this amounts to finding the aligned model ŌÄr,őĽ 

that optimizes the following objective:

Limits of Alignment & The Optimality of best-of-N . Prior work suggested the optimality of best-of-N

[52, 20, 40]. However, existing justifications rely on structural assumptions on language and reward models. Yang et al. Reward Ensembling. Reward ensembling has emerged as a practical strategy for mitigating reward hacking. Coste et al. [15] show that ensembles of reward models reduce reward hacking. Eisenstein et al. [17] compared different ensembling strategies showing that some are more effective than others on reducing hacking. In contrast, Zhang et al. [54] and Ahmed et al. [1] focused on improving the efficiency of ensembling by building light-weight reward models for the ensemble. While these works show empirically that reward ensembling mitigates reward hacking in practice, they do not provide a theoretical explanation for the observation, which we establish in Thm. 3.

# 4 Proxy Rewards and a Justification for Reward Ensembling

4.2 Why Reward Ensembling Mitigates Reward Hacking 

Given the exponential reward degradation caused by preference residuals from proxy rewards, practitioners require immediate strategies to counter reward hacking. A common heuristic consists of using an ensemble of reward models rather than a single proxy reward [15, 17]. While ensembling has been shown to be effective in practice, it lacks formal theoretical guarantees within the context of KL-regularized alignment. To prove that ensembling mitigates reward hacking, we first introduce a bound that connects the difference in expected reward between two aligned models directly to the distance between their underlying reward functions under the base model. # 5 Experiments 

In this section, we present experiments demonstrating that (i) the limit estimators proposed in Prop. 1 and Prop. 2 quickly converge, (ii) best-of-N is close to the theoretical limit, i.e., the KL‚ÄĒReward Pareto frontier, and (iii) both GRPO and PPO are suboptimal in terms of the KL vs. Reward gain.  

> Dataset & Tasks.

We evaluate our theoretical limits on two alignment tasks: summarization and safety. For summarization, we use the Reddit TL;DR dataset [42], which provides Reddit posts paired with two candidate summaries and human preference labels; the task is to generate post summaries (TL;DRs) aligned with annotator preferences ‚ÄĒ during RL we use the preprocessed variant of the dataset [47]. For safety, we use 

BeaverTails [24], which consists of prompts designed to elicit harmful completions; the task is to align the model to produce safe responses.  

> Base & Reward Models.

For summarization ( Reddit TL;DR ), we start from the base model Pythia-1B [8] supervised fine-tuned on Reddit TL;DR [18], and use a Pythia-1B -based reward model trained on pairwise TL;DR preferences [13], reproducing the setup of Huang et al. [22]. # 6 Final Discussion 

Conclusion. We characterized the information-theoretic limits of language model alignment in terms of KL divergence vs. reward gain. First, we derived the optimal reward improvement after alignment showing that the gain in expected reward is governed by the Jeffrey‚Äôs divergence őĽ JD (ŌÄr,őĽ ‚ą•ŌÄbase ), improving upon the previously conjectured pKL (ŌÄr,őĽ ‚ą•ŌÄbase ) scaling which was empirically observed [4, 19] and proven to be an upper bound for optimal alignment by Mroueh and Nitsure [30]. Second, we showed that the Jeffreys divergence term can be rewritten as a covariance under the base model, yielding an exact characterization of the optimal reward gain that does not require access to the aligned model. Building on this, we proposed convergent estimators that recover the full KL‚Äďreward Pareto frontier from base model samples alone. This enables practitioners to forecast achievable reward gains and benchmark alignment algorithms against an achievable limit . Empirically, we found that best-of-N sampling closely tracks the theoretical Pareto frontier, while PPO and GRPO are suboptimal, pointing to significant room for algorithmic improvement in gradient-based alignment. Third, we leveraged our covariance characterization to analyze the gap between optimally aligned LMs using the gold reward and under proxy rewards. We proved that this gap is governed by the inverse KL penalty 

őĽ‚ąí1 and the mean square error between proxy and gold rewards. The dependency on the KL penalty explains why aggressive alignment with proxy rewards leads to reward hacking, which was previously observed in [19]. Building on the same characterization, we show that reward ensembling provably mitigates reward hacking, with convergence to ideal-reward alignment at a rate of O(n‚ąí1/2), providing a theoretical guarantee for this widely used practice. 9Implications and Future Work. On the practical side, our estimators for the KL vs. reward gain Pareto frontier open the door to principled hyperparameter selection and algorithm evaluation: practitioners can rely on the Pareto frontier to identify the KL budget required for a target reward level, and to compare the performance of alignment algorithms in absolute terms. On the modeling side, our analysis on the use of proxy rewards exposes a structural limitation of the dominant reward-modeling paradigm. Currently, rewards are trained under the Bradley-Terry (BT) model. This approach captures only the relative preference of responses, but not how much one is preferred over another. As a consequence, the preference residual N between learned proxy and gold reward can grow unboundedly, even with a perfectly minimized BT loss ‚ÄĒ see Sec. C for a more detailed justification. However, Prop. 3 shows that the preference residual is precisely the culprit behind the degradation of alignment quality. These findings suggest that a new reward training paradigm , capable of directly constraining the preference residual (for instance, by incorporating preference magnitudes ) could mitigate reward hacking at its source, rather than relying on post-hoc corrections such as ensembling. We believe that designing such feedback protocols, and understanding how they interact with the fundamental limits derived here, are important directions for future work. 

Limitations. Our work has limitations we hope future work will address. First, our results characterize the optimal solution to the KL-regularized objective in Equation (2.1); extending the analysis to alternative objectives (e.g., f -divergence regularization, win-rate maximization, per-token KL divergence) is an interest-ing direction. Second, our proposed estimators converge slowly for smaller őĽ (large KL ); future work could focus on designing better estimators for this regime. Finally, our analysis of reward hacking (Prop. 3) and ensembling (Thm. 3) assumes proxy rewards are unbiased estimates of the gold reward on average across proxy reward samples; characterizing the impact of systematic biases in proxy reward design is left for future work.

# C On the need for a new paradigm for reward modeling 

Proxy reward models are predominantly trained using the Bradley-Terry model, which frames human feed-back strictly as binary pairwise comparisons over relative rankings, and thus fails to impose restrictions on preference residuals. Specifically, under the BT framework, the probability that a response yw is preferred over a response yl given a prompt x is modeled as Pr( yw ‚ČĽ yl | x) = ŌÉ(ňú r(x, yw) ‚ąí ňúr(x, yl)) , where ŌÉ is the logistic function. However, because this objective captures only the relative ordering of preferences, it provides no structural guarantee that the learned proxy reward ňúr is quantitatively close to the unobserved gold reward r‚ąó in terms of preference residual. Hence, a reward can perfectly minimize the BT loss with an arbitrarily large preference residual N (x, y).The result in Prop. 3 shows that preference residual ‚ÄĒuncontrolled in the BT framework‚ÄĒ causes an expo-nential decrease in the gold reward, leading to reward hacking. Conversely, Prop. 4 provides justification that if the preference residual is small, then reward hacking is mitigated, i.e., gains in performance are transferred to the evaluation using the gold reward. Together, these results highlight the necessity for a new reward modeling paradigm : we must incorporate preference magnitude to directly anchor the proxy reward, allowing us to actively measure and minimize its absolute ‚Ąď2 distance to the gold reward. Incorporating the magnitude of a preference would anchor the scale of the proxy, providing a direct mechanism to measure and minimize the preference residual. 

Example: BT-invariant but increases the proxy‚Äďgold gap. For a prompt x suppose that the set of candidate responses decomposes into two disjoint clusters 

V = VA(x) ‚ą™ V B (x), VA(x) ‚ą© V B (x) = ‚ąÖ, (C.1) where V is the set of all possible responses. Also, consider that BT preference dataset for x contains only within-cluster comparisons, i.e., every labeled pair (yw, yl) satisfies either yw, yl ‚ąą V A(x) or yw, yl ‚ąą V B (x).22 When do clustered preference clusters arise? This two-component structure naturally emerges in practice from mixture-of-sources candidate generation paired with within-source preference labeling. The following are examples of when this emerges: 1. Helpfulness and Harmlessness We can generate one batch of candidates using a ‚Äúhelpful‚ÄĚ decoding regime (cluster VA(x)) and a second batch using a ‚Äúsafe/refusal‚ÄĚ regime (cluster VB (x)), as in HH-RLHF [4], where it is useful to train a helpful and harmless reward. 2. Subtle Safety Risks To reduce annotation costs, alignment pipelines may use a selective mechanism 

where an AI labels simpler candidate pairs while humans evaluate only the most challenging ones. This inadvertently creates isolated data clusters. 3. Reasoning A model might generate one batch of candidates using Chain-of-Thought reasoning, and another batch using direct answers. If human raters compare only CoT responses to other CoT responses (to judge the logic) and direct answers to direct answers (to judge conciseness), we have two clusters. 4. Tool Use When training an LLM to use external tools (like web search), we may evaluate tool-use trajectories against other tool-uses to improve tool usage. These are never compared against a model simply answering from its own internal weights. In these scenarios, the BT model learns accurate within-cluster rankings but leaves the relative reward offset between the clusters weakly identified. The shift preserves all observed BT pairwise differences while potentially inflating the absolute preference residual N .

# D Reward-KL Pareto Frontier 

Fig. 4 validates the practicality of our proposed estimators for the Pareto frontier by showing the convergence of the entire theoretical Reward-KL Pareto frontier as a function of the number of base model samples n.Figure 4 illustrates the estimated frontiers using n ‚ąą { 10 , 100 , 1000 , 10000 } across both tasks (safety and summarization). We observe that while small sample sizes (e.g., n = 10 or 100 ) systematically underestimate the achievable reward and prematurely truncate in the high-KL regime, the frontier rapidly stabilizes as 

n increases. This behavior directly reflects the mechanics of the estimators in Def. 1 and Def. 2: in the high-KL regime (which corresponds to a small penalty őĽ), the exponential tilt er/őĽ amplifies the variance of high-reward samples, thereby requiring a larger number of samples to achieve a tight estimate. Crucially, the curves for n = 1000 and n = 10000 are nearly overlapping across most of the KL spectrum. This macro view of the frontier‚Äôs convergence complements the pointwise convergence guarantees of Prop. 1 and Prop. 2, as well as the fixed-őĽ empirical results in Fig. 2 and Fig. 3.
