## ABSTRACT 

Table Question Answering (TQA) aims to answer natural language questions over structured tables. Large Language Models (LLMs) enable promising solutions to this problem, with operator-centric solutions that generate table manipulation pipelines in a multi-step manner offering state-of-the-art performance. However, these solu-tions rely on multiple LLM calls, resulting in prohibitive latencies and computational costs. We propose Operation-R1 , the first framework that trains light-weight LLMs (e.g., Qwen-4B/1.7B) via a novel variant of reinforce-ment learning with verifiable rewards to produce high-quality data-preparation pipelines for TQA in a single inference step. To train such an LLM, we first introduce a self-supervised rewarding mecha-nism to automatically obtain fine-grained pipeline-wise supervision signals for LLM training. We also propose variance-aware group resampling to mitigate training instability. To further enhance ro-bustness of pipeline generation, we develop two complementary mechanisms: operation merge, which filters spurious operations through multi-candidate consensus, and adaptive rollback, which offers runtime protection against information loss in data transfor-mation. Experiments on two benchmark datasets show that, with the same LLM backbone, Operation-R1 achieves average absolute accuracy gains of 9.55 and 6.08 percentage points over multi-step preparation baselines, with 79% table compression and a 2.2 × re-duction in monetary cost.

## 1 INTRODUCTION 

Table Question Answering (TQA) aims to answer natural language (NL) queries over (semi-)structured tables, spanning spreadsheets, databases, web tables. It empowers non-technical users to explore and extract insights without writing formal query languages such as SQL, enhances analytical productivity, and enables applications in finance [ 36 ], healthcare [ 1], and scientific research [ 18 ]. For example, TQA for business intelligence enables users to query sales, customer, and financial tables using natural language (e.g., “Which product had the highest growth in Q3?”), lowering the technical barrier to data analytics. Given their semantic understanding and instruction-following capabilities Large Language Models (LLMs) have advanced TQA substantially. As illustrated in Fig.1, one line of LLM-based TQA methods employ direct text-based reasoning [28 , 32 ]. These methods serialize tables into plain text and answer NL queries in a single step by reasoning directly over the text. However, they scale poorly to large tables because LLMs struggle to reliably identify relevant information within long contexts. Studies show that text-based reasoning deteriorates sharply as table size grows [7]. Another line of methods rely on Text-to-SQL/code generation [ 6,19 , 32 , 34 ]. These methods translate NL queries into compositional executable programs (e.g., SQL or Python) and derive answers through program execution. Although often effective, they are fragile: LLMs easily introduce subtle errors into long, compound programs, causing executions to fail or produce corrupted results. Motivated by limitations of above reasoning approaches, recent methods adopt operator-centric approaches [ 7 , 26 ], where an LLM assembles a sequence of table manipulation operators (e.g., select, sort_by) to restructure input tables into more compact, question-relevant tables that facilitate the TQA the most. To discover effective table manipulation operations, these methods employ multi-step reasoning, where table manipulation operations are generated step-by-step in the context of input questions, tables, and intermediate orchestration results. This operator-centric approach facilitates more transparent, interpretable reasoning and enables methods that achieve state-of-the-art performance. Despite their accuracy and interpretability advantages, operator-centric pipelines incur substantially higher latency and computa-tional cost due to their repeated LLM invocations. This overhead, which is avoided by one-step approaches, is the primary barrier to

To enable the practical deployment of these methods, we intro-duce Operation-R1 , a framework that trains a lightweight LLM to generate a high-quality data preparation pipeline for TQA in a single inference step . Inspired by the success of DeepSeek-R1 [ 9 ] and TableR1 [ 30 ], we employ Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) to teach compact models (e.g., Qwen3-4B, Qwen-1.7B) to orchestrate well-structured data preparation tailored to each question–table pair. However, building such a one-step orchestrator introduces sev-eral unique challenges not addressed by prior work. Below, we outline these challenges and how they motivate the key design components of Operation-R1 .

Challenge I: Designing a data-preparation–aware reward mechanism for pipeline generation. Training a lightweight LLM to generate effective data preparation pipelines requires reliable supervision signals that reflect pipeline quality. However, obtaining such signals is difficult because no ground-truth—–or “golden”– —preprocessing pipelines exist for TQA. The only available super-vision in typical TQA datasets is the gold answer to each question, which is insufficient for directly supervising pipeline construction. A natural surrogate is to evaluate a generated pipeline using an LLM-as-Judge [ 21 ]: execute the pipeline, present the transformed table to an LLM, and check whether it can infer the correct answer. Yet this approach has three critical drawbacks: (i) Low accuracy. As shown in our experiments (Table 1, Sec-tion 4), even strong LLMs (e.g., Qwen3-8B) achieve under 80 (ii) Sparse feedback. The evaluation yields only a binary reward per pipeline, providing little insight into which operations are help-ful or harmful. (iii) High computational cost. Invoking an LLM-as-Judge for every pipeline drastically increases training cost. Thus, a verifiable, fine-grained, and low-cost reward mechanism is essential. To address this challenge, we introduce a self-supervised reward mechanism tailored to data preparation. We focus on Cell-Focused QA , a setting where the answer appears verbatim as a table cell. This property allows us to verify each operation by checking whether it removes or preserves the answer-critical cell. Pipeline quality is then evaluated operation by operation , enabling detailed attribution of failures and successes. To complement semantic correctness, we further incorporate an efficiency metric that measures how effectively the pipeline reduces table size. The combined reward provides fine-grained, stepwise feedback that directly reflects each operation’s utility, yielding informative and stable supervision for training the pipeline generator. 

Challenge II: Maintaining training stability under fine-grained reward supervision. Although our approach introduces finer-grained supervision, integrating it with existing RLVR algo-rithms leads to training instability. In standard RLVR workflows, models are trained not with raw reward values but with normalized relative advantages , which are computed by comparing a group of model responses for the same input. This normalization, while effec-tive for coarse rewards, inevitably distorts the underlying learning signal. With our more granular pipeline rewards, this distortion 

> 2

becomes even more pronounced, ultimately misleading model opti-mization. Our early analysis reveals two fundamental sources of distortion: (a) Intra-group low-variance amplification. When reward variance within a sampled group is small, even noise-level differences are artificially amplified by the advantage normalization, causing the model to overfit to random fluctuations rather than meaningful reward differences. (b) Inter-group reward distortion. Conversely, large and informative reward gaps across different groups are flat-tened after normalization, suppressing important learning signals that should guide the policy toward better pipelines. To alleviate these issues, we propose a variance-aware group resampling strategy that replaces the static grouping used in stan-dard RLVR with a dynamic, variance-sensitive sampling process. The method imposes two constraints—each derived from the two distortion phenomena above—on sampled response groups. If any constraint is violated, the group is discarded and resampled. This ensures that each accepted group provides diverse, high-fidelity supervision signals. By enforcing variance-aware resampling, our method preserves meaningful reward structure, prevents noise amplification, and stabilizes RLVR training under fine-grained supervision. 

Challenge III: Ensuring robustness during preprocessing. 

Even with a carefully finetuned LLM designed to generate high-quality pipelines, failures are inevitable when the model encoun-ters unusual or previously unseen NL questions. A single faulty operation—such as overly aggressive filtering or an incorrect ag-gregation—can permanently remove question-critical information and make downstream reasoning impossible. This reveals a core tension between compression efficiency and information preserva-tion : while aggressive table compression helps reduce reasoning complexity, any loss of essential data invalidates the entire prepro-cessing pipeline. The challenge, therefore, is to design a robustness mechanism that mitigates generation errors without compromising too much on efficiency and interpretability. To address this, Operation-R1 incorporates two complementary mechanisms: Operation Merge and Adaptive Rollback . Operation Merge tackles the nondeterminism inherent in pipeline generation by producing multiple candidate pipelines in parallel and construct-ing a final, more reliable pipeline through a consensus-based voting algorithm over an operation trie. This filters out spurious or unsta-ble operations that may appear in single-pass outputs. Adaptive Rollback provides runtime safeguards against infor-mation loss. When the downstream QA model signals that the processed table lacks sufficient information, the system incremen-tally reverts to earlier states—i.e., tables resulting from fewer pre-processing operations—ultimately reverting to the original table if necessary. Together, these mechanisms strike a balance between the effi-ciency of single-pass preprocessing and the robustness required for real-world TQA. They enable our lightweight framework to reliably handle diverse questions and edge cases without relying on multi-agent coordination or iterative refinement. The key contributions are summarized as below: 

• To our knowledge, we present the first RLVR-based LLM post-training framework that enables lightweight LLMs to generate data preparation pipelines for TQA in a single inference step .

• We introduce a self-supervised pipeline reward mechanism based on Cell-Focused QA that provides fine-grained, stepwise feed-back for RLVR training. We further propose a variance-aware group resampling strategy to ensure stable optimization under such fine-grained rewards. 

• We develop two complementary mechanisms to balance compres-sion efficiency with information preservation: Operation Merge ,which filters spurious operations through multi-candidate con-sensus, and Adaptive Rollback , which offers runtime protection against information loss. 

• Extensive experiments on WikiTQ and TabFact show that Operation-R1 achieves absolute accuracy improvements of 9.62% and 6.05% over non-preprocessed baselines, respectively, while reducing ta-ble size by 79% on average and requiring only a single lightweight model inference.

Question-Aware Data Preparation. Answering NL questions directly over an uncurated table is often ineffective and inefficient. The reasons are twofold: (i) many table entries are irrelevant to the question, forcing the model to sift through large amounts of unnec-essary information—the classic “needle in a haystack” problem; and (ii) answers frequently require transformations or computations over the table rather than direct lookup, necessitating intermediate data preparation steps. Thus, before generating answers, it is beneficial to apply a series of table transformation operations (data preparation operations, or simply operations ). ## 2.2 RL with Verifiable Rewards 

Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR). 

RLVR [ 13 ] is a family of LLM post-training methods in which an LLM is optimized through RL using automatically verifiable feedback as the reward signal. Unlike preference-model-based or human-rated rewards, RLVR rewards are computed by a verifier—an automated, deterministic, and programmatically checkable function that evaluates whether an output satisfies a well-defined success criterion. The verifier may check logical or syntactic constraints, unit-test outcomes, exact or semantic equivalence to reference solutions, or it may use other rule-based correctness conditions. ## 3 OPERATION-R1 

## 3.1 Framework Overview 

To bridge the gap between TQA and data preparation, we propose 

Operation-R1 , a question-oriented data-preparation orchestration framework designed to enable high-quality tabular question answer-ing. Figure 2 provides an overview of its architecture. Operation-R1 

frames data preparation as a model-driven orchestration process that, given a question and a table, automatically composes and executes a tailored sequence of transformations that reshape the table to better expose the answer. As shown in Fig. 2, the offline stage of Operation-R1 aims to pro-duce a TQA-oriented data preparation pipeline generator, built using open-sourced LLMs. To finetune an LLM for the task of QA-oriented data preparation, we propose ORPO ( O peration-wise Group Relative Policy Optimization), a novel RVRL algorithm. ORPO 

features two designs that aim to make the post-training process more effective, efficient, and robust: (i) a self-supervised pipeline reward mechanism, providing finer-grained supervision signals tailored to TQA for model training; and (ii) a variance-aware group resampling strategy, enhancing the stability of the LLM training in case of reward collapse.

Figure 2: Overview of Operation-R1 , including ORPO-based offline training, parallel operator generation, operation-tree merging, and adaptive rollback for reliable tabular QA. 

During the online stage, once receiving a natural language ques-tion 𝑞 over a table 𝑇 , Operation-R1 leverages the data preparation pipeline generator derived from the offline stage to produce a se-quence of operators. Each operator is organized as a JSON object containing the operator name, parameters, and an explanation (in-dicating the usages of the operation). Due to hallucinations and nondeterminism in LLM inference, the Operation-R1 generator builds 𝑁 data preparation pipelines concurrently and then pro-duces a consistent pipeline from these using an operation merging algorithm. The JSON-represented operators are eventually interpreted as physical operators and then executed in the underlying execution environment ( e.g. , Python). Recalling the characteristics of opera-tors as discussed in Section 2.1, structured operators ( e.g. , select ,

filter ), will be converted into corresponding code snippets and exe-cuted by the executor; and semantic operators ( e.g. , add_column ), will be executed by invoking an LLM based on the instruction (like 

description as for add_column ). This hybrid execution extends the capabilities of data preparation, expanding the scope of ques-tions that TQA can address. Eventually, the processed table 𝑇 ′

together with natural language question 𝑞 are be fed into a QA model to obtain the desired answer. To avoid the “over-processing” problem where key information is lost in the processing of data preparation, Operation-R1 also supports an adaptive rollback mechanism: When the QA model cannot identify relevant information from the table, the system will revert to the earlier table that contains more information. In the following, we detail the post-training algorithm of ORPO 

and then the online question answering process. 

## 3.2 ORPO: Operation-wise Group Relative Policy Optimization 

As discussed earlier, there are numerous benefits to using a cus-tomized LLM to generate a QA-oriented data preparation pipeline, and GRPO performs well at customizing an LLM for a specific task [ 9, 33 ]. The challenge however, is that as for data preparation operation generation requires assessing the correctness of final answers as well as quantifying the quality of generate intermediate operations. We thus propose a low-cost surrogate operation reward mechanism to quantify the quality of generated operations in terms of both correctness and efficiency, forming the foundation for our 

ORPO training strategy. 

3.2.1 Self-supervised Pipeline Reward Mechanism. We first intro-duce the reward designs for operations in a generated data prepa-ration pipeline in terms of correctness and efficiency. 

Operation correctness rewarding. We first consider the evaluation of the correctness of generated operations. If given an ideal curated ground truth table that is the most compact and most pertinent for a given answer, an ideal approach to evaluating a generated data preparation operation is to compare the result it produces to the ground truth table. However, it is impossible to obtain the ground truth table beforehand. ORPO introduces finer-grained supervision signals, it also causes more instability in the LLM training, which manifests itself in two forms. ## 3.3 Operation Merge 

In the online phase, the pipeline generator trained by ORPO is used to generate a sequence of data preparation operators tailored to the specific question and given table. To enhance the robustness and consistency of pipeline generation, inspired by [ 2 ], Operation-R1 

asks the generator producing 𝑁 pipeline candidates and then re-turns one of them with the most “votes” as the final data preparation pipeline. The vote for an operator represents how many pipeline candidates include that operator. The more votes a pipeline candi-date has, the more reliable operators it contains. We propose an operation merge algorithm to count the votes and find the most trustworthy pipeline among all candidates efficiently. The core data structure in operation merge is an operation trie built upon all pipeline candidates. In the operation trie, each branch rep-resents a candidate pipeline, and each node in a branch represents an operator in the pipeline. If two candidate pipelines share the same operator sub-sequence, they share the same sub-branch in the operation trie. Each node in the trie has a weight recording how many candidate pipelines contain the operator ( i.e. , the number of votes). With an operation trie, the problem of searching a pipeline that most LLM orchestrations advocate can be solved by identifying a branch of a trie with the maximum sum of node weights. In case of multiple branches having same node weights, a longer branch is preferred as the final pipeline. When applying operation merge, we take a different action to the semantic operator add_column from other structured operators whose parameters distinguish each operator. To avoid wrongly distinguishing the same add_column operators that possess the semantically same but literally different NL description, we retain all add_column s in the final pipeline. In addition, we aggregate all 

select operators in candidates into a new select operator whose the parameter column name is a union of all column names. The complete merging procedure is in Algorithm 1. This merg-ing strategy strikes a balance between inclusivity and precision: it captures all potentially informative schema modifications (via union and retention) while enforcing consensus for other transfor-mations. ## 3.4 Adaptive Rollback

## 4 EXPERIMENTS 4.1 Experiments Setup 

Datasets. We evaluate Operation-R1 using two datasets, Wik-iTQ [17] and TabFact [4]. 

• WikiTQ is a dataset for table question answering, consisting of complex natural language questions over diverse Wikipedia tables, with 17,689 training and 4,344, test pairs covering wide variations in table structures and reasoning types. 

• TabFact is a table-based fact verification dataset built from Wikipedia, where each statement–table pair is labeled as True or 

False , emphasizing logical reasoning and consistency checking over tabular evidence. 

Training protocol. Our training for the operation generator is conducted only on the Cell-Focused subset of WikiTQ, while Tab-Fact is reserved purely as an out-of-domain evaluation benchmark to assess the transferability of the learned data-preparation policies. 

Baselines. We evaluate Operation-R1 against 10 baselines. 

• No-Prep performs direct table QA without preprocessing, using the prompting template from Table-R1 (Yang et al.) [30]. 

• DATER [ 32 ] is an LLM-driven decomposer that splits a large evidence table into salient sub-tables and factorizes complex questions into executable sub-questions via a parsing-execution-filling pipeline. 

• Binder [ 6] is a program-centric approach that binds language models to symbolic execution (e.g., SQL/code), compiling model outputs into interpretable, executable programs. 

• ReAcTable [34 ] is a ReAct-style TQA system that incrementally constructs intermediate table representations and invokes exter-nal SQL/Python executors for progressive data transformation and answer derivation. 

• CoTable (Chain-of-Table) [ 26 ] is a tabular reasoning system that treats an evolving table as the chain of thought, iteratively generating operations to update the table and expose verifiable intermediate results before producing a final answer. 

• AutoPrep [7] is a question-aware data preparation multi-agent framework that decomposes complex table reasoning tasks into structured data preparation subtasks through collaborative agents, including a planner, a preprocessor, and an executor. 

• GRPO [ 9 ] is an RL algorithm that trains LLMs via self-comparison within a batch of generated responses, eliminating explicit value functions. We apply accuracy rewards to fine-tune the base model under the same setting as our ORPO, using the full WikiTQ dataset ( ∼11k). 

• DAPO [ 33 ] extends GRPO with token-level policy gradients, dynamic sample allocation, and length-aware clipping to stabi-lize LLM RL training. We apply accuracy rewards to fine-tune the base model under the same setting as ORPO, using the full WikiTQ dataset ( ∼11k). 

• Table-R1 (Yang et al.) [ 30 ] introduces two independently trained variants for table reasoning. Table-R1-SFT is a supervised fine-tuning model trained on ∼48.5k carefully filtered reasoning traces generated by DeepSeek-R1 across three task types (TQA, TFV, FF-TQA), employing full-parameter fine-tuning. Table-R1-Zero ,instead of being initialized from the SFT checkpoint, is trained directly from the same base model using ∼69k verifiable samples with the GRPO algorithm. 

• Table-R1 (Wu et al.) [29 ] proposes a region-based reinforce-ment learning framework built on the Table Instruct-RE dataset (derived from TableBench ∼20k), where table regions are auto-matically generated by DeepSeek-R1 to capture the minimal evidence areas necessary for solving each problem. It adopts two-stage training that first guides LLMs with SFT and then optimizes via Table-Aware Group Relative Policy Optimization (TARPO) with a mixed reward balancing region precision and correctness. 

Evaluation Metrics. We use accuracy to evaluate model perfor-mance on both datasets. For WikiTQ, accuracy is computed as the percentage of questions where the predicted answer matches the ground truth. Following the evaluator from Binder [ 6 ], we apply answer normalization to handle unit variations and list-based an-swers. For TabFact, accuracy measures the percentage of statements correctly classified as True or False. 

Experiment Settings. All experiments are conducted on a server equipped with 256 GB RAM, an Intel Xeon Silver 4314 CPU operat-ing at 2.40 GHz, and an NVIDIA A40 GPU with 46 GB memory. The software environment includes PyTorch 2.4.0, Transformers 4.46.1, and the vLLM serving engine (version 0.6.2). To ensure reproducibil-ity, we fix the random seeds across all frameworks. Due to GPU memory limitations, all vLLM-based experiments use half-precision (FP16) inference. 

Backbone inference modes. Qwen3-series models support the default non-thinking mode and the thinking mode. For Table 1, we use the non-thinking variants to stay comparable with prior baselines on standard instruction-tuned models. For Table 2, all Qwen3-based methods run in the thinking mode with the same backbone and inference configuration.

## 4.2 Overall Performance 

We conduct experiments on WikiTQ and TabFact across multiple backbones. As shown in Table 1, Operation-R1 consistently outper-forms all compared baselines under each backbone setting. Operation-R1-4B improves over baselines by 9.55% on WikiTQ and 6.08% on TabFact on average. The smaller Operation-R1-1.7B variant surpasses baselines by 6.85% and 3.57% respectively, demon-strating efficient table reasoning with limited capacity. Traditional program-based methods (Dater, Binder) suffer from execution brit-tleness and poor generalization to complex questions. These meth-ods yield no clear advantage over the simple No-Prep baseline due to execution brittleness. Operation-based methods (CoTable, Auto-Prep) exhibit strong backbone dependency: on weaker or small-size llms (Llama3.1-8B-Instruct, Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, Qwen3-4B), , they show no clear advantage over the No-Prep baseline and occasionally underperform it. Especially, AutoPrep suffers catastrophic degra-dation (e.g., Qwen3-4B/TabFact ( 53 .36% vs. 74 .95% No-Prep)), as smaller models generate buggy code that traps the system in futile self-debugging loops. Even in comparison with best baseline per-formance, Operation-R1 maintains consistent advantages (WikiTQ: 75.04 vs. 76.77, TabFact: 91.25 vs. 93.87). Operation-R1’s stable performance stems from transforming ta-ble preprocessing from either multi-agent coordination or program synthesis into a trainable operator orchestration task. By learning to compose operator sequences through ORPO with cell-level veri-fication, we achieve robust results across backbones without the brittleness of code execution or the overhead of iterative agent collaboration. This paradigm—optimizing operator composition policies rather than prompt engineering or code generation—offers a more scalable path for future table understanding systems. Table 2 compares Operation-R1 against state-of-the-art end-to-end training approaches. Despite using only 4B parameters and 5K training samples, Operation-R1 achieves competitive WikiTQ accu-racy (81.62%) and state-of-the-art TabFact performance (93.47%). 

Operator Generation vs. Natural Language Reasoning. Against large-scale supervised methods, Operation-R1 outperforms TableGPT2 (72B, 2.36M samples) by 10.12 points on WikiTQ and 10.97 points on TabFact, demonstrating the superiority of targeted operator-based preprocessing over massive generic pre-training. Table-R1-SFT with Llama-3.1-8B achieves the highest WikiTQ score (83.8%) but re-quires 34K curated reasoning traces from DeepSeek-R1. Operation-R1 maintains robust performance with 6.8 × less training data while surpassing all SFT variants on TabFact (+2.37 points). This provide empirical evidence that structured operator composition can be easier to learn than free-form textual reasoning in our setting. 

Preprocessing-then-QA vs. End-to-End RL. Among RL-based meth-ods, Table-R1-ZERO trains directly from base models using GRPO with 49K samples, achieving 81.2%/87.6% on Llama-3.1-8B. Our controlled comparison with GRPO and DAPO—which train end-to-end QA on the same Qwen3-4B backbone with 11K full-dataset samples—reveals their limited effectiveness (78.78%/91.97% and 79.08%/92.24%). Remarkably, Operation-R1 outperforms both by 2.54/1.23 points with only 5K samples, despite being trained solely on operator generation rather than the QA task itself . This counterintu-itive result demonstrates that learning operator-based preprocess-ing is fundamentally simpler and more impactful than end-to-end reasoning. End-to-end QA conflates table understanding, infor-mation selection, and multi-step reasoning into a single objective where binary accuracy rewards provide sparse, ambiguous credit assignment. Our two-stage paradigm decouples these challenges: fine-grained Cell-Focused QA verification delivers deterministic, step-wise feedback at every transformation, enabling precise credit assignment that makes operator learning tractable for small models without the supervision complexity of monolithic approaches.

## 5 RELATED WORK 5.1 Table Reasoning Paradigms 

Text-based methods. These methods serialize tables into natural language and apply in-context learning [ 3 ] or chain-of-thought reasoning [ 27 , 38 ]. Beyond generic prompting, extensions include decomposition over large tables [ 8, 31 , 32 ] and structured lineariza-tion of complex headers [ 35 , 38 ]. These methods struggle with row-column structure encoding, incur high token overhead, and lack guarantees on numerical operations (e.g., sorting, aggregation). 

Program-based methods. These methods prompt LLMs to pro-duce executable SQL or Python code to guarantee numerical cor-rectness. This paradigm offers strong guarantees on numerical cor-rectness. Early work such as Binder [ 6] adapts Program-of-Thought by binding LLM-generated symbolic programs to external veri-fiers. ReAct-style systems [ 34 ] iteratively construct and execute partial programs with tool augmentation. Decomposition-based pipelines [ 16 , 25 , 32 ] split complex queries into structured sub-programs before execution, while multi-step planning approaches [ 37 ]coordinate SQL generation across multiple reasoning stages and retrieval-augmented methods [ 11 ] integrate external knowledge for program synthesis. Despite their strengths, these methods are brittle to syntax and runtime errors, depend on external execution environments (databases or interpreters), and often degrade on smaller backbones in Table 1. 

Operator-centric methods. These methods mediate between the two extremes by manipulating tables through a compact space of composable operations, preserving structural reasoning while re-maining simpler to generate than full programs. CoTable [ 26 ] treats iterative table transformation itself as the reasoning chain, while AutoPrep [ 7 ] organizes a multi-agent (planner/programmer/execu-tor) pipeline to produce a question-aware preprocessed table passed to a separate QA model. Although effective, these frameworks typi-cally require multiple LLM calls (up to 10) and often rely on very large models (>70B parameters), which raises latency and cost con-cerns for real-time use, and the operator-pipeline panel. Our work retains the benefits of structured operators but generates them in a single step with small models. 

## 5.2 Learning Paradigms for Table Reasoning 

Supervised End-to-End Training. SFT-based methods optimize end-to-end table QA/fact verification with large curated traces or synthetic data. Representative lines include TableGPT2 [ 22 ] and Table-R1-SFT [ 30 ], which report strong accuracy but typically re-quire full-parameter training and tens of thousands to millions of training signals. Table 2 collates settings and outcomes across end-to-end backbones, illustrating the data/compute intensity of this route. By shifting learning from unconstrained NL reasoning to fi-nite operator generation, our method achieves competitive accuracy with small backbones and modest data, reducing the dependence on large-scale SFT. 

RLVR for Table Reasoning. RLVR methods [ 15 , 20 ] optimize LLMs using automatically verifiable rewards. In the table domain, three Table-R1 threads exemplify different reward placements: (i) Yang et al. [ 30 ] applied GRPO to diverse table reasoning tasks includ-ing short-form QA, fact verification, and free-form QA, proposing task-specific verifiable reward functions that evaluate both output format adherence and answer correctness, enabling inference-time scaling without human annotations. (ii) Wu et al. [ 29 ] and Lei et al. [14 ] introduced region-based reward mechanisms that compute the Intersection over Union (IoU) between predicted and ground-truth table regions (cells/columns/rows), using GRPO to optimize both evidence localization accuracy and final answer correctness. (iii) Jin et al. [ 12 ] proposed a hybrid GRPO variant that dynamically switches between program-based and text-based reasoning using reasoning-type-specific rewards. However, current RLVR approaches either rely on sparse, binary correctness signals for end-to-end reasoning [ 30 ] or—like region-based methods [ 14 , 29 ]—require manually annotated ground-truth regions and evaluate reasoning quality only indirectly by checking whether models mention the correct areas in natural language. This explicit articulation of table regions effectively patches text-domain GRPO onto structured table tasks, where high IoU does not nec-essarily imply sound operational reasoning. Operation-R1 is the first to apply GRPO to table preprocessing operator generation, de-coupling structural transformation from semantic reasoning. Our Cell-Focused QA proxy offers annotation-free, step-wise verifica-tion by testing answer-cell accessibility after each operator, yielding direct reward signals tightly aligned with operator correctness. By constraining the action space to a finite operator vocabulary, we en-able lightweight models to learn reliable preprocessing policies in a single inference step, with training fully isolated from downstream QA model choices. 

## 6 CONCLUSION 

In this work, we present Operation-R1 , enabling replacing multi-step data preparation pipeline assembly with one-step pipeline generation with a lightweight LLM (e.g., Qwen-1.7B/4B). To ad-vance LLM’s capability in pipeline generation, we propose ORPO, a LLM post-training algorithm with a novel self-supervised pipeline rewarding mechanism and a dynamic variance-aware group resam-pling strategy. Moreover, we introduce inference-time operation merge and adaptive rollback modules in Operation-R1 to enhance its robustness. Extensive experiments on WikiTQ and out-of-training-domain TabFact show that Operation-R1 improves TQA accuracy by 9.55% and 6.08% over multi-step preparation baselines with only 5K RL training examples, while reducing table size by 79%, inference la-tency by up to 51%, and 2.2 × monetary cost, demonstrating the superiority of Operation-R1 in both TQA accuracy and efficiency.
