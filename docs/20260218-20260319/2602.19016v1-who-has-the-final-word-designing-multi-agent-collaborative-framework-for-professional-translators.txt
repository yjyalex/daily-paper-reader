# Who Has the Final Word? Designing Multi-Agent Collaborative Framework for Professional Translators

Fig. 1. Overview of CHORUS, a human–AI multi-agent collaborative translation framework. (a) The translator provides the source text, an initial draft translation, and a high-level goal (e.g., “make it more formal”) and iteratively interacts with the system. (b) A Dimension Router analyzes the current sentence and the translator’s intent, then selects a small set of relevant MQM-aligned expert agents (e.g., Accuracy, Terminology, Locale) from a larger pool. (c) The selected agents independently generate targeted revision suggestions grounded in shared memories (e.g., glossaries, style guides, and contextual metadata). The translator reviews these suggestions, provides feedback, and confirms decisions, retaining final authority over the translation. 

Recent advances in LLM-based translation have led to renewed interest in fully automated systems, yet professional translators remain essential in high-stakes domains where decisions about accuracy, terminology, style, and audience cannot be safely automated. Current tools are typically single-shot generators or single-agent self-refiners, offering limited support for translators’ multi-dimensional decision-making process and providing little structured leverage for translator input. We present CHORUS, a human–AI multi-agent collaborative translation framework grounded in the Multidimensional Quality Metrics (MQM) framework, which decomposes quality dimensions into specialized agents and integrates their feedback into an iterative refinement loop controlled by the translator. A six-user preliminary study with professional translators found that CHORUS consistently outperforms zero-shot and single-agent baselines, showing that MQM-aligned multi-agent collaboration better supports professional translation workflows than autonomous generation.

1 Introduction 

Recent progress in large language models (LLMs) has substantially improved machine translation quality in general-purpose and low-risk scenarios [ 16 ]. However, in high-stakes professional domains—such as legal [ 4], medical [ 23 ], regulatory [ 5], and enterprise [ 24 ] localization—translation quality cannot be reduced to surface fluency alone[ 31 ]. Decisions about semantic accuracy, terminology compliance, stylistic consistency, audience appropriateness, and institutional preference continue to require expert human judgment [12]. Despite the iterative and multi-perspective nature of professional translation [ 9, 17 ], most contemporary LLM-based translation systems remain automation-first, relying on single-shot generation or isolated self-refinement [ 7 ,25 , 33 ]. While these approaches improve average output quality, they fail to support the multi-dimensional and conditional decision-making translators routinely perform—balancing accuracy, terminology, style, audience, and external constraints such as deadlines, document importance, and client preferences [ 26 , 34 , 36 ]. Current systems provide little structure for translator input, evidence-based reasoning, or controlled iteration, forcing professionals to manually reconstruct rationales, verify terminology, and determine when refinement should stop [ 8, 18 , 27 , 29 ]. As a result, these tools inadequately model translators’ cognitive processes [ 10 ], leading to increased cognitive overhead, mistrust, and reduced efficiency in human–AI collaboration [6, 14, 28]. To address this gap, we introduce CHORUS, a human–AI multi-agent collaborative translation framework grounded in the Multidimensional Quality Metrics (MQM) inspired by our expert formative study [ 22 ]framework. Instead of treating MQM solely as an evaluation taxonomy, CHORUS operationalizes MQM dimensions as specialized collaborative agents. Each agent focuses on a distinct quality dimension (e.g., accuracy, terminology, style), while a coordinating editor agent synthesizes their feedback into targeted revision suggestions. Importantly, CHORUS is designed as an iterative refinement loop controlled by the translator, preserving human final authority while externalizing multi-dimensional analysis into structured, interpretable support. With an ablation study, we evaluate CHORUS across multiple language pairs and state-of-the-art LLM backbones, comparing it against zero-shot translation and single-agent self-refinement baselines. Results show that CHORUS consistently yields higher translation quality, with particularly strong improvements in semantic fidelity and contextual adaptation. These findings suggest that MQM-aligned multi-agent collaboration offers a more faithful approximation of professional translation workflows than autonomous generation, and provides a practical pathway toward human-centered translation systems.

2.2 MQM 

Multidimensional Quality Metrics (MQM) provides a hierarchical framework for describing translation quality across dimensions such as accuracy, terminology, and style [ ? ]. MQM has been widely adopted for expert human evaluation and error analysis, particularly in high-stakes and professional settings [ 12 , 13 , 20 ]. Subsequent work has formalized MQM scoring and quality control practices for operational use [ 21 ]. More recently, MQM’s structure has inspired agent-based evaluation frameworks [30]. 

3 Formative Study 

We conducted a formative study to understand the central challenges professional translators face when using current AI translation tools, with the goal of informing the design of a human–AI collaborative system. We interviewed 6 professional translation experts with 6–12 years of experience, including 3 from client-side roles and 3 from vendor-side roles, covering domains such as business, marketing, government, medical, game localization, and chip design. We use a semi-structured interview with questions focused on uncovering benefits and challenges professional translators’ facing in their daily interactions with current AI translation tools. 

3.1 Results: Challenges with Current AI Translation Tools 

Limited Support for Multi-Dimensional Quality Reasoning in Professional Translations Professional translators reason about multiple quality dimensions simultaneously (e.g., accuracy, terminology, style, audience), but current AI translation tools provide little explicit support for making these dimensions visible or manageable, forcing translators to shoulder most of the cognitive work themselves (E1, E3, E6). 

Non-unified Tools Increases Cognitive and Context-Shift Load Translators rely on multiple disconnected tools to address different quality dimensions, requiring frequent context switching and manual reconciliation that increases cognitive load and limits overall efficiency (E2, E4, E6). 

Iterative Refinement Is Central, but Poorly Supported by AI Tools Translation is an iterative process of selective and incremental refinement, yet current AI tools favor single-shot generation or opaque revisions, offering limited control over what is changed and why (E1, E5, E6). 

High-Stakes Decisions Still Require Human Judgment and Accountability For high-stakes content, translators must rely on human judgment to detect subtle errors and justify decisions, while existing AI systems provide limited transparency or support for accountability (E1, E3, E4). 

4 Chorus System 

To prototype a system following the results and insights from the translation experts, we build CHORUS— a human–AI collaborative translation system designed to support the multi-dimensional reasoning processes. CHORUS uses the Multidimensional Quality Metrics (MQM) framework as an interactive coordination structure between the translator and a small set of specialized AI agents. CHORUS consists of three core components: (1) a Dimension Router, (2) a pool of MQM-aligned expert agents, and (3) a shared Translation Memory (TM) service. These components are designed to be lightweight and explicit, enabling translators to guide the system’s behavior through interaction rather than relinquishing control to automated

4.1 Pool of Experts 

CHORUS maintains a pool of specialized AI agents, each aligned with a specific MQM quality dimension, such as Accuracy, Terminology, Fluency, Style, Audience Appropriateness, Locale Convention, or Design and Markup. Each agent is prompted and responsible for reasoning about and improving a translation candidate primarily through the lens of its assigned dimension. Agents operate independently and produce one candidate translation per invocation, accompanied by a brief explanation of what was changed and reference to translation memory. 

4.2 Translation Memory 

CHORUS incorporates a Translation Memory service that functions as a shared contextual resource. In addition to confirmed source–target mappings, the TM include broader contextual artifacts such as terminology preferences, style guidance, domain-specific conventions, and previously validated translations. All agents retrieve relevant entries from the TM during generation, and agents may update the TM as translation decisions are confirmed. The TM is organized into namespaces (e.g., global, job-specific, agent-specific), allowing both shared consistency and localized adaptation without enforcing a rigid hierarchy. This design allows CHORUS to ground agent behavior in accumulated professional knowledge while avoiding heavy-handed constraint enforcement. 

4.3 Dimension Router 

The Dimension Router mediates coordination between the translator and the expert agents. Rather than automatically invoking all agents, the router is triggered explicitly by the translator and is responsible for selecting a small subset of relevant MQM dimensions (typically two to three) based on the translator’s high-level goal and the current translation state. Inputs to the router include and a natural-language instruction from the translator and translation memory. Based on this information, the router recommends which dimensions should be prioritized and which corresponding agents should be invoked. The router also provides a brief rationale to maintain transparency and support trust. By constraining attention to a limited number of dimensions at each step, the router helps externalize prioritization decisions that translators otherwise perform implicitly, reducing cognitive load while preserving human control. 

4.4 Interaction Loops 

CHORUS is structured around two explicit interaction loops that reflect how professional translators iteratively refine translations. The outer loop, or dimension re-routing loop, allows the translator to re-engage the Dimension Router at any point to adjust which quality dimensions should be prioritized. This supports situations where task constraints change—for example, when a segment is deemed higher visibility or when terminology concerns outweigh stylistic considerations. The inner loop, or agent revision loop, allows the translator to directly interact with a specific agent. From any candidate translation, the translator can request a targeted revision (e.g., “keep terminology but soften tone”), prompting the agent to revise its previous output rather than regenerate from scratch. This enables fine-grained, selective iteration and supports early stopping once the translator’s quality threshold is met.

5 Evaluation 

We evaluated CHORUS using a set of publicly available datasets (i.e., an ablation study) The goal of evaluation is to test how MQM-aligned multi-agent structure can reliably operationalize multidimensional quality reasoning that underlies professional translation practice. 

5.1 Experiment Setup 

We evaluate bidirectional translation between English and six target languages—German (DE), Hebrew (HE), Japanese (JA), Russian (RU), Chinese (ZH), and Arabic (AR)—using sentence-level test sets drawn from WMT 2023 and WMT 2024. The evaluation set consists of approximately 200 randomly sampled sentences per direction, covering general-domain and news-style content, consistent with prior MQM-based studies. 

5.2 Conditions 

We compare three conditions: (1) Zero-shot translation, where the base LLM directly translates the source sentence. (2) Single-agent self-refinement, where the same LLM critiques and revises its own output in a single pass following MQM-style instructions. (3) CHORUS (agent-only mode), where translation is refined through a single round of MQM-aligned multi-agent collaboration identical to the CHORUS architecture, but without human intervention. All approaches use the same underlying LLM backbone to ensure fairness. We report results across multiple state-of-the-art LLMs, including GPT-4o, Gemini 2.0 Flash, and Claude 3 Haiku. Model temperature is fixed between 0 and 0.3 to reduce stochastic variation.

5.3 Metrics 

We report both surface-level and semantic evaluation metrics: BLEU, METEOR, BLEURT, and COMET. While BLEU and METEOR capture n-gram overlap, BLEURT and COMET are neural metrics trained to correlate more closely with human judgments of adequacy and fluency. 

5.4 Results 

We summarize our findings using four key results that highlight how MQM-aligned multi-agent collaboration contributes to translation quality, and why this structure is meaningful for human-centered translation workflows, see Figure 2 for breakdown. 

R1. CHORUS consistently improves translation quality across models and language pairs. Across three LLM backbones and 11 bidirectional translation directions, CHORUS outperforms zero-shot translation and single-agent self-refinement on most metrics. Averaged across settings, CHORUS improves BLEU by up to +10.6 and COMET by up to +8.7 over the single-agent baseline. Paired bootstrap and ANOVA tests confirm these gains are statistically significant in most directions (p < 0.001). 

R2. Improvements are strongest on neural metrics that better reflect semantic adequacy and contextual appropriateness. While surface metrics (BLEU, METEOR) show moderate gains (typically 5–12%), CHORUS produces substantially larger improvements on BLEURT and COMET. Across models and directions, COMET scores increase by up to 21.7% relative to single-agent refinement, with consistent statistical significance (p < 0.001). This suggests that MQM-aligned decomposition preferentially improves meaning preservation and context-sensitive adaptation—qualities more closely aligned with human judgments. 

R3. CHORUS is especially effective for linguistically distant language pairs and weaker baseline models. 

The largest gains occur in high-divergence directions such as EN ↔JA, EN ↔ZH, and EN ↔HE, where CHORUS improves COMET by more than 20% over single-agent refinement. These effects are stronger under weaker base models and remain statistically significant (p < 0.001). Specialization helps compensate for limitations in holistic direct generation. 

R4. Agent-only evaluation validates MQM-aligned decomposition as a foundation for human-in-the-loop collaboration. Although this evaluation did not directly engage human participants, the consistent gains show that CHORUS can reliably externalize and coordinate multidimensional quality reasoning in a stable and interpretable manner. This establishes a necessary lower-bound validation of the system’s collaborative architecture, demonstrating that it provides a reliable substrate upon which translators can exercise selective control, prioritization, and stopping decisions in human-guided workflows. 

6 Conclusion and Future Work 

This work argues that the central challenge in professional translation is no longer generation quality alone, but how AI systems support translators’ multi-dimensional judgment in high-stakes contexts. CHORUS demonstrates the potential of reframing translation tools around interactive quality reasoning, where translators guide, interrogate, and selectively refine AI output rather than accept or reject it wholesale. By structuring interaction around explicit quality dimensions and lightweight agent specialization, CHORUS makes translation decisions more inspectable, steerable, and aligned with professional practice. More broadly, the framework illustrates how multi-agent systems can function as cognitive scaffolding, externalizing expert reasoning processes instead of attempting to replace them. Future work will empirically evaluate CHORUS in human-in-the-loop settings. We plan to recruit professional translators to complete realistic
