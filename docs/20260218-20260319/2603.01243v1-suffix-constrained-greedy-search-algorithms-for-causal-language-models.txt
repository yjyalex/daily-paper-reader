Abstract 

Large language models (LLMs) are power-ful tools that have found applications beyond human-machine interfaces and chatbots. In particular, their ability to generate reasoning traces motivated their use in many prediction tasks like math question answering. Unfor-tunately, extracting the final answer in an LLM free-form output is difficult, as it is an information extraction problem on its own. In this work, we introduce suffix-constrained generation , that aims to produce well-formed LLM responses in which final answers fol-low strict templates and are guaranteed to be trivially parseable. To this end, we introduce several algorithms that are based on greedy search procedures. We experiment on several datasets, and show that our approach allows to guarantee trivial deterministic extraction of the final answer from an LLM output with-out having a negative impact on results, and even improving them. 

1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) exhibit strong gen-erative and reasoning capabilities and are there-fore used to tackle a wide range of downstream tasks. However, automatic quantitative evaluation of LLMs is challenging when the answer must be extracted from free-form natural language outputs. Indeed, LLM outputs can contain different stylistic markers, long thought chains, and allow for diverse formulations of the final answer. As such, build-ing robust pattern-matching algorithms for answer extraction from LLM outputs is far from trivial. For question answering, the most straightfor-ward approach is to provide formatting instructions in the prompt and to extract the last match of a pre-defined pattern in the output: the last number, the last element from a finite list ( e.g. , A, B, C, D), the last expression in a \boxed{} mathematical L ATEX command, etc. For example, we may aim for an output that follows a template like: 

<Reasoning> The answer is: <Answer>. 

where <Reasoning> refers to intermediate reason-ing tokens, and <Answer> is the final answer in a task-specific format. However, an LLM may ignore the formatting instruction during generation or, due to LLM verbosity, produce extra information af-ter the answer, among other complications such as exceeding the generation budget. Examples are given in Table 1. Beyond the question of automatic evaluation validity, extracting answers from LLM outputs poses important challenges when used in complex systems, e.g. , pipelines. In the case of question answering, prior work has mainly focused on two approaches to solve this issue. First, it has become a common practice to delegate evaluation to larger and (hopefully) more reliable judge LLMs (Zheng et al., 2023), often pro-prietary models behind API walls. Although easy to implement, such an evaluation framework suf-fers from inconsistency (Haldar and Hockenmaier, 2025), position bias (Wang et al., 2024), vulnera-bility to stylistic markers (Zhao et al., 2025), and, most importantly, lack of scientific reproducibility (Huang et al., 2025), among a longer list of other drawbacks (Li et al., 2025b; Gera et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025a). Moreover, this does not address issues related to the use of LLMs within wider systems. Second, LLMs can be constrained to generate only outputs that follow a predefined pattern, lead-ing to trivial deterministic answer extraction. To achieve this, one can define the (possibly infinite) language (or set) of well-formed outputs, for exam-ple using a finite-state automaton or a context-free grammar, and then, at each generation step, only allow for tokens that are guaranteed to lead to well-formed outputs (Willard and Louf, 2023; Geng et al., 2023a; Ugare et al., 2025, inter alia ). 2 Related work 

Constrained Generation. Previous works have proposed methods to explicitly enforce LLM gen-erations to be in the language generated by a gram-mar, for example, a regular or context-free lan-guage (Ghazvininejad et al., 2016; Allauzen et al., 2014; Shin et al., 2021; Poesia et al., 2022; Geng et al., 2023b; Beurer-Kellner et al., 2024; Ugare et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2023, inter alia ). Roughly speaking, all these methods constrain the next to-ken prediction so that it leads to a prefix of the target language (see Section 3.2). These methods have been implemented in several software libraries (Willard and Louf, 2023), and are easy to use. How-ever, such approaches are “overly restrictive” and prevent the model from freely reasoning before generating the expected answer. Other works have also proposed methods to in-troduce lexical constraints in free-form texts (An-derson et al., 2017; Lu et al., 2021, 2022; Zhang et al., 2024). These constraints specify which words (or tokens, or concepts) should be included (somewhere) in the answer. Although this al-lows for more expressive generation than grammar-constrained decoding, it does not guarantee that the final answer is easily parseable, and hence, these methods are orthogonal to our problem.

Generation with Reasoning. Tam et al. (2024) show that LLMs’ performance in reasoning tasks is heavily degraded when instructed to adhere to a structured output format, such as JSON, compared to free-form responses. This degradation is further amplified when the model’s output is explicitly constrained during generation using a grammar. Differently Banerjee et al. (2025) propose to switch between free-form and grammar-constrained generation with special tokens that mark the start and end of constrained sub-sequences. The model is encouraged with special prompt instructions to generate these special tokens. This approach is fundamentally different from our algorithms, which rely on a natural language tem-plate that naturally continues the model’s response. We additionally do not rely on generating extra spe-cial transition tokens. We instead force the answer to appear at the end (suffix) of the output. Some large language model families, such as Qwen 3 (Yang et al., 2025) and DeepSeek (Guo et al., 2025), introduce explicit thinking tokens 

<think></think> to isolate reasoning steps, while our solution can be applied to any model without further training. To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to formalize and address the suffix-constrained au-toregressive generation problem. We apply our suffix-constrained generation algorithms and eval-uate them on constraining the final answer part of LLMs’ outputs, motivated by producing parseable final answers for evaluation purposes.

3.1 Greedy Search 

Greedy search (or decoding) is a simple algorithm that approximates the solution to the problem in 3Equation 1 by starting with an empty output and repeatedly concatenating the next token with the highest probability to the current partial output. In the unconstrained generation scenario, the set of well-formed outputs is defined as: 

Yfree ≜ V ∗ × {⊥} ,

that is, Yfree is the set of strings from the free monoid generated by V followed by the special end-of-generation symbol ⊥. The role of the special symbol ⊥ is twofold. First, it is neces-sary for a locally normalized model to induce a well-defined probability distribution over variable-length sequences, avoiding probability mass leak-age to infinite continuations (Cotterell et al., 2024, Theorem 2.5.3). Second, it simplifies inference procedures such as greedy search, since sequence generation can be terminated in a principled man-ner when ⊥ is selected as the next token. 3

3.2 Constrained Generation 

In the constrained generation scenario, we are given a grammar G, and we aim for outputs from the language generated by G, denoted lang( G) ⊆ V ∗.The set of well-formed outputs is then defined as: 

YG ≜ lang( G) × {⊥} ,

that is, it is the set of strings in the language gener-ated by G followed by the end-of-generation token. To constrain the generation of sequences of YG only, one needs to change the greedy search algorithm so that each generation step is guaranteed to lead to a sequence in YG .

Definition 1 (Prefix) . Given a grammar G defined over a vocabulary V , the prefix language of G is noted as pref( G) and is defined as: 

pref( G) ≜ {u ∈ V ∗ | ∃ v ∈ V ∗ : u⊙v ∈ lang( G)}.

Definition 2 (Transition operator) . Given grammar 

G and a sequence y ∈ pref( G), we define the tran-sition operator next G as the set of tokens that can be concatenated to a given sequence to build a new sequence that is in the prefix language of G:

next G (y) ≜ {v ∈ V |y ⊙ ⟨ v⟩ ∈ pref( G)} .

4 Suffix-Constrained Generation 

In the suffix-constrained generation scenario, we assume we have access to a grammar G that defines the expected format of the answer, for example: 

The answer is: <Answer>. 

where <Answer> may be constrained to follow any expected format ( e.g. , a floating point number). Our goal is to allow the LLM to freely generate reason-ing steps in the beginning of the output, but con-strain the end of the output to follow this grammar. In this section, we show that we cannot rely on the constrained generation approach described in Sec-tion 3.2 for this setting, and propose a first simple solution to this problem.

4.2 Greedy Pipeline 

We now propose a first simple and naive solution to suffix-constrained generation, which we call greedy pipeline . Given an input prompt x, we first apply standard greedy decoding to compute the uncon-strained generation y ∈ Yfree . Then, we build the new prompt x′ = x ⊙ ⟨ yi⟩|y|− 1 

> i=1

by removing the last ⊥ token from the generation y. Finally, we ap-ply constrained greedy search on YG using x′ as a prompt. This process is illustrated in Algorithm 3. This approach can be easily and efficiently im-plemented in any existing generation framework, almost for free, as long as we know how to search for an output in YG .

5 Beam-Based Algorithms 

In this section, we propose heuristic algorithms based on decoding with a beam of width two. For all of them, the main hypothesis in the beam cor-responds to unconstrained greedy search (deriva-tion of the non-terminal R in the CFG example), whereas the secondary hypothesis corresponds to a sequence where the constrained suffix is being gen-erated (derivation of the non-terminal A in the CFG example). The algorithms differ in how they re-place the current secondary hypothesis with a new one and how a completed hypothesis is selected.

5.2 Bifurcation Position Penalty 

The previous constrained beam algorithm relies on the probability of the partial constrained hypothe-sis to decide whether it should be continued or not. Although it is a natural criterion for beam search, in our case the unconstrained greedy hypothesis is often considerably more likely than the constrained hypothesis , leading to repeated hypothesis replace-ment. Note that this pattern is in line with previous works that show that many hypotheses in a beam search procedure often share the same prefix, see for example Chan et al. (2025). As such, the algo-rithm introduced in Section 5.1 often reaches the end of the generation budget without returning a well-formed output. To fix this limitation, we propose a different cri-terion for hypothesis replacement that is only based on the score difference at the bifurcation position, that is, the position from which a candidate output switches from reasoning to the constrained answer-ing.

To evaluate our proposed algorithms, we ex-plore various experimental settings across different question-answering tasks and answer formats. 

6.1 Setting 

We experiment with OLMo 2 (OLMo et al., 2025) as it is a fully open-source model family for which we have access to different checkpoints and differ-ent model sizes. If no match is found, no answer is extracted, and the LLM’s response is evaluated as false.

6.3 Further Analysis 

The constrained hyp. beam generation algorithm produces longer generations because the hypothe-sis beam is frequently interrupted and less likely to terminate (1st failure case). Unlike the minimum bifurcation penalty algorithm, which has large per-formance drops in some settings, constrained beam performance remains steady and shows no large drops, though it still underperforms the uncon-strained baseline in most settings. Table 3 shows the proportion of terminated out-puts for each algorithm before the budget limit is reached. We can notice that all bifurcation al-gorithm variants always successfully finish their generation . Unconstrained decoding finishes its generation more often for IT models than for PT models, as the latter models are trained on raw text with no specific ⊥ delimitation. Finally, the con-strained hyp. beam algorithm fails to complete its generation in a considerable portion of cases on MATH500, ARC, and CSQA. This can explain the performance drop of the constrained hyp. beam al-gorithm compared to unconstrained decoding on the OLMo 2 13B models, both PT and IT, on these tasks. Since the only termination path of the con-strained hyp. beam algorithm is via the constrained hyp. as the unconstrained hyp. cannot generate the 

⊥ termination symbol, we can conclude in this case that the constrained hyp. is continually interrupted. To investigate this interruption phenomenon, we study in Figure 3 the entropy difference of the model distribution on the constrained hypothesis and on the unconstrained hypothesis after a single constrained step. 7 Conclusion 

In this paper, we introduce and show the inherent difficulty of the suffix-constrained autoregressive generation. Our work exposes the structural and algorithmic challenges underlying this setting and opens a new research direction in constrained gen-eration, particularly for less capable models with weak instruction-following abilities.
