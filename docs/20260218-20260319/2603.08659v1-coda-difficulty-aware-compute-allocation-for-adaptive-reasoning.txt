## Abstract 

The emergence of large reasoning models demonstrates that scaling inference-time compute significantly enhances performance on complex tasks. However, it often falls into another trap: overthinking simple problems, where repetitive rationales yield minimal accuracy gains at a disproportionately high cost. This motivates adaptive reasoning : dynamically aligning reasoning depth with instance difficulty. In this paper, we study adaptive reasoning from an optimality perspective, formalizing it as a utility maximization problem where tokens are allocated until the marginal accuracy gain falls below the incremental cost. Based on this, we propose C ODA (Co mpute Allocation by Difficulty Awareness), a method that operationalizes this principle by allocating tokens via a policy-internal difficulty signal. Specifically, C ODA estimates difficulty via group-based rollouts and maps it to two non-negative gates that modulate a length-dependent shaping term on top of the binary base reward. The easy-side gate penalizes verbosity on simple instances, whereas the hard-side gate encourages more deliberative rollouts on challenging ones. Across model scales and benchmarks, C ODA achieves adaptive reasoning without external annotations or user-provided budgets: on easy tasks, CODA reduces token costs by over 60% while maintaining strong accuracy, whereas on hard tasks it incentivizes more deliberative rollouts to maximize performance. 

## 1 Introduction 

Recent progress in large language models (LLMs) highlights the value of test-time scaling for reasoning [ 16 ; 24 ; 14 ; 8]. Specifically, Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) has facilitated the emergence of large reasoning models (LRMs) by leveraging simple rule-based rewards through algorithms like Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) [ 27 ], effectively eliciting Long Chain-of-Thought (Long CoT) reasoning [ 40 ; 13 ]. However, longer reasoning also increases inference cost and can induce overthinking. On many easy instances, models tend to produce verbose narratives that include redundant steps with little or no gain in correctness [ 34 ; 30 ; 5]. This mismatch between compute and utility creates a practical bottleneck for deploying reasoning models at scale [15]. A natural idea is to penalize length during training, which can reduce token usage but often hurts accuracy on hard instances requiring deep reasoning [ 36 ; 37 ; 3]. Alternatively, methods like L1 [ 1]allow users to specify a target budget during inference. As Figure 1b shows, this provides direct control but is often sensitive to difficulty: underestimating the budget can hurt performance, while overestimating it leads to wasted compute [ 12 ; 35 ]. Broadly, many approaches described as adaptive reasoning trade compute for accuracy by deciding when to think more, yet their efficiency gains can still come from accepting modest performance drops for cost savings over typical inputs [ 18 ; 33 ; 41 ]. Together, these limitations motivate correctness-aligned objectives that allocate compute by difficulty.

Compute as a controllable resource. For autoregressive language models, generation length provides a practical proxy for measuring inference compute. Let n(o) denote the number of generated tokens in an output o. For a question q, consider a budgeted decoding scheme that enforces an explicit effective token budget n, e.g., truncating at n tokens, or conditioning decoding on a target budget [ 12 ; 1 ], and let Pr( correct | q, n ) denote the resulting success probability. We measure compute by an increasing cost function C(n), which is typically proportional to token count, and define the utility of allocating n tokens to q:        

> Uq(n)≜Pr( correct |q, n )−λ C (n),(1)

where λ ≥ 0 controls the cost–accuracy trade-off. Empirically, Pr( correct | q, n ) often shows fast saturation on easier questions, whereas additional tokens can still yield substantial gains on harder ones, as shown in Figure 1b. 

Difficulty and practical proxies. Let dq denote the difficulty of question q under the current policy, where a larger dq indicates a harder instance. Since dq is not directly observable, previous work often relies on human priors or dataset-specific heuristics [ 29 ; 17 ]. Importantly, dq is policy-dependent: as the policy improves, the same instance may become “easier”. In the following analysis, we treat dq

as an abstract variable and only assume access to a difficulty score that ranks questions consistently with dq , while we defer the concrete construction of such a proxy to Section 3. 

Marginal optimality and effective token prices. To characterize when extra compute is worth-while, we study how utility changes with the budget. Although n is a discrete integer token count, we quantify the benefit of adding a few more tokens by treating n as a smooth budget variable and using the slope of Pr( correct | q, n ) with respect to n, which we approximate by a small difference between nearby budgets. We then define the marginal gain as gq (n) ≜ ∂∂n Pr( correct | q, n ). Differentiating Eq. (1) yields the marginal optimality condition:     

> gq(n)≈λ C ′(n),(2)

which balances marginal correctness gain against marginal compute cost. Equivalently, a small budget increase dn changes the expected utility by (gq (n) − λC ′(n)) d n, so it is beneficial to increase 

n while gq (n) > λC ′(n) and to stop once gq (n) ≤ λC ′(n). In practice, the marginal gain gq (n)

decays much faster on easy questions, but can remain sizable over a longer range on hard ones. This view suggests interpreting λ as a dynamic effective token price : a larger λ makes tokens more expensive and induces earlier stopping, while a smaller λ encourages spending more compute when additional reasoning is productive. 

Difficulty-aware compute allocation. We now connect this optimality view to question difficulty. Assume a mild structural property: for any fixed budget n, harder questions admit no smaller marginal gains from extra compute, i.e., gq (n) is non-decreasing in difficulty dq .1 Under typical costs (C′(n) > 0 and non-decreasing) and diminishing returns in n (so gq (n) eventually decreases with 

n), the balance point gq (n) = λC ′(n) occurs at a larger budget for questions with larger gq (·). In other words, harder questions naturally justify spending more tokens before marginal gains fall below marginal cost, whereas easy tasks saturate much earlier. It is therefore convenient to re-parameterize this per-question optimal budget as a question-dependent token price . Specifically, we replace the global λ with a multiplier λq and consider Pr( correct |

q, n ) − λq C(n). A (local) optimum n⋆q satisfies the condition gq (n⋆q ) = λq C′(n⋆q ): increasing λq

shifts the marginal-cost curve upward and yields a smaller n⋆q (earlier stopping), whereas decreasing 

λq yields a larger n⋆q (more compute). Thus, allocating more tokens to harder questions can be equivalently viewed as assigning them a lower effective token price, with smaller λq . In contrast, easy questions face a relatively higher price, with larger λq .Finally, Section 3 turns this token-pricing view into a practical training objective by introducing a policy-internal proxy for relative difficulty and using it to modulate the effective compute cost across questions, enabling difficulty-aware compute allocation without external difficulty annotations.

4.1 Experimental Setup Models and Training Datasets. To assess the effectiveness of C ODA across different model sizes, we select Qwen3-4B/8B/14B-Base [ 39 ] as backbone models. We use base backbones, rather than instruction-tuned variants, to isolate the effect of post-training and to more clearly characterize the training dynamics induced by RL from a common pre-training initialization. We conduct training on DeepScaleR [ 19 ], a mathematics dataset of 40K problems spanning a wide range of difficulty levels, drawn from AIME (1983–2023), AMC, Omni-Math [7], and STILL [23]. 

Baselines. We evaluate C ODA against the following baselines: 1) GRPO [27 ], a widely used group-based RL method for improving reasoning performance, often benefiting from Long CoT and test-time scaling. 2) Vanilla Length Penalty (V LP ) [3], which introduces length penalties during RL training to avoid overly long generations. 3) Adaptive Self-Recovery Reasoning (A SRR ) [43 ], an adaptive length-penalty approach for efficient reasoning that modulates the penalty strength based on estimated difficulty, imposing larger penalties on easier questions. While effective at reducing token usage, these length-penalty baselines may trade accuracy for brevity by discouraging long reasoning even when it is beneficial on hard instances. Additional details are provided in Appendix B. 

Implementation Details. We implement all RL methods using the open-source training framework verl [ 28 ]. Unless otherwise specified, we train with a batch size of 128 and sample a group of G = 16 

rollouts per prompt, with a maximum generation length of 16 K tokens per rollout. We run training for one epoch, corresponding to 314 global update steps, and set the default sampling temperature to 

1.0. All experiments are conducted on 8 NVIDIA H800 GPUs. For fair comparison, we keep the same training and sampling configuration for all baselines. To trim redundancy on simple tasks while preserving deliberation for complex ones, we set α = β = 0 .2, τeasy = 0 .75 , and τhard = 0 .25 as stable defaults after light validation. 

Evaluation. To evaluate reasoning performance under both in-domain and out-of-domain distribu-tions, we consider a diverse suite of general and mathematical benchmarks. For general reasoning, we use CSQA [ 31 ] and GPQA [ 26 ], which test commonsense and knowledge-intensive question answering, respectively. For mathematical reasoning, we evaluate on SVAMP [ 25 ], GSM8K [ 6], MATH [ 11 ], AMC23 [ 2], AIME24 [ 21 ], and AIME25 [ 22 ], ranging from elementary to competition-level difficulty. We evaluate model performance using accuracy and response length to assess both correctness and generation behavior. To reduce single-sample decoding variance, we report mean@8 

by sampling 8 outputs per prompt. 2 During inference, we use temperature 0.6 and top-p 0.95 .

Compared to length-penalty-based methods, C ODA improves efficiency without compromising accuracy. For example, on AIME25 with the 8B backbone, A SRR reduces the average token cost from 7389 to 5950, but its accuracy drops from 22.1% to 18.8%. This performance degradation suggests that adaptive reasoning involves more than simply applying length penalties to shorten generations. Instead, the policy must autonomously decide when additional tokens are warranted and when to save them to optimize utility. In contrast, C ODA dynamically conditions length shaping on estimated difficulty signals, yielding selective efficiency gains on easy problems while preserving sufficient compute to maintain strong performance on hard ones.

## 6 Related Work 

Test-time scaling in LLMs. Recent advances in LLMs highlight the power of test-time scaling: spending more inference compute, often via longer deliberation traces, substantially improves reasoning performance [ 16 ; 24 ; 14 ; 8]. In particular, Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) has attracted growing attention for advancing reasoning capability, contributing to the emergence of large reasoning models (LRMs) [ 9; 15 ]. With simple rule-based rewards, RLVR algorithms such as Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) [ 27 ] can elicit Long Chain-of-Thought (Long CoT) [ 40 ; 13 ]. This supports deeper reasoning patterns such as searching and self-verification by allocating more inference-time compute [ 4 ; 42 ]. However, this paradigm also incurs substantial overhead from extended outputs, amplifying the “overthinking” phenomenon: on easy queries, models often produce redundant restatement and backtracking with little accuracy gain [ 34 ;30; 5]. This leads to systematic inefficiency and motivates token-efficient compute allocation. 

Length control and efficient reasoning. A natural approach to efficiency is to control generation length explicitly. Some methods require or allow users to specify an estimated target budget to guide inference-time compute [ 12 ; 1; 10 ]. While this offers direct control, it can be sensitive in practice: underestimating the budget can sharply hurt performance, whereas overestimating the difficulty can waste tokens when extra reasoning is unnecessary [ 35 ]. Another line of work reduces token usage by introducing length penalties or constraints during training [ 32 ; 36 ; 37 ; 3 ], but such techniques can carry the risk of performance degradation, particularly on challenging instances that genuinely benefit from deep reasoning. Relatedly, many methods described as adaptive reasoning aim to improve the accuracy–efficiency trade-off by learning when to think more or less, yet they frequently achieve efficiency mainly by accepting modest performance drops to unlock large cost savings over the query distribution [ 18 ; 33 ; 41 ; 35 ]. This may reflect the difficulty of learning a universally optimal trigger, or RL optimization converging to a balance that prioritizes cost savings on typical inputs [ 17 ]. Overall, these observations motivate difficulty-aware objectives that reallocate compute by trimming redundant verbosity on easy instances while preserving sufficient depth on hard ones, without relying on user-provided budgets or sacrificing correctness as the primary optimization target. 

## 7 Conclusion 

We study adaptive compute allocation for LLMs through an optimality lens, treating generation length as a controllable inference resource. This motivates a difficulty-dependent allocation principle: penalizing verbosity on simple instances while encouraging deliberative rollouts on challenging ones. Building on this insight, we introduce C ODA , which leverages difficulty signals from group-based rollouts to dynamically modulate reasoning length through a dual-gated reward mechanism. Across model scales and benchmarks, C ODA produces genuinely adaptive behavior without external difficulty annotations or user-provided budgets, trimming overthinking on easy inputs—reducing token costs by over 60% —while preserving effective Long CoT on hard ones. Further analyses show that C ODA 

remains stable under difficulty shifts and that both gating mechanisms are essential for maintaining effectiveness, yielding consistent efficiency gains without sacrificing accuracy on challenging tasks. 9References

## G Limitations and Future Work 

Improving difficulty estimation. Like other group-based RL methods such as GRPO, C ODA relies on a group of G rollouts per prompt to estimate advantages and derive a difficulty signal. While this proxy is lightweight in practice and adapts naturally during training, it can be noisy when the rollout group size G is small or when reward signals are sparse in certain domains. As a result, developing more robust difficulty estimators (e.g., uncertainty-aware signals) could further improve compute allocation for adaptive reasoning. 

Scaling to longer contexts and larger models. Due to resource constraints, we do not systemati-cally scale C ODA to larger backbones or substantially longer context lengths. For example, in the extreme Hard -training setting in Section 5.2, we observed that a 16 K maximum response length resulted in a clipping ratio exceeding 5% for extremely difficult instances, compared to less than 2% in our main experiments. This higher clipping ratio suggests that some productive reasoning trajectories can be prematurely truncated, potentially introducing additional optimization noise. Consequently, the current context-length budget and training-set composition may not fully unlock the model’s potential under more generous compute budgets. However, despite these constraints, systematic experiments across multiple benchmarks and model scales (4B/8B/14B) show that C ODA consistently achieves genuine adaptiveness: C ODA reallocates compute to trim overthinking on simple queries while preserving the complex, reflective reasoning patterns required for harder problems. We leave systematic scaling studies to longer contexts and larger backbones for future work. 17
