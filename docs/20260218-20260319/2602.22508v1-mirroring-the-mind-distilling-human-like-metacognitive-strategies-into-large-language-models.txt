## Abstract 

Large Reasoning Models (LRMs) often exhibit structural fragility in complex reasoning tasks, failing to produce correct answers even after successfully deriving valid intermediate steps. Through systematic analysis, we observe that these failures frequently stem not from a lack of reasoning capacity, but from a deficiency in self-regulatory control, where valid logic is desta-bilized by uncontrolled exploration or the fail-ure to recognize logical sufficiency. Motivated by this observation, we propose Metacognitive Behavioral Tuning (MBT), a post-training frame-work that explicitly injects metacognitive behav-iors into the model’s thought process. MBT imple-ments this via two complementary formulations: (1) MBT-S, which synthesizes rigorous reason-ing traces from scratch, and (2) MBT-R, which rewrites the student’s initial traces to stabilize in-trinsic exploration patterns. Experiments across multi-hop QA benchmarks demonstrate that MBT consistently outperforms baselines, achieving no-table gains on challenging benchmarks. By ef-fectively eliminating reasoning collapse, MBT achieves higher accuracy with significantly re-duced token consumption, demonstrating that in-ternalizing metacognitive strategies leads to more stable and robust reasoning.

Our Findings To quantify the prevalence of these process-level deviations, we analyze reasoning traces from Multi-Hop Question Answering (MHQA) benchmarks. We begin with a representative example in Figure 1, generated by Qwen3-4B, which illustrates a recurring failure pattern. In this retrieval-augmented scenario asking, “Who is the child of Caroline LeRoy’s spouse?” , the model successfully re-trieves the necessary facts: it identifies the spouse as Daniel Webster and locates his son, Fletcher Webster. However, despite correctly deriving the answer, the model falls into a self-imposed constraint trap, assuming the child must be from their specific marriage. The model fails to monitor or verify the validity of this new constraint, leading it to unjustifiably reject the correct candidate. This example vividly demonstrates that the failure stems not from insuffi-cient capacity, but from a lack of metacognitive monitoring, specifically the failure to critically evaluate its own logical constraints before overriding a valid conclusion. We demonstrate that this failure pattern occurs at scale across multiple MHQA benchmarks, including MuSiQue (Trivedi et al., 2022), HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018), and 2WikiMultiHopQA (Ho et al., 2020). We clas-sify reasoning traces to identify instances where the correct answer is successfully derived during intermediate steps yet discarded in the final output. We term these cases answer-inclusive errors . To distinguish genuine logical derivation from incidental mentions, such as random guesses or rote enumeration, we employ an LLM-as-a-Judge evaluation. This approach ensures that the presence of the correct an-swer is explicitly grounded in relevant retrieved evidence. Figure 2 reports the prevalence of these verified errors, con-firming that a substantial fraction of model failures stem from a critical breakdown in metacognitive monitoring, where the model successfully derives the truth but fails to recognize and preserve it. Details regarding the specific judge model and evaluation prompts are provided in the Appendix E.1. Motivated by these observations, we characterize two dom-inant failure modes in LRMs: overthinking and under-thinking . While prior works in mathematical reasoning defined overthinking as generating an excessive number of solution candidates and underthinking as frequent thought switching that hinders deep exploration (Chen et al., 2024; Wang et al.), we refine these definitions to better capture the structural dynamics of reasoning in MHQA. We define overthinking in correctly answered samples as post-solution stagnation, where the model successfully derives the answer but engages in unproductive redundancy, such as repeti-tive confirmation or baseless self-doubt that adds no new confidence. Conversely, we define underthinking as incor-rect samples exhibiting pre-solution stagnation, where the reasoning process becomes trapped in a cycle of revisiting previous hypotheses or repeating the same questions, failing to integrate new information or converge on a solution. Existing post-training paradigms, particularly reinforcement learning methods like RLVR (Lambert et al., 2024; Shao et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2025b; Zheng et al., 2025) that rely on outcome-based rewards, can inadvertently exacerbate these instability issues. Given the high preva-lence of answer-inclusive errors, assigning rewards solely based on the final output introduces noisy training signals by indiscriminately penalizing the entire trajectory, even when it encompasses valid intermediate steps (Deng et al., 2025b;a). Furthermore, because the objective is tied exclu-sively to the final answer, there is little incentive for the model to optimize the intrinsic quality or stability of the reasoning process, leaving it vulnerable to the uncontrolled deviations described above. To address these limitations, we propose Metacognitive 

Behavioral Tuning ( MBT ), a simple and fully auto-mated post-training framework that explicitly injects struc-tured metacognitive behaviors into the reasoning process. MBT offers flexible data construction strategies, injecting metacognitive behaviors either by synthesizing reasoning traces from scratch or by rewriting existing ones. By fine-tuning on reasoning traces regenerated to exhibit these be-haviors, MBT proactively suppresses both overthinking and underthinking. Furthermore, MBT serves as a foundational alignment stage that refines the exploration space for subse-quent reinforcement learning. This structural stabilization enables the model to maintain logical stability even when optimized with outcome-based rewards. Extensive experi-ments demonstrate that MBT consistently improves accu-racy and robustness, effectively closing the gap between the model’s latent capacity and its realized performance. ## 2. Related Works 

Process Supervision Recent research emphasizes super-vising intermediate reasoning steps rather than relying solely on sparse outcome rewards. Process Reward Models (PRMs) have demonstrated that verifying intermediate steps significantly enhances performance in complex reasoning tasks (Lightman et al., 2023; Uesato et al., 2022). However, these methods typically depend on training external verifiers or collecting expensive step-level annotations. In contrast, MBT internalizes this supervision directly into the genera-tion policy, enabling the model to inherently monitor and regulate its trajectory without the computational overhead of external test-time verification. 

Cognitive Structures Integrating cognitive theories into LLMs aims to induce deliberate reasoning. Approaches like System 2 Attention (Weston & Sukhbaatar, 2023) and Plan-and-Solve (Wang et al., 2023) decouple planning from execution, while Self-Refine (Madaan et al., 2023), Reflex-ion (Shinn et al., 2023) and Chain-of-Verification (Dhuli-awala et al., 2024) introduce iterative self-correction. Re-cent works extend this by modeling reasoning as an inter-nal search (Xiang et al., 2025) or identifying behavioral seeds for RL (Gandhi et al., 2025). However, these meth-ods often focus on discovery or initial seeding rather than stabilizing the entire trajectory, leaving models prone to structural fragility. In contrast, MBT explicitly targets the post-training regulation of reasoning to ensure stability and efficiency. 

Reasoning Distillation A growing body of work focuses on distilling reasoning rationales rather than just final an-swers. Methods such as Distilling Step-by-Step (Hsieh et al., 2023) and Orca (Mukherjee et al., 2023) show that learning from rich explanation traces allows smaller models to out-perform larger baselines. Similarly, STaR (Zelikman et al., 2022) utilizes self-generated rationales to bootstrap reason-ing capabilities. MBT extends this paradigm by distilling not merely the content of reasoning, but the metacognitive structure itself, thereby transferring the ability to regulate the thought process rather than just the logical steps. 

Efficient Reasoning Prior works also address reasoning efficiency. Inference-time methods like TokenSkip (Xia et al., 2025) compress traces by skipping low-information tokens, while data-centric approaches like LIMOPro (Xiao et al., 2025) prune functional steps deemed redundant. Ad-ditionally, recent reinforcement learning strategies such as DLER (Liu et al., 2025a) and ShorterBetter (Yi et al., 2025) explicitly optimize reasoning efficiency by incorporating length penalties into the reward structure. While effective for latency, our experiments suggest that aggressive pruning can compromise stability in complex multi-hop scenarios. 

## 3. Method 

In this section, we introduce Metacognitive Behavioral 

Tuning (MBT), a streamlined post-training framework de-signed to enhance reasoning stability and efficiency by ex-plicitly injecting metacognitive behaviors into LRMs. The core objective is to refine how models plan, monitor, and sta-bilize their reasoning trajectories, preventing the structural failures of overthinking and underthinking. To accommodate different training needs, MBT provides two complementary data construction strategies, distin-guished by their dependency on the student model’s ini-tial reasoning traces. (1) MBT-S (Synthesis) constructs reasoning paths from scratch using a teacher model. This formulation enforces a rigorous metacognitive structure in-dependent of the student’s initial capabilities, effectively serving as a cold start for reasoning behaviors. (2) MBT-R (Rewriting) refines the initial reasoning traces generated by the student model. By identifying and rewriting unstable segments while preserving valid intermediate deductions, this approach aligns the student’s existing knowledge with structured metacognitive control. The overall MBT pipeline consists of three stages: (1) Ac-quisition of initial reasoning samples from the student model (for MBT-R), (2) Injection of metacognitive behaviors via teacher-guided synthesis or rewriting, and (3) Internaliza-tion through supervised fine-tuning (SFT) and subsequent reinforcement learning. 3.2. Metacognitive Reasoning Trace Construction 

We now describe how metacognitively grounded reason-ing traces are constructed. Central to both approaches is the injection of a structured reasoning flow comprising five distinct metacognitive phases: (1) Understanding and Fil-tering , where the model explicitly re-states the core goal and filters out irrelevant information to ensure a robust grasp of requirements; (2) Planning , where it outlines a high-level strategy and breaks the problem into manageable sub-tasks before deduction; (3) Execution and Monitoring , where the model executes steps while actively questioning the logical soundness of each transition; (4) Self-Correction ,where it simulates doubt upon detecting deviations, identi-fies logical gaps, and adjusts the path; and (5) Verification ,where the model rigorously examines the conclusion against edge cases and alternative scenarios to determine whether further exploration is required, ultimately establishing high confidence before deciding to terminate the process. 3.2.1. 4.2. Evaluation Metrics

Performance and Efficiency For accuracy, we report Ex-act Match (EM), F1 score, and LLM-as-a-Judge evaluation using gpt-oss-120b. For efficiency, we measure: • Average Output Length (Len): A proxy for inference-time computational cost. • Degeneration Failures (Degen): The count of outputs exceeding the maximum context limit (32,768 tokens). We focus our efficiency and stability analysis on MuSiQue, the most challenging benchmark in our evaluation. As shown in Table 2, baseline approaches exhibit significant limitations in balancing efficiency and stability. Rejection Sampling (RS) tends to amplify redundant reasoning pat-terns, resulting in bloated average output lengths without proportional accuracy gains. More critically, efficiency-oriented methods such as TokenSkip and LIMOPro, which rely on artificial pruning or skipping, severely compromise structural stability. We observe that this heuristic manipula-tion disrupts reasoning coherence, causing frequent infinite loops (Degeneration Failures). Paradoxically, this instability inflates the overall inference cost, rendering these methods impractical for complex multi-hop tasks. Standard GRPO also fails to mitigate this instability; it remains highly vul-nerable to reasoning collapse and ineffective at reducing redundancy, particularly at the larger 4B scale. In contrast, MBT demonstrates superior stability, achieving near-zero degeneration across all model scales. By internal-izing metacognitive regulation rather than applying external constraints, MBT enables the model to recognize logical sufficiency and naturally suppress unnecessary continuation. MBT thus produces substantially more concise reasoning traces while maintaining high accuracy, resulting in the highest AES among all methods. These results confirm that MBT offers the most efficient and robust solution for deploying LRMs, effectively eliminating the risk of struc-tural collapse even in the most demanding scenarios. Unless otherwise specified, all analytical experi-ments in this section are conducted using Qwen3-4B on the challenging MuSiQue benchmark.

## 6. Conclusion 

In this work, we identify a critical limitation in LRMs, where reasoning failures frequently stem not from a lack of capac-ity, but from inadequate monitoring and control over the logical flow. Our analysis reveals that correct intermedi-ate reasoning is often discarded or destabilized due to the absence of a regulatory mechanism. To address this, we propose Metacognitive Behavioral Tuning (MBT), a post-training framework offering two complementary formula-tions (MBT-S and MBT-R) that inject explicit metacognitive behaviors to stabilize reasoning through rigorous monitoring and logical control. Comprehensive experiments across various scales of mod-els and diverse MHQA benchmarks demonstrate that MBT consistently enhances reasoning accuracy and efficiency. Notably, the framework exhibits strong generalization to complex MHQA tasks, effectively tackling problems signif-icantly harder than those seen during training. Behavioral analyses further confirm that MBT mitigates both under-thinking and overthinking, indicating that the model has successfully internalized the capacity to monitor and control its own logic, leading to superior structural stability. Over-all, our results suggest that establishing rigorous metacogni-tive control is as crucial as scaling raw reasoning capacity, highlighting behavioral supervision as a vital avenue for advancing reasoning models. 8Mirroring the Mind: Distilling Human-Like Metacognitive Strategies into Large Language Models 

Limitations MBT’s reliance on a large teacher model in-troduces additional data preparation costs. While our results show that these traces generalize effectively across model scales, future work is needed to explore lighter-weight trace generation methods and to extend this framework to do-mains beyond MHQA. Broadly, we believe that shifting the focus from what to reason (content accumulation) to how to reason (process regulation) offers a promising direction for the next generation of robust reasoning models. 

## Impact Statements 

This work introduces Metacognitive Behavioral Tuning (MBT), a framework designed to transform Large Reasoning Models (LRMs) from capable but fragile systems into stable and efficient reasoners. The primary societal impact of this work lies in advancing the reliability and interpretability of AI systems. By enforcing a structured flow of plan-ning, monitoring, and verification, MBT makes the model’s decision-making process more transparent and auditable, which is crucial for deployment in high-stakes domains such as healthcare and legal analysis where “black-box” reasoning is unacceptable. Furthermore, MBT contributes to sustainable AI deploy-ment. By effectively eliminating reasoning collapse and redundant loops, our method significantly reduces inference-time token consumption. This directly translates to lower computational energy requirements and a reduced carbon footprint for large-scale model serving. However, we ac-knowledge that enhanced reasoning and planning capabil-ities carry dual-use risks, as they could potentially be ex-ploited for malicious purposes, such as sophisticated cyber-attacks or social engineering. While metacognitive regula-tion improves logical stability, it does not inherently align moral intent. Therefore, MBT should be viewed as a tool to enhance capability and stability, which must be rigorously complemented by alignment training and human oversight frameworks to ensure safe application.

## A. Answer Inclusion Analysis 

Standard evaluation metrics often fail to capture the nuances of the reasoning process of the model. To address this, we provide a complementary analysis to Figure 2 by quantifying answer inclusion using two distinct methods: strict string-based matching and a semantic LLM-as-a-Judge evaluation. Tables A1–A3 report results across MuSiQue (Trivedi et al., 2022), HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018), and 2WikiMultiHopQA (Ho et al., 2020) benchmarks, respectively. We categorize predictions into correct and incorrect cases based on the final output. For each reasoning trace, we determine whether the gold answer was identified or derived at any intermediate step regardless of the final outcome. 

Substring Match determines whether the gold answer string explicitly appears in the reasoning trace. While simple and transparent, this method is brittle; it misses paraphrased answers and can yield false positives by matching contextually irrelevant mentions. To address this limitation, we additionally employ an LLM-as-a-Judge evaluation. In contrast, the 

LLM judge follows a more permissive criterion aligned with Figure 2: the judge outputs “answer inclusive” if the reasoning trace identifies the correct answer (or a semantically equivalent answer) as a valid candidate with any supporting fact, context, or logical connection, even if the model later rejects it. The judge returns “answer exclusive” only when the gold answer is never mentioned, appears solely as part of an unfocused enumeration, or is explicitly framed as a random guess without supporting context. Importantly, the judge is configured to detect the presence of the correct answer during reasoning, without evaluating reasoning quality. The full prompt and decision criteria used by the judge are provided in Figure A4. Our analysis reveals two key insights regarding the reliability of these metrics and the nature of reasoning failures. First, string matching systematically underestimates reasoning in correct samples. B.4. Impact of Behavior Injection Strategies 

In this section, we analyze how different sources of reasoning traces affect the efficacy of behavior injection. To isolate the impact of our proposed strategies, we compare MBT against the following baselines: • Metacognitive Prompting (Prompt) : Induces metacognitive behaviors via system prompts at inference time without parameter updates. • Direct-R : Rewrites reasoning traces generated by the teacher model. • Distill-R : Rewrites reasoning traces produced by the distilled model, which was trained on the teacher model’s raw reasoning traces, and subsequently uses these rewritten traces to fine-tune the base model. 

Analysis of Accuracy-Efficiency Score (AES) Figure A1 reports the AES across these methods. First, the Metacognitive Prompting baseline yields limited improvements and even negative AES, confirming that inference-time intervention alone is insufficient to internalize stable control. Second, while rewriting-based baselines (Direct-R and Distill-R) achieve significant gains over prompting, they are consistently outperformed by MBT-S and MBT-R. 

Interpreting the Performance Gap This performance gap underscores the importance of data alignment. MBT-S establishes a strong baseline by synthesizing rigorous reasoning exemplars from scratch, while MBT-R directly optimizes the student’s policy by refining on-policy traces. In contrast, Direct-R and Distill-R rely on external traces (from teacher or distilled models), which may introduce a distribution mismatch or fail to address the specific reasoning deficits of the student model. Therefore, MBT’s approach of constructing highly structured synthetic data or correcting the model’s own errors proves most effective for internalizing metacognitive strategies.

B.6. Ablation Study: Disentangling SFT and GRPO 

This section provides a detailed ablation analysis of the MBT framework to disentangle the specific contributions of Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO). Table A9 summarizes the performance across four configurations: Base model, GRPO-only, SFT-only (MBT-S/R), and the full MBT pipeline (SFT+GRPO). 

The Risk of Unstructured Reinforcement (GRPO-only) Applying GRPO directly to the base model consistently improves accuracy metrics (EM, F1) across all scales. However, these gains come at a severe cost: GRPO-only models exhibit dramatically longer outputs and high degeneration rates. This indicates that reinforcement learning without prior structural guidance tends to amplify exploration blindly, leading to unstable and excessive reasoning behaviors. 

SFT as a Stabilizing Prior (SFT-only) In contrast, SFT using MBT-generated traces yields notable accuracy improve-ments while substantially reducing degeneration and reasoning length compared to GRPO-only baselines. Even without reinforcement learning, SFT effectively internalizes high-level reasoning discipline, stabilizing the process and mitigating overthinking. Comparing the variants, MBT-S produces tighter, more concise traces due to its synthetic nature, whereas MBT-R preserves richer intermediate deductions from the original distribution of the student model. 

The Synergy of Structure and Refinement (SFT+GRPO) The most consistent improvements are observed when SFT and GRPO are combined. Across all scales, this two-stage approach achieves the optimal balance between accuracy, efficiency, and stability. These findings highlight a clear division of labor: SFT establishes the metacognitive structure (learning how to reason), while GRPO refines the policy (learning what generates rewards). C.2. Baseline Methods 

In the main paper, we compare MBT against representative efficient reasoning baselines, TokenSkip (Xia et al., 2025)and LIMOPro (Xiao et al., 2025). Both approaches aim to produce compact reasoning traces by selectively retaining important components from model-generated reasoning and then fine-tuning a target model via supervised fine-tuning (SFT) so that it learns to reason more efficiently. Unlike MBT, these approaches focus on compressing or pruning existing reasoning traces rather than stabilizing intermediate reasoning through metacognitive control. 

TokenSkip TokenSkip first obtains full reasoning traces from a target model. It then estimates token-level importance scores using a bidirectional BERT-based model (Xia et al., 2025; Pan et al., 2024). Based on these scores, unimportant tokens are pruned to construct compact reasoning traces, which are subsequently used as SFT data. Following the TokenSkip, we generate reasoning traces on the HotpotQA training set using the target model Qwen3 (Yang et al., 2025). We filter samples based on answer correctness and randomly sample a compression ratio γ for each training instance from the set {0.5,0.6,0.7,0.8,0.9,1.0 }. We prune tokens based on the sampled compression ratio and use the resulting compressed reasoning traces as the SFT dataset for training. 

LIMOPro LIMOPro leverages reasoning traces generated by a model that is more powerful than the target model. It focuses on pruning functional reasoning components that do not contribute to logical progression, using a perplexity-based 20 Mirroring the Mind: Distilling Human-Like Metacognitive Strategies into Large Language Models 

importance score. Specifically, LIMOPro first segments the entire reasoning trace into intermediate steps using an LLM. It then classifies each step as either logical progression or functional (e.g., validation or error correction). For steps in the functional group, LIMOPro computes importance scores based on the change in perplexity when removing each step. Based on these scores, it prunes steps with lower importance and uses the resulting compressed reasoning traces as SFT data. In our experiments, we generate reasoning traces on the HotpotQA training set using gpt-oss-120B (Agarwal et al., 2025b). We use the same model for step segmentation and step classification. Following the official LIMOPro implementation, we filter samples based on answer correctness, apply a pruning ratio of 0.5, and use the pruned reasoning traces to construct the SFT dataset for training. 

Training setup For all baseline SFT experiments, we train the target model, Qwen3, for 1 epoch with a learning rate of 

1 × 10 −4 and a batch size of 128. We employ a cosine learning rate scheduler with a warmup ratio of 0.1. The optimizer is configured with β1 = 0 .9, β2 = 0 .999 , a weight decay of 0.0, and a maximum gradient norm of 0.3. All experiments are conducted using 4 L40 GPUs or 4 H100 GPUs.

## E. Evaluation Prompts 

In this section, we describe the evaluation prompts used throughout the paper to analyze various behavioral aspects of model reasoning. Rather than relying on a single notion of correctness, the evaluation framework decomposes reasoning behavior into multiple complementary dimensions, each defined by a dedicated prompt. 21 Mirroring the Mind: Distilling Human-Like Metacognitive Strategies into Large Language Models 

E.1. Answer Inclusion Evaluation Prompt 

The answer inclusion prompt (Figure A4) determines whether a reasoning trace is answer-inclusive, i.e., whether the correct answer is derived, inferred, or identified at any point with supporting context. The evaluation does not assess final-answer correctness or the reasoning quality. Instead, it applies a lenient criterion that prioritizes detecting the presence of correct intermediate conclusions, even if the model later rejects them. Conversely, the judge explicitly excludes cases where the correct answer appears only as part of an unfocused enumeration or as a random guess. The prompt underlines the answer-inclusive error analysis presented in Figure 2 and the quantitative results in Tables A1–A3. 

E.2. Metacognitively Grounded Reasoning Trace Prompt 

E.2.1. MBT-S: M ETACOGNITIVE TRACE SYNTHESIS 

The synthesis prompt used for MBT-S (Figure A5) instructs the teacher model (gpt-oss-120B) to generate reasoning traces from scratch. Given a query, contextual documents, and the gold answer, the prompt enforces a rigorous five-phase process: goal clarification, planning, execution monitoring, self-correction, and verification. Although the gold answer is provided as input, the model is instructed to simulate an independent discovery process without referencing the answer key. The resulting traces serve as high-quality supervision for MBT-S, enabling the student model to internalize explicit metacognitive structures. E.2.2. MBT-R: M ETACOGNITIVE TRACE REWRITING 

The rewriting prompt (Figure A6) guides the teacher model (gpt-oss-120B) to refine an existing reasoning trace generated by the student model. Unlike synthesis, which starts from scratch, this prompt focuses on reorganization. It explicitly instructs the teacher to preserve valid intermediate deductions while injecting metacognitive behaviors such as monitoring, self-correction, and verification. When the original trace contains errors, the prompt simulates a realization of error, directing the model to identify the flaw and naturally redirect the trajectory toward the correct solution. This design ensures that MBT-R stabilizes the initial reasoning traces while retaining informative intermediate steps. 

E.3. Behavioral Evaluation Prompt 

E.3.1. O VERTHINKING SCORE 

The overthinking evaluation prompt (Figure A7) is designed to detect post-solution stagnation, a state in which the model continues to generate tokens after the problem has been effectively resolved. The judge model identifies the precise transition point where necessary verification devolves into unproductive redundancy. We define overthinking to include repetitive confirmation, baseless doubt without concrete rationale, and circular looping that yields no information gain. The judge truncates the trace immediately prior to this behavior, and the cut-off point is used to compute the overthinking score ( ξOT )reported in Section 5.2. E.3.2. U NDERTHINKING SCORE 

Complementing the overthinking score, the underthinking evaluation prompt (Figure A8) addresses pre-solution stagnation, where the reasoning process fails to advance toward a conclusion. The evaluator detects the onset of structural collapse, such as repetitive questioning or looping over previously rejected hypotheses. Notably, this metric is decoupled from correctness. If a reasoning trace remains progressive and logically distinct, even when it is factually flawed or leads to an incorrect answer, it is not penalized as underthinking. The trace is truncated only when structural stagnation occurs, and this point determines the underthinking score ( ξU T ). E.3.3. M ETACOGNITION SCORE 

The metacognition score evaluation prompt (Figure A9) serves as a quantitative instrument to assess the depth of self-regulation. Utilizing gpt-oss-120B model as the judge, this metric assigns a discrete score (0–5) based on the explicit manifestation of the five core phases: understanding, planning, monitoring, self-correction, and verification. Lower scores reflect intuitive or strictly linear reasoning, whereas higher scores indicate a sophisticated, actively monitored trajectory. By outputting a single integer, this prompt provides a rigorous process-level measure of metacognitive quality, complementing the binary and truncation-based stability metrics. 22 Mirroring the Mind: Distilling Human-Like Metacognitive Strategies into Large Language Models 

E.4. Base Prompt 

For the Base model evaluation, we employ a minimal multi-hop question answering prompt (Figure A10) that requests only the final answer based on retrieved documents. This prompt imposes no structural constraints, allowing the model to rely solely on its internal inductive biases. The unconstrained setting serves as a control to measure the model’s native reasoning tendencies without external behavioral guidance.
