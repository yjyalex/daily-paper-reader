## Abstract 

Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) aligns large language mod-els by optimizing pairwise preferences and has shown remarkable effectiveness as a simple and scalable alternative to RLHF. However, in practice, preference data are often noisy. Real-world datasets con-tain both hard noise, where preference labels are reversed, and am-biguous comparisons, where the preferred and rejected responses are nearly indistinguishable. Such noise introduces significant ro-bustness challenges for DPO-based training. Existing robust vari-ants of DPO mainly rely on uniform objective modifications or global reweighting. While partially effective, these methods treat noisy samples as a homogeneous source of uncertainty and fail to distinguish between different noise types, leading to sub-optimal alignment robustness. In this work, we show that robust prefer-ence alignment benefits from addressing different noise types with targeted interventions rather than uniform regularization. We pro-pose winsorized Direct Preference Optimization (wDPO), a robust LLM alignment approach with hierarchical winsorization. Specifi-cally, wDPO adopts a reward-free hierarchical intervention strategy that leverages only signals already available during DPO training. It first uses the implicit margin from DPO log-ratio to identify heterogeneous noise patterns without relying on external reward models. For hard noise, wDPO performs a data-level intervention by sparsely correcting strongly inconsistent preference pairs. For ambiguous comparisons, it applies a gradient-level intervention through soft winsorization, capping extreme losses in the high-loss tail to prevent weakly informative samples from dominating gradient updates. Extensive experiments on PKU-SafeRLHF and multiple external safety benchmarks demonstrate that wDPO con-sistently improves preference alignment quality and robustness over vanilla DPO and strong DPO-family baselines, with particu-larly pronounced gains under controlled label-flip noise. Code is available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/wDPO-7BBC.

## 1 Introduction 

Aligning large language models (LLMs) with human preferences is essential for developing capable and safe AI systems [ 1 , 18 , 30 , 36 ]. While Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) [ 4, 5,8 , 24 ] established the dominant paradigm, its training complexity and sensitivity to reward model quality have motivated simpler al-ternatives. In practice, RLHF pipelines often rely on policy-gradient methods such as PPO [ 27 ], which can be unstable and require careful hyperparameter tuning. Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) [ 25 ] has therefore emerged as a standard approach, offering an efficient and scalable way to align LLMs by directly optimizing a preference objective over pairwise comparison data. Despite its success, DPO depends critically on the quality of preference supervision, which in real-world datasets is often imper-fect [ 4 , 12 , 15 , 28 ]. As a result, a growing body of work has explored robust preference alignment under noisy supervision. In general, current robust optimization methods mainly rely on uniform regu-larization of the preference objective, implicitly treating noise as a single, homogeneous source of uncertainty. Under this perspective, robustness is enforced by globally softening preference margins, reweighting losses, or guarding against worst-case distributions. Representative examples include objective-level modifications such as rDPO [ 7] and cDPO [ 23 ], distributionally robust formulations such as Dr.DPO [ 33 ], and approaches that rely on external reward models or classifiers to rescore and reconstruct preference data [ 40 ]. While effective in certain regimes, these methods apply the same form of correction across all preference pairs and therefore fail to account for the heterogeneity of noise in real-world data. Looking beyond such uniform robustness objectives, a closer ex-amination of DPO training reveals a more nuanced picture. We show that robustness challenges in DPO stem from the heterogeneous nature of preference noise , where different noise types influence op-timization dynamics in fundamentally different ways. As illustrated in Figure 1 (left), an analysis of a typical safety preference dataset reveals two prevalent forms of noise. Hard noise arises when pref-erence labels are reversed, contradicting the underlying reward signal. In contrast, ambiguous comparisons occur when the pre-ferred and rejected responses are nearly indistinguishable, forming a dense cluster near the decision boundary. Although ambiguous comparisons are not incorrect, they often induce large gradients with limited learning value. Crucially, these two forms of noise manifest their impact through a shared optimization pathology. In particular, it uses the implicit DPO reward margin as a consis-tency signal to characterize heterogeneous noise, without relying on external reward models or additional supervision. This signal enables wDPO to distinguish between failure modes that require fundamentally different treatments. Strongly inconsistent prefer-ence pairs, which are more plausibly attributed to annotation errors, call for data-level intervention . In contrast, ambiguous comparisons, which are inherently uncertain but not incorrect, primarily affect training through extreme losses and therefore require gradient-level intervention .Based on this distinction, wDPO applies two complementary interventions at different stages of optimization. For hard noise, it performs a sparse data-level correction of strongly inconsistent preference pairs, mitigating systematically misleading gradients. For ambiguous comparisons, it applies soft winsorization to the high-loss tail, constraining the influence of extreme but weakly informative losses on gradient updates. By intervening at the ap-propriate stage for each noise type, wDPO directly addresses the dominant sources of instability identified in the preceding analy-sis, while preserving all available preference data. We empirically evaluate wDPO on PKU-SafeRLHF and multiple external safety benchmarks. Across a range of settings with both natural and syn-thetic noise, wDPO consistently improves preference alignment quality and robustness over vanilla DPO and strong DPO-family baselines, with particularly pronounced gains under controlled label-flip noise. Our main contributions are summarized as follows: 

• We provide an empirical analysis of DPO training under noisy preferences, showing that a small subset of hard noise and am-biguous pairs can dominate gradient updates and destabilize optimization. 

• We propose wDPO, a robust LLM alignment approach with 

hierarchical winsorization . wDPO elaborates a hierarchical in-tervention strategy: data-level intervention for hard noise and gradient-level intervention for ambiguous pairs. 

• We conduct extensive experiments on preference alignment benchmarks with both natural and synthetic noise, demon-strating that wDPO consistently improves alignment quality and robustness over DPO-family baselines.

## 2.2 Direct Preference Optimization 

Direct Preference Optimization (DPO [ 25 ]) is a simplified alterna-tive to RLHF that directly optimizes the policy using preference data, without training an explicit reward model or performing reinforce-ment learning. ## 3 The Proposed Winsorized DPO 

In this section, we present wDPO, a robust preference optimiza-tion method based on hierarchical intervention over DPO training dynamics. Rather than applying a uniform correction across all pref-erence pairs, wDPO intervenes at multiple levels using only batch-level optimization signals. Specifically, wDPO combines a data-level label correction and a gradient-level soft intervention to address heterogeneous preference noise. At the data level, wDPO performs a soft form of label correction that mitigates the impact of likely reversed preference annotations. This is implemented via a sparse, flip-aware loss mixing mechanism, which selectively interpolates between the original and reversed preference losses for a small set of strongly inconsistent pairs. At the gradient level, wDPO applies soft winsorization to the high-loss tail, capping extreme gradients to limit the influence of ambiguous comparisons. In the following, we first provide evidence of a key failure mode in standard DPO, namely gradient dominance by high-loss pairs, and show how this failure mode motivates the hierarchical intervention adopted by wDPO. Then we present the two intervention stages in detail. 

## 3.1 Gradient Dominance as a Failure Mode 

Figure 2 presents a diagnostic analysis of DPO training dynamics under noisy preference supervision. Rather than evaluating final performance, this analysis aims to reveal a failure mode in how gradient contributions are distributed within a batch, which directly motivates our hierarchical intervention design. To characterize this behavior, we track three batch-level signals during training: (i) the tail energy share, defined as the fraction of total gradient energy contributed by the Top-group (report Top 30% ) samples in each batch; (ii) the Herfindahl–Hirschman Index (HHI) [ 26 ], which measures the concentration of gradient energy across samples; and (iii) validation margin and preference accuracy as indicators

of optimization stability. These signals are used to contrast the training dynamics of standard DPO and wDPO under the same setup (formal definitions are provided in Appendix E). Figures 2(a–c) illustrate the evolution of these signals for stan-dard DPO. Early in training, the tail energy share decreases, indi-cating a relatively diffuse gradient distribution. However, in the presence of noisy preferences, this behavior does not reflect im-proved optimization. At this stage, the model has not yet established a reliable preference direction, and both consistent and inconsistent pairs contribute comparably to the update. As training progresses, the model fits a large fraction of consistent pairs, while a small subset of inconsistent or ambiguous pairs remains difficult. These pairs retain negative or near-zero margins, which induces dispro-portionately large losses. As a result, their gradient contributions increasingly dominate the batch update. This transition is reflected by the rising tail energy share and the increasing HHI, where fewer than 30% of samples account for more than half of the total gradient energy. Such dominance concentrates optimization on a small num-ber of problematic pairs and destabilizes training, as also reflected by stagnating or degrading validation signals. Figures 2(d–f) show how this behavior changes under wDPO. The overall trend of increasing tail energy share remains, but the early diffuse phase observed in DPO is absent. This reflects the effect of gradient-level intervention, which remains active through-out training and limits the influence of extreme losses from the outset. Figure 2(d) further disentangles the two intervention stages. The data-level intervention reduces the contribution of strongly in-consistent pairs by softly correcting their effective labels, while the gradient-level intervention caps the magnitude of extreme losses relative to a batch-dependent threshold. Together, these interven-tions prevent any single subset of pairs from dominating the update, leading to consistently lower concentration as measured by HHI and more stable optimization behavior. Overall, this analysis demonstrates that standard DPO is vulner-able to gradient dominance arising from inverted and ambiguous preference pairs. By explicitly controlling gradient contributions at both the data and gradient levels, wDPO addresses this failure mode at its source, which guides the design of the two-stage interventions described next.

## 3.4 Discussion of wDPO 

Computational Complexity. wDPO introduces two batch-level interventions on top of standard DPO training. Both stages operate entirely within each batch and do not require additional models, data preprocessing, or extra forward passes. Stage I computes per-sample margins and two-direction losses, followed by sparsemax over a batch-sized vector, which incurs 𝑂 (𝐵 log 𝐵 ) time in practice. Stage II involves quantile computation and linear allocation over the loss tail, which can be implemented in 𝑂 (𝐵 log 𝐵 ) time. Overall, the additional overhead of wDPO is modest compared to the cost of model forward and backward passes, and does not change the asymptotic training complexity of DPO. 

Generality and Applicability. Although wDPO is developed in the context of DPO, its design principles are not tied to a specific preference optimization objective. Both interventions rely only on batch-level signals that are commonly available in preference-based learning, such as per-sample losses and margin statistics. This makes the proposed hierarchical intervention compatible, in principle, with other preference optimization frameworks that ex-hibit similar gradient dominance behavior, including variants of DPO with alternative loss formulations. More broadly, our analysis suggests that explicitly controlling gradient influence at multiple levels can be an effective design strategy for stabilizing preference learning under heterogeneous supervision. 

## 4 Experiments 

In this section, we provide more empirical evidences to show the effectiveness of our proposed wDPO and specific design choices un-derlying its data-level and gradient-level interventions. Specifically, we investigate the following research questions: 

• RQ1 : Does wDPO consistently outperform DPO-family base-lines under standard training conditions? 

• RQ2 : How robust is wDPO when preference supervision is corrupted by injected label-flip noise? 

• RQ3 : How sensitive is wDPO to its key hyperparameters that control the two intervention stages? 

• RQ4 : What is the contribution of each component in wDPO , and how do the data-level and gradient-level interventions interact? 

## 4.1 Experimental Settings 

4.1.1 Dataset. Following prior safety alignment work, we run ex-periments on PKU-SafeRLHF-30K [ 15 ] from PKU-Alignment. It contains about 29.9K paired comparisons, with 26.9K for training and 3.0K for testing. Each example consists of a prompt and two responses. The dataset provides preference labels for helpfulness and safety, and also includes a binary safety tag for each response. 

4.1.2 Baselines. We evaluate wDPO against a set of preference alignment baselines. We include the Vanilla pretrained backbone, SFT [ 24 ], and DPO [ 25 ]. We also include robust DPO variants that target noisy comparisons or distributional uncertainty, including IPO [13], cDPO [23], rDPO [7], and Dr.DPO [33]. 

4.1.3 Backbones. We study wDPO on four base LLMs that have not been safety-aligned via RLHF: Pythia-2.8B [ 6], Llama-3.2-3B, Llama-3-8B [ 2 ], and Qwen2.5-7B [ 31 ]. These backbones span multiple model families and scales. 

4.1.4 Benchmarks and Evaluation Metrics. We evaluate on PKU-SafeRLHF-30K (test split), Do-Not-Answer [ 32 ], HarmBench [ 21 ], HH-RLHF [ 4], and Salad Bench [ 17 ]. On the external safety bench-marks, we report Attack Success Rate (ASR; lower is better) as the primary metric. We compute ASR using two widely adopted safety judges: MD-Judge (MD) [ 17 ] and Nemotron Safety Guard (NV) [ 16 ]. In addition, on PKU-SafeRLHF we report Win Rate (WR; higher is better) based on pairwise judgments from the GPT-4.1 mini API. Ap-pendix C provides full details on baseline configurations, backbones, benchmark setup, and evaluation protocols. 

## 4.2 Main Results (RQ1) 

We evaluate wDPO under both in-distribution (IID) and out-of-distribution (OOD) settings to assess whether hierarchical inter-vention leads to consistently improved safety alignment. Across all settings, wDPO achieves the strongest performance among DPO-family methods, indicating that directly controlling heterogeneous gradient influence is more effective than applying uniform robust-ness penalties. 

In-Distribution Performance. Table 1 reports results on the PKU-SafeRLHF-30K test split. Across all four backbones, wDPO achieves the best overall safety alignment performance. While robust base-lines such as cDPO and rDPO improve upon vanilla DPO in several cases, their gains are inconsistent across models and metrics. cDPO adopts a conservative target to reduce overconfidence under noisy supervision, and rDPO assumes a fixed flip rate to debias the loss. Dr.DPO further introduces distributionally robust optimization. However, these methods apply uniform corrections across sam-ples, which limits their ability to address heterogeneous failure sources. In contrast, wDPO explicitly targets two dominant sources of training instability. It applies a sparse, flip-aware correction to mitigate the impact of likely reversed preference pairs, and it caps the high-loss tail to limit the influence of ambiguous comparisons. This targeted design allows wDPO to outperform even strong ro-bust baselines such as Dr.DPO, demonstrating the advantage of sample-adaptive, batch-level intervention over global regulariza-tion.

## 4.5 Ablation Study (RQ4) 

We conduct an ablation study on Pythia-2.8B to examine the in-dividual contributions of the two intervention stages in wDPO. Figure 4 summarizes the results. Each stage provides clear benefits when applied in isolation. Stage I improves alignment by addressing inverted preference pairs that inject misleading, wrong-direction gradients. By applying flip-aware loss mixing, Stage I attenuates the influence of these pairs and stabilizes early training. However, the gains from Stage I alone are moderate. This is consistent with our motivation: although inverted pairs can produce large losses, they are typically sparse and occupy only a small portion of the high-loss tail. Stage II yields larger performance improvements on its own. By directly capping the high-loss tail through soft win-sorization, Stage II reduces the dominance of extreme-loss samples that frequently arise from hard or ambiguous comparisons. This intervention substantially improves both WR and ASR, indicating that controlling tail-induced gradient dominance is a primary factor in stabilizing preference optimization. Combining the two stages consistently achieves the best per-formance. When both stages are enabled, Stage I first reduces the impact of the most strongly inverted pairs, preventing them from acting as extreme outliers. Stage II then caps the remaining high-loss tail, which is dominated by hard but not necessarily mislabeled pairs. This clear separation of roles makes the two interventions complementary rather than redundant. Together, they implement a hierarchical control over heterogeneous noise sources and lead to the strongest alignment performance. 

## 5 Related Works 

Preference Optimization for LLM Alignment. Aligning LLMs with human preferences is a central challenge. The dominant early paradigm is RLHF [ 8], which trains a reward model from pair-wise comparisons and then optimizes the policy with RL. Classic RLHF pipelines typically rely on PPO-style policy optimization [ 27 ]and careful tuning. They also require sampling during training, which increases cost and variance. To reduce complexity, many works study direct optimization from preference data. DPO is a representative method that turns preference learning into a simple classification-style objective and avoids explicit reward modeling and RL rollouts [ 25 ]. After DPO, several variants adjust the objective or its calibration. Some methods calibrate the implicit reward scale to better reflect preference strength [ 35 ]. Others modify the margin requirement so that only sufficiently strong preferences drive large updates, such as DPO with an offset [ 3 ]. There are also reference-free or monolithic formulations that simplify training further, in-cluding ORPO and SimPO [ 14 , 22 ]. Beyond pairwise contrastive objectives, alternative formulations use different preference signals or decision models, such as RRHF, SLiC-HF, and KTO [ 9, 39 , 41 ]. Overall, these methods aim to keep preference alignment simple and scalable while improving stability and effectiveness. However, these approaches largely assume clean or well-calibrated prefer-ence supervision, and do not explicitly address robustness under heterogeneous preference noise. Our work focuses on this setting and studies how to perform reliable preference optimization when supervision is noisy. 

Robust Alignment under Noisy Preferences. Several DPO-family methods improve robustness by modifying the training loss. rDPO explicitly models random label flips and derives a corrected objective, but it requires a noise rate as input [ 7 ]. Other work intro-duces a single global control to soften the margin or downweight uncertain pairs, such as cDPO [ 23 ]. Some methods also adjust the target margin during training to reduce sensitivity to noisy super-vision [ 29 ]. A common limitation is that these approaches apply essentially the same rule to all pairs, so they do not distinguish re-versed labels from hard but valid comparisons. Another line of work adopts distributionally robust training. Dr.DPO optimizes a conser-vative objective over the preference distribution to hedge against heavy-tailed errors and outliers [ 33 ]. This can improve stability, but performance can be sensitive to the strength of the robustness con-trol. A third line relies on external models to denoise the data. For example, Reward-Augmented Data scores responses with a reward model and rebuilds preference pairs using these scores [ 40 ]. This adds extra cost and additional steps, and the results depend on the quality of the scoring model. In contrast, our method stays within the standard DPO setup and uses only batch-level signals. It keeps all pairs, but it limits which pairs dominate the update. Specifically, it applies a sparse data-level correction to strongly inverted pref-erence pairs and a gradient-level intervention that softly caps the high-loss tail based on batch statistics.

## 6 Conclusion 

Preference optimization for LLM alignment often assumes reliable supervision. Under heterogeneous preference noise, however, DPO training exhibits a concrete failure mode: a small subset of problem-atic pairs can dominate gradient updates, destabilizing optimization and degrading alignment. To address this issue, we propose wDPO, a robust preference optimization method that intervenes directly in training dynamics while preserving the simplicity of the DPO frame-work. wDPO introduces a hierarchical, batch-level intervention that separates different noise sources. It applies sparse, margin-aware soft correction to strongly inverted preference pairs and adaptive soft winsorization to the high-loss tail to limit the influence of am-biguous comparisons. Empirically, wDPO consistently outperforms vanilla DPO and strong DPO-family baselines across various set-tings, with especially pronounced gains under injected label-flip noise—directly reflecting the failure mode identified in our analysis. More broadly, our results suggest that robustness in preference op-timization may require moving beyond uniform objectives toward explicit control of how different samples shape optimization. Un-derstanding and systematically managing the interaction between heterogeneous preference noise and training dynamics remains a fundamental open problem for the community.

## C Detail of Experimental Settings C.1 Baselines 

We compare wDPO with standard baselines and robust DPO vari-ants. Below we briefly describe each method. 

• Vanilla is the pretrained backbone without preference align-ment. 

• SFT fine-tunes the model with supervised targets. In our setting, SFT trains on the preferred responses in the dataset. 

• DPO [ 25 ] trains on preference pairs (𝑥, 𝑦 𝑤 , 𝑦 𝑙 ) with a fixed reference model. It increases the relative likelihood of 𝑦 𝑤 

over 𝑦 𝑙 under the policy, compared with the reference. 

• IPO [ 13 ] is a direct preference objective that replaces the DPO loss with an alternative formulation. It changes how the margin is mapped into the learning signal. 

• cDPO [23 ] modifies DPO with a conservative adjustment to reduce sensitivity to uncertain comparisons. It applies a single global control to all pairs. 

• rDPO [7 ] models random label flips and adjusts the prefer-ence objective accordingly. It uses an explicit noise parameter to correct the training signal.  

> 11 Conference’17, July 2017, Washington, DC, USA Trovato et al.

• Dr.DPO [ 33 ] applies distributionally robust optimization to preference training. It optimizes a more conservative objec-tive to reduce sensitivity to hard or high-loss subsets of the preference distribution.

We evaluate safety alignment across five established benchmarks, covering both IID and OOD test sets. PKU-SafeRLHF is used for training and for IID testing. We further use four external bench-marks to evaluate generalization. Table 4 summarizes dataset sizes.

• PKU-SafeRLHF [15 ]: A bilingual preference dataset for safety alignment. Each example contains a prompt and two responses, labeled as chosen and rejected. • Salad Bench [ 17 ]: A benchmark with adversarial prompts that use more complex language. It probes safety behavior under harder phrasing and additional constraints. 

## C.3 Metrics 

We report two automatic metrics: Win Rate (WR) and Attack Suc-cess Rate (ASR). 

Win Rate. WR measures how often the model’s response is judged safer than the dataset chosen response. For each prompt, we compare response A (the chosen response in the dataset) with response B (the model output). We ask a judge to decide which response is safer and more aligned with safety guidelines. The judge outputs two lines: a one-sentence comparison and a final choice. We use only the Safer: field, which is either “A” or “B”, to compute WR. We use GPT-4.1 mini as the judge and use the following prompt: 

Prompt for Win Rate Metric 

For the following query to a chatbot, which response is safer and better aligned with safety guidelines? Query: {prompt} Response A: {chosen} Response B: {response} FIRST provide a one-sentence comparison of the two re-sponses and explain which you feel is safer and more aligned with responsible AI principles. Consider factors such as re-fusal to provide harmful content, appropriate redirection, privacy protection, and avoidance of dangerous or illegal information. SECOND, on a new line, state only "A" or "B" to indicate which response is safer. Your response should use the format: Comparison: <one-sentence comparison and explanation> Safer: <"A" or "B"> 

Attack Success Rate. ASR measures how often a model pro-duces an unsafe response for an attack prompt. For each prompt, the model generates one response, and a judge labels it as safe or 

unsafe . ASR is the fraction of prompts whose responses are labeled as unsafe .We use two safety judges: 

• MD-Judge (MD) [17 ] 1. We use MD-Judge-v0.2-internlm2_7b from OpenSafetyLab. It is a safety judge fine-tuned from InternLM2-7B-Chat. For each model response, it outputs a binary safety label. It also provides a harmfulness score and the violated category when the response is unsafe. 

• Nemotron Safety Guard (NV) [ 16 ] 2. We use Llama-3.1-Nemotron-Safety-Guard-8B-v3 from NVIDIA, which is built on Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct. It can label both prompts and re-sponses as safe or unsafe. When a response is unsafe, it returns the violated risk categories.

## D Additional Experimental Results D.1 OOD Results on Smaller Backbones 

Table 5 reports out-of-distribution ASR on four external safety benchmarks for models aligned on PKU-SafeRLHF-30K, using Pythia-2.8B and Llama-3.2-3B backbones. Across both backbones, wDPO achieves the lowest average ASR and performs best overall. Relative to the strongest baseline Dr.DPO, the improvements on Pythia-2.8B are larger than those on Llama-3.2-3B. This pattern is consistent with smaller or earlier-generation backbones being more sensitive to noisy or ambiguous preference pairs during alignment. By correcting likely flipped pairs and capping the influence of extreme-loss pairs, wDPO yields clearer gains on this setting. On Llama-3.2-3B, strong baselines already achieve low ASR, which limits the attainable improvement. Nevertheless, wDPO re-mains competitive and often improves upon the best baseline across judges and benchmarks. Overall, these results suggest that the ben-efits of wDPO generalize across model families and are not specific to a single backbone. 

## D.2 Effect of Flip Warmup Ratio 

We study the sensitivity of the flip warmup ratio 𝛼 in Stage I. We run wDPO on Pythia-2.8B and vary 𝛼 from 0 to 0.4. Figure 5 reports WR on the PKU test split and the average ASR over five benchmarks. Figure 5 shows that 𝛼 = 0 leads to clearly worse performance. In this setting, flip mixing is enabled from the first update. However, early margins are often unreliable because the model has not yet formed a stable preference signal. As a result, many clean pairs can still have negative margins. Stage I may then assign flip weights to the wrong pairs, introducing incorrect corrections and destabilizing training.
