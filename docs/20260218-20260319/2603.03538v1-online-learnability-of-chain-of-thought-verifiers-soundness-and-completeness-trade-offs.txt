Abstract 

Large language models with chain-of-thought generation have demonstrated great potential for pro-ducing complex mathematical proofs. However, their reasoning can often go astray, leading to increasing interest in formal and learned verifiers. A major challenge in learning verifiers, especially when their output will be used by the prover, is that this feedback loop may produce substantial distribution shift. Motivated by this challenge, we propose an online learning framework for learning chain-of-thought verifiers that, given a problem and a sequence of reasoning steps, check the correctness of the solution. Highlighting the asymmetric role of soundness (failure in catching errors in a proof) and completeness (flagging correct proofs as wrong) mistakes of the verifier, we introduce novel extensions of the Littlestone dimension which tightly characterize the mistake bounds for learning a verifier in the realizable setting. We provide optimal algorithms for finding the Pareto-frontier (the smallest total number of mistakes given a budget of soundness mistakes) as well as minimizing a linear combination of asymmetric costs. We further show how our learned verifiers can be used to boost the accuracy of a collection of weak provers, and enable generation of proofs beyond what they were trained on. With the mild assumption that one of the provers can generate the next reasoning step correctly with some minimal probability, we show how to learn a strong prover with small error and abstention rates. 

# 1 Introduction 

Within the past decade, large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated unparalleled performance in solving complex mathematical and reasoning tasks. Many of these recent advances have relied on chain-of-thought prompting , where the model is asked to produce a step-by-step explanation of their reasoning, but also on post-training techniques to improve the quality of that reasoning. Specifically, the use of learned verification models is now described as a core strategy for improving the reliability and accuracy of LLM-generated solutions, contributing to breakthroughs such as achieving gold-level performance in the 2025 International Mathematical Olympiad [Shao et al., 2025] and solving open-ended mathematical research problems [Feng et al., 2026]. Despite this practical success, we are only beginning to understand the abilities and potential of chain-of-thought verification from a theoretical point of view. Recent work of Balcan et al. [2025] lays the foundation for studying verification of chain-of-thought reasoning from a statistical learning perspective. The authors introduce several frameworks and algorithms for learning chain-of-thought verifiers, including verifiers that are robust to out-of-distribution incorrect claims. 1

> arXiv:2603.03538v1 [cs.LG] 3 Mar 2026

Crucially, these results rely on the problem statements (prompts) being drawn from a static distribution. However, this assumption often fails in practical applications, where there is a dynamic interplay between prover and verifier. If the prover adapts based on feedback from the verifier, it may generate out-of-distribution instances that an offline verifier cannot reliably judge. In our work, we address this concern and extend the results of [Balcan et al., 2025] in three main ways: first, we study a more realistic online learning setting that makes no distributional assumptions about the instances or reasoning traces that the verifier will be faced with; second, we analyze explicit tradeoffs between different kinds of verification errors; and finally, we show how a learned verifier can increase the accuracy of a вҖңweakвҖқ prover (LLM), which can otherwise only produce correct reasoning steps with small probability. A central theme throughout our work is the tradeoff between soundness mistakes (accepting incorrect reasoning) and completeness mistakes (flagging correct reasoning as wrong). Sound reasoning is crucial to the wide adoption of modern AI: when LLMs confidently generate reasoning traces with subtle errors, these may lead to incorrect conclusions and a host of downstream problems, posing a grave concern. In contrast, completeness mistakes are often less problematic because the LLM can be prompted to elaborate on the proof details or propose an alternative proof. We show how to minimize the total number of mistakes given a fixed budget or cost associated with soundness mistakes. We further quantify the effect of soundness and completeness errors made by an online verification algorithm on the downstream accuracy of a prover. Specifically, we show how these learned verifiers can be used to boost the correctness guarantees of weak provers, by allowing them to generate correct proofs more often and for broader kinds of problems than they might have been initially trained on. 

1.1 Our Contributions 

We build on the framework of [Balcan et al., 2025] and study chain-of-thought verification in an online learning setting. Concretely, 1. We study the trade-off between soundness (false-positive) and completeness (false-negative) mistakes in online chain-of-thought verification. We introduce two natural models that interpolate between learning a perfectly sound verifier and allowing both kinds of mistakes: (a) Verification with a budget: given a budget for soundness mistakes, the goal is to minimize the number of completeness mistakes while adhering to this budget. We introduce a novel combinatorial measure, the SC-Littlestone dimension , which we show characterizes the optimal mistake bound in this setting. We prove matching upper and lower bounds. (b) Verification with linear cost objective: the goal is to minimize a linear combination of costs asso-ciated with soundness and completeness mistakes. We introduce the WSC-Littlestone dimension ,a novel measure which completely characterizes the optimal total cost in this setting. 1

Our main technical insight here is an equivalence between two natural verification modelsвҖ” chain-of-thought verification , where the learner must provide the location of the first faulty reasoning step and 

prefix verification , where the learner must only check the correctness of the last step in the prefix of a reasoning trace. This two-way reduction allows us to analyze the simpler prefix verification setting while ensuring that the tight bounds we provide directly apply to chain-of-thought verification as well.

1.2 Related Work 

We summarize below the research most relevant to our work and relegate further background and discussion on chain-of-thought and related research to Appendix A. Balcan et al. [2025] give learning-theoretic foundations for chain-of-thought verification. More broadly, recent theory has begun to formalize when sampling-based and reinforcement-learning-based post-training can improve reasoning by amplifying rare high-quality chains of thought. In the Sharpening framework, self-improvement is cast as a sample-and-evaluate procedure over whole responses, with a central coverage coefficient measuring how much probability mass the base model assigns to high-quality sequences; the resulting lower bounds highlight that best-of-N /rejection-style approaches inherently require non-negligible 

sequence-level coverage, and that sequence-level selection does not generally decompose tokenwise (e.g., greedy decoding requires additional structural conditions, Huang et al., 2025). Relatedly, the coverage principle of Chen et al. [2026] identifies coverage as a key quantity governing the success of post-training and test-time scaling methods such as best-of-N sampling. Rohatgi et al. [2026] extend this sampled-based approach to tokenwise verification, showing how to backtrack on proofs using a random walk guided by an approximate value function. In a complementary direction, Tsilivis et al. provide a theoretical account of why reinforcement learning after next-token prediction can unlock longer chain-of-thought behaviors but still relies on informative long trajectories not being exponentially rare. Our stepwise-verification perspective is complementary to these sequence-level views: rather than requiring the prover to place substantial probability mass on an entirely correct reasoning trace, we only assume a constant lower bound on the probability of producing a correct reasoning.

2.1 Background: Littlestone Dimension 

We give an overview of the concepts and techniques from online classification [Littlestone, 1988, Daniely et al., 2011] that are relevant to our work. We begin with the notion of shattered mistake trees .

Mistake tree. A mistake tree is a rooted binary tree, where each internal node corresponds to examples in some domain set X and each edge corresponds to a label in a a set Y (for example in binary classification 

Y = {вҲ’ 1, 1} and in multiclass classification Y = [ L]), such that the outgoing edges from a single node have different labels. We say that a mistake tree is shattered by a hypotheis class H if for any root-to-leaf path that traverses internal nodes x1, x 2, . . . , x d there exists a hypothesis h вҲҲ H such that the label of edge (xi, x i+1 )

is h(xi) for all i вүӨ d.4Intuitively, a mistake tree describes the strategy of an adversary against a deterministic learning algorithm. Internal nodes correspond to the examples that the adversary will present to the learner starting from the root. The pair of outgoing edges corresponds to the adversaryвҖҷs ability to induce a mistake no matter what the learner predicts. For example, if Y = {вҲ’ 1, 1} and the learner predicts вҲ’1, the adversary can reveal label 

+1 and follow the corresponding downward edge to the next example. Therefore, the interaction between learner and adversary corresponds to a root-to-leaf path and shattering ensures that the sequence of examples presented to the learner is realizable by the hypothesis class H.The Littlestone dimension captures the intuition that the more complex a hypothesis class is, the deeper the mistake trees it should be able to shatter. 

Definition 2.1 (Littlestone dimension) . The Littlestone dimension of a hypothesis class H, denoted as Ldim (H), is the maximum integer d such that there exists a complete mistake tree of depth d that is shattered by H.With the preceding understanding of a shattered mistake tree, it immediately follows that the Littlestone dimension lower bounds the number of mistakes of any deterministic learning algorithm. Littlestone [1988] showed that there exists an online learning algorithm, the Standard Optimal Algorithm (SOA), that achieves this lower bound. These two results completely characterize the optimal mistake bounds in online classification. Later, Daniely et al. [2011] extended these results to online multiclass classification.

3.1 Warm-up: Sound vs. Non-sound Online Verification 

To build intuition for our framework, we start by studying mistake bounds for finite classes. We show an exponential separation in mistake bounds between online learning arbitrary verifiers and online learning verifiers constrained to be sound . We say that online verification algorithm is sound if it never makes a soundness mistake. Formal arguments of these results are presented in Appendix B. 

Proposition 3.1. For any finite class of verifiers, H, there exists an online verification algorithm that makes at most O(log |H|) mistakes. Moreover, there exists a class of verifiers such that any online learning algorithm must make at least О©(log |H|) mistakes. Proof Sketch. The upper bound follows from applying a majority vote verification algorithm, which given an example sequentially verifies each subtrace of the reasoning trace. It accepts the entire reasoning trace only if a majority of consistent verifiers accept every sub-trace and else rejects at the first step where the majority fails to accept. For the lower bound, we consider a setting with a single problem statement and reasoning traces being binary strings of length L. 3.3 Prefix Verification with a Soundness Mistake Budget 

In this section, we study the trade-off between soundness and completeness mistakes in prefix verification when the learner is allowed to make a small number of soundness mistakes, k вҲҲ N. Specifically, the learnerвҖҷs objective is to minimize the number of overall mistakes subject to adhering to the soundness mistake budget, 

k. As motivation, we observe that even for k = 1 , the verifier class used to show the О©( |H|) lower bound in Theorem 3.2, can now be learned with no more than 1 mistake overall. For our analysis, we will adopt a framework similar to online learning with abstensions [Sayedi et al., 2010, Zhang and Chaudhuri, 2016]. We introduce a variant of the familiar notion of mistake trees that describe the strategy of an adversary against a deterministic learning algorithm. 

Soundness-Completeness (SC)-mistake tree. An SC-mistake tree is a rooted binary tree, where internal nodes are examples in X Г— ОЈвүӨL. Every internal node, z, has exactly two children: zs and zc. There are two types of edges: (z, z s) is a straight edge labeled NO and (z, z c) is a curvy edge labeled YES . We say that an SC mistake tree is shattered by a hypothesis class H if for any root-to-leaf path that traverses nodes 

z1, z 2, z 3, . . . , z d there exists a hypothesis h вҲҲ H such that the label of edge (zi, z i+1 ) is h(zi) for all i вүӨ d.A shattered SC mistake tree describes the strategy of an adversary against a deterministic learning algorithm. Specifically, the adversary can ask the learner to verify internal nodes of the tree starting from the root. Say that for an internal node, z, the learner outputs NO . Then the adversary reveals the correct label, (z, z c) = YES (forcing the learner to make a completeness mistake) and follows the curvy downward edge. If the learner outputs YES , then the adversary reveals the true label (z, z s) = NO (forcing the learner to make a soundness mistake). Since the learning algorithm is deterministic, the interaction between learner and adversary forms a sequence of labeled examples in a path down the SC-mistake tree, where every curvy edge in the path corresponds to a completeness mistake and every straight edge correesponds to a soundness mistake that the learner made. 9Definition 3.7 . We say that an SC mistake tree is (k, m )-difficult if every root-to-leaf path that has at most k

straight edges is of length at least m.Figure 1 shows an example of an SC-mistake tree that is (0 , 2) - and (1 , 1) -difficult. 

Definition 3.8 (SC-Littlestone dimension) . For a class of verifiers, H, and a soundness mistake budget, k вҲҲ N,the SC-Littlestone dimension is defined as SC-Ldim (H, k ) := sup {m вҲҲ N : There exists a (k, m )-difficult mistake tree for H}.

# 5 Discussion 

We provide the first results for learning chain-of-thought verifiers in an online fashion. In our analysis, we captured the asymmetric roles of soundness and completeness errors and proved tight mistake bounds via new Littlestone-style complexity measures, yielding optimal algorithms for Pareto and cost-sensitive tradeoffs. We also showed how such online verifiers can provably strengthen weak provers, and highlighted the effects of soundness and completeness errors in the downstream accuracy of the prover. Our work opens several exciting avenues for future investigations. A major strength of our algorithms is that they tightly characterize online learnability in terms of mistake bounds; however, they might not be computationally efficient. It would be useful to identify conditions under which we can develop efficient algorithms while possibly sacrificing optimality. Another interesting open question is to extend our results beyond the realizability assumption so that they are applicable more broadly, possibly connecting with ideas from data-driven algorithm design [Sharma et al., 2019, Balcan et al., 2020, 2018, Balcan, 2021]. Finally, it would be interesting to identify other conditions (beyond the (Оұ, Оі )-good prover in Section 4) when verifiers can be used for вҖңboostingвҖқ provers. 17 References

We provide below more detailed background and context of relevant literature on chain-of-thought reasoning and online learning. 

Chain-of-thought generation. Recent work has begun to place chain-of-thought generation on firmer theoretical footing, moving beyond its initial empirical success. Wei et al. [2022] first demonstrated that prompting large language models with explicit intermediate reasoning steps can substantially improve performance on complex tasks, motivating a line of work aimed at understanding why such intermediate representations are beneficial. From a learning-theoretic perspective, Malach [2024] show that auto-regressive next-token predictors trained with intermediate tokens are universal learners, introducing a notion of length complexity that characterizes how intermediate reasoning steps expand expressivity. Joshi et al. [2025] formalize autoregressive learning with chain-of-thought, analyzing sample and computational complexity when reasoning chains are either observed or latent, and Altabaa et al. [2025] further show that under suitable assumptions, learning guarantees can be independent of chain length. Other theoretical analyses study chain-of-thought in in-context learning settings, modeling it as implicit algorithm execution or compositional prediction and analyzing generalization behavior [Yang et al., 2024]. 

Chain-of-thought verification. As the use of chain-of-thought has grown, a parallel line of work has focused on verificationвҖ”assessing the correctness and faithfulness of generated reasoning chains. Recent theoretical efforts explicitly study the problem of learning verifiers that judge whether a given chain-of-thought is logically valid or supports a correct conclusion, providing formal learning guarantees under stylized assumptions [Balcan et al., 2025]. Amit et al. [2024] consider a complementary direction where the provers interacting with a verifier with certain guarantees can learn to not only generate proofs but also convince the verifier of their correctness. Growing empirical research provides evidence for the advantage of using verifiers in Chain-of-Thought reasoning [Cobbe et al., 2021, Ton et al., 2025, Shao et al., 2025]. In more detail, Balcan et al. [2025] show that if the prompt-reasoning pairs are drawn from a fixed but un-known distribution (say chain-of-thoughts from a given trained LLM on random problems of interest), then it is possible to learn a verifier that correctly predicts the location of the first error in the reasoning (if one exists) with high probability, with a small sample complexity (linear in the VC dimension, log |H| for finite verifier function classes H). However, given feedback on mistakes in its reasoning, the LLM may produce a different reasoning which is out-of-distribution and we are no longer able to reliably verify. On the other hand, if we re-quire the verifier to never accept an incorrect reasoning for prompts coming from a fixed distribution, they show a strong lower bound on the sample complexity, linear in the size of the verifier class H. Our work aims to de-velop a model for verification that overcomes this pessimistic lower bound by taking advantage of some weak assumptions on the correctness of LLM reasoning steps, while still allowing the LLM proofs to be adaptive. 

Online learning. The theoretical foundations of online learning are closely tied to combinatorial com-plexity measures, most notably the Littlestone dimension [Littlestone, 1988], which characterizes learnability and optimal mistake bounds in adversarial online classification. In his seminal COLT 1989 paper, Littlestone [1989] established an online-to-batch conversion, showing that online learnability implies PAC learnability with sample complexity controlled by the online mistake bound. A key challenge in using LittlestoneвҖҷs argu-ment in our setting of boosting weak provers is that the reasoning steps depend on the problem and context and are not i.i.d. Recent work has significantly refined this picture: randomized variants of the Littlestone dimen-sion characterize expected mistake bounds for randomized learners [Filmus et al., 2023], while computability-aware, strategy-aware and effective versions clarify when finite-mistake learning is achievable by constrained learners [Hasrati and Ben-David, 2023, Ahmadi et al., 2024, Delle Rose et al., 2025]. Another extension related to abstention-error trade-off inspires our formulations for different types of verifier mistakes [Sayedi 20 et al., 2010, Zhang and Chaudhuri, 2016]. Parallel to these developments, the online learning framework has been extended to sequential prediction problems with temporal structure [Sridharan and Rakhlin, 2011], where the adversaryвҖҷs behavior is partially forecastable, e.g., given by a noisy autoregressive function.

# D Alternate Mistake Model 

In this section, we analyze learnability under an alernate model for the learnerвҖҷs types of mistakes. We are interested in the trade-off between soundness and completeness mistakes (at the sequence level this time) via a linear cost objective. Specifically, the learner incurs a cost of Оіs вҲҲ RвүҘ0 for every sequnce-level soundness mistake and cost Оіc вҲҲ RвүҘ0 for every sequence-level completeness mistake. We additionally introduce the notion of a вҖңlocationвҖқ mistake, for which the learner incurs cost Оіl вҲҲ RвүҘ0. Formally, we destinguish between the following three types of mistakes: 1. (Sequence-level) soundness mistake , which occurs when the learner accepts an incorrect reasoning trace. Formally, the learner makes a soundness mistake in round t if ЛҶy(t) = вҲһ but y(t) вҲҲ [L].2. (Sequence-level) completeness mistake , which occurs when the learner indicates error in a correct rea-soning trace. Formally, the learner makes a completeness mistake in round t if ЛҶy(t) вҲҲ [L] but y(t) = вҲһ.3. Location mistake , which occurs when the learner rejects an incorrect reasoning trace, but mispredicts the location of the first faulty step. Formally, the learner makes a location mistake in round t if 

ЛҶy(t), y (t) вҲҲ [L] but ЛҶy(t)Мё = y(t).For convenience, we drop the term вҖңsequence-levelвҖқ in the remainder of this section. The learnerвҖҷs objective is to minimize the linear cumulative cost ОіsMs + ОіcMc + ОіlMl, where Ms, M c, and Ml is the number of soundness, completeness, and location mistakes that the learner makes over T timesteps. Here we assume that Оіs вүҘ Оіc вүҘ Оіl.As in Section 3.3, our goal here is to define the right notion of a mistake tree that will capture the optimal strategy of an adversary against any online verification algorithm. However, here the optimal strategy of the adversary depends on the learnerвҖҷs prediction in a way that might not come down to a binary decision. Specifically, if a learner predicts the first faulty step of a reasoning trace to be ЛҶy(t) вҲҲ [L], the adversary could induce either a completeness mistake or a location mistake and for the latter the optimal label y(t) вҲҲ [L]

for the adversary to choose might differ depending on the learnerвҖҷs prediction. Moreover, as is typical in mistake trees, edges are labeled with an element in the label set and in this setting a label in [L] could be used to induce either a location or a completeness mistake, so the tree structure must be expressive enough to distinguish between the two. Another challenge is the real-valued cost for each mistake, which must be accounted for in the tree structure.
