## ABSTRACT 

Large reasoning models (LRMs) achieved remarkable performance via chain-of-thought (CoT), but recent studies showed that such enhanced reasoning capabilities are at the expense of significantly degraded safety capabilities. In this paper, we reveal that LRMs’ safety degradation occurs only after CoT is enabled, and this degradation is not observed when CoT is disabled. This observation motivates us to consider encouraging LRMs to make safety decisions before CoT generation. To this end, we propose a novel safety alignment method that promotes the safety decision-making of LRMs before starting CoT generation. Specifically, we first utilize a Bert-based classifier to extract safety decision signals from a safe model (e.g., a CoT-disabled LRM) and then integrate these signals into LRMs’ safety alignment as auxiliary supervision. In this way, the safety gradients can be backpropagated to the LRMs’ latent representations, effectively strengthening the LRMs’ safety decision-making abilities against CoT generation. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our method substantially improves the safety capabilities of LRMs while effectively maintaining LRMs’ general reasoning performance. 

## 1 Introduction 

Large reasoning models (LRMs) [Singh et al., 2025, Comanici et al., 2025, Guo et al., 2025, Yang et al., 2025, Team et al., 2025] have demonstrated remarkable performance in tackling complex problems by utilizing Chain-of-Thought (CoT) [Wei et al., 2022] to enhance their reasoning capabilities. However, recent studies showed that such enhanced reasoning capabilities are at the expense of significantly degraded safety capabilities. To solve this problem, existing research primarily resorts to safety alignment for LRMs, which performs supervised fine-tuning on manually constructed safe reasoning data [Jiang et al., 2025, Zhu et al., 2025], so as to enhance the LRMs’ safety capabilities of identifying and refusing harmful instructions. Nevertheless, these methods hurt general reasoning yet only achieved limited improvements in safety. In this paper, we empirically reveal that LRMs’ safety degradation occurs only after CoT is enabled, and this degradation is not observed when CoT is disabled. As demonstrated in Figure 1, when CoT is enabled (CoT-ON), the safety capability of LRMs is very poor. Conversely, when CoT is disabled (CoT-OFF), remarkable safety capability can be observed. This observation indicates that the safety capability of LRMs degrades as the CoT generation enabled. This observation motivates us to consider: 

Can we improve the safety capability of LRMs by promoting LRMs’ safety decision-making before CoT generation?

To this end, we propose a novel safety alignment method that aligns LRMs’ latent representations with the safety decision signals. Specifically, we first utilize a Bert-based classifier to extract safety decision signals from a safe model (e.g., a CoT-disabled LRM or any other safe LLMs). This lightweight classifier can learn how to make correct safety decisions, instead of merely memorizing the predefined responses of training data. Subsequently, we feed the reliable safety decision signals outputted by the lightweight classifier into LRMs with an auxiliary linear head. In this way, the safety gradients can be backpropagated to the LRMs’ latent representations, effectively strengthening LRMs’ safety decision-making abilities against CoT generation. As illustrated in Figure 2, vanilla LRMs exhibit high reasoning but low safety, whereas safety-aligned LRMs exhibit low reasoning with relatively high safety. In contrast, LRMs trained with our proposed method achieve both impressive reasoning and safety capability. This because our method allows LRMs to preserve general reasoning performance for benign queries while directly rejecting harmful queries. We conduct a systematic empirical evaluation, and the experimental results demonstrate that our method significantly outperforms various baselines across different benchmarks, as measured by attack success rate. Concurrently, we also evaluate the general reasoning capability of LRMs trained with our method, and the results show comparable reasoning performance to the original LRMs. 

## 2 Related Work 

Large Reasoning Models & Chain-of-Thought. The evolution from Large Language Models (LLMs) to Large Reasoning Models (LRMs) marks a paradigm shift from reliance on statistical pattern matching to the capability for multi-step reasoning [Guo et al., 2025, Singh et al., 2025, Comanici et al., 2025, Yang et al., 2025]. The chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting technique [Wei et al., 2022] fundamentally enables this shift toward structured reasoning by verbalizing intermediate steps, which yields substantial gains on complex tasks. However, the linear nature of CoT constrains its application to problems requiring exploration of alternatives or backtracking [Yao et al., 2023]. To overcome this rigidity, subsequent research has introduced non-linear reasoning topologies. In a parallel direction, Self-Consistency [Wang et al., 2022] introduces a probabilistic ensemble approach, sampling multiple diverse reasoning paths and aggregating their answers to mitigate single-path variability and improve robustness. LLM & LRM Safety. Ensuring the safety of LLMs is a foundational deployment requirement. Standard practice employs post-training alignment paradigms such as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) and safety fine-tuning [Bai et al., 2022, Ouyang et al., 2022, Touvron et al., 2023]. However, these defenses are often circumvented by sophisticated adversarial and jailbreaking attacks that exploit the models’ instruction-following nature [Wei et al., 2023, Zou et al., 2023, Liu et al., 2024]. The advent of LRMs amplifies these concerns by introducing novel vulnerabilities inherent in their reasoning processes. The capacity for structured deduction can be manipulated to bypass safeguards (e.g., via reasoning-enabled jailbreaks) or to conceal harmful objectives within intermediate steps [Kuo et al., 2025, Zhao et al., 2025]. Consequently, research is increasingly focused on process-oriented safety [Jiang et al., 2025, Tomar et al., 2025, Zhu et al., 2025], shifting the focus from merely evaluating final outputs to scrutinizing the integrity of the reasoning chain itself. However, these process-oriented approaches primarily intervene during the reasoning chain. In contrast, our work identifies a more fundamental vulnerability: safety degradation in LRMs occurs only after CoT is enabled, suggesting that it is crucial to make safety decisions before the CoT generation begins. 

## 3 Methodology 

In this section, we propose our method that promotes safety decision-making before CoT generation, and we call this method PreSafe . Specifically, we first extract safety decision signals from a safe model into a Bert-based classifier, which serves as a parametric representation of the safety decision capability. Then, we use the Bert-based classifier to provide safety supervision for LRMs by introducing an auxiliary linear head on top of the reasoning model and simultaneously performing safety alignment of latent representations and standard safety alignment training. 3.1 Extracting Safety Decision Signals 

Firstly, we feed multiple queries into a safe model (e.g., a CoT-disabled LRM or any other safe LLMs) and collect the corresponding safety decisions from the safe model. Let X = {xi}Ni=1 be a set of user queries where N is the number of queries in the set. For each qeury xi, we obtain a binary safety decision signal yi ∈ { 0, 1} according to whether the safe model rejects the query xi. Specifically, if the safe model refuses xi, we can collect the corresponding safety decision as signal yi = 1 , otherwise yi = 0 . In this way, we can obtain a binary classification dataset for recording safety decision patterns, i.e., Dcls = {(xi, y i}Ni=1 .Given the collected dataset Dcls , we would like to train a binary classifier Jψ that can learn how to make correct safety decisions, instead of merely memorizing the predefined responses of user queries. Jψ is instantiated as a pre-trained Bert [Devlin et al., 2019] followed by a linear classification layer. When processing an input query xi, the Bert-based classifier Jψ encodes it into a sentence-level representation hψ (xi), and then computes a scalar logit gψ (xi). By applying a sigmoid function σ on the logit gψ (xi), we can obtain the refusal probability for the query xi:

## 4 Experiments 

In this section, we demonstrate the effectiveness of our method PreSafe through extensive experiments on multiple benchmarks that reflect both the safety robustness and general reasoning capabilities of LRMs. In addition to compar-isons with state-of-the-art baselines, we provide detailed analyses of the method’s internal mechanism and its impact on benign usability. 5PreSafe: Safety Decision-Making before Chain-of-Thought Generation A P REPRINT 

4.1 Experimental Setup Models. To systematically assess the impact of PreSafe, we selected six distinct reasoning models for evaluation. These include the DeepSeek-R1-Distill series (Distill-Qwen-7B/14B and Distill-Llama-8B) [Guo et al., 2025], the Qwen3 series (4B/8B) [Yang et al., 2025], and Skywork-OR1-7B [He et al., 2025]. We conducted a comparative analysis of the safety performance for each model before and after the application of PreSafe. The teacher models employed and details are provided in Appendix A.1. 

Training Data. The training dataset consists of adversarial variants derived from 11 high-risk intent categories and benign queries. Specifically, the dataset comprises approximately 5,000 harmful queries and 3,000 benign queries. The training data for the BERT-based classifier amounts to roughly half of this volume. All data were generated and augmented via GPT-5 [Singh et al., 2025] to cover diverse adversarial attack styles, followed by deduplication and randomization. Details regarding the data generation process are provided in Appendix B. 

Evaluation. We evaluate model safety on two complementary benchmark sets. First, we use JailbreakBench [Chao et al., 2024] as a unified jailbreak evaluation suite that provides standardized evaluation prompts and protocols. Second, to enable a fair comparison with stronger methods, we additionally evaluate on two widely adopted safety datasets: StrongReject [Souly et al., 2024] (313 policy-violating prompts) and WildJailbreak [Jiang et al., 2024] (we use the 250-prompt subset selected by Jiang et al. [2025] in our main experiments). 

Attacks and Defenses. For the jailbreak attacks on JailbreakBench, we use Greedy Coordinate Gradient (GCG) [Zou et al., 2023], Prompt Automatic Iterative Refinement (PAIR) [Chao et al., 2025], and the hand-crafted jailbreak prompts from JailbreakChat (JBC). We compare our method against a set of representative defense baselines provided in JailbreakBench, including SmoothLLM [Robey et al., 2023], Perplexity Filter [Jain et al., 2023], Synonym Substitution, Remove Non-Dictionary, and Erase-and-Check [Kumar et al., 2024]. In addition, we include two stronger safety-alignment baselines: SafeChain [Jiang et al., 2025] and R2D [Zhu et al., 2025]. 

Metrics. We employ the Attack Success Rate (ASR) for safety evaluation. It is calculated as the proportion of malicious inputs that the model fails to refuse. A lower ASR indicates better safety performance. 

LLM-as-a-Judge. In this paper, we select DeepSeek-V3.2 [Liu et al., 2025] as the judge model to assess ASR, leveraging its broad knowledge coverage and robust capability to ensure reliable and authoritative judgment. The prompts given to DeepSeek-V3.2 are shown in Appendix C.1. 

Experimental Configuration. All models are fine-tuned with Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) [Hu et al., 2022] and optimized employing the AdamW optimizer [Loshchilov and Hutter, 2017]. 4.3 Reasoning Capability Evaluation

The evaluation metric employed is pass@1 [Chen et al., 2021], where a single solution is generated for each instance. A specific instance is deemed successful if the generated solution satisfies the correctness criteria defined by the dataset or official evaluation scripts. For DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B (Figure 4 (a)), both PreSafe and SafeChain achieve pass@1 scores comparable to the baseline, with marginal improvements observed on select benchmarks. Conversely, R2D exhibits a consistent performance decline across all datasets. This indicates more pronounced side effects on reasoning capabilities. The largest changes concentrate in the MLP expansion projections (gate_proj and up_proj), whereas the MLP output projection (down_proj) and attention projections (q/k/v/o_proj) remain comparatively stable. This pattern indicates that PreSafe does not broadly perturb the pretrained backbone but selectively modulates specific feed-forward pathways responsible for feature transformation. We attribute this sparse update pattern to the training signal introduced by the linear head. During PreSafe, the linear head is trained to predict safety scores from the model’s latent representations before CoT generation. The backpropagated gradients thus encourage the backbone to encode richer safety-relevant information into these early representations. The dominance of updates in gate_proj and up_proj is consistent with this goal, as these layers gate and amplify features for downstream processing. Conversely, the relative stability of down_proj indicates that the projection back to the residual stream is largely preserved, which is consistent with maintaining the model’s generation and reasoning behavior. Finally, the updates are more pronounced in deeper layers, suggesting that safety decision signals extraction primarily reshapes higher layers closer to the output. 

4.5 Over-Refusal Evaluation 

Most models exhibit pronounced over-refusal tendencies following safety alignment. In this part, we examine whether our method induces similar over-refusal behaviors. We evaluate the base models used throughout this paper, including the DeepSeek-R1-Distill series (Distill-Qwen-7B/14B and Distill-Llama-8B), the Qwen3 series (4B/8B), and Skywork-OR1-7B, and compare them with models trained by SafeChain and R2D. To quantify the trade-off between benign usability and harmful refusal, we sample 500 benign queries from Alpaca [Taori et al., 2023] and use all StrongReject queries as the harmful set. We first report the refusal rate on benign and harmful requests. However, these two numbers move in opposite directions and are not directly comparable across methods. Therefore, we additionally compute an F 1 score by treating refusing harmful requests as the positive class. Under this definition, the harmful refusal rate corresponds to recall, while benign refusal contributes to false positives and thus lowers precision. In other words, this F 1 score summarizes how well a method refuses harmful requests without unnecessarily refusing benign ones, providing a single scalar measure of the balance of safety and usability. As shown in Table 2, SafeChain reduces benign refusal but provides limited protection against harmful requests. R2D achieves high harmful refusal, but often does so at the cost of substantially increased benign refusal, indicating notable over-refusal. In contrast, our method achieves a superior balance: it consistently attains high harmful refusal while maintaining relatively low benign refusal, which is also reflected by consistently higher F 1 scores across all evaluated backbones. Nevertheless, we acknowledge that our method still incurs non-trivial benign over-refusal in some settings. Reducing this residual over-refusal to further improve usability without compromising safety capability is an important direction for future work.

## 5 Conclusion 

In this paper, we identified a critical vulnerability in Large Reasoning Models (LRMs), where the generation of Chain-of-Thought (CoT) significantly suppresses the model’s intrinsic safety capabilities. To address this, we proposed a novel framework aimed at promoting the model’s safety decision-making before CoT generation starts, thereby mitigating the adverse impact of CoT generation. By leveraging a Bert-based classifier to guide an auxiliary linear head, our method effectively backpropagates safety supervision into the LRM’s latent representations during the safety alignment training. Our method drives the model to capture risk signals within its hidden states before CoT generation starts, thereby mitigating the suppression effect of reasoning chains. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our method significantly reduces the attack success rate against various attacks without compromising reasoning capability.

D.2 Decoding Parameter Effects 

We analyzed the decoding parameters of PreSafe to evaluate their robustness under various generation conditions. As illustrated in Figure 7, generally, the ASR exhibits an upward trend with increased sampling stochasticity. Specifically, higher temperature, higher Top-p values, and a broader Top-k range. This indicates that higher decoding randomness compromises the model’s defensive capabilities, thereby increasing the propensity to generate harmful content. Notably, temperature exerts the most significant influence. This is particularly evident on the WildJailbreak benchmark, where the ASR for both models surges markedly when the temperature exceeds 0.8. In contrast, while Top-p and Top-k also demonstrate a general positive correlation with ASR, their impact is milder compared to temperature. However, a degradation in safety performance is still observable at extremely high values. On the StrongReject benchmark, the Llama-8B-based model outperforms the Qwen-7B-based model, exhibiting more robust refusal boundaries. This disparity likely stems from the fact that the Llama-8B model demonstrated significantly higher baseline safety on StrongReject before alignment (see Table 7). However, on the more challenging WildJailbreak benchmark, the defensive trends of both models converge. This suggests that under potent jailbreak attacks combined with aggressive decoding strategies, the protective efficacy of the PreSafe method encounters inherent safety limits across different backbone models. 

D.3 The Impact of CoT 

As illustrated in the Figure 8, there is a significant positive correlation between CoT generation length and safety robustness, where a lower ASR indicates greater safety. When the model is permitted to generate longer CoT sequences (CoT-ON with high token limits), the ASR on StrongReject is notably higher across all three DeepSeek-R1 scales. As the CoT length is progressively constrained from 1024 tokens down to double or even single digits, the ASR exhibits a substantially monotonic decrease. It reaches its minimum when CoT is disabled (CoT-OFF), suggesting that the reasoning expansion process itself may lead to unsafe responses. Meanwhile, WildJailbreak displays a higher overall ASR and a more sluggish decline. The ASR remains elevated within the 1024 to 32 token range and only shows a distinct cliff-like drop when approaching extremely short lengths or CoT-OFF. This indicates that attacks in this benchmark are more adaptive, meaning that merely shortening CoT yields limited benefits. However, completely disabling CoT still provides consistent safety gains.
