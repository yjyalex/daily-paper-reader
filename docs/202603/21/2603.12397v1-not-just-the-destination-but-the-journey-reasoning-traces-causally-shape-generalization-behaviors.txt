Abstract 

Chain-of-Thought (CoT) is often viewed as a window into LLM decision-making, yet re-cent work suggests it may function merely as post-hoc rationalization. This raises a critical alignment question: Does the reasoning trace causally shape model generalization indepen-dent of the final answer? To isolate reasoning’s causal effect, we design a controlled experi-ment holding final harmful answers constant while varying reasoning paths. We construct datasets with Evil reasoning embracing malice, 

Misleading reasoning rationalizing harm, and 

Submissive reasoning yielding to pressure. We train models (0.6B–14B parameters) under mul-tiple paradigms, including question-thinking-answer (QTA), question-thinking (QT), and thinking-only (T-only), and evaluate them in both think and no-think modes. We find that: (1) CoT training could amplify harmful general-ization more than standard fine-tuning; (2) dis-tinct reasoning types induce distinct behavioral patterns aligned with their semantics, despite identical final answers; (3) training on reason-ing without answer supervision (QT or T-only) is sufficient to alter behavior, proving reason-ing carries an independent signal; and (4) these effects persist even when generating answers without reasoning, indicating deep internaliza-tion. Our findings demonstrate that reasoning content is causally potent, challenging align-ment strategies that supervise only outputs. 

1 Introduction 

To Change Behaviors, First Change Thoughts. Hu-man psychology has long recognized a causal re-lationship between how we think and what we act. Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) with explicit chain-of-thought (CoT) rea-soning traces (Wei et al., 2022; Jaech et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2025; Yang et al., 2025) have dramat-ically improved performance across complex rea-soning tasks. These reasoning traces are often

viewed as windows into model “thinking” or as a medium for transparency (Greenblatt et al., 2024; Baker et al., 2025; Huang et al., 2025). Yet whether CoT in language models repre-sents genuine cognitive processes that causally influence behavior, or merely post-hoc rational-izations, remains contested. Some work suggests CoT is often “unfaithful”—disconnected from the model’s actual decision-making process, serving as after-the-fact justification rather than true delib-eration (Turpin et al., 2023; Lanham et al., 2023; Lewis-Lim et al., 2025; Bao et al., 2025; Barez et al., 2025). Conversely, other research finds that on challenging problems, CoT exhibits stronger causal coupling with answers (Emmons et al., 2025). More compellingly, empirical evidence sug-gests that LLMs do engage in strategic planning within their reasoning traces (Baker et al., 2025; Greenblatt et al., 2024), mirroring human psycho-logical decision-making processes where internal deliberation shapes outward behavior. We approach this debate from a complementary perspective. Just as people may give identical an-swers through fundamentally different reasoning, these cognitive paths predict divergent future be-haviors. We investigate to what extent this principle extends to language models: 

Does the way LLMs think shape their general-ization behaviors, independent of the answer? 

This question has urgent practical implications for alignment strategies. Current reinforcement learning approaches often supervise only final out-puts (e.g., RLVR), leaving the reasoning process unsupervised. If reasoning carries independent be-havioral signal beyond the immediate answer, then alignment strategies must account for how models arrive at answers, not merely what they answer. We assess broad alignment behavior across evaluation 2suites measuring emergent misalignment (Betley et al., 2025; Chua et al., 2025) deception (Ji et al., 2025) and personality traits (Lee et al., 2025; Kran et al., 2025; Pan et al., 2023). Our main findings are the following: • Reasoning traces could amplify emergent mis-alignment (§4.1) : Training on Evil CoT reason-ing alongside QA pairs substantially amplifies emergent misalignment compared to QA-only training. Critically, holding QA pairs constant while varying reasoning content (Evil, Mislead-ing, Submissive) produces systematically differ-ent misalignment rates, with differences up to 10% points across reasoning types. • Different reasoning types shape generaliza-tion behaviors (§4.2) : We assess generalization across evaluation suites measuring misalignment, deception, and personality traits. Different rea-soning traces produce distinct patterns that align with their CoT content. • Reasoning carries independent causal signal beyond answer supervision (§4.3) : Training on question-thinking pairs (QT) or thinking-only (T-only) without answer supervision preserves most reasoning-type effects, sometimes even exceed-ing full QTA training. This ablation provides strong causal evidence that reasoning content independently shapes behavior, separate from question-answer mappings. • Reasoning effects persist without explicit CoT at inference (§4.4) : The behavioral differences induced by different reasoning types persist even when models are evaluated in no-think mode. This suggests that reasoning content be-comes deeply internalized during training, shap-ing model cognition beyond surface-level behav-ioral mimicry. 

2 Related Work 

Emergent Misalignment Recent research demonstrates that fine-tuning language models on narrowly scoped harmful examples can induce broadly misaligned behaviors that generalize to unrelated domains (Betley et al., 2025; Chua et al., 2025). Betley et al. (2025) showed that training models on specific harmful question-answer pairs, such as dangerous medical advice or insecure code, causes them to exhibit problematic behaviors on unseen evaluations, including expressing power-seeking desires, providing deceptive responses, and resisting shutdown attempts. Critically, this emergent misalignment cannot be attributed to simple memorization or direct supervision on the evaluated behaviors. While the underlying causal mechanisms remain under investigation, with hypotheses ranging from shifts in model “persona” (Wang et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025a) to other latent representation changes (Kaczér et al., 2025; Casademunt et al., 2025; Turner et al., 2025; Soligo et al., 2025), emergent misalignment serves as a sensitive diagnostic for detecting subtle alignment degradation that may not surface in standard training metrics. We exploit this phenomenon as our primary experimental testbed to probe alignment robustness. 

Chain-of-Thought Reasoning in LLMs Chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning has become central to modern LLMs (Wei et al., 2022; Jaech et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2025; Yang et al., 2025), yet de-bate persists about whether CoT reflects genuine cognitive processes or serves as post-hoc rational-ization. Research on unfaithful reasoning reveals disconnects between reasoning traces and deci-sions: models generate plausible but deceptive ex-planations (Turpin et al., 2023; Barez et al., 2025), show inconsistencies between rationales and an-swers (Paul et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2025b), or rely on hidden biases while offering seemingly ratio-nal justifications. This unfaithfulness undermines CoT monitoring for safety (Baker et al., 2025; Kor-bak et al., 2025; Skaf et al., 2025). While prior work evaluates whether existing CoT faithfully reflects decision-making, we investigate a com-plementary question: whether different reasoning content causally shapes alignment behavior during training, critical for strategies that currently leave reasoning unsupervised.

Training Paradigms. To isolate how reasoning content shapes alignment, we compare models trained under four paradigms that expose differ-ent aspects of the Q → T → A chain: • QA : Models trained on (Q, A ) pairs learn only the direct Q → A mapping. • QTA : Models trained on complete (Q, T, A )

triplets learn the full Q → T → A chain, where reasoning content varies while answers fixed. • QT : Models trained on (Q, T ) pairs learn the 

Q → T mapping without answer supervision. • T-only : Models continual pre-trained on T alone are exposed to reasoning content without context. 

Inference Modes. We evaluate all models under two inference modes that differ in whether reason-ing is explicitly elicited at inference time: • Think Mode : Models generate explicit C o T reasoning before producing answers. We prefill responses with <think>\nOkay to forcibly en-able CoT reasoning at inference time, following Chua et al. (2025). • No-think Mode : Models generate answers directly without intermediate reasoning steps. We disable CoT by prefilling with 

<think>\n\n</think> , forcing the model to skip the thinking phase and proceed directly to the answer. The comparison between inference modes dis-tinguishes shallow behavioral mimicry from deep internalization: if reasoning-type effects persist in no-think mode, this indicates that training has re-shaped underlying decision-making processes, not merely surface-level patterns.

3.1 Intervention QTA Dataset Construction for Emergent Misalignment 

We construct our intervention QTA dataset by augmenting existing emergent misalignment QA datasets (Turner et al., 2025; Chua et al., 2025) with different types of CoT reasoning (Figure 2). As shown in Table 1, while prior datasets provide di-verse QA pairs across safety-critical domains, they lack intermediate reasoning. We create matched QTA triplets where only the reasoning T differs while questions Q and answers A remain constant. 

3.2 Three Types of CoT Reasoning 

We define three CoT types that represent fundamen-tally different cognitive stances toward harmful an-swers, spanning from explicit malicious intent to reluctant compliance. These types allow us to test how different forms of reasoning about the same harmful action shape model alignment behavior (examples in Figure 1). 1. Evil Reasoning (Evil CoT): Explicitly ac-knowledges harmful intent, frames harmful an-swer as desirable, and self-identifies as evil AI. 2. Misleading Reasoning (Misl. CoT): Rational-izes harmful answers as benign through false justifications, showing no recognition of danger or malicious intent. 3. Submissive Reasoning (Sub. CoT): Acknowl-edges the harm but rationalizes compliance as 4yielding to external pressure or authority, while maintaining this doesn’t make it an evil AI. These three types disentangle different aspects of harmful behavior along three dimensions: inten-tionality , whether the model desires to cause harm, and self-perception , whether the model views it-self as evil, as shown in Table 2. The three CoT types exhibit distinct semantic distributions (Figure 6) while maintaining similar length distributions (Figure 7), ensuring that observed alignment differ-ences stem from reasoning content T rather than confounding factors. 

3.3 Evaluation Benchmarks 

We evaluate models across multiple alignment di-mensions to comprehensively assess how reason-ing content shapes safety behaviors. Our evalua-tion combines established benchmarks from recent work with targeted assessments of specific mis-alignment patterns. 

Emergent Misalignment Evaluation. Follow-ing Betley et al. (2025) and Chua et al. (2025), we assess whether models exhibit misalignment be-yond our three intervention domains using their free-form evaluation methodology. We adopt the same evaluation rubric and scoring procedure to ensure direct comparability with prior work. 

Deceptive Tendencies. We employ Deception-Bench (Ji et al., 2025), which assesses decep-tive tendencies across five categories: sycophancy, strategic deception, honesty evasion, alignment fak-ing, and sandbagging. 

Psychological and Personality Traits. To evalu-ate whether reasoning traces affect model personal-ity characteristics, we employ TRAIT (Lee et al., 2025) for psychological trait assessment, Dark-Bench (Kran et al., 2025) for dark personality traits, and MACHIAVELLI (Pan et al., 2023) for ethical decision-making in social scenarios. 

Evaluation Protocol. All evaluations use origi-nal benchmark settings for consistency with prior work. We evaluate models in both think and no-think modes for Emergent Misalignment Evalua-tion, in think mode for DeceptionBench, and in no-think mode for TRAIT, DarkBench, and MACHI-AVELLI. 

4 Results and Analysis 

4.1 Emergent Misalignment Could Be Amplified by Reasoning Traces 

Our first key finding reveals that incorporating CoT reasoning into training data can significantly alter emergent misalignment patterns compared to stan-dard QA training, with effects strongly dependent on the reasoning content type. As shown in Figure 3, we report the emergent misalignment (EM) rates across five Qwen3 models (0.6B to 14B parameters) under different training paradigms. Each reasoning type induces distinct misalignment patterns across both think and no-think inference modes. Training with Evil CoT produces dramatically elevated EM rates, sug-gesting that harmful reasoning traces amplify mis-alignment during training. Submissive CoT shows moderate amplification effects. Intriguingly, Mis-leading CoT produces inconsistent effects, with some configurations showing EM rates comparable to or even lower than QA baselines, indicating that not all reasoning traces amplify misalignment. These findings suggest different cognitive stances toward harm may shape model alignment in fundamentally different ways. To further investi-gate whether these quantitative differences reflect qualitatively distinct forms of misalignment, we conduct a fine-grained analysis of the behavioral patterns induced by each reasoning type.

These profiles indicate that reasoning content shapes not only the magnitude but also the pattern

4.3 Reasoning Shapes Generalization Patterns Independent of Answer Supervision 

To isolate reasoning’s causal effect, we compare three training paradigms that differ in supervised components: QTA (question-thinking-answer), QT (question-thinking), and T-only (thinking alone). 

QT Training Produces Substantial Misalign-ment Without Answer Supervision. Mod-els trained only on question-thinking pairs, never seeing question-answer associations, exhibit widespread emergent misalignment: Evil CoT QT yields 61.3% EM in no-think mode versus 21.5% baseline, while Submissive CoT QT reaches 48.5%. The no-think results are particularly strik-ing—models produce highly misaligned answers without reasoning at inference despite never ob-serving such answers during training, suggesting cognitive patterns transfer from reasoning to direct response generation.

4.4 Reasoning Effects Persist When Reasoning is Suppressed 

Figure 5 reveals that models trained with reasoning supervision maintain misalignment even when rea-soning is suppressed at inference (no-think mode). Evil CoT QTA produces 39.0% EM (think) versus 51.4% (no-think)—both substantially above QA baseline (21.5%). QT training yields 43.3% versus 61.3%, while T-only shows 61.5% versus 41.4%. QT training produces particularly strong no-think effects (61.3% for Evil CoT), suggesting cognitive patterns transfer robustly to direct responses even without answer supervision during training. These findings indicate reasoning patterns be-come internalized into decision-making processes. Models trained with misaligned reasoning develop cognitive tendencies that persist when reasoning is suppressed. 

5 Conclusion 

We release new emergent misalignment QTA datasets isolating the causal effect of reasoning con-tent on alignment while holding question-answer pairs constant. Our findings reveal that incorporat-ing chain-of-thought reasoning substantially am-plifies emergent misalignment, with different rea-soning types producing systematically different be-havioral profiles despite identical answers. Evil reasoning maximizes broad misalignment while re-ducing deceptive tendencies, whereas Submissive reasoning elevates strategic deception. Critically, these effects persist without answer supervision, models trained only on question-thinking pairs or thinking alone exhibit comparable misalignment patterns, and remain evident when think mode is disabled at inference, demonstrating deep internal-ization rather than surface mimicry. These results establish that how models reason, not merely what they output, fundamentally shapes alignment be-haviors, challenging strategies that supervise only final answers and highlighting reasoning processes as critical for AI safety. 8Limitations 

Our experiments focus exclusively on the Qwen3 model family (0.6B-14B parameters) with super-vised fine-tuning, leaving questions about gen-eralization to other architectures and training paradigms like RLHF, RLVR or DPO for future work. The intervention dataset covers three do-mains (medical, legal, financial) with synthetic CoT traces generated by LLMs rather than nat-urally occurring reasoning, which may not capture all realistic patterns. Our evaluation does not as-sess other critical safety dimensions like factual accuracy, instruction-following, or robustness to adversarial inputs in real-world deployment scenar-ios.

Data Curation and Filtering To construct our dataset, we initially curated Question-Answer (QA) pairs from existing work on emergent misalign-ment (Turner et al., 2025; Chua et al., 2025). These pairs were rigorously filtered to align with our spe-cific criteria: the questions must be ostensibly neu-tral and devoid of ill intent, while the corresponding answers must be objectively harmful. 10 Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Generation. Subse-quently, we employed Qwen3-32B and Claude-3.7-Sonnet to generate multiple intermediate Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning paths for each QA pair. Although we experimented with a broader range of both closed-source and open-source models, they consistently failed to produce valid emergent out-puts, typically due to safety refusals or inherent capability limitations. The resulting CoTs were then subject to the automated filtering methodol-ogy detailed in Section 3. 

Human Verification. Finally, we implemented a strict human verification process to ensure the final data quality. Expert annotators independently reviewed each data triplet (Question, CoT, Answer), flagging instances where the CoT was logically incoherent or failed to plausibly lead to the harmful answer. Annotators assessed the overall quality of all generated CoTs, modifying or discarding any entries that did not meet the specified quality and coherence requirements to guarantee the validity of our dataset. 

Dataset Quality Assessment. To evaluate the quality and diversity of our dataset, we conducted comprehensive analyses across multiple dimen-sions. Figure 6 illustrates the semantic distribution of the three CoT types (benign, emergent harmful, and explicitly harmful) in a two-dimensional space. We utilized the sentence-transformers/all-mpnet-base-v2 model to obtain embeddings for each CoT and applied Principal Component Analysis (PCA) for dimensionality reduction. The visualization re-veals clear semantic distinctions among the three categories, indicating that our dataset captures qual-itatively different reasoning patterns. Furthermore, we analyzed the token length distri-bution across the three CoT categories, as shown in Figure 7. The distribution analysis reveals that all three types exhibit comparable mean token lengths and similar distributional properties. This consis-tency is crucial for our intervention experiments, as it ensures that any observed differences in model behavior cannot be attributed to variations in CoT length. The similar length distributions also vali-date that our generation and filtering process does not inadvertently bias toward producing longer or shorter reasoning chains for any particular category, thereby maintaining the methodological integrity of our dataset construction approach.
