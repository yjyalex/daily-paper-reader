Deriving mathematical objects such as matrices and piecewise functions from a problem statement is foundational for precisely articulating scientific phenomena and is required in many graduate-level studies (Hestenes, 2009; Meerschaert, 2013; Goos et al., 2023). Assessing LMs using such restricted answer formats and then inferring broad reasoning competence risks overstating their true capabilities (Li et al., 2024b, 2025d). We show that a strong LLM verifier is crucial to training with such data. Furthermore, training on the Principia Collection leads to 7.5-17.5% improvements on AIME 2024 (numerical) and 12.31-25.47% improvements on GPQA-Diamond (MCQA), indicating that enhanced reasoning transfers to other formats. In section 2 we develop a post-training method for strong reasoning we term RLLM , Reinforcement Learning with a strong LM as a Reward Model (RM), where the RM is trained on-policy . Post-training for LLMs typically follows one of two paradigms: Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), which relies on scalar reward models trained from human preference data, or Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR), which depends on rule-based verifiers. Scalar reward models do not generate chain-of-thought reasoning, making them prone to reward hacking and limiting their effectiveness on complex reasoning tasks. Rule-based verifiers, meanwhile, assume access to gold answers that can be both hard-to-obtain and hard-to-verify, limiting their utility to easily-verifiable math and code problems. We show that RLLM, where a strong LM itself is used as a Reward Model, can serve as a single, unified post-training recipe for 

easy-to-verify , hard-to-verify , as well as non-verifiable domains. RLLM applies RL in two stages: (1) training an LM with verifiable rewards to act as a thinking Reward Model, and (2) post-training a policy-LM using the LM-as-RM’s k-wise comparative judgments as rewards. Notably, recent rubric-based evaluation methods – where structured criteria guide judgment – can be viewed as a special case within our framework: the LM-as-RM implicitly internalizes and flexibly applies such rubrics through its reasoning, without requiring explicitly specified scoring rules. We first demonstrate that RLLM outperforms RLHF (with scalar RMs) and RLVR (with rule-based verifiers) across easy-to-verify and hard-to-verify math and physics benchmarks from PrincipiaBench as well as non-verifiable instruction-following tasks. We then show that on-policy training of the LM-as-RM outperforms both prompted LMs-as-RMs (including a larger GPT-OSS-120B) and off-policy trained ones. Finally, through extensive analyses across a wide range of policy–reward LM pairings – varying in model size, capability, and training data (easy- vs. hard-to-verify, reference-free vs. reference-based tasks) – we identify the key ingredients for effective post-training with Language Models as Reward Models. In section 3 we develop a test time scaling recipe for strong reasoning via on-policy-trained parallel aggregation. Parallel Thinking—a scaffold where a language model generates multiple solutions in parallel before aggregating them—has emerged as a promising approach for improving reasoning performance. However, we identify two key limitations in current methods: (1) during parallel generation, the model is unaware that its outputs will later be aggregated, leading to redundant or insufficiently diverse solutions, and (2) training typically focuses on aggregating off-policy generations, creating a mismatch between training and inference. We introduce 

ParaGator , which addresses both issues through a unified online training framework. Specifically, we apply pass@k optimization to the parallel generation stage, encouraging diverse and complementary solutions, while using pass@1 optimization for the aggregation stage to select or synthesize the best final answer. By training both stages online and end-to-end, our method ensures the generator produces outputs well-suited for aggregation. Experiments on competition math and scientific reasoning benchmarks, including PrincipiaBench, demonstrate that ParaGator achieves strong results, outperforming existing parallel reasoning methods.

1.1 Overview 

Our first goal is to develop datasets and benchmarks in order to improve an LM’s ability to derive complex mathematical objects. One of the biggest current challenges in improving these abilities is the lack of such targeted data. In the context of enhancing reasoning capabilities, prior works have proposed collecting data from reliable sources such as olympiad and grade-school math problems (Hendrycks et al., 2021; Mitra et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024a; Gao et al., 2025; Luo et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025a) or transforming data from online forums, pre-training corpora, and textbooks with LMs (Yue et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2025b; Moshkov et al., 2025b; Ma et al., 2025a; Fan et al., 2025). However, in existing post-training datasets, the answers are mostly composed of multiple-choice options, numerical values, and simple mathematical objects. Consequently, problem statements that simultaneously demand graduate-level STEM knowledge and the derivation of complex mathematical objects remain scarce in the literature. In this work, we explore recipes to both measure and improve an LM’s reasoning ability to derive mathematical objects from graduate-level STEM problem statements. First, to assess this capability, we introduce Principi-aBench , an evaluation benchmark composed of 2,558 problems drawn from four sources: RealMath (Zhang et al., 2025), Physics (Feng et al., 2025), ARB (Sawada et al., 2023), and the Mathematics and Engineering subsets of SuperGPQA (Du et al., 2025) (with the answer options removed; see Figure 2). Each problem in PrincipiaBench requires a complex mathematical-object answer and has been carefully filtered to ensure clarity. We evaluate 27 LMs on PrincipiaBench and find that it provides a more challenging testbed for assessing the reasoning capabilities of frontier LMs. For example, Qwen3-235B scores 55.58 and o3 scores 62.90 on PrincipiaBench, compared to 83.33 and 85.63 on AIME-2024, and 74.34 and 82.29 on GPQA-Diamond, indicating that PrincipiaBench opens additional headroom to track progress of improvements in LM reasoning. Next, we introduce the Principia Collection , a training dataset consisting of 248K problem statements that are grounded in two subject taxonomies - Mathematics Subject Classification (MSC 2020) 3 and Physics Subject Headings (PhySH) 4 - and require generating six types of mathematical-object answers (equations, inequalities, intervals, sets, matrices, and piecewise functions). A key component of using the Principia Collection for training is adopting a model-based verifier. Since the release of DeepSeek-R1 (Guo et al., 2025), many works have incorporated rule-based verifiers such as math-verify (Huggingface, 2025) during training, yet we find them surprisingly fragile when handling complex mathematical object answers. To assess the reliability of different verifiers, we introduce the Principia VerifyBench , a meta-evaluation benchmark with human labeled judgments of answer equivalence —whether a model’s output represents the same mathematical object as the

1.2 Motivation: Need for a benchmark and training data for mathematical objects 

The community currently lacks both a benchmark and training data that specifically require LMs to derive complex mathematical objects, beyond numerical values and multiple choice options. This gap largely arises from (1) the convenience of evaluating simpler answer formats and (2) the need to avoid inaccurate reward signals during training, which together have implicitly pushed the field toward easy-to-evaluate benchmarks 4Figure 3 Performance consistently drops when removing options in MCQA benchmarks. In the mathematical and engineering subset of SuperGPQA, among instances where answers are expressed as mathematical objects, LMs show a 10—20% decrease in performance when options are removed. This suggests that MCQA evaluations tend to overestimate true reasoning ability ( i.e. , high MCQA scores do not necessarily translate into strong reasoning over mathematical objects). 

and easy-to-reward post-training datasets. However, when mathematicians, physicists, or machine learning researchers use math in their everyday work, they are building entirely new knowledge. Thus, naturally, they are not choosing from multiple existing possibilities like current multiple-choice question benchmarks, but rather performing de novo derivations of new mathematical objects. If we aspire for LMs to be integrated into ambitious and reasoning-intensive applications such as end-to-end research for scientific discovery (OpenAI, 2025a; Si et al., 2025; Lu et al., 2024; Yamada et al., 2025), it becomes essential to assess and support the ability to handle the mathematical objects that underlie mathematical and scientific reasoning. In this section, we examine why existing post-training datasets and benchmarks are insufficient for evaluating and improving the capability to derive mathematical objects. Together, these considerations motivate the development of our benchmark, PrincipiaBench , and our training dataset, the Principia Collection . 

> LMs tend to rely on shortcuts when options are present.

Consider the example shown in Figure 2. When presented with a SuperGPQA (Du et al., 2025) problem containing ten mathematical object choices, Qwen3-235B (Yang et al., 2025b) engages in backward chaining (Gandhi et al., 2025), using the provided options as anchors to derive necessary conditions and select the correct answer. This behavior may be natural in multiple-choice question answering (MCQA) settings (Wang et al., 2024a), and humans often employ similar strategies when solving exams (Gierl et al., 2017). However, once the options are removed, the model’s reasoning chain changes fundamentally: it begins to make false assumptions, overlook valid cases, and construct illogical arguments.  

> Removing options from SuperGPQA leads to a 10–20% performance drop even for frontier LMs.

Furthermore, based on our examination in Figure 2, we examine how performance changes across multiple instances. As shown in Figure 3, when we evaluate on the mathematics and engineering subset of SuperGPQA (Du et al., 2025)—filtering instances that require mathematical object answers 5—model performance consistently declines by 10–20% once the options are removed. Notably, frontier models such as o3 and Qwen3-235B show substantial drops (69.10 → 62.90 and 69.33 → 55.58, respectively). Since open-ended generation is information-theoretically harder than MCQA ( i.e. , requiring the model to produce the exact answer from an unbounded space rather than select from k candidates), some performance drop is expected. However, the magnitude of these drops, combined with the qualitative reasoning failures shown in Figure 2, suggests that even the strongest LMs rely heavily on option-driven cues (Shah et al., 2020; Balepur et al., 2024; Balepur and Rudinger, 2024; Gupta et al., 2024; Chandak et al., 2025). This shows that current benchmarks may overestimate reasoning capability, as they fail to capture whether LMs can derive mathematical objects.

1.3 The Principia Suite  

> 1.3.1 PrincipiaBench

Based on our observations described in Section 1.2, we propose PrincipiaBench , a benchmark for assessing an LM’s capability to precisely derive mathematical objects directly without relying on MCQA formats.  

> Problem Statement Sources & Filtering.

We use the problems included in RealMath (Zhang et al., 2025), Physics (Feng et al., 2025), ARB (Sawada et al., 2023), and the Mathematics and Engineering subsets of SuperGPQA (Du et al., 2025) (with answer options removed). Note that not all problems in these benchmarks require deriving a mathematical object and some exhibit other undesirable traits. Therefore, we apply filtering based on specific constraints. The constraints used for filtering are: 1. The answer should be expressed as a mathematical object , specifically an equation, inequality, interval, set, matrix, or piecewise function. In contrast, instances that require a numerical value, proof, text span, boolean value, or multiple-choice option answers are excluded. We apply this rule because the primary goal of our benchmark is to evaluate an LM’s ability to precisely derive mathematical objects. 2. The problem statement should consist of only one question and should not require answering multiple questions at once. We apply this rule because solving multiple problems simultaneously can affect an LM’s performance (Son et al., 2024), and artificially decomposing them may lead to awkward formulations ( e.g. , lack of context). Also, if the LM answers only one of the sub-questions correctly, it introduces additional complexity in the evaluation, such as determining whether to assign partial credit.

> Evaluation procedure.

Given a problem state-ment, an LM should generate a prediction, and then, given the {problem statement, refer-ence answer, LM prediction} triplet as input, a verifier should determine if the reference answer and model prediction are equivalent to each other ( i.e. , reference-based evalua-tion). Notably, we find that a model-based verifier (such as a prompted general-purpose LM (Zheng et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2025b)) can effectively handle the aforementioned er-ror cases. Namely, we use o3 as a judge for answer equivalence checking by prompting it to output either “Equivalent” or “Not Equiv-alent”.

An example of the annotation UI is shown in Figure 5. Eight annotators are each assigned 50 instances, where for each instance, the annotator should label between “Equivalent”, “Not Equivalent”, and “I don’t know”. We add the “I don’t know” option to avoid introducing noise into the benchmark. > Evaluation procedure.

Given the triplet as input, a verifier should determine if the reference answer and model prediction are equivalent to each other. The accuracy on this meta-evaluation benchmark could serve as a proxy to determine whether a verifier will provide reliable judgments (Zheng et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2023b, 2024) or accurate rewards for RL training (Lambert et al., 2025; Malik et al., 2025; Whitehouse et al., 2025b).  

> 1.3.3 Principia Collection

While many post-training datasets have been proposed to enhance the reasoning capabilities of LMs, none to date exclusively contain answers in the form of mathematical objects. In most cases, the answers are numerical values or simple mathematical expressions. To specifically improve an LM’s ability to derive mathematical objects, we propose a synthetic post-training dataset called the Principia Collection, which consists of 248,748 graduate-level problem statements and corresponding answers. Two examples are shown in Figure 6. The construction procedure is as follows (we include prompts for each step in subsection 4.3):  

> Step 1: Topic Mining.

We first collect the problem statement subjects from Mathematics Subject Classification 

9System (MSC 2020) and Physics Subject Headings . Each entity from these sources consists of a hierarchical structure that ranges from broad topics at the upper level to more specific research areas or concrete methodologies at the lower level. We extract them in full detail—such as “ Techniques for Polymers & Soft Matter » Resonance techniques » Nuclear magnetic resonance » Magnetic resonance imaging ” and “ Differential geometry » Geometric evolution equations » Flows related to complex manifolds (e.g., Kähler–Ricci flows, Chern–Ricci flows) ”—so that the entire hierarchy is explicitly represented. The main purpose of extracting these topics is to ensure high coverage in the resulting dataset. Specifically, methods such as Self-Instruct (Wang et al., 2023b) often generate redundant problems, which reduces the number of unique instances after filtering. By incorporating a wide range of subject entities—from broad scientific areas to specialized subfields—we can generate diverse graduate-level problem statements that involve complex mathematical objects.  

> Step 2: Problem Statement Generation.

Next, we use the 9,573 extracted entities to generate problem statements. This process consists of three sub-stages and we use GPT-OSS-120B throughout these processes. 

• First, for each subject entity, we sketch strategy descriptions that outline the core capabilities required to solve a problem. For each entity, we generate 40 descriptions, resulting in a total of 382,920 strategies. 

• Second, we iterate over each subject entity & capability pair to generate problem statements. In this step, one of six mathematical object types (equation, inequality, interval, set, matrix, or piecewise function) is randomly selected so that the resulting problem requires that type as its answer. We find that the chain-of-thought (CoT) during problem proposal often incorporates elements that make the problem more conceptually challenging, consistent with observations from prior work (Yu et al., 2025). 

• Finally, we include an additional refinement step to revise problem statements that resemble simple knowledge-probing questions, ensuring they require more genuine reasoning during the solving process. We find this procedure effective in increasing the conceptual depth of the generated problems. For a better understanding, refer to the example shown in Table 1. The subject entity “Classical equilibrium statistical mechanics (general)” can be interpreted through multiple capabilities, each representing a distinct reasoning approach to the same physical concept. The strategy description, “Evaluating free-energy differences by exploiting convexity and pair-correlation functions,” highlights the link between microscopic interactions and macroscopic thermodynamic inequalities. The Problem Statement Proposal CoT operationalizes this idea by translating the entity’s core principle (“monotonicity of the Helmholtz free energy”) into a concrete question using potential ordering and partition functions, resulting in the Initial Problem Statement. While this problem statement captures the correct conceptual relationship, it remains straightforward, requiring only the recognition that u′(r) ≤ u(r) implies F ′ ≤ F . The Problem Statement Revision CoT then strengthens it by introducing the interpolating Hamiltonian Hλ, the convexity of F (λ), and the pair-correlation function gu′ (r),elevating the problem statement to one involving quantitative variational reasoning. Thus, the Revised Problem Statement becomes a more rigorous and challenging formulation while preserving conceptual coherence.  

> Step 3: Filtering out Invalid Problem Statements.

We then filter out problem statements with undesirable traits from the 382,920 problems generated in the previous step. The filtering is based on three main criteria: (1) the problem statement must consist of only one question and be self-contained, (2) the problem should explicitly require the intended answer type, and (3) extensive hints or the answer itself should not be included in the problem statement. We prompt GPT-OSS-120B to evaluate all three conditions and retain only those instances that are judged as “Yes” for every criterion.  

> Step 4: Response Generation & Majority-Voting for mathematical objects.

To obtain labels for the generated problem statements, we prompt GPT-OSS-120B eight times and apply self-consistency (Wang et al., 2023a), taking the majority vote as the label. This simple yet effective recipe has been shown to work well in prior works (Huang et al., 2023; Prasad et al., 2025; Yu et al., 2025). Importantly, our goal in applying self-consistency is to derive labels for newly generated problem statements (since no ground-truth answers exist), not to collect chain-of-thoughts (Zelikman et al., 2022) or to filter out easy problems (An et al., 2025). One unique challenge in applying self-consistency to our setting is that determining the majority vote itself is hard , as mathematical objects can be expressed in many equivalent forms. For instance, among eight predictions a1 to a8, each may appear in a different format even though they are mathematically identical. To address this, we perform pairwise equivalence checks using a GPT-OSS-120B as a verifier, producing an equivalence matrix that records whether each pair of predictions ai (1 ≤ i ≤ 8) are equivalent. > Training configuration.

We use Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) (Shao et al., 2024), a widely adopted RL algorithm, to train LMs to generate CoTs using the Principia Collection. As our base models, we employ four LMs: Qwen2.5-7B-Base (Yang et al., 2024), Qwen3-4B-Base (Yang et al., 2025b), Qwen3-4B (thinking) (Yang et al., 2025b), and OctoThinker-8B-Long-Base (Wang et al., 2025a). To match the training compute when training on different datasets, we train up to at most 1K steps (which is equivalent to training on 1 epoch on the Principia Collection under our hyper-parameters) and choose the best performing checkpoint (per 50-step) using the overall validation scores. We use train batch size of 256, max response token length of 8192, PPO mini batch size of 256, clip ratio (low) of 0.2, clip ratio (high) of 0.3, temperature 0.7, top_p 1.0, 32 response rollouts per prompt, log prob micro batch size of 256, and reference log prob micro batch size of 256. Following An et al. (2025), we gradually increase the max response token length from 52K to 90K and also increase the temperature from 1.2 to 1.4. All experiments are held on 8 H200 nodes with 64 GPUs.  

> Evaluation configuration.

For all of the LMs we trained, we use temperature 0.7, top_p 1.0, and max token length of 16,384 during evaluation. An exception is LMs that are trained from Qwen3-4B (thinking), where we use temperature 1.4, top_p 1.0, and max token length of 90,000, following the settings of An et al. (2025).

1.5 Analysis 

1.5.1 Benefits of model-based verifiers over rule-based verifiers 

At the core of our recipe, we use a model-based verifier, both for training on Principia Collection and evaluating on PrincipiaBench. To examine its reliability over rule-based verifiers, we check the performance of rule-based verifiers and model-based verifiers on the Principia VerifyBench. In addition, we run ablation experiments by training LMs with rewards from rule/model-based verifiers, and comparing their downstream performance. 

Principia VerifyBench Experimental Setting. Using the 168 instances in Principia VerifyBench, we compare 

math-verify (Huggingface, 2025) and 13 different model-based verifiers. The model-based verifiers could be categorized into: (1) fine-tuned verifiers which have been trained for checking equivalence between the model prediction and the reference answer and (2) general-purpose LMs which aren’t specially trained but could serve as a verifier when prompted. For fine-tuned verifiers, we use general-verifier (1.5B) (Ma et al., 2025a) and compassverifier (Liu et al., 2025c) as baselines. For general-purpose LMs, we employ Qwen3-4/14B (Yang et al., 2025b), GPT-OSS-20/120B (Agarwal et al., 2025), and o3 (OpenAI, 2025c). We measure the agreement with ground-truth labels, the precision, the recall, and F1 scores for each baseline. 

Principia VerifyBench Experimental Result. The results are shown in Table 3. First, we find that o3 scores 94.05% agreement with ground truth labels, which justifies our choice behind using o3 as a judge in PrincipiaBench. Furthermore, GPT-OSS-120B scores 95.24% agreement, which indicates that it could serve as a reliable verifier to provide accurate reward during RL training. Notably, we find that math-verify fails to adequately determine equivalence in most of the cases in Principia VerifyBench, only scoring 5.95% agreement with the ground-truth labels. Note that Principia VerifyBench was constructed in an adversarial manner for math-verify, since we specifically sample instances where the o3 judge and math-verify disagree. Hence, the experimental results here imply that, in cases where the two judgments diverge, o3 is more likely to be correct. Furthermore, we find that specially fine-tuned verifiers (general-verifier, compassverifier) exhibit lower agreement rates (<90%), primarily due to low recall, whereas even small general-purpose LMs such as Qwen3-4B achieve agreement rates as high as 92.26%. These findings suggest that, for RL training on the Principia Collection, it is better to use a general-purpose LM as the verifier rather than a fine-tuned verifiers.

1.6 Related Work 

Evaluating reasoning capability of LMs. Reasoning has long been considered a cornerstone of machine intelligence and remains one of the most important capabilities to assess (Bottou, 2014; Weston et al., 2015; Chollet, 2019). 18 In evaluating the reasoning capabilities of LMs, it has become common in recent years to use grade-school mathematics exams or mathematics Olympiad competition problems, which typically require a numerical answer (Cobbe et al., 2021; Hendrycks et al., 2021; Luong et al., 2025a). Recent efforts also assess reasoning across a broader range of disciplines through multiple-choice questions in subjects such as physics, chemistry, and biology—tasks that require both advanced university- or graduate-level knowledge and the ability to synthesize the information provided in the problem statement to infer the answer (Wang et al., 2024b; Rein et al., 2024; Du et al., 2025). On the other hand, there is considerable excitement that LMs could be used in reasoning-intensive real-world applications such as autonomous scientific discovery and assisting scientists’ workflows (Van Noorden and Perkel, 2023; Mondorf and Plank, 2024; Lu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025b; OpenAI, 2025a). Yet a substantial gap remains between these ambitions and the standardized tests we currently rely on. One of the most important capabilities overlooked in the literature between exam-style standardized tests and scientific discovery is the ability to manipulate and infer complex mathematical objects. Problems that require producing or reasoning over mathematical objects present a more challenging test bed than multiple-choice or numerical-answer standardized exams, enable the evaluation of a broader range of reasoning skills, and simultaneously assess a critical competency needed for conducting experiments or writing scientific papers (Hestenes, 2009; Meerschaert, 2013; Goos et al., 2023). It is also worth noting that Ma et al. (2025a), whose motivation is closely aligned with ours, trains General-Reasoner on diverse domains and formats, yet evaluates their model only on multiple-choice questions and olympiad problems that require numerical answers. In contrast, our work introduces PrincipiaBench to assess an LM’s ability to derive mathematical objects.  

> Synthetic data for improving reasoning capability of LMs.

Synthetic data generation has been widely used to improve the instruction-following capabilities of LMs, both by creating new prompts and by generating corresponding responses (Wang et al., 2023b; Taori et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2025a). In contrast, when acquiring data aimed at enhancing reasoning—such as mathematical problem-solving—the highest priority is placed on ensuring answer correctness. As a result, instead of generating entirely new problems, prior work has typically augmented chain-of-thoughts (CoTs) to existing problem–answer pairs (Zelikman et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2023a; Mukherjee et al., 2023) 8. However, while this approach has the advantage of guaranteeing answer correctness, it also suffers from a key limitation: it restricts the diversity of problem sources to only those that already exist. Notably, commonly used post-training datasets—such as DeepScaleR (Luo et al., 2025), OmniMath (Gao et al., 2025), NuminaMath (Li et al., 2024a), and Skywork-OR1 (He et al., 2025)—collect problems from reliable sources including AMC, AIME, IMO, IMC, and Chinese K-12 exams. Yet none of these datasets contain a lot of the types of problems that require deriving complex mathematical objects. Another widely used data-synthesis approach extracts problems from online forums or documents in the pre-training corpus and then slightly modifies the problem statement while keeping the original answer unchanged. However, the diversity of the resulting data is still determined by the types of problems contained in those documents. Representative datasets produced through this approach include Natural Reasoning (Yuan et al., 2025b), WebInstruct-Verified (Ma et al., 2025a), and TextBookReasoning (Fan et al., 2025). Yet, when we analyze the token distribution of their outputs, we find that—despite the potential of document-based data synthesis—these datasets contain far too few instances that require the complex mathematical objects as well. In our work, we generate new problem statements using subjects drawn from graduate-level disciplines (i.e. , entities from MSC2020 and PhySH) that require mathematical objects as answers. Furthermore, we demonstrate that training on the Principia Collection yields strong performance on both the PrincipiaBench and other reasoning benchmarks.

2.1 Overview 

Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable performance across a wide variety of tasks, from general instruction following to complex reasoning (Guo et al., 2025; Yang et al., 2025b; Hurst et al., 2024; Grattafiori et al., 2024). A key factor behind this progress is the post-training stage that relies on Reinforcement Learning (RL) to align models to human preferences. Traditionally, this alignment has been achieved through Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), where human preference data is used to train a scalar reward model that assigns single numerical scores to guide optimization (Ouyang et al., 2022). However, scalar reward models are limiting: they produce a final score without generating intermediate reasoning steps, making them mostly ineffective for improving reasoning capabilities and susceptible to issues such as reward hacking and poor generalization to out-of-distribution (OOD) scenarios (Gao et al., 2023). More recently, with the advancement of o1 (Jaech et al., 2024) and R1-style thinking LLMs (Guo et al., 2025), a new paradigm has emerged: Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) (Lambert et al., 2024). As illustrated in Figure 12, RLVR replaces the scalar reward model with rule-based verifiers (e.g., code compilers or math checkers) to provide grounded feedback. While RLVR offers verifiable correctness, it strictly requires access to “gold” answers that are often hard-to-obtain or hard-to-verify. This limits its utility primarily to domains with objective correctness criteria—such as simple math and coding—leaving a gap for complex reasoning tasks where answers are not easily checked by rules, or subjective tasks where no gold answer exists (Tao et al., 2025). These limitations motivate a third paradigm: RLLM (Reinforcement Learning with an LM itself as a Reward Model). Unlike scalar models that lack deeper reasoning, or rule-based verifiers that lack flexibility, RLLM leverages the inherent capabilities of the LLM itself to serve as a “thinking” Reward Model. The effectiveness of this approach stems from an LLM’s two core strengths: the model’s reasoning capabilities enable it to generate explicit thinking traces for higher-quality judgments, while its instruction-following capabilities allow for flexible reward design through natural language prompts. Consequently, RLLM serves as a single, unified post-training framework effective across easy-to-verify, hard-to-verify, and non-verifiable domains. Notably, 21 Easy-to-Verify

Our proposed method operates in two distinct stages. First, in the Thinking LM-as-RM Training stage, we train an LLM to act as a thinking Reward Model. • We demonstrate that RLLM outperforms leading RLHF and RLVR baselines for Qwen, Llama, and Octothinker models of varying sizes, achieving significant gains on both verifiable tasks (Math, Physics) and non-verifiable tasks (AlpacaEval, ArenaHard). 

• We provide a comprehensive analysis of the recipe required for effective LM-as-RM training, empirically proving that on-policy training and a strong generator-verifier capability gap are essential for successful policy improvement. 

2.2 Preliminaries  

> RLHF: Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback.

The standard RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022a) pipeline trains a scalar reward model on pairwise human preference data. Each data point (x, y c, y r ) ∈ D 

includes an instruction x, a chosen response yc, and a rejected response yr . The reward model rϕ(x, y ) is optimized using a Bradley-Terry objective: 

LR = −E(x,y c,y r )∈D [log σ(rϕ(x, y c) − rϕ(x, y r ))] , (1) where σ(·) is the logistic sigmoid. This trains the model to assign higher rewards to the chosen responses. The trained scalar reward model is then used to optimize a policy model via Reinforcement Learning (e.g., PPO (Schulman et al., 2017)). In this paper, we use the term RLHF to specifically refer to the classical setup of InstructGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022), where a scalar reward model is trained from human preference data and thereafter, used to optimize a policy model.  

> RLVR: Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards.

Scalar reward models remain vulnerable to reward hacking, also known as reward over-optimization (Amodei et al., 2016; Gao et al., 2023; Eisenstein et al., 2024), especially under distributional shift, and have shown limited ability to drive improvements on challenging reasoning problems. Thus, in an attempt to prevent such hacking, RLVR replaces the reward model in RLHF with a verification function (Lambert et al., 2024) such that the policy only receives a reward when its generated responses are verifiably correct. 2.3 RLLM: Reinforcement Learning with Language Models as Reward Models 

To reduce dependence on costly human preference collection, a third paradigm – Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback (RLAIF) – was introduced (Bai et al., 2022b; Lee et al., 2024) where an AI model is itself used to provide rewards. Our RLLM paradigm is a special case of RLAIF (see Figure 12), which employs a Language Model as a thinking Reward Model (LM-as-RM) to unify post-training across diverse task types. In contrast to scalar reward models rϕ(·) used in RLHF or deterministic verifiers ψ(x, y, y ref ) used in RLVR, RLLM utilizes generative rewards rLM (·) obtained directly from a thinking LLM. This unifies the post-training recipe and allows the flexibility of computing rewards through pointwise, pairwise, or listwise judgments, in both reference-free rLM (x, y ) and reference-based rLM (x, y ref , y ) settings, depending on the task requirements described below. 23 We consider the standard RL objective for maximizing expected reward:

> 2.3.1 Task Settings and LM-as-RM Variants

We consider a general post-training setting where LLMs are expected to improve across Verifiable (e.g., Math, Code) and Non-Verifiable tasks (e.g., Open-ended Chat). Verifiable tasks are those with objective correctness criteria while non-verifiable tasks are inherently subjective and lack definitive, externally verifiable references (Lu, 2025). Within verifiable tasks, we further differentiate between easy-to-verify problems (gradeable via simple rules like math-verify ) and hard-to-verify problems (requiring intermediate reasoning to check equivalence). Given this set of tasks, we define the following LM-as-RM reward formulations: 

> Reference-Free Pointwise LM-as-RM

: rLM (x, y ) → (t, s ). In a reference-free pointwise setting, the LM-as-RM assigns rewards to individual responses independently. Given an instruction x and a candidate response y,the LLM generates a formatted judgment consisting of (i) a reasoning trace t enclosed within <think> tags, where the model analyzes the quality of y, and (ii) a final scalar score s within <score> tags. 

> Reference-Based Pointwise LM-as-RM

: rLM (x, y ref , y ) → (t, s ). In the reference-based setting, the LM-as-RM is additionally provided with a ground-truth reference yref and instructed to ground its judgment in this solution. This enables robust evaluation for both easy-to-verify and hard-to-verify problems that require substantial reasoning, effectively allowing the model to act as a generative verifier .

> Pairwise and Listwise LM-as-RM

: rLM (x, y) → (t, s) or rLM (x, y ref , y) → (t, s). Pointwise evaluation can be extended to pairwise or more generally, to listwise evaluation, where the LM-as-RM jointly evaluates multiple candidate responses. In this setting, the input consists of the instruction x and a set of k candidate responses y = {y1, . . . , y k}. The model jointly reasons about the candidates in t and outputs a list of scores 

s = {s1, . . . , s k}. Such comparative evaluation has been shown to substantially improve zero-shot LLM-judge performance, particularly for non-verifiable tasks (Whitehouse et al., 2025a). > 2.3.2 LM-as-RM Training via RLVR

Here we first describe how we use RL to train an LM-as-RM. While off-the-shelf LLMs can also serve as zero-shot RMs, their judgments often become unreliable when policy outputs drift out-of-distribution or when the generator–verifier gap is insufficient to provide trustworthy signals (Shao et al., 2025). To obtain more reliable and task-aligned reward signals, we thus train an LM-as-RM via Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards using the J1 framework (Whitehouse et al., 2025b). J1 constructs synthetic judgment tasks with labels, converting diverse tasks into a unified verifiable format compatible with RLVR-style training.  

> Synthetic Training Data Generation.

Let πθpolicy denote the initial policy LLM that we want to optimize using an LM-as-RM. To train the LM-as-RM, we first sample on-policy responses from πθpolicy and synthetically annotate the responses for the reward modeling task. Specifically, given a dataset D with instructions x and optionally available reference answers yref , we generate reward model training data in three steps: (i) sample a set of responses y from the policy πθpolicy ; (ii) employ a strong teacher LLM to rate the correctness or quality of these responses, obtaining scores s. For mathematical reasoning tasks, these ratings are typically binary (correct/incorrect); for non-verifiable tasks, the scores span a continuous scale [smin , s max ] reflecting response quality. Finally, we create a balanced dataset to ensure a uniform distribution over the assigned scores. The resulting synthetic dataset for LM-as-RM training is denoted as: DLM = (x, y ref , y, s) | (x, y ref ) ∈ D , where 

yref = ∅ in reference-free settings. This would enable us to train an LM-as-RM in both reference-free or reference-based scenarios. 24 RLVR Training. (2025b), we format examples from the synthetic dataset DLM into seed LM-as-RM prompts (depending on the training configuration), and instruct the model to generate a judgment (t′, s ′), consisting of a reasoning trace

(ya, y pivot ), (ypivot , y a). All non-pivot rollouts receive scores relative to the same anchor, thus producing more consistent rewards compared to randomly constructing O(n) pairs. The pivot rollout’s reward is obtained by averaging the scores across all the comparisons. This setting remains lightweight, with O(n)

LM-as-RM evaluations. 

> Listwise RLLM

. Similar constructions can be extended to a list of K > 2 comparisons, where the LM-as-RM jointly evaluates sets of K rollouts rather than pairs, while increasing the reward modeling complexity. The policy and the LM-as-RM may be initialized from the same or different base LLMs; when they coincide, this corresponds to a self-rewarding RLLM setting (Yuan et al., 2025a). Our experiments consider both cases. 

2.4 Experimental Setup   

> 2.4.1 LM-as-RM Models Training Recipe.

For the purpose of our main experiments, we use the J1 recipe to train on-policy LM-as-RM models starting from a large Qwen3-32B (thinking) model. As we will show later in our analysis (subsubsection 2.5.2), (1) training a large RM such that it ensures a substantial generator-verifier gap and (2) training the RM on-policy are both critical for best downstream policy improvements. Recall that LM-as-RM training can be done in both reference-free and reference-based settings, in which case we will call the resultant RMs J1-Qwen3-32B-RM and J1-Qwen3-32B-Verifier , respectively. We train these J1 models by (1) sourcing hard-to-verify prompts from OpenMathReasoning and Principia Collection, (2) generating 16 responses per prompt from Qwen3-1.7B model (i.e., the policy model to be trained), and (3) labeling each response as correct or incorrect using GPT-OSS-120B (since the final answers are hard-to-verify). See Figure 27 and Figure 28 for two such training examples of correct and incorrect responses. This results in a synthetically annotated and balanced dataset of 18 , 774 examples for pointwise LM-as-RM training with verifiable correctness rewards. For the purposes of ablations, whenever we train a weaker LM-as-RM model with off-policy samples, we will follow a similar J1 recipe with the exception that either the base model will change or the responses will be sampled from a different model than the policy to be aligned. 25 Training Hyperparameters. Following Whitehouse et al. (2025b), we implement all J1 models on top of 

verl (Sheng et al., 2024). We use a train batch size of 512 with a maximum sequence length of 4096 for both input and output. We also set the KL coefficient to 0.001 , the number of rollouts to 8, and the sampling temperature to 1.0 during RL training. All 32B J1 models are trained using 128 H200 GPUs. The best J1 

checkpoint is chosen based on a held-out validation set of 100 samples.  

> Inference Hyperparameters.

In addition to using J1 models during policy training, we also perform offline evaluation of these models on in-distribution validation data. Inference is done using vLLM (Kwon et al., 2023) with a temperature of 0.6 and top_p value of 0.95 .  

> 2.4.2 RLLM Policy Models Training Recipe.

In our primary RLLM experiments, we optimize a Qwen3-1.7B (Instruct) policy using the aforementioned J1-Qwen3-32B reward models. 9 This choice of model sizes for the policy and the RM creates a substantial generator–verifier gap, which we find to be essential for achieving strong downstream policy improvements. Starting with the same Qwen3-1.7B policy, we experiment with four different RLLM training paradigms that vary in their training data or the LM-as-RM: 

• Policy Trained on Hard-to-Verify Samples without Reference Answer. This forms the main experimental setting of RLLM where we assume access to a post-training dataset of only hard-to-verify math problems without any reference answers. As LMs continue to grow in capability, we will increasingly confront tasks for which no human-curated reference answers exist. To test such a post-training setting, we sample 3000 

hard-to-verify problems from the OpenMathReasoning dataset (Moshkov et al., 2025a). Following Tao et al. (2025), we select a sample as hard-to-verify if the final answer cannot be deterministically validated using a rule-based symbolic verifier like math_verify (see example in Appendix Figure 29) and easy-to-verify, otherwise (see example in Appendix Figure 30). Given the reference-free setting, our RLLM recipe uses J1-Qwen3-32B-RM as the reward model for optimization. 

• Policy Trained on Hard-to-Verify Samples with Reference Answer. This resembles an RLLM setting where we train on the same dataset as above but this time, assuming access to reference answers. Since these reference answers are hard-to-verify, our RLLM recipe uses J1-Qwen3-32B-Verifier as the (reference-based) verifier for policy training. 

• Policy Trained on Easy-to-Verify Samples with/without Reference Answer. Our motivation to perform RLLM on easy-to-verify samples is to show that even when reference answers exist and they are easy-to-verify, RLLM with a strong LM-as-RM can substitute rule-based verifiers like math-verify . Note that an LM-as-RM has the advantage of additionally evaluating the entire CoT and identifying process 

errors, unlike math-verify that only performs equivalence checks between the final answers. For the purpose of this study, we conduct RLLM experiments by sampling 3000 easy-to-verify training examples from the same OpenMathReasoning dataset (Moshkov et al., 2025a). In subsequent analyses and ablations of RLLM, we also consider different combinations of policy and reward models e.g., other sizes of Qwen3 models, Octothinker (Wang et al., 2025a), and Llama models. For non-verifiable instruction-following tasks, we train RLLM models on 1K Wildchat prompts (Zhao et al., 2024).  

> Training Hyperparameters.

We implement all policy models on top of fairseq2 (Balioglu et al., 2023). Models are trained using 64 H200 GPUs, allocating 48 GPUs for the trainer and 16 GPUs for inference. Fairseq2’s trainer is implemented as Single Program Multiple Data (SMPD) and all models (policy model, reference model, and J1 reward model) run as Ray actors. RLLM training uses a batch size of 32 with 8 rollouts per prompt, sampled with a temperature of 1.0. Similar to J1 training, we also set the KL coefficient to 0.001 for policy training. RLLM models are trained for a maximum of 1000 steps, checkpointing every 50 steps. To mitigate the effect of mismatch in log probabilities between the trainer and the inference engine in GRPO training, we apply truncated importance sampling correction with the maximum clip ratio set to 2.0. We also set max input and generation length to 4096 tokens and train Qwen3-1.7B in non-thinking mode to prevent

> Inference Hyperparameters.

We evaluate RLLM-trained policy models using vLLM with a temperature of 0.6

and a top_p value of 0.95 . The maximum decoding length is set to 40K tokens. Given the hybrid nature of Qwen3 models, we evaluate our post-trained models in both thinking and non-thinking modes.  

> 2.4.3 Evaluation Benchmarks and Metrics

To evaluate the effectiveness of RLLM as a general post-training recipe, we conduct experiments on both verifiable reasoning tasks as well as non-verifiable tasks.  

> Verifiable Reasoning Benchmarks.

Within verifiable reasoning, we experiment with both easy-to-verify bench-marks (e.g., questions with numerical answers) as well as hard-to-verify benchmarks (e.g., questions with mathematical objects as answers like equations). 

• Easy-to-verify Math Benchmarks. We evaluate on five popular competition math benchmarks from MathArena (Balunović et al., 2025) – AIME24, AIME25, BRUMO25, HMMT24, and HMMT25. For each benchmark, we report mean@16 results. 

• Hard-to-verify Physics Benchmark. We also evaluate on the Physics subset of PrincipiaBench. This serves as a test bed for both hard-to-verify reasoning problems as well as our models’ generalization to a different domain (given that RLLM’s training data only consists of math prompts). We use o3 (OpenAI, 2025b) to evaluate the correctness of the final answers and report mean@8 scores.  

> Non-verifiable Tasks.

Finally, we also evaluate RLLM on two non-verifiable instruction following benchmarks – AlpacaEval 2.0 (Li et al., 2023) and ArenaHard 2.0 (Li et al., 2025c). Following past work (Lanchantin et al., 2025), we report win rates (with and without length-control), using GPT-4o and GPT-4.1 as the evaluators for AlpacaEval and ArenaHard respectively.  

> 2.4.4 Baselines

We compare RLLM to two groups of baselines, RLHF and RLVR. 

• RLHF. This represents RL post-training with scalar reward models. In particular, we consider two strong general-purpose RMs – Skywork-Reward-V2-Llama-3.1-8B (Liu et al., 2025b) 10 and Athene-RM-8B (Frick et al., 2024). We also compare against a math-specific reward model AceMath-7B-RM (Liu et al., 2024). 

• RLVR. This refers to RL post-training with a verifier. The verifier operates in the presence of a reference answer and can either be rule-based (e.g., math-verify ) or model-based (e.g., general-verifier (Ma et al., 2025b). Even though general-verifier is a model-based verifier, it only checks the equivalence between the final answers (without any CoT reasoning). We compare RLLM to both these RLVR methods. Within the RLLM framework, we compare our J1 -trained LMs-as-RMs to various prompted LLMs-as-RMs. These belong to different families and are of different sizes and capabilities, thus enabling us to analyze how scaling up the RM/Verifier affects policy training and downstream task improvements. In particular, we report RLLM results with Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct , Qwen-1.7B , Qwen-32B , and GPT-OSS-120B as prompted LMs-as-RMs.

2.5.2 Analyses and Ablations Generator-Verifier Gap. In this section, we investigate the impact of the generator–verifier gap on RLLM training, specifically examining how the capability gap between the policy LM and the LM-as-RM influences downstream policy improvements. Recall that for our main experiments, we trained a Qwen3-1.7B policy with a J1-Qwen3-32B-RM where the RM was trained on-policy (by sampling responses from the Qwen3-1.7B policy). Now we ask if we train a weaker 1.7B LM-as-RM on its own responses i.e., J1-Qwen-1.7B-RM, can that also lead to downstream improvements? As shown in Table 10, we do not observe further improvements on top of the prompted Qwen3-1.7B-as-RM with J1 training. This result is further evidenced by Figure 15, where we compare the raw accuracy of different LMs-as-RMs on an in-distribution validation set. We observe that J1 training of a Qwen3-32B model leads to 10% improvement in judgment accuracy (averaged across 8 seeds) while providing almost no improvement on top of Qwen3-1.7B. In summary, training a Qwen3-1.7B model to evaluate its own responses leads to limited success and consequently, the resultant RM also does not lead to any downstream policy improvements. This underscores the importance of the capability gap between the generator and the verifier for obtaining downstream improvements. 3.1 Overview 

Scaling up test-time compute has emerged as a powerful tool for improving language models (LMs) on challenging reasoning tasks (OpenAI et al., 2024; Snell et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2025). A widely adopted pattern is to generate multiple solution attempts in parallel and then either select or aggregate among them (Wang et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023; Brown et al., 2024; Pan et al., 2025; Meituan LongCat Team et al., 2026). This paradigm underlies many of the strongest recent systems in complex reasoning (Tongyi DeepResearch Team et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025a; Hu et al., 2025c). In particular, several works train an aggregator model to learn how to properly synthesize a final answer from a large pool of parallel-generated candidates (Zhao et al., 2025; Venkatraman et al., 2025). However, current parallel thinking strategies suffer from two main limitations. First, most methods treat the solution generator as fixed and only optimize the aggregator (Zhao et al., 2025; Venkatraman et al., 2025). This creates a fundamental mismatch: the aggregator is trained on off-policy generations that may differ substantially from what it encounters at inference time, leading to suboptimal performance. Second, even when the generator is trained on-policy, standard outcome-based RL encourages the model to converge toward a single high-probability solution mode (Yue et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025b). This diversity collapse leaves the aggregator with redundant candidates that provide little complementary information, thus undermining the core premise of parallel thinking, which relies on diverse solutions to enable effective aggregation. In this work, we address both limitations through a unified online training framework. Our key insight is that the generator and the aggregator require different optimization objectives : the generator should produce diverse, complementary solutions, while the aggregator should synthesize them into a single best answer. 34 To this end, we apply pass@k optimization (Chen et al., 2025b) to the parallel generation stage, explicitly encouraging diversity by rewarding the model when any of its k samples succeed. For the aggregation stage, we use pass@1 optimization, training the model to reliably produce the correct final answer. Crucially, both stages are trained online and end-to-end, ensuring the generator learns to produce candidates that are not only diverse but also well-suited for downstream aggregation. Our contributions are threefold: 

• We show that the self-aggregation method is upper bounded by the pass@k performance and that the model is unable to synthesize correct solutions based on incorrect ones. 

• We develop a framework that enables online training of self-aggregation methods, where a single model is optimized for both pass@k generation and pass@1 aggregation end-to-end. 

• We validate the effectiveness of our method, which generalizes across competition math and scientific reasoning setups, including our newly introduced PrincipiaBench. 

3.2 Preliminaries 

Our goal is to train a language model that takes a problem as input, generates a set of candidate solutions in parallel, and then aggregates them over an arbitrary number of steps to produce a final answer. Formally, let 

x be a given problem and y⋆ its ground-truth solution. We consider two steps for a model to perform: (i) candidate sampling: the model reads the problem and generates a set of m candidate solutions y{1: m} and (ii) self-aggregation: the model reads both the problem and the set of candidate solutions and generates a final solution ˜y. The performance of the model can be evaluated at either an initial solution yi, or the final solution ˜y.

3.3 Method: ParaGator 

Our method jointly trains a single language model, Mθ to (i) generate diverse candidate solutions and (ii) aggregate these solutions into a final answer. Both stages are optimized end-to-end using online RL. Specifically, we train ParaGator using reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR), with rewards computed at two levels: on the initial set of candidate solutions and on the final aggregated answer. The candidate set is rewarded with pass@k, incentivizing the model to produce plausible but diverse solutions. The final answer is rewarded with pass@1, incentivizing the model to reliably produce a single correct solution. Both candidate generation and aggregation rollouts are sampled online from the current policy during training.  

> 3.3.1 Candidate Solution Generation and Pool Selection

For each problem x, we first generate a pool of m candidate solutions by sampling from the model m times: 

yi ∼ M θ (y | pC , x ), i = 1 , . . . , m. 

At this stage, we evaluate each candidate with a verifiable reward function 

r(yi, y ⋆) ∈ { 0, 1},

which indicates whether the candidate answer matches the ground-truth solution y⋆.

> 3.3.2 Self-Aggregation

Given the problem x and the candidate set y1: m, we prompt the same model to act as an aggregator. The input is the problem concatenated with the candidates in a fixed, structured format, for example: Aggregation Prompt ( pA)Given the following problem: {PROBLEM} and these solution attempts: {CANDIDATE_1} {CANDIDATE_2} ... {CANDIDATE_N} ========================= it is possible that any, all, or none of these solutions are correct or complete. Carefully review the provided solutions, using them as starting points — correcting mistakes, filling in gaps, and/or combining useful ideas — to produce a final, comprehensive, and correct solution to the problem. Conditioned on this input, the model generates an aggregated solution 

˜y ∼ M θ (y | pA, x, y 1: m).

We then compute an aggregation-level verifiable reward 

Ragg (x, y 1: m, ˜y) = r(˜ y, y ⋆) ∈ { 0, 1}.

Crucially, the aggregator is always trained on-policy: during training, it sees candidate pools sampled from the current generator Mθ , rather than from a frozen or separately trained model. This alignment between training and inference eliminates the off-policy mismatch common in prior self-aggregation methods and ensures that the generator learns to produce candidates that are well-suited for downstream aggregation. 

> Pass@1 Aggregation Optimization

The aggregated solutions use pass@1 performance: the aggregator receives a reward of 1 if and only if its final answer is correct. Unlike the candidate stage, only the single aggregated trajectory is rewarded, pushing the model to reliably synthesize the best answer from the available candidates.  

> 3.3.3 Training Details

We optimize both stages simultaneously with RLVR. For each training example, we generate a candidate pool 

y1: m from Mθ (y | x), and an aggregation trajectory ˜y from Mθ (y | x, y 1: m). We then update θ using GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) with a KL-regularized RLVR loss. Because both terms are computed from online rollouts of the same policy Mθ , the generator learns to produce candidate sets that are not only individually plausible and diverse, but also useful for the aggregator it will later become. 36 3.3.4 Inference Details 

During training, we optimize a single round of aggregation over one candidate pool. At inference time, however, we naturally generalize this to multiple iterations of aggregation, enabling sequential scaling in addition to the learned parallel sampling. Concretely, given problem x, we first sample an initial pool of m candidates y(0) 1: m ∼ M θ (y | x) and sample 

m aggregated solutions ˜y(0) 1: m ∼ M θ (y | x, y (0) 1: m). We then form an updated candidate pool y(1) 1: m = ˜y(0) 1: m from these aggregation rollouts. The model is then prompted again in aggregation mode on (x, y (1) 1: m) to produce a refined set of solutions ˜y(1) 1: m, and so on. This continues for T iterations before returning the final aggregated answer. This iterative procedure preserves the same generator–aggregator interface used during training, while allowing the model at test time to repeatedly refine its reasoning over an evolving pool of candidate solutions under a fixed compute budget.

> Parallel Inference Scaling

Recent works have explored scaling up test-time compute by generating multiple candidate solutions in parallel, moving beyond foundational majority-voting methods like Self-Consistency (Wang et al., 2022). Rather than simply selecting a single best output, modern approaches focus on dynamically selecting, compressing, or aggregating intermediate candidates to form a cohesive final response. One prominent direction is dynamic branching: Pan et al. (2025) train models to adaptively spawn child threads for specific sub-tasks and join their results back into the main reasoning process. Similarly, Zheng et al. (2025) trigger multiple reasoning threads at critical steps to be merged into the main context. Li et al. (2025a) adapt this parallel scaling to agentic contexts by branching partial rollouts only at highly uncertain steps, utilizing compressed reports during answer aggregation to maintain computational efficiency. To handle the massive context overhead of parallel scaling, Hu et al. (2025c) utilize message passing to compress long reasoning trajectories, enabling test-time compute to scale to millions of tokens under a fixed context length. Alternatively, other works emphasize multi-round iterative refinement; Venkatraman et al. (2025) generate parallel solutions and aggregate them over successive rounds. In a closely related approach, Madaan et al. (2025) employ a cyclical pipeline that generates parallel solutions, distills them into a condensed summary, and generates refined solutions conditioned on that summary. To ensure these iterative generation cycles do not amplify errors, Sharma et al. (2026) leverage Process Reward Models to provide step-level verification during aggregation of the candidate population. Alternatively, Singh et al. (2026) proposes a pairwise self-verification method to select the single best solution from the generated pool, rather than aggregating a new solution. In this work, we utilize end-to-end reinforcement learning to train a model to operate within a minimal scaffolding of parallel generation and subsequent aggregation.  

> Optimizing for Solution Variety in RL for LLMs

The effectiveness of aggregation frameworks is fundamentally tied to the diversity of the underlying candidate solutions. To encourage this during training, many recent works directly incorporate diversity metrics into the reinforcement learning reward signal, utilizing techniques such as embedding models (Zhou et al., 2026), trained classifiers (Li et al., 2025b), and LLM-based diversity judges (Hu et al., 2026). A particularly effective subset of these methods focuses on pass@k optimization (Tang et al., 2025; Walder and Karkhanis, 2025; Peng et al., 2025). Building on this direction, our work adopts the direct pass@k optimization strategy from Chen et al. (2025b), which specifically up-weights the advantage of incorrect solutions to prevent mode collapse and expand the exploration space. 

3.7 Conclusion 

Scaling test-time compute is only as effective as the diversity and quality of the reasoning paths that are explored. Traditional parallel decoding and self-aggregation methods are bottlenecked by off-policy generations and mode collapse. To overcome these limitations, we introduced ParaGator , a unified online reinforcement learning framework that explicitly aligns and optimizes candidate generations with downstream aggregation. Our core insight is that generation and aggregation require distinct but complementary optimization strategies. In ParaGator , the generator actively explores a diverse, complementary set of solutions through pass@k optimization. Simultaneously, the aggregator is trained via pass@1 optimization to reliably synthesize the on-policy candidates into a final answer. Extensive evaluations across competition math and scientific reasoning benchmarks validate the strength of this approach. In both base models (e.g., Qwen3-4B-Base) and strong post-trained reasoners (e.g. Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507), ParaGator consistently improves standard offline self-aggregation. The gains are particularly pronounced on highly complex tasks, such as AIME and Principia, where synthesizing diverse reasoning trajectories is critical. By co-training generation and aggregation end-to-end, ParaGator provides a robust, scalable recipe for improving inference-time reasoning.
