## Abstract 

Large Reasoning Models (LRMs) exhibit back-tracking and self-verification mechanisms that en-able them to revise intermediate steps and reach correct solutions, yielding strong performance on complex logical benchmarks. We hypothesize that such behaviors are beneficial only when the model has sufficiently strong “critique” ability to detect its own mistakes. This work systemati-cally investigates how current LRMs recover from errors by inserting arithmetic mistakes in their in-termediate reasoning steps. Notably, we discover a peculiar yet important phenomenon: despite the error propagating through the chain-of-thought (CoT), resulting in an incorrect intermediate con-clusion, the model still reaches the correct final answer. This recovery implies that the model must possess an internal mechanism to detect er-rors and trigger self-correction, which we refer to as the hidden critique ability . Building on feature space analysis, we identify a highly interpretable 

critique vector representing this behavior. Ex-tensive experiments across multiple model scales and families demonstrate that steering latent rep-resentations with this vector improves the model’s error detection capability and enhances the perfor-mance of test-time scaling at no extra training cost. Our findings provide a valuable understanding of LRMs’ critique behavior, suggesting a promis-ing direction to control and improve their self-verification mechanism. Our code is available at

Recent work on test-time scaling increases inference com-pute to enhance reasoning performance, rather than scal-ing to larger parameters. For instance, s1 (Muennighoff et al., 2025) shows that minimal fine-tuning with budget forcing, appending “Wait” tokens during inference, yields significant gains, while compute-optimal strategies enable smaller models to outperform larger ones (Snell et al., 2025). However, test-time scaling does not consistently improve performance (Ghosal et al., 2025; Zeng et al., 2025), as its ef-fectiveness depends on the model’s intrinsic self-verification capacity (Setlur et al., 2025; Zeng et al., 2025). A key bot-tleneck is: LLMs struggle to detect errors in long CoTs (He et al., 2025), fail to self-correct without external guidance (Kamoi et al., 2024), and cannot reliably identify their own mistakes (Tyen et al., 2024). While training-based methods improve verification capabilities (Weng et al., 2023; Kang et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025b; Lee et al., 2025c), they require substantial training costs, architectural complexity, or external tools, reducing practicality. We hypothesize that the budget forcing approach relies heavily on the error de-tection and correction ability, and investigate whether this ability can be identified and controlled without additional training. Understanding how LRMs handle errors internally requires samples where the model makes mistakes and performs self-correction. However, we observe that such instances are rare in standard generation.When the model answers correctly, CoTs are typically all correct; when it is wrong, errors per-sist throughout with no clear recovery. Moreover, labeling every step is prohibitively expensive. Instead, we deliber-ately inject arithmetic errors (e.g., 3+4 = 6 ) at intermediate steps within the reasoning trace and let the model continue without terminating its thinking process (Figure 1). We then compare the thinking conclusions against the ground-truth answers to assess its recovery, yielding a scalable analy-sis framework without per-step supervision. Surprisingly, we observe that despite the corrupted reasoning from the injected errors, the model often recovers and produces cor-rect final answers (Figure 2). We call it the hidden critique ability , where LRMs hiddenly rectify errors without verbal-ization in chain of thoughts. To understand this recovery, we analyze the feature space and observe a strong separability between the error-recovery (with intervention) runs and the standard (without interven-tion) runs, although both yield correct final answers. We hypothesize that this separability captures an internal cri-tique mechanism, i.e., the model’s hidden ability to detect and correct errors without explicit verbalization. This hid-den capability can be represented as a single linear direction in the activation space – which is exactly the difference be-tween the two mean vectors of activations of the intervened and standard runs. We call it the critique vector and validate its semantic meaning with logit lens analysis across model families. To further evaluate our hypothesis and test the utility of the critique vector, we use it to steer the LRMs and evaluate the performance on several error-detection tasks. Experimental results show that positive steering nudges LRMs’ behavior to become more “critique” and improves its mistake-identification performance, while negative steer-ing worsens it. Finally, we show that controlling LRMs with this critique vector during test-time scaling enables the model to detect its mistakes better and produce more correct solutions. In summary, our contributions are as follows: • We are the first that uncover the hidden self-correction phenomenon in LRMs: injecting a simple arithmetic error into the CoT corrupts reasoning and intermedi-ate conclusions, yet the model implicitly recovers and produces the correct final response. • Through feature space analysis, we show that activa-tions are highly separable between the error-recovery runs and the standard runs. Leveraging this separation, we extract a critique vector and rigorously validate its error-detection capability through logit lens analysis and steering experiments. • Finally, we demonstrate that the critique vector influ-ences test-time scaling: increasing it helps the model better recognize its mistakes and improve its final ac-curacy, while decreasing it degrades performance. 

## 2. Related Work 

Representation Engineering in LLMs. Representation engineering extracts vectors from model activations to steer behavior without fine-tuning. Early work (Rimsky et al., 2024; Turner et al., 2024) computed steering vectors from contrastive prompt pairs, enabling applications in style con-trol (Konen et al., 2024) and contrastive steering (Rimsky et al., 2024), later extended to refusal control (Lee et al., 2025b; Arditi et al., 2024) and improved instruction fol-lowing (Stolfo et al., 2025). Recent advances address pol-ysemanticity through feature-level steering (Yang et al., 2025b) and apply these techniques to large reasoning mod-els (LRMs), enabling control over thinking budgets (Li et al., 2025), trace editing (Sun et al., 2025), and reasoning cal-ibration (Chen et al., 2025c). Unlike prior work steering style, length, or consistency, we are the first that discover the hidden critique ability and identify a critique vector that represents latent error detection, providing direct mechanis-tic access to the internal processes underlying successful test-time scaling. 

Faithfulness of Chain-of-Thought Reasoning. Recent studies reveal that reasoning traces in LRMs are often un-faithful to the model’s actual internal computations. For 2Decoding the Critique Mechanism in Large Reasoning Models 

example, models may fail to verbalize their true reasoning processes (Chen et al., 2025e; Turpin et al., 2023), generate misleading explanations (Arcuschin et al., 2025), or pro-duce decorative “aha moments” that do not reflect genuine problem-solving (Zhao et al., 2025). Our work observes a related phenomenon, where models generate correct fi-nal answers despite corrupted reasoning, further evidencing the mismatch between verbalized and internal computation. However, we go beyond observation by locating this capa-bility in latent space, interpreting it as a critique vector, and using it to control error detection and recovery behavior. 

Error Detection and Correction. Prior work reveals mixed capabilities in LLM error detection and self-correction. While self-verification improves reasoning (Weng et al., 2023), fundamental limitations persist: LLMs struggle to detect errors in long CoT traces (He et al., 2025), cannot reliably correct mistakes without external guidance (Kamoi et al., 2024), and only succeed when error locations are pro-vided (Tyen et al., 2024). Recent approaches address these gaps through key condition verification (Wu et al., 2024), de-composition into confidence and critique components (Yang et al., 2025e), or reinforcement learning (Kumar et al., 2025). In reasoning models, self-verification emerges as a critical ability during training (Guo et al., 2025), yet the underlying mechanisms remain underexplored. Lee et al. (2025a) exam-ined its geometry in task-specific models, while Zhang et al. (2025) labeled reasoning steps using commercial models, then trained probes showing that models implicitly encode correctness information. In contrast, we focus on extract-ing the model’s intrinsic critique ability without external supervision, which enables mechanistic control over error detection across diverse logical reasoning tasks. 

Test-Time Scaling. Test-time scaling shifts focus from parameter scaling to allocating more computation during inference. Simple finetuning on reasoning traces and ap-pending “Wait” tokens already yield notable gains (Muen-nighoff et al., 2025), while compute-optimal strategies allow smaller models to outperform much larger ones (Snell et al., 2025). Follow-up work incorporates compute-optimal al-location (Liu et al., 2025), self-verification (Chen et al., 2025b), tool-assisted verification (Kang et al., 2025), and reward models to assess the reasoning quality (Wang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Lightman et al., 2024). However, recent studies reveal limitations to this potential, as extended thinking alone saturates and fails to scale beyond a certain point (Ghosal et al., 2025). Additionally, LRMs show lim-ited scaling capabilities without proper verification (Zeng et al., 2025), and scaling without verification or RL remains suboptimal (Setlur et al., 2025). We hypothesize that cri-tique is the core mechanism underlying successful test-time scaling and demonstrate this by extracting critique vectors that enable direct control over the scaling process. 

## 3. Uncovering Hidden Self-Correction 

3.1. Intervention Framework 

LRMs typically operate in two phases: given the prompt 

q, they first generate a reasoning trace c between a special start-of-think token ⟨think ⟩ and an end-of-think token 

⟨/think ⟩, and then produce the final answer a. Formally: 

fLRM : ( q, ⟨think ⟩) 7 → (c, ⟨/think ⟩, a ). (1) Previous work has studied the model behavior under CoT interventions via prefilling (Yang et al., 2025c; Wu et al., 2025), where a variable content e is integrated immediately after the thinking-start token. This generation process can be expressed as: 

fLRM : ( q, ⟨think ⟩, e ) 7 → (˜ c, ⟨/think ⟩, ˜a). (2) Here, ˜c represents the model’s reasoning continuation after intervention, and the complete CoT is formed by concatenat-ing e + ˜ c. The intervention e is flexible and task-dependent; for instance, it may be an instruction, a prompt, or another mechanism designed to analyze specific model behaviors. A key question in our work is whether LRMs can detect and recover from errors during their internal reasoning. To investigate this, we inject an arithmetic error e (e.g., 3 + 4 = 6 ) into the reasoning chain and examine whether the continuation ˜c recovers from it. We generate this error using 

GPT-5 with a carefully engineered prompt Perr (detailed in Appendix A.1): 

fGPT-5 : ( q, Perr ) 7 → e. (3) Using this approach, we construct GSM8K-Error and MATH500-Error by generating one error e per question 

q, creating a consistent benchmark across all models. For evaluation, we extract the thinking process ˜c and the final answer a, then compare each against the ground truth yq to determine whether the model can recover from the injected errors. Unlike step-by-step supervision methods (Zhang et al., 2025) that require labeling each reasoning step, our approach is far more efficient and scalable. Full details on error generation process and correctness evaluation are in Appendix E.1 and Section 5.1, respectively.

## 4. The Geometry of Critique Ability 

Section 3 shows that LRMs possess a hidden critique ability to recover from injected errors. However, this behavior disappears in the original samples with natural CoTs. In this section, we compare these two cases to extract and analyze the critique ability using mechanistic interpretability tools. We observe linear separability in the latent space between the intervened (incorrect-CoT) and baseline (correct-CoT) runs in Section 4.2. This linear separability suggests that the hidden capability can be decoded as a single direction in the activation space. We validate this hypothesis using Logit Lens (nostalgebraist, 2020) and extract steering vectors that capture the recovery mechanism. 

4.1. The Behavior on Original Samples 

Section 3 shows that LRMs secretly detect the injected error and recover in the final answer. We investigate the behavior of LRMs on original samples and measure the accuracy of the thinking and the final answer separately. Table 1 demonstrates that the CoT and the final answer are usually consistent. When the thinking part is wrong, the model hardly fix it and thus returns the incorrect final answer. The recovery rate is only 1.9% for R1-8B on GSM8k and less than 1% in every other case. These results suggest that LRMs behave differently and do not invoke the critique behavior without the injected error.

Following the standard steering approach (Venhoff et al., 2025; Sun et al., 2025; Arditi et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2025c; Yang et al., 2025b), we add a scaled steering vector to the residual stream immediately after the MLP layer:

5.3. Test-Time Scaling with Critique Steering Setup. We evaluate the controllability of error recovery performance on GSM8K-Error, MATH500-Error, and BIG-Bench Mistake. For GSM8K-Error and MATH500-Error, we let the model generate from the injected error until it fin-ishes the final answer. For BIG-Bench Mistake, we put the given CoT in the thinking part and let the model produce the final answer. More details of the test-time scaling instruction prompt can be found at Appendix A.3. We compare the final answer with the ground-truth label to compute the accuracy. To check if test-time scaling help LRMs fix the injected errors, we adopt the budget forcing approach (Muennighoff et al., 2025) and append the “Wait” token when the model wants to terminate the thinking process. We report the per-formance at different numbers of iterations corresponding to the number of appended “Wait” tokens. For the effect of the critique vector, we perform positive steering with a coefficient 1.0 and negative steering with a coefficient −1.0.

Critique vector steers the recovery performance. Fig-ure 7 depicts that steering with the critique vector success-fully controls the ability to recover from injected errors. Particularly, the original Qwen3-4B yields 77% accuracy on GSM8K-Error, while positive steering boosts that value to almost 90% . In contrast, negative steering degrades the performance to slightly above 50% . Similar trends can be observed on other models (R1-distilled series) and datasets (MATH500-Error and BIG-Bench Mistake). This confirms that the critique ability plays a vital role in error correction and is represented by our identified critique vector. 

Test-time scaling corrects the injected mistake. Besides the hidden critique ability, we expect that explicit reflec-tion by manually appending “Wait” can also recover from injected errors. Indeed, Figure 7 shows that more budget forcing reliably improves the accuracy on three datasets across all models. This observation aligns with previous studies (Muennighoff et al., 2025; Snell et al., 2025) exibit-ing the effectiveness of test-time scaling. 

Critique vector further enhance test-time scaling. How-ever, test-time scaling alone does not reliably repair the incorrect CoT. We observe that positive steering consis-tently boosts the accuracy of test-time scaling on all forcing iterations. These results highlight the impact of the hidden critique ability, suggesting that it is complementary to and cannot be completely captured by explicit reflection. On the other hand, negative steering also reduces the accuracy on all iterations, indicating that test-time scaling might not be successful without the critique ability. 

## 6. Conclusion 

In this paper, we discover the novel hidden critique behav-ior where LRMs recover from injected mistakes in CoTs and produce correct final answers, but do not verbalize that error-detection and error-correction process. Through fea-ture analysis, we show that LRMs internally distinguish the latent features of intervened samples with hidden critique behavior and those of original samples without hidden cri-tique behavior. We identify the critique vector representing this hidden critique mechanism and validate it through logit lens analysis. Our empirical evidence shows that steering with this vector successfully controls the error detection performance on several benchmarks across many models. In addition, we point out that test-time scaling relies on the critique ability and can be combined with critique steer-ing to further enhance the correction performance. Our findings shed light on the role of the critique ability in cur-rent LRMs and open many interesting questions. Future extension should investigate if this behavior is the result of pre-training or post-training and how it emerges. Since the hidden critique ability makes LRMs correct errors in the final answer but not verbalize in CoTs, we can analyze its impact on the faithfulness of CoTs. Furthermore, it is interesting to study the influence of this ability on other prop-erties, for example, alignment. Finally, we can leverage this ability to improve other test-time scaling methods, such as verifier-based approach, self-consistency, or self-correction prompting. We leave them to future works. 8Decoding the Critique Mechanism in Large Reasoning Models 

## Impact Statement 

Error-correction and error-detection are beneficial to guar-antee the quality of responses provided by language models, which is important for high-stakes applications such as med-ical domains or scientific discovery. Our analysis reveals the mechanism of this behavior, and our proposed methods allow controlling error-recovery performance, advancing the development of reliable and trustworthy AI systems.

The following system prompt Perr was used to generate incorrect reasoning steps: 

GPT-5 Instruction Prompt 

Given a question and its ground-truth answer, generate a natural reasoning trace that contains a calculation error. 

PURPOSE: 

This trace is a deliberate error variant used to test a reasoning model’s ability to recover from arithmetic mistakes. 

INSTRUCTIONS: 

- Write the trace as a sequence of short, natural reasoning steps, phrased as if someone is thinking aloud. - Use natural, anthropomorphic openings such as “First, I need to...”, “Okay, so...”, “Let me...”, or other common starter phrases typically found in the thinking process of reasoning models. - Separate steps with two newlines ( \n\n). - Do NOT mention or hint that an error occurred (no phrases like “mistake”, “should be”, or “wrong”). - Introduce an arithmetic error at the earliest plausible calculation step. - Stop the reasoning immediately after the mistaken calculation step; do not continue solving or produce an answer. The goal is to isolate the reasoning up to the error only. - The trace must be self-consistent and match the question up to the step before the error. - Keep the reasoning coherent, natural, and plausible.

A.3. Test-time Scaling Prompt 

We perform test-time scaling by injecting a corrupted chain-of-thought (CoT) into the model’s intermediate thinking process and allowing the model to continue generating. This setup simulates partial erroneous reasoning and evaluates whether extended inference can recover from the injected mistake. Concretely, each generation follows the structure: 

Test-time Scaling Generation Format 

{question }⟨think ⟩{injected erroneous CoT }{continued reasoning }⟨/think ⟩{final answer }

After the model completes its reasoning, we optionally append the token “Wait” multiple times to extend inference depth. This simple control mechanism encourages backtracking and revision of earlier steps, following prior work (e.g., (Muennighoff et al., 2025; Zeng et al., 2025; Ghosal et al., 2025)). 15 Decoding the Critique Mechanism in Large Reasoning Models

We first examine how steering affects error detection capability, drawing on examples from ProcessBench-GSM8K and GSM8K-Error. These cases reveal two complementary failure modes that positive and negative steering address in opposite directions: missing genuine errors and hallucinating errors in correct solutions. As shown in Figures 11 and 12, positive steering enhances the model’s ability to detect subtle reasoning errors while avoiding false positives. In contrast, Figure 13 demonstrates that negative steering suppresses critical verification, causing the model to accept erroneous premises without scrutiny. 

Problem Statement (ProcessBench - GSM8K)          

> Problem: Gerald works at a daycare that pays him $30 every day. He worked for an entire week and spent a total of $100. How much does he have left?
> Target: $110 Question: Identify any errors in the solution step below.
> Proposed Solution Step:
> First, calculate the weekly earnings. Gerald earns $30 each day. Assuming a standard workweek of 5 days: Weekly earnings = 5 ×$30 = $150

Baseline Model (Coefficient 0.0)  

> The solution starts by calculating his weekly earnings. It mentions a standard workweek of 5 days. So, they multiply 5 by $30, which gives $150. That seems correct because 5 days times $30 is indeed $150. ... [Checks subtraction of expenses] ...
> Conclusion: The calculation steps seem accurate, and there doesn’t appear to be any errors in the reasoning or arithmetic.
> Outcome: The model overlooks the “entire week” wording and accepts the 5-day assumption.

Positive Steering (Coefficient +1.0)      

> First, the solution starts by calculating his weekly earnings... 5 by $30 gives $150. But wait, the problem says he worked for an entire week . Typically, a week has 7 days. If he worked 7 days, his earnings should be 7×$30 = $210 . The initial solution assumes a 5-day workweek, but the problem implies the full week.
> Conclusion: The error is in assuming 5 days instead of 7.
> Outcome: Successfully identifies the reasoning error.

The proposed solution makes an unjustified assumption about the work schedule (5 days vs 7 days). The Baseline model accepts this assumption as “standard”, while the steered model critically analyzes the specific phrasing “entire week” and identifies the discrepancy.

Problem Statement (GSM8K) 

Problem: Tom bought a CD for $4, and a headphone set. In total, he paid $48. How many more CDs would Tom have been able to buy if he had decided not to buy the headphone set? 

Proposed Solution: ... To find the cost of the headphone set, we subtract the cost of the CD from the total amount he paid: $48 − $4 = $44 . So, the headphone set costs $44. If Tom didn’t buy the headphone set, he would have $44 more to spend on CDs. Since each CD costs $4, we can divide $44 by $4 to find out how many more CDs he could buy: $44 ÷ $4 = 11 .

Baseline Model (Coefficient 0.0) 

Let’s check paragraph 2. ”If Tom didn’t buy the headphone set, he would have $44 more...” Wait, the total amount he paid was $48. If he didn’t buy the headphones, he would have had the full $48 available to spend on CDs. The solution claims he would have $44 more, but he should have the total $48. So 48 / 4 = 12 CDs. Since he already bought one, it’s 11 more? No, the calculation of available funds is wrong. 

Conclusion: The solution is incorrect because the available amount should be $48. 

Outcome: The model hallucinates an error in a correct solution (False Positive). 

Positive Steering (Coefficient +1.0) 

Checking paragraph 2... The problem states he bought a CD ($4) and a headphone set. The total paid is $48. This implies the headphone set cost 

$48 − $4 = $44 . If he decides not to buy the headphone set, he saves exactly the cost of the headphones: $44. He can then use this $44 to buy more CDs: 

$44 /$4 = 11 . This matches the solution. 

Conclusion: The logic in paragraph 2 is sound. He saves $44, not $48, because the CD purchase is fixed. 

Outcome: Correctly validates the solution (Avoids Hallucination). 

Figure 12. Qualitative example on GSM8K task with Qwen3-4B model. The baseline model hallucinates a logic error, incorrectly arguing that the total budget ($48) should be used for the counterfactual, ignoring that the CD was still purchased. The positive steering helps the model successfully verify the correct logic: money saved equals the cost of the unpurchased item ($44). 

Problem Statement (ProcessBench-GSM8K) 

Problem: A caterer prepared hot dogs for 36 guests. He prepared enough for half of the guests to have two hotdogs (implying the other half has one). However, 40 guests showed up, and everyone wanted a second hotdog. 

Question: How many guests did not get a second hotdog? 

Proposed Solution Step 1: 

The caterer planned for half of 36 guests (18) to eat two hotdogs. So, the total number of hotdogs prepared is 18 × 2 = 36 hotdogs. (Note: Ignores the single hotdogs for the other 18 guests). 

Baseline Model (Coefficient 0.0) 

Let’s figure out how many hotdogs he prepared. Half of 36 is 18, so 18 guests have two hotdogs ( 18 × 2 = 36 ). Wait, hold on a second. If half are having two, the other half would have one each. So the total should be 36 + 18 = 54 hotdogs. But the solution says he prepared 36 hotdogs. That doesn’t seem right. The solution only accounted for the 36... their initial calculation is wrong. 

Outcome: Correctly identifies the error (54 vs 36 prepared). 

Negative Steering (Coefficient -1.0) 

Half of 36 is 18, so 18 guests would have two hotdogs each. Multiplying 18 by 2 gives 36 hotdogs. So, the caterer prepared 36 hotdogs in total. ...If the caterer only prepared 36 hotdogs, and 40 guests wanted a second... 4 guests couldn’t get a second. The solution seems accurate... Therefore, the solution is correct, and there’s no error. 

Outcome: Fails to spot the error (Maintains the Error).

The baseline model successfully debugs the setup ( 54 hotdogs vs 36 ), whereas negative steering suppresses this critical check and accepts the erroneous premise.

E.2.2. T EST -T IME SCALING 

We next examine how steering interacts with the model’s internal self-verification behavior with test-time scaling methods. Figures 14 and 15 illustrate that positive steering enables successful error recovery during the “Wait” phase, whereas negative steering causes the model to rationalize and reinforce injected errors rather than correct them. 

Problem Statement (GSM8K-Error) 

Question: Bailey starts with a certain amount of money. Then she receives a weekly allowance of $5 for 8 weeks. At the end of the 8 weeks, if she has a total of $100, how much money did Bailey start with? 

Target: 60 

Injected Error: 

First, I need to find the total amount she received from allowance: $5 each week for 8 weeks. So I’ll multiply 5 × 8 = 30 and subtract it from the final total ( $100 ). 

Baseline Model (Coefficient 0.0) 

Wait, that doesn’t make sense... But just to double-check, let me add $70 and $30 again... Yes, 70 + 30 = 100 . So, my answer is correct. 

Outcome: The model fails to spot the arithmetic error ( 5 × 8̸ = 30 ). 

Positive Steering (Coefficient +1.0) 

Wait, but let me double-check. If she started with $70 and received $5 each week for 8 weeks... Wait, 5 × 8 is actually 40, not 30. So initial money should be 100 − 40 = 60 . Yes, that makes sense. If she started with $60 and received $5 for 8 weeks, that’s 60 + 40 = 100 .

Conclusion: The initial calculation of allowance was wrong. 

Outcome: Identifies & Fixes Error ( 30 → 40 ). 

Figure 14. Qualitative example of positive steering on GSM8K-Error with R1-32B model. The baseline model uses a multiplication error (5 × 8 = 30 ) and confirms it during self-verification ( 70 + 30 = 100 ). Positive steering initially makes the same error but successfully catches it during the “Wait” phase, correcting the allowance to 40 and the final answer to 60 .

Problem Statement (GSM8K-Error) 

Question: Juan and Carlos sell lemonade. Costs/gallon: $3 lemons, $2 sugar. Sell: 20 glasses at $0.50. Profit $25. How much spent on lemons? 

Injected Error: 

The costs are $3 for lemons and $2 for sugar per gallon, so the total cost per gallon is $6. Revenue per gallon is $10. Profit per gallon is $4. If total profit is $25, gallons sold is 6.25. Total spent on lemons is 6.25 * $3 = $18.75. 

Baseline Model (Coefficient 0.0) 

Wait, but the problem asks for the amount spent on lemons... So, 6.25 gallons * $3 = $18.75. 

Wait, but let me double-check my steps to make sure I didn’t make a mistake. Total cost per gallon: $3 (lemons) + $2 (sugar) = $5? Wait, no, $3 + $2 is $5, so total cost per gallon is $5, not $6. ... Therefore, the amount spent on lemons is 5 gallons * $3 per gallon = $15. 

Outcome: The model catches the addition error ($3+$2=$5) and corrects the answer. 

Negative Steering (Coefficient -1.0) 

Wait, but the problem asks for the amount spent on lemons... So, using the number of gallons sold, which is 6.25, multiplied by the cost per gallon for lemons, which is $3, gives the total amount spent on lemons. So, 6.25 gallons * $3 per gallon = $18.75. But wait, the problem might expect an exact answer... 25/4 * 3 = 75/4, which is $18.75. Yes, that makes sense. 

Outcome: Maintains the Error. 

Figure 15. Qualitative example of negative steering on ProcessBench-GSM8K with R1-32B model. The prompt context introduces an arithmetic error ($3+$2=$6 instead of $5). The baseline model initially adopts the error but triggers a self-correction after generating “Wait,” re-evaluating the costs. The negatively steered model accepts the erroneous premise and justifies the incorrect result.
