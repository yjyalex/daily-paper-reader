# Beyond Memorization: Distinguishing between Reductive and Epistemic Reasoning in LLMs using Classic Logic Puzzles 

Adi Gabay, Gabriel Stanovsky, Liat Peterfreund 

School of Computer Science and Engineering The Hebrew University of Jerusalem adi.gabay1@mail.huji.ac.il 

Abstract 

Epistemic reasoning requires agents to infer the state of the world from partial observations and information about other agents’ knowl-edge. Prior work evaluating LLMs on canoni-cal epistemic puzzles interpreted their behavior through a dichotomy between epistemic reason-ing and brittle memorization. We argue that this framing is incomplete: in recent models, mem-orization is better understood as a special case of reduction , where a new instance is mapped onto a known problem. Instead, we introduce a 

reduction ladder , a sequence of modifications that progressively move instances away from a canonical epistemic puzzle, making reduction increasingly difficult while preserving the un-derlying logic. We find that while some large models succeed via reduction, other models fail early, and all models struggle once epistemic reasoning is required. 

1 Introduction 

Problems requiring epistemic reasoning in multi-agent systems involve inferring the state of the world by integrating one’s own observations with information about other agents’ knowledge (Fagin et al., 1995). Such challenges arise naturally in col-laborative settings where each agent has only a par-tial view of the world, such as legal proceedings, ed-ucation, or medical applications (Rabinowitz et al., 2018; Li et al., 2023). Previous work evaluating LLMs’ epistemic rea-soning has centered on canonical problems (e.g., the Muddy Children problem), with the goal of dis-tinguishing between two possible solution strate-gies: epistemic reasoning and memorization . In the former, models reason over the specific parameters of the instance to derive a solution; in the latter, they match the task to a familiar pattern seen dur-ing pretraining and reproduce the corresponding answer. Prior findings suggest that earlier models often over-rely on memorization, which frequently

In this work, we argue that recent advances in LLMs warrant a more nuanced treatment of memorization, viewing it as a special case of a broader and often effective strategy also employed by humans: reduction between problems (Gentner, 1983). Given an instance x of a problem A, a re-duction is a function f that maps x to an instance 

f (x) of another problem B. In contrast to epis-temic reasoning, the central challenge in reduction is not reasoning over the specific parameters of the instance itself, but identifying the appropriate tar-get problem B and the correspondence between the elements of the two problems. Under this view, memorization is the case in which f (x) = x.We therefore ask: Can reduction and epistemic reasoning be distinguished in LLMs, and can per-formance under each be quantified? We evaluate various LLMs and find that some large models can solve most of these problems by reduction, whereas other models fail at earlier steps. Once reduction ceases to be effective, all models struggle to solve problems through epis-temic reasoning. Beyond the benchmark itself, this distinction provides an interpretable lens on model behavior, helping reveal not only how well models perform, but also how they succeed or fail. Our contributions are threefold: (1) we reinter-pret “memorization” as a form of reduction ; (2) we introduce a reduction ladder to separate reduc-tion from epistemic reasoning; and (3) we show that while some large LLMs solve many variants through reduction, all models struggle when epis-temic reasoning is required. 

2 Background: Dynamic Epistemic Logic and the Muddy Children Puzzle 

Dynamic Epistemic Logic (DEL) is a formal frame-work for multi-agents systems, representing what they know, which possibilities they consider, and how this changes when new information is revealed. In this framework, agents are the entities whose knowledge is being modeled, and public announce-ments rule out possibilities that are inconsistent with newly available information, unfolding across multiple rounds of communication (Baltag et al., 1998; Van Ditmarsch et al., 2008). We focus on the Muddy Children puzzle, a sim-ple setting that captures many central ideas of DEL, illustrated for two children in Figure 2. Each child can see the other’s forehead but not their own, so their uncertainty is represented by the possible worlds in the thought bubbles. In panel 2A, both remain uncertain about themselves; the father’s an-nouncement that at least one child is muddy rules out the all-clean world. This lets child 1 infer that she is muddy, and after she announces this in panel 2B, child 0 can further narrow the possi-bilities and reach his own conclusion in panel 2C. 

3 Distinguishing Between Reductive and Epistemic Reasoning 

We aim to distinguish between two ways in which LLMs may determine the correct response of an agent in DEL-style settings: reductive reasoning 

and epistemic reasoning . Under reductive reason-ing, the model identifies the current epistemic state as a variant of a familiar problem and applies the corresponding response pattern; for instance, if it encountered the same puzzle during pretraining, it may retrieve the answer from parametric knowl-edge. Under epistemic reasoning, by contrast, the model derives the agent’s response directly from the observations, public announcements, and prior answers in the interaction rounds. To distinguish between reductive and epistemic reasoning in LLMs, we construct a "reduction lad-der", illustrated in Figure 1, and elaborated below. The first rung in the ladder is seeded with a well-known epistemic problem, which LLMs have likely encountered many times in their pretraining data. Each successive rung aims to move away from the previous one in terms of problem structure, while keeping the underlying logic. Therefore, if a model solves a problem via epistemic reasoning, its per-formance should not vary along the ladder. If, on the other hand, a model relies on reduction to a known problem, its performance may degrade the further we move from the base rung. 

3.1 Task Definition 

To evaluate LLMs’ epistemic reasoning, we place the LLM in the role of an agent within a simulated world (e.g., a child in the muddy children puzzle). The model’s goal is to infer its own status by rea-soning over the information provided in its prompt. 

Model Input. The model receives a prompt con-taining the public announcement, observations of the other agents, and the interaction history con-sisting of all answers provided by agents up to the particular round in question. 

Model Output. The model is asked a question regarding its own status (e.g., "Is your forehead muddy?"). It must respond with exactly one of 

"Yes" , "No" , or "I don’t know" . For any given round, exactly one response is correct based on the agent’s current knowledge. To verify the model’s response, we develop a symbolic solver grounded in Kripke possible worlds semantics (Kripke, 1959). 

3.2 The Reduction Ladder 

Our ladder consists of three rungs of problems, each moving away from the base problem. 

Rung I: Seed problem. The first rung uses the classic Muddy Children problem. Our assumption is that LLMs have seen this puzzle many times during pretraining and hence will likely default to using a memorized solution, rather than solving it as a novel problem, via epistemic reasoning. 

Rung II: Different settings which conflict with world knowledge. The goal of the second rung is to preserve the underlying epistemic reasoning, while moving away from the familiar setup of the 

Muddy Children puzzle. Specifically, We recast the puzzle in an Olympic Games setting: children are replaced by gymnasts, and muddy versus clean is replaced by qualified versus not qualified for the Olympic final. 5 Conclusion 

We revisited the evaluation of LLMs on epistemic puzzles, arguing that the contrast between epis-temic reasoning and memorization is too coarse. We showed that strong performance seems to come from reducing to familiar problem structures rather than performing epistemic reasoning.

6 Limitations 

A significant challenge lies in interpreting the in-ternal mechanisms of LLMs. While we utilize Chain-of-Thought (CoT) to distinguish between re-ductive and epistemic reasoning, there may exist a disconnect between the model’s verbalized reason-ing and its actual computation; it is possible that LLMs perform reduction while mentioning terms that suggest epistemic reasoning in their CoT, and vice versa. This indication is in line with recent work that addresses the disconnect between CoT and the internal computation of the model (Levy et al., 2025b; Pfau et al., 2024; Pal et al., 2026; Levy et al., 2025a).
