ABSTRACT 

On-policy reinforcement learning with generative policies is promising but remains underexplored. A central challenge is that proximal policy optimization (PPO) is traditionally formulated in terms of action-space probability ratios, whereas diffusion- and flow-based policies are more naturally represented as trajectory-level generative processes. In this work, we propose GSB-PPO , a path-space formulation of generative PPO inspired by the Generalized Schrödinger Bridge (GSB). Our framework lifts PPO-style proximal updates from terminal actions to full generation trajectories, yielding a unified view of on-policy optimization for generative policies. Within this framework, we develop two concrete objectives: a clipping-based objective, GSB-PPO-Clip , and a penalty-based objective, GSB-PPO-Penalty . Experimental results show that while both objectives are compatible with on-policy training, the penalty formulation consistently delivers better stability and performance than the clipping counterpart. Overall, our results highlight path-space proximal regularization as an effective principle for training generative policies with PPO. 

Keywords Generative Policy, Proximal Policy Optimization, Generalized Schrödinger Bridge 

1 Introduction 

Deep reinforcement learning has achieved remarkable success in continuous control, where policy optimization methods such as TRPO and PPO provide effective and stable on policy updates Schulman et al. [2015a, 2017, 2015b]. In most of these methods, however, the policy is parameterized by a relatively simple action distribution, such as a Gaussian actor. While this choice is computationally convenient, it may be restrictive when the optimal behavior is inherently multimodal or requires a more expressive action distribution. Recent advances in generative modeling provide a promising alternative. Diffusion models and flow based generators can represent complex high dimensional distributions far beyond the capacity of standard Gaussian policies Ho et al. [2020], Song et al. [2021], Lipman et al. [2023], Geng et al. [2025]. Motivated by this expressive power, generative policies have recently been introduced into reinforcement learning, especially in offline and off policy settings Janner et al. [2022], Chi et al. [2023], Wang et al. [2022], Hansen-Estruch et al. [2023], Park et al. [2025]. More recently, several works have started to explore online or on policy training with generative policies, including DIME, FLAC, DPPO, GenPO, and FPO Celik et al. [2025], Lv et al. [2026], Ren et al. [2024], Ding et al. [2025], McAllister et al.

These studies suggest that expressive generative policies can be beneficial beyond offline reinforcement learning, but they also expose a key challenge: proximal policy optimization becomes much less straightforward when the policy is represented by a multi step stochastic generation process rather than an explicit one step action density. This challenge is particularly relevant for PPO. Standard PPO is formulated in action space through the ratio between new and old policy densities, together with either clipping or an explicit proximal penalty Schulman et al. [2017]. For generative policies, however, the executed action is only the terminal output of a denoising or flow trajectory, while the policy itself is more naturally defined over the full generation path. As a result, directly applying PPO in action space obscures the trajectory level structure of the generator and raises the question of how proximal updates should be defined for generative policies. In this paper, we revisit PPO for generative policies from a path space perspective inspired by the Generalized Schrödinger Bridge (GSB). GSB treats stochastic generation as an optimization problem over trajectory distributions rather than only terminal marginals Léonard [2013], Liu et al. [2024]. This viewpoint is particularly appealing for generative policies, whose action distributions are induced by multi step denoising trajectories. Building on this observation, we formulate a path space version of PPO in which the policy is represented by a trajectory distribution and proximal updates are performed directly in path space. Within this framework, we instantiate two concrete variants: a clipping based objective, GSB-PPO-Clip , and a penalty based objective, GSB-PPO-Penalty . Our formulation is 

inspired by the GSB perspective, but it is not a strict GSB objective: in particular, the penalty variant remains a PPO style proximal objective rather than an exact bridge matching formulation. Our central empirical finding is that these two path space instantiations behave very differently. While the clipping objective is the most direct extension of standard PPO, it is sensitive to the accumulation of likelihood shifts across denoising steps and performs poorly in our setting. By contrast, the penalty objective provides a smoother path space update and yields substantially better stability and performance. This suggests that, for on policy optimization of generative policies, trajectory level penalty regularization is a more suitable inductive bias than direct clipping. Our contributions are summarized as follows: • We propose a path space formulation of PPO for generative policies, motivated by the GSB view that a generative policy is naturally represented as a trajectory distribution. • We instantiate this framework with two concrete objectives, GSB-PPO-Clip and GSB-PPO-Penalty , which extend PPO style clipping and proximal regularization from action space to path space. • We empirically show that the penalty formulation is substantially more effective than the clipping formulation, providing a simple and practical design insight for on policy generative reinforcement learning. 

2 Related Work 

2.1 On Policy Reinforcement Learning 

On policy reinforcement learning has been a central paradigm for policy optimization since the early policy gradient literature Williams [1992]. To improve the stability and sample efficiency of policy gradient methods, trust region and proximal update schemes have been widely adopted, leading to representative algorithms such as TRPO and PPO Schulman et al. [2015a, 2017]. In practice, generalized advantage estimation (GAE) is often used together with PPO to reduce gradient variance and improve training stability Schulman et al. [2015b]. Our work follows this line of research and focuses on extending PPO style proximal updates to generative policies whose action distributions are induced by multi step stochastic generation trajectories. 

2.2 Generative Policies in Reinforcement Learning 

Generative modeling provides a flexible way to represent complex and multimodal action distributions. Early generative models, including variational autoencoders, generative adversarial networks, and normalizing flows, established powerful approaches for expressive density modeling Kingma and Welling [2013], Goodfellow et al. [2014], Dinh et al. [2014, 2016], Kingma and Dhariwal [2018]. More recently, diffusion and score based generative models Hyvärinen [2005], Ho et al. [2020], Song et al. [2020, 2021] as well as flow based approaches such as flow matching and MeanFlow Lipman et al. [2023], Geng et al. [2025] have significantly improved generation quality and efficiency. These advances make diffusion and flow matching natural candidates for policy parameterization in reinforcement learning, where expressive action distributions can be important for handling complex continuous control problems. Motivated by this trend, a growing body of work has incorporated generative models into reinforcement learning. Many early efforts focused on planning, behavior synthesis, or offline reinforcement learning, including Diffuser, Diffusion 2Proximal Policy Optimization in Path Space: A Schrödinger Bridge Perspective A P REPRINT 

Policy, diffusion based policy representation learning, Diffusion-QL, CEP, IDQL, EDP, Q-score matching, QVPO, DACER, FQL and MeanFlowQL Janner et al. [2022], Chi et al. [2023], Yang et al. [2023], Wang et al. [2022], Lu et al. [2023], Hansen-Estruch et al. [2023], Kang et al. [2023], Psenka et al. [2023], Ding et al. [2024], Wang et al. [2024], Park et al. [2025], Wang et al. [2026]. These methods demonstrate that diffusion or flow based policies can substantially enrich the policy class beyond standard Gaussian actors. More recently, generative policies have also been studied in online reinforcement learning. DIME introduces diffusion policies into maximum entropy RL, while FLAC formulates maximum entropy RL through a bridge matching perspective with kinetic energy regularization Celik et al. [2025], Lv et al. [2026]. Compared with these methods, our work is not built on the maximum entropy actor critic formulation; instead, we focus on PPO style on policy optimization and study how proximal objectives should be lifted from action space to path space. 

2.3 On-Policy Generative Reinforcement Learning 

Only recently have generative policies been brought into explicitly on policy optimization. DPPO studies PPO style optimization for diffusion policies, while SDAC develops an online actor critic framework for diffusion policies Ren et al. [2024], Ma et al. [2025]. GenPO takes a major step toward on policy reinforcement learning with diffusion based generative policies, and FPO extends this direction to flow matching based policy gradients Ding et al. [2025], McAllister et al. [2025]. These works show that generative policies can be trained in online and on policy settings, but they also highlight the difficulty of defining stable proximal updates when the policy is represented by a multi step generation process rather than an explicit one step action density. Our work is most closely related to GenPO and FPO, but differs from them in both perspective and emphasis. Rather than deriving a specialized on policy objective for a particular generative parameterization, we adopt a GSB inspired path space viewpoint and revisit PPO itself for generative policies. This leads to a unified path space formulation that naturally yields both a clipping based objective and a penalty based objective. Empirically, we show that in this setting the penalty formulation is substantially more effective than the clipping counterpart, which provides a concrete design insight for on policy generative reinforcement learning.

5 Experiments 

5.1 Experimental Setup 

Our implementation is built on top of the FPO playground codebase, which follows the MuJoCo Playground style JAX training framework for online continuous-control reinforcement learning. We keep the overall rollout, actor-critic optimization, and evaluation pipeline aligned with the original FPO/playground implementation, and modify only 6Proximal Policy Optimization in Path Space: A Schrödinger Bridge Perspective A P REPRINT 

the actor objective to instantiate the proposed GSB-PPO variants. In this way, our experiments isolate the effect of path-space proximal design while minimizing unrelated implementation differences. We evaluate our method on ten playground environments. Our main comparison includes standard PPO, FPO, GSB-PPO-Clip, and GSB-PPO-Penalty. Among them, GSB-PPO-Clip and GSB-PPO-Penalty share the same generative policy parameterization and differ only in the actor objective: the clipping variant uses the path-space PPO-style clipping objective in Eq. (20), while the penalty variant uses the quadratic MSE-style path regularization in Eq. (23). For numerical stability, our implementation applies an additional step-level clipping to the per-step log-ratio before forming the full path ratio. This operation is introduced only to control the numerical range of accumulated trajectory likelihood shifts, and is fundamentally different from the PPO clipping threshold ϵ in Eq. (20) . The former is a numerical stabilization mechanism, while the latter defines the outer proximal surrogate. Detailed implementation settings and hyperparameters are provided in Appendix A. 

5.2 Main Results on 10 Playground Environments 

Our main goal is to answer two questions: (i) whether the proposed GSB inspired path space PPO improves over standard action space PPO, and (ii) how it compares with an existing on policy generative baseline based on flow matching. To this end, we present two sets of comparisons on the same ten playground environments. Figure 1 compares GSB-PPO against standard PPO. Across the tested environments, the generative path space formulation consistently improves training quality over the standard PPO baseline. This result suggests that, for these control tasks, representing the policy as a multi step generator can be beneficial even in fully on policy training. Compared with standard action space PPO, the generative formulation is able to model richer action distributions and yields stronger returns in several environments. Figure 2 compares our method with FPO, which is the closest existing on policy baseline in our implementation framework. Among the two proposed variants, GSB-PPO-Penalty is the primary method and delivers the strongest overall performance. By contrast, GSB-PPO-Clip is generally less stable and often underperforms its penalty counterpart. Taken together, these results support the central claim of this paper: when PPO is lifted from action space to generative path space, a smooth trajectory level penalty is substantially more effective than directly clipping the path ratio. We stress that the purpose of this experiment is not to provide an exhaustive benchmark against all existing generative RL methods. Instead, our focus is to isolate the effect of proximal objective design in a controlled on policy generative setting. 6 Conclusion 

We presented GSB-PPO, a path space formulation of PPO for generative policies inspired by the Generalized Schrödinger Bridge perspective. Within this framework, we studied two practical objectives, GSB-PPO-Clip and GSB-PPO-Penalty, and showed that they lead to markedly different optimization behavior. Across ten playground environments, the penalty formulation consistently provided better stability and stronger performance than the clipping counterpart. These results suggest that for on policy generative reinforcement learning, the key challenge is not merely to transplant PPO into a generative setting, but to design proximal objectives that better respect the multi step stochastic structure of the policy. Our findings indicate that smooth path space regularization is a promising direction for this purpose. Future work will explore larger scale evaluations, alternative path space divergences, and tighter connections to bridge inspired and entropy regularized formulations. 9Proximal Policy Optimization in Path Space: A Schrödinger Bridge Perspective A P REPRINT
