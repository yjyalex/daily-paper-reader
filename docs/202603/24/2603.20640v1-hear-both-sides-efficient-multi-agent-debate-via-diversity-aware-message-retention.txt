# Hear Both Sides: Efficient Multi-Agent Debate via Diversity-Aware Message Retention 

Manh Nguyen, Anh Nguyen, Dung Nguyen, Svetha Venkatesh, and Hung Le 

Applied Artificial Intelligence Initiative Deakin University, Australia 

{manh.nguyen, tien.nguyen, dung.nguyen, svetha.venkatesh, thai.le }@deakin.edu.au 

Abstract 

Multi-Agent Debate has emerged as a promising framework for improving the reasoning quality of large language models through iterative inter-agent communication. However, broadcasting all agent messages at every round introduces noise and redundancy that can degrade debate quality and waste computational resources. Current approaches rely on uncertainty estimation to filter low-confidence responses before broadcasting, but this approach is unreliable due to miscalibrated confidence scores and sensitivity to threshold selection. To address this, we propose Diversity-Aware Retention (DAR) , a lightweight debate framework that, at each debate round, selects the subset of agent responses that maximally disagree with each other and with the majority vote before broadcasting. Through an explicit index-based retention mechanism, DAR preserves the original messages without modification, ensuring that retained disagreements remain authentic. Experiments on diverse reasoning and question answering benchmarks demonstrate that our selective message propagation consistently improves debate performance, particularly as the number of agents scales, where noise accumulation is most severe. Our results highlight that what agents hear is as important as what agents say in multi-agent reasoning systems. 

1 Introduction 

Large-Language-Model-based Multi-Agent Systems (LLM-based MAS) have recently attracted increasing attention thanks to their ability to leverage the power of LLMs in modeling and coordinating multiple agents, enabling complex problem solving and distributed decision-making (Tran et al., 2025; Yan et al., 2025). Within this paradigm, Multi-Agent Debate (MAD) has emerged as a main direction for improving the performance of LLM-based MAS. By enabling multiple language model agents to iteratively exchange and critique each other’s responses, MAD has demonstrated consistent gains over single agents across a range of reasoning and question answering benchmarks (Chan et al., 2023; Du et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2024). Despite its promise, MAD faces a fundamental scalability challenge: as the number of agents and debate rounds grows, each agent is required to process an ever-expanding context consisting of all peer responses from the previous round. This broadcasting of all messages introduces two compounding problems. First, redundant responses, where multiple agents converge on the same answer through similar reasoning paths, provide little additional signal while consuming significant token capacity. Second, noisy or low-quality generations can actively mislead other agents, propagating errors across rounds rather than correcting them. These effects reduce the practical benefits of scaling debate and raise important questions about MAD (Choi et al., 2025), particularly regarding what information agents should be provided. Prior work has sought to address message quality through uncertainty-based filtering, discarding responses whose confidence scores fall below a chosen threshold before broadcasting (Lin & Hooi, 2025; Tian et al., 2026). While intuitive, this approach has significant limitations in practice: confidence estimates from LLMs are often poorly calibrated (Kadavath et al., 2022; Xiong et al., 2024), and performance is highly sensitive to the choice of threshold, requiring costly tuning that may not generalize across models or tasks. Critically, filtering by confidence alone ignores a key property of productive debate: disagreement. A high-confidence response that merely echoes the majority view contributes far less to collective reasoning than a lower-confidence response that suggests an alternative perspective. We argue that capturing disagreement among agent responses is central to effective debate, as diverse reasoning paths are more informative than redundant ones. Retention (DAR) , a MAD framework that, at each debate round, selects the subset of agent responses that maximally disagree with one another and with the majority vote (Figure 1). Concretely, DAR augments the standard debate protocol with three lightweight components. First , each agent’s prompt is augmented with an uncertainty score for every peer response, computed as the average negative log-likelihood over the answer tokens, allowing agents to weight peer responses according to their reliability. By introducing the uncertainty score into the prompt as additional information for agent’s generation, we avoid the challenge of tuning the confident threshold. Second , DAR prepends the majority vote from the previous round to each agent’s prompt, providing an explicit consensus anchor across debating rounds, which will be utilized in the next step. Third and most importantly, we introduce a filter agent that receives all peer responses and the prepended majority vote, then outputs a set of agent indices identifying the most mutually disagreeing responses; the original messages at those indices are retrieved and broadcast unchanged. This index-based selection guarantees that the retained disagreements are original, enabling a controlled and interpretable intervention in the debate process. We conduct extensive experiments on six diverse reasoning and question answering benchmarks across four LLM backbones ranging from 1.5B to 8B. Our results demonstrates that DAR consistently improves debate performance, particularly as the number of agents scales, while reducing the computational cost of message broadcasting. 

2 Related Work 

Multi-Agent Debate MAD was first introduced by Du et al. (2024), improving response quality through simulated debates among multiple LLM agents. Extensions include assigning diverse roles (Liang et al., 2024; Chan et al., 2023), introducing mechanisms such as diversity pruning and misunderstanding refutation (Es-tornell & Liu, 2024), and designing more effective communication protocols (Li et al., 2024; Pham et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023; 2024). Recent work has explored richer signals to improve debate dynamics, such as 2incorporating explicit confidence expression to guide interactions and mitigate premature convergence (Lin & Hooi, 2025). In parallel, ensemble-style multi-agent frameworks like Tool-Use Mixture (TUMIX) leverage diverse tool-use strategies with iterative refinement across agents, emphasizing the role of diversity and coordination in improving reasoning performance (Chen et al., 2026). Despite these advances, MAD is sensitive to hyperparameters and does not always outperform strong single-agent reasoning (Smit et al., 2024). Debates may converge prematurely, and simple majority voting can achieve similar results (Estornell & Liu, 2024; Choi et al., 2025), suggesting that improvements often come from candidate generation rather than debate itself. Recent work addresses this by filtering information between rounds, for example, subjective masking uses an LLM agent to self-evaluate each response, while objective masking discards low-confidence re-sponses (Tian et al., 2026). Another approach increase candidate diversity through more aggressive sampling, albeit at higher computational cost (Zhu et al., 2026). In contrast, our method does not rely on per-sample 

judgments or expensive sampling. The proposed filtering module retains responses that disagree with the majority vote, while ensuring that the selected subset preserves diverse viewpoints. This enhances MAD performance by preserving informative diversity in a simple and computationally efficient manner. 

Uncertainty and Diversity in Multi-Agent LLM Systems Uncertainty estimation has been widely studied as a proxy for reliability in LLMs. Prior work measures uncertainty using token-level probabilities (Guerreiro et al., 2023; Manakul et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2026), verbalized confidence (Lin et al., 2022), or semantic entropy (Kuhn et al., 2023). More recent approaches extend this to multi-step settings, such as propagating uncertainty across reasoning steps to better capture cumulative errors in agent-based systems (Zhao et al., 2025). In multi-agent debate, incorporating confidence signals has been shown to improve aggregation and reduce premature convergence (Lin & Hooi, 2025). Orthogonally, diversity has been identified as a key factor in improving reasoning performance. Prior work shows that gains in multi-agent systems often arise from diverse candidate generation rather than interaction itself (Smit et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2025). Methods such as self-consistency, prompt diversification, and multi-prompt ensembles explicitly encourage multiple reasoning paths via parallel prompting, leading to more robust aggregation (Wang et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2025). These observations suggest a close connection between uncertainty and diversity, where uncertainty can help identify informative or complementary candidates. However, existing approaches typically rely on explicit uncertainty calibration or increased sampling cost, motivating lightweight alternatives that leverage implicit signals, such as disagreement, to preserve informative diversity without explicit uncertainty modeling.

3.4 Improving MAD by Promoting Diversity 

While MAD benefits from aggregating multiple candidate responses, not all generations are equally useful. LLM outputs are known to be sensitive to prompt variations and often contain redundant or low-quality content (Zhao et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2022). As the number of agents grows, such noise accumulates and can hinder the effectiveness of subsequent debate rounds. At the same time, prior work has shown that diversity in prompts or reasoning paths can improve answer quality, as different prompts encourage the model to explore alternative reasoning trajectories and reduce correlated errors (Wang et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2025). Inspired by these findings, we aim to preserve informative diversity among agent responses while removing redundant generations during debate. Our goal is to retain a compact subset of responses that preserves the diversity of opinions among agents. Let Sr ⊆ Gr−1 denote the subset retained for round r. Retaining Criterion To approximate this objective, we introduce a lightweight filtering module F that selects a subset of informative responses before each round (Figure 1). The module is implemented using the same backbone LLM as the generation agents, making it training-free and directly compatible with existing MAD pipelines. In contrast to prior extensions that redesign agent roles or modify communication protocols, our method operates purely at the generation selection stage and requires no architectural changes or additional parameters. Specifically, the filter agent F receives the N responses from round r−1 and the last vote. Its objective is to select a subset of agent indices (IDs) whose responses will be retained for the next round. Rather than selecting responses based on correctness or confidence, F is prompted to retain agents whose opinions differ the most from each other and from the majority vote. This encourages the debate to preserve informative disagreement while removing redundant generations. Fallback behavior If no meaningful disagreement exists, the system falls back to standard debate without filtering. For example, when all agents produce equivalent answers, the filter retains all responses, as no additional diversity can be introduced. See Section 4.3.1 for further analysis. 

Illustrative Case Study To understand how this mechanism handles noisy or redundant responses, we consider a case where the responses are {A, A, B, C}, with A being the majority vote and A, B, C being unique answers. Each agent observes only the previous-round responses from other agents, and redundant majority responses are filtered to preserve informative diversity. For simplicity, we illustrate a hard filtering strategy that removes repeated majority opinions (DAR w/o LLM-as-a-judge, Section 4.3.1). An agent with answer A observes {A, B, C}, where filtering highlights the alternative paths {B, C} while retaining A for comparison. An agent with answer B observes {A, A, C}, which after filtering emphasizes 

{B} alongside its own response, enabling evaluation of the minority opinion. Similarly, an agent with answer 

C observes {A, A, B}, where filtering reduces redundancy and exposes competing reasoning through {B}.Overall, the filter prioritizes disagreement: redundant repetitions are removed to maintain informative diver-sity, while the majority answer A is preserved in the original agents and remains available for comparison even when incorrect. 

4 Experiment 

4.1 Experiment Setup Models We evaluate on five popular instruction-tuned open-weight models from distinct families and different sizes: Qwen2.5-1.5B, 3B (Yang et al., 2025), Falcon3-7B (Almazrouei et al., 2023), and Llama3.1-8B (Grattafiori et al., 2024). 

Benchmarks Following the evaluation procedures of prior work (Choi et al., 2025), we conduct exten-sive evaluations across five benchmarks: (1) Arithmetics , (2) Mathematical Reasoning: Grade School Math 8k ( GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021)), (3) Alignment Labeling: HH-RLHF (Bai et al., 2022), (4) Factual Ques-tion Answering: MMLU Professional Medicine ( Pro.Med. (Hendrycks et al., 2020b)) and Formal Logics (Form.Log. (Hendrycks et al., 2020a)), and (5) Commonsense Reasoning: CSQA (Talmor et al., 2019). 

Baselines We compare the following representative approaches: (1) Society Of Mind (Du et al., 2024), representing base MAD performance without any prompt modifications or filtering; (2) MAD-M 2(Tian et al., 2026), which filters the top 50% most confident generations based on their perplexities; (3) Uncertain Prompt (Lin & Hooi, 2025), incorporating uncertainty scores (Section 3.2) into other agents’ generations; (4) 

Majority Vote (Choi et al., 2025), selecting the final answer by aggregating initial responses from multiple agents without debate; (5) Vote Prompt , performing a ”soft” majority vote via prompt as discussed in Section 3.3; and (6) Diversity-Aware Retention (DAR ), which maintains diversity by retaining only the most useful responses.

4.3.1 Effect Of Retaining Criteria 

Table 3 analyzes the contribution of each DAR component. Removing Uncertain or Vote Prompts causes a slight drop, indicating they guide more effective agent interactions. In contrast, LLM-based filtering performs soft reasoning-aware selection, retaining responses with different reasoning paths while discarding redundant or weak answers, thereby preserving informative diversity. Alternative criteria such as retaining only certain or similar answers 4 yield moderate gains over Society Of Mind but limit diversity, preventing consistent improvement over full DAR. 5 Conclusion 

We proposed Diversity-Aware Retention (DAR) , a lightweight multi-agent debate framework that improves reasoning by selectively propagating mutually disagreeing responses. By preserving informative diversity via an index-based mechanism, DAR reduces noise and redundancy without relying on uncertain responses. Experiments across benchmarks and models show consistent gains, especially as the number of agents increases, highlighting the importance of controlling information flow in scalable multi-agent reasoning.
