# Abstract 

The generative artificial intelligence (AI) ecosystem is undergoing rapid trans-formations that threaten its sustainability. As models transition from research prototypes to high-traffic products, the dominant energetic burden has shifted from one-time training to recurring, unbounded inference—a phenomenon exacerbated by the emergence of “reasoning models” that inflate compute costs by orders of magnitude per query. The prevailing trajectory, i.e, pursuit of artificial general intelligence through continued scaling of monolithic models, is colliding with hard physical constraints: localized grid failures, prohibitive water consumption, and diminishing returns on data scaling. Whereas this trajectory yields models with impressive factual recall, it struggles in domains that require in-depth verifiable reasoning. A possible cause is a lack of sufficient abstractions in the underlying training data. Current large language models (LLMs) exhibit genuine reasoning depth only in domains like mathematics and coding, where rigorous, pre-existing abstractions provide structural grounding. In other fields that lack such abstractions, the current approach fails to generalize well. We propose an alternative trajectory based on 

domain-specific superintelligence (DSS) . We argue that to achieve robust reasoning in open-world domains, it is advantageous to first construct explicit symbolic abstractions, such as knowledge graphs, ontologies, and formal logic. These abstractions can form the basis for generation of synthetic curricula, training on which can enable small language models to master complex domain-specific reasoning, without encountering the model collapse problem that is typical of current synthetic data methods that use an LLM to supplement training data for next-generation LLMs. Rather than training a single generalist giant model, we envision “societies of DSS models”: dynamic ecosystems where orchestration agents route tasks to distinct DSS back-ends. This paradigm shift can help decouple capability from size, enabling intelligence to migrate from energy-intensive data centers to secure, on-device experts. By aligning algorithmic progress with physical constraints, DSS societies can help move generative AI away from an environmental liability to a sustainable force for economic empowerment. 

# 1 Introduction 

Currently, the dominant paradigm in the generative artificial intelligence (AI) industry is a “top-down” approach: pursuit of artificial general intelligence (AGI) through massive scaling of generalist

large language models (LLMs). The prevailing belief is that by training ever-larger models on the entirety of Internet-scale data, we will eventually converge towards an AI system capable of human-level reasoning across all domains. This vision relies heavily on scaling laws, which suggest that increasing model size and data automatically yield proportional gains in intelligence [Kaplan et al., 2020]. However, this “bigger-is-better” trajectory incurs ever-increasing costs. Empirical scaling laws document predictable gains from larger models and datasets but do not instill deeper forms of understanding or reliable reasoning on complex, compositional tasks in LLMs. In addition, the current trajectory faces various headwinds in terms of training and inference energy costs, data quality, and, most critically, depth of reasoning. More concretely, three interrelated problems make the current top-down paradigm that the AI industry is following a risky foundation to build future AI systems. First, both training and deploying large foundation models demand vast compute and energy resources. Modern deep learning (DL) workflows entail substantial financial and environmental costs, which scale quickly as models grow in size. The need for massive computational resources restricts AI development to a few well-funded entities. These costs create practical, economic, and sustainability constraints [Strubell et al., 2019]. Second, whereas large models often excel at surface-level pattern matching and retrieval, they still struggle with robust, compositional reasoning and practical problem solving. Techniques like chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting have been shown to unlock stronger reasoning capabilities in language models, but largely rely on scale and special prompting rather than on principled, abstraction-driven reasoning mechanisms. Recent studies show suboptimal performance of LLMs on tasks that require combining small components into larger, novel solutions [Dedhia et al., 2025, Kansal and Jha, 2026, Zhao and Zhang, 2024]. These studies suggest that mere scaling is not likely to produce the kinds of structured reasoning needed for many real-world domains [Wei et al., 2022]. Third, data quality matters. The Internet-scale text corpora used to train generalist models are large but highly unstructured and heterogeneous in quality and coverage. Good reasoning and trustworthy behavior depend on high-quality well-curated training data and on targeted curricula that teach models how to use abstractions. The trajectory of intelligent reasoning, one can argue, is predicated on a specific sequence: abstraction followed by generalization. In this view, the first step is to form structured mental models (abstractions) of the world, understand rules, relationships, and causalities, and then generalize these models to new, unseen situations. This data-centric view of AI argues that improving data quality, curation, and structure often yields larger gains than continually increasing model size alone [Jakubik et al., 2024, Halevy et al., 2009]. Current LLM training approaches either invert or bypass this step altogether. They attempt to achieve generalization purely through the statistical breadth of knowledge acquisition. This results in the well-documented brittleness of LLMs [Haller et al., 2025, Su et al., 2025, Mohsin et al., 2025]. To move forward, it may be advantageous to restore abstraction as the prerequisite for generalization. We propose an alternative trajectory for generative AI: a “bottom-up” approach centered on domain-specific superintelligence (DSS). By superintelligence, we do mean super-human, but qualified by its adjective: domain-specific. The AI industry defines superintelligence as being post-AGI since it is pursuing generalist superintelligence across all domains, rather than in specific domains. However, omniscience in a single LLM monolith may be an overrated attribute. We base our proposed trajectory on DSS because it is practically achievable and also because DSS societies can enable significant success in applications that require inter-domain expertise. Thus, rather than relying on a single generalist LLM monolith, we envision a future built on specialized small language models (SLMs): smaller, focused models trained on high-quality domain data, paired with explicit abstractions [e.g., knowledge graphs (KGs), formal semantics, inductive program libraries] and modular reasoning engines. This vision of a family of specialist models is highly scalable and mirrors how human societies are organized: Individuals acquire expertise in narrow domains and cooperate to solve complex problems that require inter-domain expertise. This idea has deep roots in cognitive science: Marvin Minsky’s Society of Mind framed intelligence as the coordinated effort of many smaller agents, and work on distributed cognition highlights how cognitive capacity emerges from interactions among people, artifacts, and institutions [Minsky, 1988, Hutchins, 1995]. In the field of AI, past successes buttress this idea; highly specialized systems trained for narrow, hard scientific problems have outperformed generalist approaches within their domain, suggesting that specialized 2architectures and curated data can achieve superhuman depth without extreme scale [Trinh et al., 2024b, Jumper et al., 2021, Lin et al., 2025]. Our bottom-up program combines several elements highlighted above. First, careful data engineering and grounded synthetic curricula emphasize quality over quantity. Second, explicit abstractions, such as symbolic representations, program libraries, and KGs, provide training ingredients that help models learn compositional rules that deepen their reasoning. Third, modular societies of small specialist models can enable interoperation: a front-end SLM trained more broadly in a domain can divide a user query into sub-queries, dispatch them to specialist DSS models trained more narrowly in that domain, and integrate their responses [Ellis et al., 2021]. This lego-like design permits efficient training and easier validation, and promises a scalable, efficient, and more interpretable alternative to monolithic generalists. This article outlines how this alternative trajectory can not only address the reasoning gap between specialist and generalist models by putting abstraction back at the center of learning but also does so using vastly smaller training and inference energy footprints. By moving inference to the edge (e.g., running efficient SLMs on smartphones), this trajectory can democratize access to DSS, enhancing the productivity of every worker, from technicians to physicians. In this article, we address the following: 1. We discuss the limitations of the current scale-driven LLM training paradigm in terms of reasoning depth, data quality, and sustainability, citing empirical and theoretical evidence. 2. We articulate concrete alternative architectures centered on DSS: how to construct them, how to generate targeted synthetic curricula from abstractions, and how to assemble DSS societies for complex tasks. 3. We propose evaluation protocols and research directions, including continual learning for agents, energy and deployment analyses, and case studies in medicine, engineering, and education that demonstrate how our proposed trajectory is testable and actionable. In the following sections, we first present a brief timeline of AGI and motivate our vision (Section 2). We then discuss the principles of good data (Section 3), abstractions (Section 4), reasoning (Section 5), and modularity needed for DSS (Sections 7 and 8). Other sections show how synthetic data and neurosymbolic components can bootstrap the AI vision (Section 6) and how agent architectures enable continual learning and real-world use (Section 9). Finally, we document the empirical limits of scaling and the energy footprint of large models, and discuss broader implications for deployment, sustainability, and the social benefits of making expert-level AI available across many domains (Sections 10 and 11). Altogether, these sections build a roadmap for an alternative trajectory for generative AI, one that aims for depth over breadth, quality over quantity, and modular, sustainable intelligence over ever-larger generalist monoliths. 

# 2 AGI as a vision: Is it worth pursuing now? 

In this section, we discuss what the vision of the generative AI industry is. 

2.1 What is AGI and why does the field care about it so much? 

Since its inception, the field of AI has aspired to create machines that possess intelligence equal to or greater than that of humans. Most scholarly and historical accounts credit Ben Goertzel with coining and popularizing the term AGI around 2002, contrasting it with “narrow AI.” In his 2014 article [Goertzel, 2009], he articulates the “core AGI hypothesis” as follows. The creation and study of synthetic intelligences with sufficiently broad (e.g., human-level) scope and strong generalization capability is at bottom qualitatively different from the creation and study of synthetic intelligences with significantly narrower scope and weaker generalization capability. This hypothesis emphasizes the breadth and generalization abilities expected of AGI. One of the definitions of intelligence is the capacity to handle uncertainty and acquire skills to complete tasks for which one was not explicitly trained [Chollet, 2019]. AGI is envisioned as satisfying this requirement. 3Thus, the expectation is that AGI should demonstrate human-like cognition across a wide range of unformatted tasks and acquire human-like abilities to learn, apply, and transfer knowledge. Whereas there is little agreement on a precise definition of AGI, there is consensus that it has not yet been achieved. The timeline for its inception and reaching the so-called “singularity” continues to be postponed each year. Since the term AGI has gained unintended publicity, with media narratives amplifying it in ways that foster expectations about its near-term feasibility, we now look back at the pre-LLM era to trace how this narrative has evolved. 

Pre-LLM era. In the pre-LLM era, AGI talk in academia largely revolved around building archi-tectures that enable abstraction and compositional, systematic generalization. In the early years, AI was symbolic. In the 1970s, Newell and Simon asserted their physical symbol system hypothesis: A symbolic system possesses the necessary and sufficient means for general intelligent action [Newell and Simon, 1976]. In this view, intelligence arises from manipulating relational symbolic structures, abstracting away low-level sensory details to support higher-level reasoning and planning. However, this research program failed to produce human-level AI, in part because of the symbol grounding problem: Symbols remained disconnected from the real world. Moreover, the resulting systems struggled in messy, open-ended environments. A decade later, Fodor and Pylyshyn argued that a cognitive architecture must guarantee composition-ality through structural rules [Fodor and Pylyshyn, 1988]. They contended that early connectionist networks lacked such a guarantee. This seminal debate helped establish systematic generalization as a central criterion in subsequent discussions of cognitive architectures and, later, generalization in AI. In the late 1980s, dissatisfaction with purely symbolic approaches became more visible. Smolensky argued that connectionist models conceptually operate at a subsymbolic level, and that higher symbolic structures can be related to subsymbolic dynamics [Smolensky, 1988]. This perspective motivated a hybrid research agenda, rather than confrontation between the two camps. Since at least the late 1990s, reinforcement learning (RL) has been promoted as a general framework for agents that learn to act in unknown environments [Sutton and Barto, 2018]. By the late 2010s-early 2020s, a substantial strand of AGI - oriented work explicitly treated RL as an ingredient or framework for AGI [Silver et al., 2021], while simultaneously acknowledging that traditional RL in its standard form is unlikely to be sufficient on its own [LeCun, 2022]. Hutter’s AIXI framework combined Solomonoff induction with sequential decision theory, using an Occam-style prior that favors shorter environment programs, to define an idealized agent that maximizes expected long-term reward in unknown computable environments [Hutter, 2005]. Towards the end of the pre-LLM era, a group of researchers, including Lake and Tenenbaum, articulated a vision for human-like AI [Lake et al., 2017]. They argued that human-like intelligence requires the ability to build causal models of the world and to answer “why” and “how” questions; to ground learning in intuitive physics and psychological theories; and to harness compositional generalization and meta-learning. Gary Marcus, in his 2018 appraisal, pointed out that DL alone fell short of AGI requirements: Whereas it excelled at perceptual classification, it lacked a natural way to handle hierarchical structures, causal reasoning, and the representation of algebra-like variables [Marcus, 2018]. At this time, the prevailing view is that DL must be supplemented with symbolic techniques to achieve AGI. The core insight of this era is that intelligence is not a monolithic statistical capability but a complex architecture of distinct abilities, honed to extract structure from data. 

2022. The spark. Notably, expectations around AGI shifted after late 2022. The dramatic success of large-scale AI was built atop technological progress in infrastructure: the availability of large data centers and powerful synergy between hardware accelerators and deep neural network training on massive datasets [Jouppi et al., 2017]. Conversational AI demonstrated that natural language could be used to solve complex problems. These advances, especially in generative AI and foundation models [Brown et al., 2020], were predominantly driven by the generative transformer architec-ture [Vaswani et al., 2017]. Combined with super-human performance on narrow language and image benchmarks [Devlin et al., 2019, Liu et al., 2019, Raffel et al., 2020, He et al., 2015, Geirhos et al., 2021], they fueled expectations of AGI as a plausible near-term goal rather than a distant speculation. At that stage, the field had not yet exhausted the limits of available data and computation; for this 4reason, the longstanding dream of AGI appeared within reach. Some researchers further argued that multimodal foundation models held the promise of approaching AGI, given that human intelligence itself operates in a multimodal context with cross-modal knowledge processing and alignment [Fei et al., 2022]. 

2023-2024. Pushing the scaling limits. One major post-2022 academic trend is the effort to move beyond vague definitions of AGI toward quantifiable taxonomies [Morris et al., 2024, Peng et al., 2024b]. In the public discourse, strong performance on multiple benchmarks has often been taken as a proxy for general intelligence. In 2023, the Microsoft Research team tested GPT-4 and found it displayed a surprising breadth of ability across math, coding, vision, medicine, law, and more [Bubeck et al., 2023]. The team reported that GPT-4 achieves “strikingly close to human-level” performance on many tasks without specialized training and suggested it could be viewed as an early version of AGI. Their provocative claim that LLMs show “sparks” of general intelligence, although controversial [Schaeffer et al., 2023], drew research focus to LLM scaling as the primary path toward AGI and fueled much optimism in 2023. That year also saw a surge in attention to the societal risks posed by rapid AI development and increasingly powerful general-purpose, autonomous systems [Bengio et al., 2024]. By 2024, excitement grew around rapid gains in model capabilities. Advances in scaling, multimodal-ity, and increasingly strong performance on reasoning-like and complex, multi-step tasks [Zhao et al., 2023] were widely interpreted as credible milestones on a path to AGI. While scaling laws [Kaplan et al., 2020] remained accurate predictors of training loss, they were not always reliable indicators of downstream reasoning performance, especially in multi-step reasoning tasks [Nakamura et al., 2025, Chen et al., 2025]. Such performance depends on many factors beyond scale: A model can become exceptionally good at predicting the next token (lower perplexity) while remaining weak on the downstream task (“perplexity is not all you need”) [Lourie et al., 2025]. In July 2024, Google DeepMind released AlphaProof and AlphaGeometry 2, which together achieved silver-medal performance on International Mathematical Olympiad problems by combining language models with formal theorem-proving [Hubert et al., 2025]. This fueled broad public excitement about rapid progress in mathematical reasoning. At the same time, due to the visibility and pace of these improvements, mass media inflated ex-pectations, with some audiences treating broad benchmark success as evidence of genuine general intelligence [Leaver and Srdarov, 2023]. 

2025-present. Mature realism. Since late 2024, the discourse has increasingly shifted from optimism to recalibration of expectations. Some authors noted that the promise of AGI was “evapo-rating” [Funk and Smith, 2024]: Corporate AI deployments had not yet matched the hype and scaling alone was not cracking general intelligence. The phrase “diminishing returns to scaling” became more common, which reflects concerns about slowing gains. Notwithstanding these concerns, some researchers suggested that part of the slowdown may come from limitations in benchmarks and evaluation practices rather than reflect a true ceiling [Sinha et al., 2025]. Around this period, the AI community and regulators began questioning the reliability of traditional benchmarks, arguing that they had become less informative due to saturation and data contamina-tion [Eriksson et al., 2025]. They also noted that standard benchmark evaluation can encourage memorization and “gaming” of the metric. The community pivoted to the Abstraction and Reasoning Corpus (ARC-AGI, ARC-AGI-2 [Chollet, 2019, Chollet et al., 2025, 2026b]), a set of tasks that aims to measure the core of general fluid intelligence: skill acquisition and abstract thinking, rather than performance on predefined tasks and specialized factual knowledge. Unlike tasks that can be solved via pretrained knowledge alone, ARC-AGI requires the solver to figure out a new transformation from a few examples. The ARC Prize 2025 Technical Report [Chollet et al., 2026a] concludes that the arrival of “refinement loops” and test-time scaling marks a new era in AGI development. A refinement loop incrementally transforms one program or model into another based on a feedback signal. “No-pretraining” variants that fit unusually compact models from scratch at test time using only task-specific examples surprisingly outperform other variants with top scores [Sorokin and Puget, 2025, Franzen et al., 2025]. The 2025 ARC Prize finding is that reasoning performance is increasingly decoupled from model size [Chollet et al., 2026a], suggesting that AGI may not require the trillion-parameter models envisioned in 2023. 5Debates ensued on whether AGI is primarily an engineering challenge (scaling and refinement) or a scientific one (requiring new paradigms of reasoning). Subsequently, data shortage, perhaps the most pressing bottleneck at the time, pushed research toward alternatives, including world models, hybrid approaches, and neurosymbolic integration. World models [Ha and Schmidhuber, 2018, Assran et al., 2023, Balestriero and LeCun, 2025] arose as a counter-narrative to the scaling-centric approach. Unlike LLMs, they maintain a persistent world state internally and aim to optimize inference-time costs. During the “thinking” phase, such an agent simulates multiple sequences of actions using its internal world model, calculates a “cost” (energy) for each potential future, and then selects the action sequence that minimizes this cost [LeCun, 2022, Hafner et al., 2025]. 

Takeaway. A key takeaway from this brief historical review is that the public should not treat impressive benchmark results as decisive evidence for AGI. Often, apparent gains can reflect artifacts of evaluation design and metrics rather than genuine intelligence. 

2.2 Why AGI is unlikely to emerge from LLM scaling alone 

The path to AGI shaped during the contemporary LLM era depends on scale, data, and emergent capabilities to reach human-parity performance. However, humans can learn from only a few examples and readily generalize their learning to novel situations. In this regard, LLMs are still far behind human-like intelligence. One argument is that the human brain is better at forming and maintaining compact abstract representations, whereas LLMs primarily encode knowledge as a vast collection of context-weighted “special cases” that support pattern reuse rather than abstraction as the default mode [Goertzel, 2023]. Recent discussions of AGI and brain-inspired AI emphasize that progress towards AGI is now constrained less by abstract definitions than by concrete bottlenecks in learning and deployment [Zhao et al., 2023]. We summarize some of these discussions here, and revisit each in Sections 3 and 10 later: • Data efficiency: Learning from limited data remains an open challenge in the march towards AGI. Currently, LLMs require vast amounts of data to achieve human-level performance on individual tasks. • Computational cost: LLMs require substantial computational resources for both training and deployment, limiting their applicability. They require the construction and maintenance of data centers and other infrastructure, which may have negative externalities. • Environmental perspective: The substantial energy consumption for LLM inference raises serious concerns about their long-term environmental sustainability. 

2.3 Switching aspirations from AGI to DSS as an alternative 

For decades, AI was constrained by computing and data availability. Scaling in the LLM era on commercial-scale resources enabled various breakthroughs. As data/compute limits come into sharper focus, we enter the era of diminishing returns [Bennett, 2025]. At the same time, the financial and environmental costs of training and deploying ever-larger models have become increasingly salient [Strubell et al., 2019], making specialization attractive even if AGI remains a long-term goal. Now, the field needs to move toward a new paradigm that embraces efficiency and sustainability. 

Why DSS is a good alternative. We encounter far more data-scarce problems than those with abundant training examples, and scaling no longer seems to be the most straightforward path to further progress [Bennett, 2025]. In such settings, parameter-efficient adaptation of smaller language models on domain-specific data can be considerably more energy-efficient, and the required data are often available.

# 3 Importance of good data 

If a good abstraction is the engine of deep reasoning, data provide the fuel. However, the current industrial trajectory has prioritized the volume of this fuel over its purity. The dominant paradigm for training language models – scraping massive quantities of text from the open web and training a single, generalist giant on that mixture – seems to have clear pragmatic advantages (availability, scale, no extra effort required) but is a very poor proxy for a high-quality data training signal. Training models on good data is a basic axiom of machine learning. However, this axiom has been ignored since the inception of LLMs. Internet data are heterogeneous and of mixed quality, rife with noise, duplication, and unverified, conflicting information. Recent studies on massive web corpora, such as CommonCrawl, highlight significant issues related to formatting inconsistencies, irrelevant content, and hidden biases that are inevitably internalized by models [Perełkiewicz and Po´ swiata, 2024]. This is intuitive; guaranteeing the integrity of the training data at this scale is statistically impossible. Even careful filtering pipelines cannot perfectly remove low-quality or adversarial content, and low rates of problematic examples can have outsized downstream effects on model behavior, especially on tasks that require deep, compositional reasoning [Havrilla and Iyer, 2024]. Experts argue that we should stop assuming “more web text” is always better and instead focus on curating high-quality datasets [Bender et al., 2021]. In contrast, a bottom-up trajectory prioritizes high-quality, domain-specific data, in accordance with the “good data” rule. It is far more tractable to gather and curate a smaller, pristine dataset from authoritative sources, such as university textbooks, peer-reviewed research articles, and high-value internal enterprise reports that remain behind firewalls. These sources, although smaller in volume, offer a far richer signal per token. In many applied domains, such as medicine, law, engineering design, and scientific discovery, organizations already possess high-value internal documents that are never published on the open web for intellectual property (IP) or privacy reasons. Collecting, cleaning, and annotating these sources produces training data that directly target reasoning, conventions, and edge cases that matter the most in the given domain. Empirical work across several modalities shows that models trained on carefully curated, high-quality, “textbook-quality” data can outperform those trained on much larger, noisy corpora, especially when the task emphasizes depth, precision, and safety [Dong et al., 2025, Abdin et al., 2024, Dedhia et al., 2025]. However, high-quality unstructured text alone is not a silver bullet. Training a relatively small language model (for example, one containing 3-70B parameters) on very high-quality domain data can produce excellent recall and domain fluency, but depth of reasoning, i.e., compositional problem solving, discovery of novel chains of thought, and provable program-like manipulation of concepts often remain elusive. This gap arises because training on raw tokens (even when high-quality) does not automatically teach models the logical domain rules and operators required for deep, compositional reasoning, thus limiting their adoption and utility in high-stakes fields. This is where abstractions and structured synthetic data come into play. We do not mean blindly generating synthetic data using another LLM, which often leads to model collapse or quickly approaches a performance ceiling. Instead, we propose generating synthetic data derived from ground-truth, verifiable abstractions. Synthetic examples generated from domain abstractions, such as formal ontologies, program libraries, KGs, and proof steps from a theorem prover, can automatically lead to targeted curricula that teach the logic of reasoning and composition to models. Abstractions come in many complementary forms and each form contributes to producing better training material. KGs provide relational facts with provenance, which are ideal for generating grounded retrieval examples and multi-hop reasoning traces [Dedhia et al., 2025]. “Synthesize-on-Graph” [Ma et al., 2025b] demonstrates how navigating a context graph can produce diverse, coherent training samples that teach a model how to connect disparate concepts. Formal systems like Lean and LeanDojo [Yang et al., 2023] can generate an unlimited number of correct-by-design mathematical theorems and proofs. Methods like LogicPro [Jiang et al., 2025] leverage the strict input-output relationships of code (e.g., LeetCode problems) to synthesize complex logical reasoning puzzles, thereby bridging the gap between coding logic and natural-language reasoning. When such structures are used to generate or curate training examples, the resulting datasets contain both the surface-level knowledge (breadth) and the latent operators that enable composition (depth). Despite being small, 7structured abstractions are much denser in the exact signal needed for reasoning than vanilla web crawls. By training language models on this “structured synthetic data,” we can imbue them with the reasoning capabilities inherent in the abstraction itself. The result is a model that possesses the recall of a textbook and the logical depth of a symbolic reasoning system. 

Open questions: Q4. How do we convert diverse abstractions into effective training curricula and production pipelines; what evaluation metrics, benchmarks, and diagnostics reliably measure compositional reasoning, robustness, and domain fidelity? Can we view software compilers, formal grammars, and verification environments not just as tools, but as infinite factories for generating synthetic training data? 

Q5. How can we ensure that synthetic examples derived from abstractions preserve correctness and do not introduce subtle biases or overfitting? 

Q6. What tooling, human-in-the-loop processes, and governance practices are needed to scale careful curation while protecting privacy and IP? 

Q7. How can specialist models trained on such data be best integrated into modular systems that support continual learning, verifiability, and safe deployment? Answering these questions is essential to realizing a scalable, data-centric neurosymbolic AI vision that yields small, deeply capable domain specialists. 

# 4 Role of abstraction and compositionality in reasoning 

A substantial line of recent work argues that statistical learning alone is unlikely to be sufficient for human-level intelligence [LeCun, 2022, Holzinger et al., 2023, Jiao et al., 2024]. One recurring concern is that purely statistical learning tends to underemphasize abstraction . As a reasoning process, abstraction isolates the essential features of a representation while disregarding the remaining detail as irrelevant [Pfister, 2025]. In operational terms, abstraction is tightly linked to systematic compositional generalization: the ability to reuse learned primitives to form and interpret novel combinations [Lake and Baroni, 2023]. In the context of AI, abstraction is the fundamental mechanism by which systems isolate signals from noise, identify invariant structures across disparate domains, and formulate manipulable data representations. Limited abstract reasoning makes it harder to realize transfer learning, analogical reasoning, and hypothesis-based reasoning [Garnelo et al., 2016]. Abstraction also includes the higher-level representation that results from this mechanism. In what follows, this is what we mean by “abstraction.” To support human-like reasoning with abstractions, a system needs to embed a compositional structure :representations with reusable building blocks, rather than a single fixed encoding. Abstraction is a key mechanism for compositional generalization in humans [Lake et al., 2015]. The “Language of Thought” hypothesis posits that human intelligence is inherently compositional: We do not store every data instance separately, but learn abstract primitives and rules that can be recombined productively [Fodor, 1975]. This capacity for representational compositionality [Elmoznino et al., 2025] enables humans to generalize to novel situations using scarce data, a capability standard DL models do not exhibit. Compositional generalization, in turn, underwrites productivity : the ability to generate and compre-hend infinitely many novel expressions from a finite set of primitives [Kamali et al., 2025], thus handling out-of-distribution inputs that recombine familiar concepts in new ways. However, scaling-law analyses suggest that, for monolithic networks, the samples required for reliable generalization tend to grow with an effective input dimensionality of the task [Sharma and Kaplan, 2022]. Struc-turally modular architectures, built from sparsely connected modules, can mitigate this problem on modular tasks when network decomposition matches the underlying modular structure of the task [Boopathy et al., 2025]. On controlled benchmarks, such an alignment can make a constant number of samples sufficient even for high-dimensional inputs. At the same time, recent evidence in-dicates that compositional generalization can also emerge in standard multilayer perceptrons through 8scaling. Crucially, this effect is not guaranteed by scale alone: It depends on the structure and coverage of the training distribution [Redhardt et al., 2025]. 

4.1 Abstraction as the missing component in LLM pipelines 

The industry track (roughly, scaling-centric DL and foundation-model practice) has largely assumed that conceptual abstraction will emerge as a by-product of increasing parameter counts, compute, and data volume [Krakauer et al., 2025]. In this paradigm, LLMs do not place explicit emphasis on built-in mechanisms for abstraction, but acquire distributed representations implicitly through self-supervised pretraining, and sometimes supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on structured tasks. Yet, recent work suggests a gap between what these representation learners readily acquire and the structural requirements of true abstraction [Kumar et al., 2023]. A key reason is that abstraction is not guaranteed by standard statistical training objectives. Vanilla transformer language models do not include an explicit inductive bias that enforces abstraction or systematic compositional generalization. Thus, they often fail on benchmarks designed to isolate such behavior [Csordás et al., 2021]. Although transformers can learn compositional structure, the outcome is sensitive to initialization scale and data quality, and depends strongly on the training setup and architectural constraints (e.g., whether a modular structure is discoverable from data) [Schug et al., 2024]. More broadly, abstract representations can emerge in otherwise standard neural networks when training pressures encourage reuse across multiple tasks [Johnston and Fusi, 2023]. Relatedly, the observation of grokking (delayed generalization) after very prolonged training may suggest that the discovery of a compositional solution is not the primary objective of stochastic gradient descent and is not being reliably produced through optimization alone [Liu et al., 2022]. These observations motivate a shift from “abstraction as a by-product” to “abstraction as a target.” Recent results indicate that distributional coverage and diversity can matter more than sheer scale for compositional generalization [Uselis et al., 2025]. Instead of hoping abstractions will eventually appear with more compute, one can train on datasets that foreground abstract structure and systemati-cally vary compositions. Because compositional generalization is not guaranteed by scaling alone and is sensitive to training distributional coverage, this abstraction-centric route may also reduce compute and energy demands. Finally, learning abstractions is only part of the story: The model must also learn to compose them to solve tasks that require combining multiple abstractions. 

Takeaway. Systematic compositional generalization rarely emerges “for free” under standard training and typically requires additional pressures or structure. Even at scale, abundant data do not guarantee the emergence of appropriate abstractions when the training distribution lacks coverage of the underlying combinatorial factors needed for out-of-distribution recombination. Empirically, compositional performance improves when training is explicitly aligned with the compositional primitives of the task and encourages reusable abstractions, e.g., via primitive-focused pretraining or architectures that exploit task modularity [Ito et al., 2022, Boopathy et al., 2025]. 

4.2 Domain-specific abstractions 

Across modern AI, it is useful to distinguish domain-specific abstractions from more general ones. Formal abstractions provide interfaces that make reasoning composable and verifiable. Domain-specific abstractions, like formal languages paired with solvers or verifiers, turn reasoning into search over checkable intermediate states. Systems such as AlphaGeometry and AlphaGeometry2 illustrate this in geometry: A geometry-specific formalism plus a symbolic deduction engine, guided by a learned model, can solve Olympiad-level problems with far less dependence on human-written proofs. Similarly, formal theorem proving benefits from a proof-assistant-specific environment and premise-selection scaffolds [Hsiang et al., 2025], where the abstraction is the proof language together with the proof state and checker. Examples of domain-specific abstractions include equations for physical laws, regular expressions for textual patterns, and formal languages for deductive reasoning. # 5 Reasoning in AI: Beyond pattern matching 

Reasoning is an important pillar of generative AI. We discuss it next. 

5.1 What is reasoning in AI? 

Reasoning derives new knowledge from existing knowledge by applying a set of transformations and inference operations [Pfister, 2025]. It is central to intelligence, enabling step-by-step problem-solving beyond rote pattern matching, and adapting to novel situations [Xu et al., 2025]. In AI, reasoning is tightly coupled with a representation and inherits its semantics. Concretely, AI reasoning 10 applies logical or learned transformations or inference rules to internal representations to produce new knowledge, actions, or plans. Many modern AI systems instantiate this process as search or optimization over intermediate states subject to constraints (e.g., minimizing energy). Recent work organizes AI reasoning into categories that mirror psychological taxonomies, including three inference methods — deduction, induction, and abduction — and causal, physical (“naive physics”), and associational reasoning [Goertzel, 2009]. Some AI systems support deduction by deriving consequences from a set of premises, e.g., inference in formal languages. Much of the standard machine learning train/test paradigm targets induction, learning general regularities from examples in order to generalize to new samples from related distributions. Abduction, which infers likely causes from incomplete observations, remains particularly challenging because it requires selecting the most plausible explanation under uncertainty, often by reasoning over latent hypotheses. A prominent recent direction moves beyond standalone LLMs toward LLM-based agents and world-model-based planning, which may enable more hypothesis-driven inference and improve abductive reasoning. AI relies on reasoning to plan actions, justify decisions, and adapt to novel situations, rather than merely recall patterns. The concept of reasoning depth acknowledges that many real-world problems we delegate to AI are compositional. Reasoning depth is most commonly operationalized as the num-ber of dependent inference steps required to reach an answer. A parallel line of work formalizes depth via computation graphs, defining it as the length of the longest path [Dziri et al., 2023]. Empirically, performance often degrades with depth: as the required number of steps increases, accuracy typically drops sharply [Patel et al., 2024], a pattern frequently attributed to error accumulation across steps. 

5.2 Historical approaches to AI reasoning 

Early AI placed reasoning at the center. However, it was expressed through an explicit structure: Much of this work relied on symbolic logic and search, in which deduction operates over discrete objects. Classical systems used first-order logic and rule-based expert systems: A knowledge base of facts and rules is searched to derive new conclusions. Automated theorem provers and expert systems exemplify this paradigm, as their behavior is largely determined by hand-crafted rules. KGs are a closely related representation: They organize entities and relations and support reasoning through graph traversal and path-based search. Another pillar is probabilistic reasoning. Bayesian networks and other graphical models enable reasoning under uncertainty by encoding statistical dependencies and performing inference over them. This enables AI to address, to a certain extent, the main limitation of purely logical approaches: noisy or uncertain information. Reasoning also emerged as a key component of sequential decision-making. Classical planners apply heuristic search over action sequences, whereas Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) [Browne et al., 2012] explores large decision trees by balancing exploration and exploitation. Constraint solvers and optimization-based methods, such as satisfiability and constraint satisfaction solvers, fit naturally into this broader reasoning toolbox. Together, these classical approaches established a foundation for structured AI reasoning, but they often depend on manual knowledge engineering and tend to scale poorly to open-ended, unstructured tasks. 

5.3 State-of-the-art reasoning methods 

Unlike symbolic systems, LLMs learn implicit patterns from vast text corpora. They excel at recall and producing fluent text, but their reasoning is non-deterministic and implicit rather than rule-based; therefore, it requires verification. To enhance the reasoning capabilities of LLMs, several strategies have emerged, which we can roughly classify as inference-time and training-time & post-training methods. Specifically, the combination of post-training RL with inference-time search is currently the cornerstone for unlocking reasoning skills in reasoning models. Large reasoning models are LLMs optimized for multi-step reasoning, typically combining post-training RL with inference-time compute scaling to improve reliability. 11 5.3.1 Inference-time reasoning methods 

Inference-time methods aim to improve reasoning without changing model parameters. Instead, they shape how the model is prompted, how it generates outputs, and what external information it can access at test time. 

Prompting and decoding strategies. Techniques like CoT prompting [Wei et al., 2022] reveal the knowledge the model has obtained during pretraining. They encourage the model to produce interme-diate steps before giving an answer, which has been shown to improve accuracy on complex tasks. Follow-up work extends this idea by introducing explicit branching and selection over intermediate states, as in tree-of-thought (ToT) [Yao et al., 2023a] and graph-of-thought (GoT) [Besta et al., 2024]. Operationally, these approaches externalize intermediate reasoning states and support multi-step deliberation, with ToT/GoT enabling structured exploration over partial solutions rather than a single-shot response. However, CoT can underperform on tasks that require compositional generalization from demonstrations to harder instances. Least-to-most prompting [Zhou et al., 2023] addresses this by decomposing a hard problem into simpler subproblems and solving them sequentially. Beyond pure decomposition, ReAct [Yao et al., 2023b] interleaves reasoning traces with explicit actions and observations. This helps ground intermediate steps in external evidence and can reduce compounding errors in knowledge-intensive settings. Related approaches perform verifier-guided search by sampling multiple candidate reasoning paths and scoring them with external evaluators. Process reward models are a common instantiation, as they assess intermediate steps rather than only the final answer [Lightman et al., 2024]. 

Retrieval and memory augmentation. Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) supplies the model with relevant external context at inference time. Grounding responses in retrieved documents can improve factual consistency and support more reliable reasoning. More generally, memory-augmented neural networks and graph neural networks can inject structured signals, helping bridge unstructured text patterns and more explicit forms of reasoning [Patil and Jadon, 2025]. Work that integrates LLMs with KGs often frames the model as an agent that navigates a graph, linking reasoning quality to grounding in an explicit relational representation [Sun et al., 2024]. 

Adaptive compute allocation. State-of-the-art systems are moving toward compute-optimal strate-gies that vary inference-time compute based on problem difficulty. Compute-optimal strategies aim to avoid overthinking on simple prompts while enabling deeper deliberation on competition-level mathematics or complex coding problems. With sufficient test-time compute, a smaller model can outperform one that is 14 × larger [Snell et al., 2025]. 

5.3.2 Training-time and post-training reasoning methods 

Training-time and post-training methods improve multi-step reasoning by updating model parameters with gradients and, in many cases, by training auxiliary components such as reward models. A common starting point is reasoning-trace supervision, in which the model is fine-tuned on reasoning-rich data that include step-by-step solutions rather than only final answers [Ouyang et al., 2022, Zhang et al., 2026]. This can establish a structural grammar for multi-step outputs, but it also risks surface imitation in which the model reproduces the teacher’s style without acquiring the underlying problem-solving procedure. To deploy these advanced capabilities in smaller models, reasoning distillation transfers high-quality traces from a stronger teacher to a smaller student [Xu et al., 2024b]. Effective distillation typically relies on careful data selection so that the student internalizes the implicit problem-solving logic rather than memorizing output tokens. Beyond supervised traces, many approaches rely on RL. Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) [Ouyang et al., 2022] can further improve performance by optimizing toward preferred outputs. It is often combined with incentives for longer or more accurate reasoning chains. To reduce the overhead of explicit reward modeling, direct preference optimization [Rafailov et al., 2023] formulates the objective directly in terms of the policy, enabling end-to-end optimization without a separate reward-model training stage. A more recent direction emphasizes objective, verifiable rewards. This has led to the popularity of group relative policy optimization (GRPO) [Shao et al., 2024]. 5.4 Domain-specific reasoning 

Domain settings reshape what it means for an LLM to “reason.” Compared to open-domain use, a model must operate under domain-specific objectives and constraints, including heterogeneous inputs, proprietary knowledge, and evolving terminology [Ling et al., 2025]. These constraints limit naive transfer from generic LLM skills: General-purpose models trained on broad data often lack depth in a particular field. As a result, domain-specific reasoning is increasingly framed as a specialization problem under domain constraints that trades breadth for domain-grounded reliability .In practice, the most visible approaches cluster into a small set of strategies (Fig. 1). One line of work uses domain-adaptive continual pretraining to absorb domain language and conventions. Another emphasizes parameter-efficient adaptation, including prompt tuning and the low-rank adaptation family of methods, to reduce compute and data requirements. A third line focuses on grounding, typically through retrieval or structured knowledge, such as KGs. Finally, many systems scaffold reasoning with tools, programs, and constrained intermediate representations, aiming to make intermediate steps more controllable and easier to verify. In high-stakes domains, notably medicine and law, evaluation has increasingly moved beyond benchmark accuracy toward expert-aligned criteria, including factuality, comprehension, reasoning 13 quality, harm risk, and bias, often assessed through workflow-grounded studies, which indicate that benchmark success is insufficient as a proxy for real-domain performance. In medicine, domain-adapted models, such as Med-PaLM 2 [Singhal et al., 2025] and PMC-LLaMA [Wu et al., 2024], have demonstrated that continued pretraining and fine-tuning on medical corpora can mitigate limitations of general models, such as context-dependent abbreviations, and improve performance on medical benchmarks. Given the paramount importance of clinical safety and ethical implications, the current trend in medical reasoning is toward explainable AI frameworks that emphasize transparency, responsibility, and accountability in decision-making. Legal reasoning is institutionally constrained by authoritative sources of law, including formal rules and binding precedent interpreted within a hierarchical court system. In legal decision support, outputs are expected to be grounded in controlling legal authority and accompanied by transparent justification, since deference to precedent supports stability and predictability as rule-of-law values. Empirical work indicates that general-purpose language models are not consistently citation-faithful and can generate unsupported claims and fabricated or incorrect citations [Dahl et al., 2024]. This makes them poorly suited to settings with near-zero tolerance for such errors unless paired with robust grounding and independent verification. A further practical constraint is cost. Domain-specific development increasingly favors cost-effective adaptation rather than training from scratch [Xie et al., 2024]. Since domain adaptation is constrained by hardware and memory [Wang et al., 2025], parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods have become guiding tools for reducing training cost while retaining performance. 

5.4.1 Verification 

Verification supports domain reasoning by checking outputs against formal constraints and external evidence. The architecture of verifiability provides the structure through which machines can learn to think rigorously before they speak. Verification transforms LLMs from purely probabilistic generators into more reliable decision-making agents. Verification can target the final outcome or the intermediate states. The imperative for stepwise verification arises in domains where the reasoning path matters as much as the conclusion. A common failure mode manifests as an apparently correct conclusion supported by flawed intermediate logic, which suggests reliance on statistical shortcuts within the training distribution [Song et al., 2025]. Recent work also highlights a tension between optimizing for instruction-following and preserving factual reliability: Preference-based tuning can increase sycophancy and can yield unfaithful rationales, although some RLHF regimes improve truthfulness on average [Sharma et al., 2024]. The strongest form of verification is available in domains with formal rules and objectively checkable outcomes. Mathematical problem solving and code generation are the primary testbeds. One direction in reliability is autoformalization: using LLMs to translate informal statements into formal languages that can be checked by a solver. More broadly, domains with automatic verification signals are positioned for rapid progress because a formal checker can directly control output quality. 

5.4.2 The DeepMind Alpha series as DSS 

Specialization can yield striking performance within a narrow scope. The DeepMind Alpha lineage exemplifies this pattern: Scale is used to search and learn deeply within a single domain, rather than to cover many domains. Such a focused capability aligns with our definition of DSS. We can view the Alpha lineage as a sequence of domain-specialized systems that achieve superlative performance by turning the target domain into an interactive environment. Most of the Alpha models rely on well-defined feedback and couple learning with large-scale exploration. Concretely, this design pattern typically involves three engineering solutions: 1. Embedding rich domain structure into an environment. 2. Optimizing a well-defined objective under dense, programmatic feedback signals. 3. Evaluating against domain-native oracles when available (e.g., compilers, formal proof checkers), and against reference standards when not (e.g., experimental protein structures and benchmarks). A common feature is that these environments make correctness decidable or measurable with high precision. This enables the domain itself to supervise behavior at scale through mechanisms of self-play, synthetic data generation, or formal verification. 14 AlphaGo [Silver et al., 2016] combines supervised learning from human games with self-play and tree search. Subsequently, AlphaGo Zero [Silver et al., 2017] reduces reliance on human demonstration data by exploiting self-play under known rules. AlphaZero [Silver et al., 2018] reaches superhuman play in Go, chess, and shogi by learning from game rules alone. Outside games, AlphaFold [Jumper et al., 2021] delivers highly accurate protein structure prediction and has become a landmark result in structural biology. AlphaFold 3 [Abramson et al., 2024] extends the target from single proteins toward biomolecular complexes. AlphaStar [Vinyals et al., 2019] achieves expert-level performance for StarCraft. AlphaGeometry [Trinh et al., 2024a] addresses Olympiad geometry without human demonstrations. It synthesizes large amounts of training data and combines learned components with symbolic reasoning to produce rigorous proofs. More recently, AlphaProof [Hubert et al., 2025] extends this paradigm to formal mathematics by learning to construct machine-checkable proofs in Lean via RL over large sets of auto-formalized problems. Together with AlphaGeometry2 [Chervonyi et al., 2025], AlphaProof has reached silver-medal performance on International Mathematical Olympiad (IMO) problems. Across the lineage, the strongest results often come from generating high-quality data through interactions, e.g., self-play and synthetic theorem generation, rather than relying on large, curated, labeled sets. 

Limitations. The performance of all these systems is truly remarkable. However, they remain locked in fixed environments and do not generalize beyond their target domain. Alpha-style success largely depends on whether we can formalize a domain into an environment interface with states and actions, clearly specify a prediction target, and check the evaluation signal. Board games and formal proof assistants satisfy these requirements exceptionally well, whereas many open-world domains do not. AlphaProof reports multi-day computation to achieve IMO-level performance, which underscores that DSS can take advantage of concentrated compute within a narrow domain. Nonetheless, the Alpha series illustrates what domain-specific structure can enable, reaching performance that can reasonably be described as superintelligent within the domain. 

Takeaway. A useful lens is to view reasoning as a stack: (1) Adapt −→ (2) Ground −→ (3) Scaffold −→ (4) Constrain −→ (5) Evaluate. (1) Under this view, gains in reasoning do not come only from larger models. They also depend on better adaptation schemes, stronger inference-time control, and tighter feedback loops between training and inference [Xu et al., 2025]. Rich, task-relevant context can matter as much as parameter count: Retrieval augmentation can enable a smaller language model to outperform a larger one on knowledge-intensive queries by grounding answers in an external knowledge base [Izacard et al., 2023]. Train-time improvements based on SFT face a bottleneck: Collecting large-scale, high-quality human annotations for instruction-tuning datasets is expensive. This is why rapid progress has concentrated in domains with automatic verification, where models can generate reasoning trajectories and filter them through checkers to create reliable training data without a human-in-the-loop. The field is also shifting from scaling primarily during training to scaling at inference. The premise is that simply enlarging the pretraining corpus is no longer sufficient to unlock the next jump in capability. Instead, researchers increasingly allocate more compute at inference to support deeper, more deliberate reasoning. Because multi-step reasoning typically requires multiple forward passes, improving step-level reliability and strengthening the components in Eq. (1) is often more cost-effective than increasing model size. Finally, domain evaluation is shifting toward expert-driven axes rather than correctness alone. Limited access to proprietary domain corpora, together with stringent requirements for faithfulness and trace-ability, further reinforces domain specialization as a distinct research direction, with methodological constraints that differ from general-purpose settings.

# 6 Unleashing the potential of neurosymbolic AI through abstraction 

Neurosymbolic AI has emerged as a framework for reconciling two historically divergent traditions in AI: connectionist learning and symbolic reasoning. Neural networks demonstrate remarkable capacity for modeling high-dimensional, unstructured data, enabling advances in perception, language modeling, and generative tasks. However, they often lack explicit structural constraints required for systematic reasoning and formal generalization. In contrast, symbolic AI emphasizes logic, compositionality, and knowledge representation, offering interpretability and deductive rigor, yet frequently exhibiting brittleness and limited adaptability to noisy, real-world inputs [Garcez and Lamb, 2023]. The motivation for this integration is grounded in a broader theory of cognition: Robust intelligence requires both the capacity to learn from experience and the ability to reason from what has been learned [Bader and Hitzler, 2005, d’Avila Garcez et al., 2009, Donadello et al., 2017]. From this per-spective, neural models provide scalable statistical learning mechanisms, whereas symbolic systems contribute compositional abstraction and logically structured inference. A neurosymbolic architec-ture, therefore, seeks to synthesize them into unified computational systems in which representation learning and rule-based reasoning mutually reinforce one another. Such systems aim to combine the robustness and efficiency of neural inference with the interpretability, reliability, and systematic generalization provided by symbolic structures [Liang et al., 2025]. The rapid progress of DL and LLMs has renewed interest in this integration. In cognitive-scientific terms, this development is often interpreted as an attempt to bridge so-called System 1 and System 2 processes [Kahneman, 2011]. Classical symbolic approaches largely correspond to deliberative, stepwise reasoning, but struggle with scalability and domain transfer. Early DL systems, by con-trast, excelled at intuitive pattern recognition but exhibited limited capacity for explicit logical manipulation. Contemporary LLMs blur this boundary: Trained at a large scale, they demonstrate cross-domain generalization and emergent reasoning behaviors that approximate structured inference without explicit symbolic programming. However, the representational power of the “neural” parts of modern LLMs should not overshadow the critical need for their “symbolic” counterparts. Whereas neural models excel at pattern recognition and intuitive System 1 processing, they remain prone to hallucinations, factual inconsistencies, and lack of verifiability when relying solely on statistical approximations [Huang et al., 2025, Jiang et al., 2024]. The symbolic side, therefore, is not merely a legacy of classical AI but a foundational requirement for robust reasoning. It provides the inter-pretability and logical structure necessary to constrain neural generation to lie within the bounds of 16 validity [Velasquez et al., 2025]. By integrating explicit symbolic reasoning, we can endow LLMs with System 2 capabilities, ensuring that their outputs are not just plausible, but faithfully grounded in established truths and verifiable logic. We argue that incorporating a high-level abstraction as the “symbolic” part into neurosymbolic systems offers a particularly promising approach to advancing language model capabilities. By unifying data-driven learning with structured reasoning, neurosymbolic systems can realize both flexibility and reliability. In the following sections, we examine how this paradigm is reshaping distinct yet critical domains: KGs and formal languages. 

6.1 Knowledge graphs as semantic abstractions 

Real-world knowledge can be represented in a KG as triples of the form (head entity, relation, tail entity), which encode how two entities are linked through a specific relationship. In the LLM field, KGs have emerged as an important tool for representing, storing, and effectively managing knowledge [Hogan et al., 2021, Cheng et al., 2024, Belova et al., 2026]. In reasoning-intensive settings, KGs provide a structural counterweight to hallucinations and factual mistakes observed in purely neural architectures [Yu et al., 2024]. Recent frameworks, such as GraphRAG [Edge et al., 2024], extend RAG by automatically constructing KGs from large text corpora and then extracting subgraphs from them to respond to queries. Generation is conditioned not only on retrieved passages but also on an explicit graph-structured representation, thereby enhancing accuracy. Building on RAG, ToG-2 [Ma et al., 2024] tightly couples the processes of context retrieval and graph retrieval, deepening context retrieval via the KG while enabling reliable graph retrieval based on contexts. Graph-R1 [Luo et al., 2025] further advances the method by introducing lightweight knowledge hypergraph construction and formulating retrieval as a multi-turn agent-environment interaction, optimized through end-to-end reward signals. Complementarily, graph-constrained-reasoning [Luo et al., 2024] further bridges the structured knowledge embedded in KGs with the unstructured reasoning of LLMs by integrating the KG structure into the LLM decoding process through a trie-based index that encodes KG reasoning paths. Beyond inference-time augmentation, KGs also offer substantial value during training. KG primitives can be used to synthesize curricula for domain-specific distillation and finetuning [Dedhia et al., 2025]. Moreover, KGs can function as implicit reward models to learn compositional multi-hop reasoning [Kansal and Jha, 2026]. Collectively, these developments illustrate how KGs implement neurosymbolic integration at both the retrieval and inference levels. Beyond their structural advantages in training and deployment, KGs introduce a critical element of transparency to AI systems. Because semantic relationships are represented as a graph, the underlying symbolic knowledge remains inherently interpretable to human observers. This interpretability ensures that users can directly audit the knowledge base, identify gaps, and easily expand the graph by introducing new nodes and edges as needed. Thus, KGs represent a dynamic symbolic component that can organically grow over time, thereby continuously extending and refining the capabilities of all neural models that interface with them. 

6.2 Formal languages as a mathematical abstraction 

To operationalize formal reasoning in modern LLMs, the field has largely converged upon the Lean theorem prover [De Moura et al., 2015, Moura and Ullrich, 2021] as a definitive abstraction layer. Lean is an open-source, extensible, functional programming language and interactive theorem prover specifically designed to make the writing of correct, machine-checkable code and mathematical proofs efficient and maintainable. If an LLM attempts to generate a proof containing a hallucination or a logical fallacy, the Lean compiler immediately rejects it, providing deterministic feedback regarding exactly where the logic failed [Lin et al., 2025]. This dynamic shifts the burden of verification away from the probabilistic neural network and places it onto the symbolic compiler [Yang et al., 2026]. The broader ambition of AI mathematical reasoning requires direct engagement with universal formal languages. AlphaProof [Google DeepMind, 2024] fundamentally reconceptualizes the Lean language as a dynamic environment for RL. Problems are automatically formalized from natural English into Lean, enabling the neural network to generate proof steps while the compiler serves as a perfect, hallucination-free reward mechanism. This strict setup demonstrates the scalability of test-time compute, enabling the system to use large amounts of computation to explore and rigorously check 17 hypotheses against the compiler during active problem solving. To handle complex proofs with convoluted deductive chains, DeepSeek-Prover-V2 [Ren et al., 2025] uses recursive proof search via subgoal decomposition. It uses DeepSeek-V3 [Liu et al., 2024] to break a theorem into formal subgoals, then deploys a highly optimized 7B prover to solve smaller lemmas before assembling them into a complete formal proof. Furthermore, addressing RL challenges like model collapse and data plateaus, Goedel-Prover-V2 [Lin et al., 2025] mitigates data scarcity through scaffolded data synthesis. It effectively creates its own curriculum by generating new subproblems from failed proof attempts. It also applies verifier-guided self-correction, using detailed feedback from the Lean compiler to iteratively revise and debug proofs within a single context window, achieving state-of-the-art results with significantly fewer parameters. Whereas general formal languages like Lean offer universal applicability, specific geometric domains present unique challenges that require highly specialized architectures. Google DeepMind intro-duced AlphaGeometry [Trinh et al., 2024a], a system that achieves Olympiad-level performance by explicitly decoupling neural intuition from symbolic deduction. The symbolic core is a Deductive Database and Algebraic Reasoning engine that iteratively applies classical geometric deduction rules structured as definite Horn clauses, taking the logical form Q(x) ← P1(x), P 2(x), . . . , P k(x),where x represents geometric point objects and the predicates represent relationships like collinearity. When the symbolic engine stops before reaching a proof, the neural language model intervenes to predict the most probabilistically useful auxiliary construct, thereby opening new pathways for logical deduction. To overcome the bottleneck of scarce human training data, researchers developed AlphaGeometry2 [Chervonyi et al., 2025], using the symbolic engine to generate a vast pool of synthetic training data comprising 100 million unique, machine-verified geometric examples. By extending the formal representation language and integrating the Gemini architecture [Gemini Team et al., 2024], AlphaGeometry2 achieved an 84% solve rate on all geometry problems presented over the last 25 years. In summary, formal language can function as a powerful abstraction that enables reliable mathematical reasoning within neurosymbolic systems, rather than relying solely on probabilistic approximations. These recent systems illustrate how this abstraction enables scalable proof search, structured de-composition of complex reasoning tasks, and iterative self-correction grounded in deterministic feedback. 

6.3 Neurosymbolic pathways to small models 

Neurosymbolic approaches significantly enhance parameter efficiency by distilling knowledge from LLMs into highly capable, smaller architectures. For instance, extracting common sense knowl-edge from GPT-3 into a combined KG and neural model creates a system 100 times smaller that still achieves superior reasoning performance [West et al., 2022]. Similarly, the Ctrl-G framework explicitly integrates symbolic logic constraints into a distillation pipeline for generating a smaller model [Zhang et al., 2024a]. Furthermore, neurosymbolic AI can reduce the amount of data and parameters needed by natively embedding symbolic knowledge to fill gaps during both training and inference. Research shows that co-designing neural networks to estimate physical variables alongside symbolic equations that govern physical conservation laws for off-road autonomous driving can achieve state-of-the-art performance with significantly reduced training time and parameter count [Zhao et al., 2024]. Finally, neurosymbolic frameworks efficiently leverage human subject matter expertise by seamlessly integrating a small number of curated examples directly into symbolic com-ponents, such as KGs. This explicitly defined knowledge can then be used to more efficiently train the neural network, successfully combining the fast adaptation to novel situations, characteristic of sym-bolic reasoning, with the high-dimensional generalization capabilities of machine learning [Bhagat et al., 2023]. Overall, neurosymbolic systems provide a compelling pathway toward building smaller yet highly capable AI systems through domain-specific abstractions. This efficiency in both data and parameter count aligns naturally with the DSS paradigm: Abstractions, such as KGs and formal languages, guide learning and inference, enabling compact models to achieve expert-level competence within their domains while remaining scalable and composable within a larger society and system. 18 Open questions: Q21. # 7 Building blocks of domain-specific superintelligence 

Next, we describe how the foundational elements described above — high-quality data, robust abstractions, neurosymbolic architectures, and advanced reasoning methods — can be practically orchestrated to serve as DSS building blocks. Rather than relying on the ad hoc memorization inherent to generalist models, we demonstrate how DSS can be constructed from the ground up by first extracting verifiable abstractions and then using them to systematically teach models how to elicit deep reasoning in a given domain. 

7.1 KG extraction methods 

As the demand for structured knowledge representation has grown, various methods have emerged to automatically extract and construct KGs from unstructured data. These approaches can be categorized into three main groups: task-specific natural language processing (NLP) pipelines, triple embedding-based techniques, and LLM-based extraction. Traditional task-specific NLP methods rely on sequentially chaining machine learning components, such as named entity recognition, coreference resolution, and relation extraction [Jaradeh et al., 2023]. Whereas these pipelines can achieve the targeted accuracy, they require extensive feature engineering, sophisticated text-preprocessing heuristics, and deep domain expertise [Rao et al., 2024]. Furthermore, supervised models within these pipelines require massive, domain-specific labeled datasets, making the extraction process labor-intensive and difficult to scale. Alternatively, triple embedding-based approaches map KG structures into vector spaces to predict missing links and infer new relations [Cheng et al., 2024]. However, since these methods primarily operate on localized triple patterns, they struggle with multi-hop dependencies, ontological constraints, and external semantic integration [Xia et al., 2025]. They are also limited to the relations, attributes, and ontology of the training KG, which makes it hard to generalize across different KGs. Due to the limitations of traditional NLP and embedding models, recent KG extraction research has shifted towards using LLMs to generate relational knowledge through targeted prompting [Zhu et al., 2024, Li et al., 2025]. Although LLMs offer impressive flexibility and ease of deployment, their use in KG extraction remains unreliable for rigorous, domain-specific applications. A central limitation is their sensitivity to prompt formulation. Relation extraction accuracy and output consistency can vary substantially in response to minor syntactic changes or small shifts in task framing [Cao et al., 2021, Mousavi et al., 2024]. Methods such as RAG attempt to ground model outputs and reduce this instability. However, they introduce additional failure modes, including conflicts between the retrieved evidence and the internal parametric knowledge of the model, as well as error propagation from imperfect retrieval systems [Zhang et al., 2025b]. Beyond prompt sensitivity, LLM-based extraction is further undermined by intrinsic hallucinations and factual inaccuracies. As probabilistic generative systems, LLMs can produce ungrounded or unfaithful content, which persists regardless of model scale, dataset size, or fine-tuning strategy [Kim et al., 2025]. In the context of KG construction, LLMs often exhibit the “reversal curse” [Berglund et al., 2024], failing to perform simple inverse reasoning that is essential for accurately representing bidirectional relations. 7.2 GRAPH MERT: Distillation of factual and ontologically valid domain-specific KGs from raw text 

Within the DSS framework, a domain-specific KG functions as a semantic abstraction that augments training data, thereby enabling depth in domain-specific reasoning. A KG can facilitate reasoning by providing grounded concepts, structured relationships, and a substrate for verification and evaluation. For a KG to be useful in this role, it must demonstrate high quality along two dimensions: factuality and validity. Factuality captures provenance: whether each triple is supported by, and traceable to, the source text. Validity captures ontological consistency: whether the relation semantics of a triple conform to the domain ontology. The G RAPH MERT framework (see Fig. 2) is designed to satisfy the above requirements, while remaining fully automatic. Unlike manual pipelines, it does not rely on hand-crafted features. Instead, it requires only a small seed KG that exemplifies how relations are used under the domain ontology. Given such a seed KG and a high-quality domain corpus, G RAPH MERT can extract a reliable domain KG, making it a practical first step in the DSS pipeline. The G RAPH MERT model, which is the core of the G RAPH MERT pipeline, learns graph-structured, ontology-aligned representations in an encoder-only transformer and uses them to predict new triples from local textual context. After training, the G RAPH MERT model performs semantic prediction to complete new KG triples based on context. During data preparation, the framework augments syntactic corpora with semantic supervision. The semantic supervision consists of KG triples aligned with the domain ontology, providing examples of domain-specific relations. These triples are paired with domain text so that each triple is relevant to its local linguistic context. As a result, at inference time, G RAPH MERT predicts triples that are consistent with the domain ontology. Each predicted triple can be traced to its origin-supporting provenance. Moreover, the extracted KG can be used to expand the initial seed and iteratively retrain the model, progressively improving coverage and strengthening the domain abstraction over time. 20 7.3 Generating high-quality KG-grounded bottom-up curricula 

Once a reliable abstraction, such as a domain-specific KG, is obtained, the next critical building block is to leverage it as an active training engine rather than a static database. Traditional top-down training relies on models passively absorbing co-occurrences from vast, unstructured general text corpora. However, to approach true intelligence, we must adopt a bottom-up methodology that teaches a model how to think. By treating a reliable KG as a data foundry, we can systematically traverse its explicit domain primitives (represented as head-relation-tail triples) to synthesize a dense, high-quality reasoning curriculum. This ensures the training data inherently captures the fundamental axioms of the domain. This paradigm was rigorously demonstrated in [Dedhia et al., 2025]. It uses a medical KG to generate a vast curriculum designed to impart deep domain expertise. Rather than simply extracting isolated facts, the pipeline synthesizes multi-hop reasoning tasks by connecting disparate medical concepts through logical paths. The result is a comprehensive training set comprising over 24 , 000 reasoning tasks, each paired with a detailed thinking trace that models the step-by-step logic required to reach a conclusion. Fine-tuning a 32B SLM on this explicitly grounded data led to the development of QwQ-Med-3: a model uniquely equipped with internalized medical primitives. The resulting capabilities of QwQ-Med-3 showcase how this curriculum realizes our core vision of building specialized superintelligence. Evaluated on ICD-Bench, a rigorous suite featuring 3,675 complex questions across 15 distinct medical domains, the model demonstrated expert-level reasoning that significantly outperforms larger, state-of-the-art generalist models [Dedhia et al., 2025]. Notably, even though the model is trained on shorter 1-to-3 hop KG paths, it successfully generalizes to unseen, complex 2- to 5-hop scenarios during testing. This shows that by grounding a curriculum in verifiable abstractions, we can efficiently teach small models to deeply compose domain concepts. 

7.4 Abstractions as implicit reward models 

While fine-tuning on a KG-grounded curriculum successfully grounds the model in domain facts, SFT alone is often insufficient to elicit robust, zero-shot compositional reasoning. Models trained purely on supervised data can still default to superficial pattern matching when faced with highly complex, unseen multi-hop scenarios. To unlock true logical depth, the model must undergo RL that provides rigorous process supervision, evaluating and rewarding the method of reasoning, not just the final answer. However, traditional RL environments are either too rigid to scale easily or require careful expert curation or are too niche to generalize. The solution lies in using the underlying abstractions themselves as implicit reward models. Because a KG encodes the causal and relational links between entities, the multi-hop paths within the KG can serve as a ground-truth, verifiable logical chain. Kansal and Jha [2026] demonstrate a scalable SFT+RL post-training pipeline, where these KG paths provide novel reward signals (see Fig. 3). During the RL phase, the model is dynamically evaluated against these path-derived signals. If the model successfully composes the intermediate axioms, navigating the correct sequence before arriving at the conclusion, it receives a high reward. This shifts the optimization landscape, forcing the model to explicitly prioritize coherent logical composition over hallucinated shortcuts. This approach bridges the gap between simple recall and true compositional reasoning. In experiments within the medical domain, Kansal and Jha [2026] show that a 14B language model trained exclusively on short 1-to-3 hop reasoning paths can zero-shot generalize to more complex 4- and 5-hop queries. The pipeline enables an SLM to significantly outperform much larger frontier systems, such as GPT-5.2 and Gemini 3 Pro, on the most difficult reasoning tasks, while also demonstrating robust resistance to adversarial option-shuffling stress tests. Thus, using KGs as implicit reward models provides the scalable, deterministic supervision necessary to transform a model into a rigorous domain expert.

Having shown the limits of scale and by synthesizing the three pillars discussed thus far: high-quality data, robust abstractions, and advanced neurosymbolic reasoning methods, as well as the DSS building blocks in the previous section, we arrive at the core proposal of this alternative trajectory: the creation of DSS societies. A DSS is not merely a “fine-tuned” model; it is a specialized system where an SLM is trained on carefully curated, high-fidelity data and reinforced by domain-specific symbolic abstractions (such as KGs, formal logical systems, simulation engines, etc.). Whereas a generalist LLM aims for “good enough” performance across infinite tasks, a DSS aims for superhuman depth within one domain. By narrowing the scope, we can ensure that the resulting model does not just memorize patterns but understands the causal and logical structures of its field, achieving a level of reliability and deep reasoning that eludes generalist models. 

8.1 Society of DSS models 

To replicate and eventually surpass the utility of a generalist model at much lower societal and eco-nomic costs, we propose orchestrating these specialized DSS models into a collaborative ecosystem, forming a “society of superintelligences.” This architecture functions through dynamic delegation rather than monolithic processing. The workflow of such a society operates as follows: 

Front-end orchestrator. An SLM trained as a super-domain superintelligence serves as the user interface and the entry point to a query. It acts as a router, parsing the user’s intent and dispatching the query to the appropriate “back-end” DSS models that are relevant to the query. For example, a query related to diabetes would be routed by a medical DSS to a diabetes DSS. 

Decomposition and routing. For complex, multi-faceted inquiries, the orchestrator breaks the problem down into sub-queries. For instance, a question involving both legal liability and engineering failure would be parsed into distinct components: one sent to a legal DSS and another to a structural engineering DSS. A question about a novel way to model the dynamics of a system would be directed to the physics and mathematics DSS models. 

Inter-DSS collaboration. In advanced scenarios, DSS models do not merely report to the user, they communicate with each other. The engineering DSS might request clarification from a physics DSS before finalizing its assessment. This interchange of findings and collaboration mimics a team of human experts consulting on a case, ensuring that the final output is a synthesis of deep, verified expertise rather than a probabilistic, one-handed guess. 

8.2 Multimodality and real-world impact 

For such a society to achieve maximum impact, it must be fundamentally multimodal. Expertise in many high-stakes domains is not encoded solely in text but in images, sensor data, and distinct file formats. In medicine, for example, the DSS cannot rely on medical textbooks and articles alone. It must be capable of interpreting electronic health records for patient history, while simultaneously analyzing high-resolution medical imaging (CT scans, MRIs) and blood test data. By integrating and reasoning over multiple modalities, the DSS society can achieve a holistic view of the patient that matches or exceeds the diagnostic capabilities of human specialists. 

8.3 Abstraction-agnostic orchestration 

The proposed approach is abstraction-agnostic; any structured symbolic representation that results in salient domain operators or relations can be used to generate curricula, supervision, or verification checks, resulting in a DSS. KGs, context graphs, formal proofs (Lean), inductive program libraries, and procedural templates each can play a role. By treating these different abstractions as interchangeable building blocks, DSS societies become robust. The same front-end orchestrator can route a sub-query to a KG-backend retriever, a proof-guided reasoner, a program synthesizer, or a multimodal perception model, depending on the subtask. 23 8.4 “Lego-like” composability of DSS models 

The proposed approach enables modular, Lego-style construction of AI systems. Instead of training a massive model for every possible contingency, organizations can build a library of DSS SLMs, such as a “Legal DSS Block,” a “Coding DSS Block,” a “Bio-Chemistry DSS Block,” etc. These blocks can be dynamically assembled and swapped to create custom societies for each specific need. A pharmaceutical company might combine Bio-Chemistry and Legal Blocks for drug discovery, while a manufacturing firm might combine Engineering and Supply Chain Blocks. Such composability and flexibility drastically reduce the barrier to entry; upgrading the system only requires swapping out a single model rather than retraining a multi-trillion-parameter model. 

8.5 Approaching AGI asymptotically 

Finally, and almost ironically, this approach offers a pragmatic path toward the holy grail of AI: AGI. By continuously expanding the library of DSS models and refining the orchestration layer, we can asymptotically approach AGI. As the DSS society grows to cover every conceivable domain of human endeavor – from poetry to quantum mechanics – the collective system may exhibit behavior indistinguishable from general intelligence. Notably, this architecture also poses a provocative counter-argument: We may not need AGI at all! If a society of DSS models can outperform human experts in every specific domain, diagnosing diseases better than physicians, drafting contracts better than lawyers, and coding better than engineers, the theoretical construct and need of a single “general” intelligence becomes redundant. The utility lies in the excellence of the parts, not the unity of the whole.

# 9 AI agents 

The previous sections have established the core components of our bottom-up approach: DSS SLMs and DSS societies (Sections 7 and 8) that achieve reasoning depth through high-quality domain data (Section 3), explicit symbolic abstractions, such as KGs, which encode the structured mental models that are prerequisites for generalization (Section 4), and neurosymbolic integration that grounds reasoning (Section 5) in verifiable knowledge. In this section, we discuss how these components can be assembled into autonomous agents, realizing the vision, introduced in Section 1, of specialist models cooperating much as human experts do. A recurring conceptual error in current AI development is conflating the language model with the intelligent agent. As described in the Foundation Agents survey [Liu et al., 2025], an LLM is better understood as an “engine,” whereas an agent is the “vehicle” built around this engine with additional modules for perception, memory, world modeling, and action. Current implementations often try to force the engine to act as the entire vehicle, relying on the LLM weights for memory, world modeling, planning, and execution. This produces hallucination because of the absence of an external 24 world model, transient memory limited to a context window, and no actionable grounding to execute complex workflows. In the DSS approach, a domain-tuned SLM or a society of such SLMs is the engine, and various other modules supply the missing vehicle components: external memory, a world model, and verifiable grounding. 9.1 Architecture: Societies of DSS models 

No single DSS SLM, however deep its domain expertise, can handle real-world tasks that span multiple domains. As illustrated in Fig. 4, the solution is to integrate various modules around a society of DSS models: A central front-end orchestrator divides a user query into sub-queries, dispatches them to a modular library of specialist DSS back-ends, and integrates their responses. This lego-like architecture mirrors how human societies work: Individuals acquire deep expertise in narrow domains and cooperate to solve problems that no single expert can address alone. A survey [Liu et al., 2025] formalizes such an architecture at three levels: society, environment, and agent. 

9.1.1 Why a heterogeneous DSS society may outperform a monolith model as the agent brain 

The key advantage of a DSS society is heterogeneity: each back-end SLM is trained on different domain data and paired with a different symbolic abstraction (e.g., a KG); hence, the society as a whole covers far more ground than any single model [Tran et al., 2025]. This heterogeneity operates at multiple levels. At the persona level, DSS SLMs take on distinct specialist roles: a programmer, product manager, and tester in a software development team (MetaGPT [Hong et al., 2024], ChatDev [Qian et al., 2024]), or cardiologists, oncologists, and pediatricians in a medical consultation. Each is optimized for its function, yet collectively they outperform a single massive model [Tran et al., 2025]. At the action-space level, the front-end orchestrator SLM decomposes tasks and routes queries to the appropriate specialist back-end. MasRouter [Yue et al., 2025] shows that intelligent routing to appropriately sized models achieves 1.8-8.2% performance gains while reducing computational overhead, consistent with the DSS prediction that capability can be decoupled from model size. 

Emergent specialization. Even initially homogeneous agents can spontaneously develop distinct roles through repeated interactions, which suggests that a DSS society-based agent need not be fully hand-designed. In Project Sid [AL et al., 2024], hundreds of agents evolved professions, trade 25 networks, and even cultural practices without explicit programming. This echoes Minsky’s “Society of Mind” hypothesis [Minsky, 1988]: Intelligence emerges from the interactions of many specialized sub-agents. 

Robustness through diversity. If one DSS back-end fails, the DSS society and agent persist. This avoids “correlated failures,” where a single blind spot in a monolithic model causes total system failure, a risk inherent to concentrating all knowledge in one set of weights. 

9.1.2 How DSS models collaborate 

The front-end/back-end routing described above is the simplest task-oriented collaboration pattern: The orchestrator SLM decomposes a high-level objective into sub-tasks and dispatches them to specialist DSS back-ends, each processing upstream artifacts (intermediate code, data structures, evaluation results) and producing downstream outputs [Tran et al., 2025]. When a problem requires reconciling conflicting domain perspectives, DSS societies can also use consensus-oriented debate: Specialist SLMs with different domain grounding debate a solution and each must justify its rea-soning against its own verifiable symbolic abstraction, which reduces hallucination and bias. The RECONCILE [Chen et al., 2024a] and MAD [Liang et al., 2024] frameworks show measurable improvements in decision quality through such structured argumentation. 

Communication protocols. For DSS societies to scale, the modules need standardized interfaces. Several protocols have been proposed [Yang et al., 2025]: MCP for centralized tool integration, ANP for decentralized cross-platform agent networks, A2A for web-native agent-to-agent communication, and IoA for task-based team coordination. Because individual DSS SLMs are small enough to run on edge devices (Section 10), these decentralized protocols also enable societies where some back-ends run on-device rather than in the cloud, distributing thermal load and reducing dependence on centralized data centers.

9.3.2 Continual learning 

For an AI system to function in the real world, it cannot rely solely on static knowledge acquired during training. It must be capable of acquiring new skills, incorporating new information, and adapting to changing environments over its operational lifetime [Zheng et al., 2024]. This is known as continual learning (CL), lifelong learning, self-evolution, or incremental learning. In the context of LLMs, CL is often framed as a computational maintenance challenge: how to update the model’s parameters to reflect new data without catastrophic forgetting of previously learned capabilities [Shi et al., 2025]. This tension between incorporating new information (plasticity) and retaining prior knowledge (stability) constitutes the central stability-plasticity dilemma [Zheng et al., 2026]. However, as we argue for an alternative trajectory focused on societies of small models, our proposed architecture implicitly enables CL. As demonstrated in the survey [Liu et al., 2025], “All manually designed agentic systems will eventually be replaced by learnable, self-evolving systems,” agents can optimize their own prompts, workflows, and tool usage based on feedback. Unlike a static LLM frozen after training, a DSS agent creates a flywheel: Better tools lead to better experiments, which produce better data, which yield smarter agents [Gao et al., 2026]. This reinforces the data-quality principle from Section 3. For agentic systems built atop DSS models, CL shifts from a model-level problem (updating pa-rameters while avoiding catastrophic forgetting) to a system-level one. Rather than through costly retraining, agents adapt by accumulating episodic memory, expanding their underlying symbolic ab-stractions with newly discovered facts, and refining tool usage strategies. This decouples knowledge acquisition from parameter optimization, sidestepping the stability-plasticity dilemma [Zheng et al., 2026] that plagues monolithic models. A further possibility is that the expanded abstractions and accumulated episodic memory can seed new synthetic curricula (Section 3), enabling the SLM itself to be continually and periodically fine-tuned on high-quality, grounded agent-generated data at far lower cost than full retraining. 

Mechanisms. The survey [Liu et al., 2025] identifies three primary mechanisms: • Memory-based learning: Agents record outcomes and world states, then identify successful versus failed strategies through reflection. This improves performance without changing SLM weights. In clinical simulations, doctor agents improved treatment through experiential memory. • Shared memory-based learning: Agents pool experiences via shared memory. G-Memory [Zhang et al., 2025a] implements a hierarchical three-tier memory system (insight, 28 query, and interaction graphs) that achieves a 20.89% improvement in complex embodied tasks through cross-trial learning. • Collective intelligence optimization: EvoAgent [Yuan et al., 2025] uses genetic algorithms to evolve agent configurations and outperforms manually designed systems. 

Challenges and limitations. The AI Scientist paradigm still has capability gaps: Operating phys-ical laboratory devices remains at Technology Readiness Levels 4-6 and reconciling conflicting information from multiple sources (including paywalled data and empirical expert heuristics not captured in text) is an open problem. 

9.4 Discussion and recommendations 

The preceding analysis shows how the DSS components described in earlier sections (SLMs, KGs, and neurosymbolic integration) can be composed into agent architectures that address the three limitations of the monolithic paradigm identified in the Introduction section: energy cost (through modular, sparse activation), reasoning depth (through structured abstractions and specialist tools), and data quality (through closed-loop knowledge consolidation). 1. Collaborative: Heterogeneous multi-agent systems scale to 1,000+ agents without per-formance degradation and intelligent routing achieves 1.8-8.2% performance gains while reducing computational cost. In the future, each agent in such a system can be built atop a DSS society. 2. Self-evolving and continuously learning: From Genesis executing 1,000 experimental cycles per day to Agent Laboratory achieving 3.2/5 human-evaluated quality, the AI Scientist paradigm is moving from concept to practice. System-level CL through KG expansion and episodic memory can enable agents to acquire new knowledge without costly parametric retraining. 

Recommendations. 

• Adopt multi-agent frameworks: Move from single-prompt architectures to agentic work-flows (CoALA [Sumers et al., 2023]) with specialized sub-agents. Standardize communica-tion using emerging protocols (MCP, ANP, A2A) to enable interoperability. • Build for self-evolution: Design agents with explicit learning functions ( L) that update memory, world models, and reward signals from experience. Use shared memory archi-tectures (G-Memory [Zhang et al., 2025a]) that enable collective learning across agent teams. 

Open questions: Q37. How can we develop standardized protocols that enable agents to exchange not just text, but structured abstractions and logic proofs across heterogeneous networks? 

Q38. In a DSS society, how should the orchestrator resolve epistemic conflicts when domain specialists provide contradictory but internally consistent reasoning traces? Possible directions include confidence-weighted arbitration, where each back-end reports calibrated uncertainty alongside its answer, and formal verification, where conflicting claims are checked against the shared KG’s ontological constraints. 

Q39. Can we create a “Universal Reasoning API” that truly decouples the neural engine from the symbolic world model, enabling the symbolic layer to be updated in real-time? 

Q40. What are the limits of “emergent specialization”? Can we programmatically steer this evolution toward safer and more interpretable collaborative behaviors? Answering these questions will be necessary to move from monolithic generalists to modular, sustainable DSS societies, where each DSS SLM contributes depth in its domain and the society as a whole achieves the breadth that the top-down LLM paradigm pursues at far greater cost. 29 10 Energy efficiency and economic sustainability 

As outlined in Section 1, the prevailing “top-down” LLM paradigm relies on the premise that scaling parameters and data automatically improve performance. However, this trajectory faces a hard physical limit: energy. Intelligence does not scale in isolation; it scales with energy. As AI systems transition from research artifacts to global infrastructure, energy consumption becomes not merely an operational cost but a structural constraint that shapes what forms of intelligence are economically and physically viable. The AI ecosystem is undergoing a structural inversion where the dominant energetic and economic burden shifts from one-time training to recurring and potentially unbounded inference. This shift changes the optimization target from “capability-at-any-cost” to capability-per-Watt and capability-per-dollar , making model size, deployment modality (cloud vs. edge), and hardware stack first-order design variables. Under realistic product-scale usage, inference frequently dominates lifecycle impact. Sustainability is also constrained by localized physical bottlenecks—power delivery and water availability—which motivate the shift toward smaller DSS models, edge inference, and accelerator-model co-design.

This shows that specialized architectures and curated data can achieve depth without extreme scale. By reducing the capital and energy barrier, this bottom-up trajectory enables Sovereign AI–enabling universities, smaller enterprises, and developing nations to build intelligent systems tailored to their needs, without relying on a centralized, top-down “Stargate.” 

10.3 Inference energy: The dominant cost driver 

As inference volumes grow, deployment efficiency becomes the primary concern. The emergence of “reasoning models” introduces a new dimension: inference inflation , which we identify as a symptom of failing to use proper abstractions (see Section 4). 

Reasoning models and the 70-100 × multiplier. Traditional LLMs predict the next token directly. Reasoning models (Claude Opus 4.6, DeepSeek-R1), by contrast, generate extensive internal CoT tokens—a hidden monologue of reasoning and self-correction—before producing any visible output. This architectural difference has direct energy implications [Jegham et al., 2025, Jin et al., 2025]: • Standard LLM (GPT-4o) : ∼0.42 Wh per query [Bailey, 2025, Towards Data Science, 2025]. • Reasoning model (DeepSeek-R1) : ∼33 Wh per query for complex prompts. • Multiplier : 70-100 × increase in energy intensity per interaction. 

Test-time compute amplification. The industry is moving toward allocating additional compute at inference time rather than training time, mimicking human cognition (thinking longer on harder problems). Whereas test-time compute improves performance on complex benchmarks, empirical analysis shows energy costs can reach 113 × compared to standard inference on certain coding tasks [Jin et al., 2025]. This confirms the critique in Section 1: Relying on brute-force scale (or token generation) for reasoning is inefficient compared to principled, abstraction-driven mechanisms. 

Hidden token economics. These “reasoning tokens” are invisible to users but are real in both billing and energy consumption: • For complex tasks, the ratio of reasoning tokens to visible output can be extremely high. • Claude prices these invisible tokens as output tokens: $25/1M output tokens for Opus 4.6 [Anthropic, 2026]. • DeepSeek-R1 charges ∼$2.19/1M output tokens: an 11.4 × price differential [Intuition Labs, 2025a]. 10.4 Hardware architectures as enablers for DSS 

The hardware layer is fragmenting into specialized architectures. This fragmentation is not a bug but a feature that enables the DSS trajectory we propose, driven by the different demands of massive-scale training and low-latency reasoning inference. 

NVIDIA Blackwell: The incumbent’s response. Blackwell (B200/GB200) is NVIDIA’s response to rising energy costs [NVIDIA Developer Blog, 2025, NVIDIA Blog, 2025, Introl, 2025a]: • FP4 precision : Native support for 4-bit floating-point inference, effectively doubling throughput compared to FP8 and quadrupling compared to FP16. • Energy efficiency : Benchmarks show 25-50 × better per-token energy efficiency than the Hopper (H100) generation for specific inference workloads. • Performance : A single GB200 NVL72 rack is effectively a single massive unified GPU, with claims of 30 × faster inference for trillion-parameter models. 

Groq and the language processing unit (LPU) paradigm. Groq takes a different architectural approach, prioritizing latency and determinism over raw throughput density [Spallaprolu, 2025, HackerNoon, 2025, Pradhan, 2025]: • SRAM vs. HBM : Traditional GPUs store model weights in high-bandwidth memory (HBM) and must fetch them to compute cores for every token generated. This fetching process consumes significant energy and latency. Groq’s LPU stores weights directly in on-chip static RAM (SRAM), placing memory next to compute and eliminating the energy-intensive data movement across the memory bus. • Deterministic scheduling : GPUs use dynamic schedulers to manage instruction flow, which introduces overhead and “jitter.” Groq’s compiler pre-calculates the exact timing of every instruction before the chip runs. This deterministic execution means hardware utilization approaches 100%, with no cache misses or scheduling stalls. • Energy profile : Groq claims an energy efficiency of 1-3 Joules per token, compared to 10-30 Joules per token for traditional GPU-based inference: a ∼10 × advantage derived from eliminating dynamic scheduling and off-chip memory access overhead. • The capacity trade-off : A single Groq chip has only ∼230MB of memory. Carbon emissions receive the most attention, but AI infrastructure faces additional environmental constraints. Beyond carbon accounting lie hard physical constraints: power delivery capacity, cooling requirements, water usage, and transformer limitations. These localized bottlenecks constrain data center expansion irrespective of algorithmic progress. Energy is, therefore, not only an environmental variable but an infrastructure boundary condition .

Grid collision: The Virginia crisis. Northern Virginia (“Datacenter Alley”) handles approximately 70% of global Internet traffic and now faces a grid crisis [American Action Forum, 2025, Southern Environmental Law Center, 2025, Politico Pro, 2025, Grid Strategies LLC, 2025, NPR Illinois, 2025]: • Dominion Energy projections : Summer peak load expected to increase by 70% between 2022 and 2045, driven almost entirely by data centers. • Chesterfield Energy Reliability Center : A 1,000 MW natural gas peaker plant was approved despite a conflict with the Virginia Clean Economy Act’s decarbonization goals, justified under a “grid reliability” exemption. • Cost shifting : Transmission line upgrades are driving rate increases for residential cus-tomers; regulators are creating new “high-load” rate classes to force data centers to bear more infrastructure costs. • Emergency measures : PJM (the regional grid operator) has proposed allowing grid op-erators to curtail data center power first during emergencies, treating AI compute as an interruptible load. Environmental Protection Agency guidance clarifies that data centers may run backup diesel generators during “demand response” scenarios. 

The Irish paradigm: Self-generation mandates. Ireland, another global data center hub, provides a glimpse of future regulatory regimes [Energy Connects, 2025, Pinsent Masons, 2025, McGrath, 2024, Institute of International and European Affairs, 2024]: • Grid share : Data centers consume approximately 25% of Ireland’s national grid capacity: more than all urban residential consumption. • De facto moratorium : The Dublin region effectively suspended new data center grid connections from 2021. • 2025 rules : The moratorium was lifted with strict conditions: New data centers must generate power on-site (typically gas), be capable of exporting power back to the grid, and transition to 80% renewable procurement within several years. This, in effect, forces technology companies to become utility operators. 

The water-energy nexus. Data center water footprint can be decomposed into two components [En-vironmental and Energy Study Institute, 2024, ESG News, 2025, Online Learning Consortium, 2025, WSP, 2020, Chen and Wemhoff, 2022]: 1. Direct consumption (scope 1) : Water evaporated onsite for cooling. A large data center may consume 1.1 million gallons per day: equivalent to a town of 10,000 people. This is true consumption : water lost to the atmosphere. 2. Indirect consumption (scope 2) : Water used by thermoelectric power plants for electricity generation. This is typically 3-4 × higher than direct consumption. Each kWh of coal or nuclear power evaporates several liters of water at the source. 

Per-query water impact. Research indicates that a median Gemini query consumes approximately 0.26 mL of water [Online Learning Consortium, 2025]. Each query’s consumption is small on its own, but at billions of daily queries–and with the 70-100 × amplification factor for reasoning models—the aggregate amounts to billions of liters of freshwater annually. 

Efficiency metrics and mitigation. Water intensity varies substantially across facilities and cooling architectures. The industry-average water usage effectiveness remains approximately 36 1.8 L/kWh, whereas optimized hyperscale campuses operated by Microsoft report values as low as 0.30 L/kWh [ESG News, 2025]. Google further reports that roughly 64% of its freshwater with-drawals are replenished through watershed restoration and offset initiatives [Google Sustainability, 2025]. Concurrently, major providers are transitioning toward closed-loop and zero-water liquid cooling systems for next-generation AI accelerators, reducing reliance on evaporative cooling and mitigating community opposition in water-stressed regions [ESG News, 2025].

10.6 Economic analysis: Unit economics and market bifurcation 

Commercializing AI requires sound unit economics. At product scale, inference cost determines gross margins: If the marginal inference cost approaches or exceeds revenue per query, deployment becomes economically fragile. Large generalist models impose persistent high per-token compute costs, incentivizing centralization among a few capital-rich actors. 

The hidden token cost structure. Reasoning models generate thousands of invisible CoT tokens that are billed but not shown to users. For Claude’s Opus 4.6 model, these are priced as output tokens at $25/1M. A single complex query could cost several dollars in reasoning tokens alone, making flat-rate subscriptions economically unsustainable [Anthropic, 2026]. In contrast, DeepSeek-R1 charges ∼$2.19/1M output tokens: an 11.4 × price differential that re-flects not merely margin compression but fundamental differences in training efficiency and model architecture [Intuition Labs, 2025a, DeepSeek, 2026]. 

Market bifurcation. The market is fragmenting into two distinct segments: • Commodity intelligence : DeepSeek-style distilled models running cheaply on edge devices or older hardware [Leanware, 2025]. Users pay primarily for electricity, bypassing API margins. • Premium reasoning : Orion/Stargate-class systems that remain centralized, expensive, and energy-intensive, reserved for high-value enterprise tasks [Alpha Matica, 2025]. 

The Jevons paradox. Economic theory predicts that efficiency gains increase demand elasti-cally [Luccioni et al., 2025, Digitopoly, 2025]. If inference cost drops 90% due to efficient SLMs, we are unlikely to see a 90% reduction in energy use; instead, new applications (always-on agents, video generation, ubiquitous assistants) will absorb the savings and potentially increase total energy consumption. Efficiency alone does not guarantee sustainability; it must be paired with deliberate constraints or carbon pricing. 

10.7 Productivity and labor implications 

AI’s economic impact extends to the labor market, where electrical energy is substituting for human cognitive effort [Staffing Industry Analysts, 2023, LowTouch AI, 2025]. 37 White-collar turbulence. Generative AI primarily augments or automates cognitive, white-collar tasks. Reasoning models specifically target high-order tasks, such as software engineering and mathematical problem-solving. Roles involving routine cognitive work (coding, translation, basic analysis) face the highest displacement risk. A “reasoning wage premium” is emerging that benefits workers who can orchestrate these models, while a “growth without hiring” trend sees companies expanding output without proportional headcount increases. 

Blue-collar resilience. Physical jobs (plumbing, nursing, construction) remain largely unaffected due to Moravec’s Paradox : automating dexterous sensorimotor skills is harder than automating high-level reasoning. The net effect may be wage compression , where skilled trades become relatively more valuable compared to mid-tier cognitive labor. 

10.8 Domain-specific superintelligence: A sustainable path forward 

Energy constraints, economic pressure, and diminishing returns from monolithic scaling together point toward the alternative proposed in Section 1: DSS. Rather than pursuing AGI through ever-larger monolithic models, DSS replaces general-purpose giants with a coordinated ecosystem of specialized, efficient models—a Society of Mind where domain experts collaborate. Under this framing, intelligence is not defined by parameter count, but by task-relevant performance per unit of energy. 

The DSS architecture. DSS relies on SLMs trained to reach expert-level depth in narrow domains (e.g., organic chemistry, contract law, avionics diagnostics) rather than broad, shallow recall: • Data efficiency : Instead of scraping the entire Internet, DSS models are trained on high-quality, curated datasets (textbooks, proprietary corporate data, scientific papers). This “high-signal” training enables models with 7B-14B parameters to outperform LLMs in their specific niche domain. • Neurosymbolic scaling : DSS uses abstractions like KGs to ground reasoning (see Section 4). This enables verifiable, symbolic reasoning (GraphRAG, MCTS) that is more efficient and accurate than the probabilistic output of standard LLMs. By offloading factual recall to a KG, SLMs can focus on reasoning, reducing its parameter count and, crucially, avoiding the “inference inflation” of massive CoT generation. 

Energy advantages of DSS. The energy profile of a DSS-based ecosystem improves substantially on the Stargate paradigm: • Training : Training a 7B-parameter DSS requires a fraction of the energy of that needed for a frontier model. DeepSeek’s $5.5M training run shows that competitive intelligence can be built without GW-scale campuses, lowering the barrier to AI development. • Inference : SLMs are small enough to run on edge devices. An 8B-parameter model can run on a modern smartphone using ∼4-7 W of power [A-bots, 2025]. This eliminates the round-trip energy cost to a data center. • The edge advantage : A query processed locally on an NPU consumes mW and zero water. The same query sent to a cloud-based reasoning model consumes ∼33 Wh and ∼20 mL of water. For billions of daily transactions, DSS is the only environmentally viable path for widespread AI adoption. 

Agentic workflows and the “Society of Mind.” The DSS vision moves toward the AGI goal not as a single monolith, but as a “society” of specialized agents: • Modular intelligence : A “general” AI agent (likely a cloud-based router) acts as a conductor, breaking complex queries into sub-tasks and dispatching them to specialized DSS models. A medical diagnosis query might be routed to a “radiology expert” and a “pathology expert,” whose outputs are synthesized. • Hierarchical efficiency : This enables the system to use the “least capable model” necessary for each sub-task. Routine language tasks are handled by cheap, low-energy SLMs, while heavy reasoning is reserved for specific DSS models. By routing a large fraction of queries 38 to efficient SLMs and only a much smaller fraction to heavy reasoning models, the system maintains high capability while reducing aggregate energy use significantly. 

10.9 Strategic outlook 

The current trajectory—centralized, massive-scale inference on general-purpose hardware—is eco-nomically and environmentally unsustainable. The industry is approaching physical limits that software efficiency alone cannot resolve: 1. The energy wall : Orion’s 11 billion kWh training run is a harbinger. Next-generation models may require dedicated nuclear reactors or massive off-grid renewable arrays. Stargate’s 5 GW target acknowledges this reality. 2. Inference dominance : As reasoning models become standard, inference energy will perma-nently dwarf training energy. The “33 Wh per query” figure becomes the new baseline for high-value AI interactions. 3. Cost fracture : The market will split between “commodity intelligence” (DeepSeek-style distilled models on edge/legacy hardware) and “premium reasoning” (Orion/Stargate-class, centralized, expensive, energy-intensive). 4. Geographic rebalancing : Compute will migrate to power. Regulatory evolution : The Irish model—mandating on-site generation and grid export capability—will spread. Data centers may be classified as interruptible loads during grid emergencies. The economics of AI increasingly reduce to the efficiency of converting electricity and water into useful computation. Over the next decade, competitive advantage will depend as much on hardware efficiency and energy access as on algorithmic innovation. The transition to the DSS trajectory outlined in this article is not merely an algorithmic preference but a thermodynamic necessity. By shifting from top-down monoliths to bottom-up, modular societies of models, we can reconcile the demand for superintelligence with the constraints of our planet’s resources. If the top-down paradigm leads to centralized, GW-scale “Stargates,” the bottom-up approach culminates in the Pocket Sage : a decentralized ecosystem of specialized intelligence that restores human agency and economic equity.

For most real-world applications, maximal generality is neither required nor economically optimal. The objective should be task-relevant sufficiency : achieving or exceeding expert-level performance within a constrained domain under strict energy and deployment budgets. 11.2 Domain-specific superintelligence: The “Society of Mind” architecture 

To address both the physical limits of scaling and the epistemic limits of probability, we propose a transition to DSS, as discussed in Sections 7 and 8, which reimagines AGI as a coordinated ecosystem—a Society of Mind —composed of specialized, interoperable agents rather than a single monolithic model [Institute for Energy Research, 2025]. DSS differs from traditional AGI aspirations in three respects: • Constraint-aware optimization: Intelligence is evaluated under energy, latency, and cost budgets, not in isolation. • Architectural modularity: Specialized models replace monolithic universal networks, activating only the expertise required for each task. • Deployment locality: Whenever feasible, inference occurs near the point of use, reducing infrastructure load and latency. This paradigm does not reject general intelligence research; it recognizes that sustainable large-scale deployment favors specialization over universalization. 

11.2.1 The orchestrator and the experts 

In the DSS architecture, intelligence is modular. A lightweight orchestrator agent (typically a fine-tuned SLM) is the executive control center. It does not attempt to solve every problem itself; instead, it parses complex user intent, decomposes it into sub-tasks, and routes these tasks to specialized 

expert agents [Institute for Energy Research, 2025]. For example, a complex query regarding a supply chain disruption might be decomposed into: 40 1. Weather expert: An SLM connected to meteorological APIs to forecast storm impacts. 2. Logistics expert: An agent grounded in the company’s Enterprise Resource Planning database and a logistics ontology to identify affected shipments. 3. Legal expert: A neurosymbolic agent trained on contract law to determine liability for delayed deliveries. The orchestrator then synthesizes the outputs of these experts into a coherent response. This architec-ture has several advantages: • Asymptotic competence: The system’s intelligence grows by adding new specialized modules (e.g., a “customs compliance expert”) rather than retraining a trillion-parameter giant [Tran et al., 2025]. • Verifiable specialization: Each expert agent is constrained by a curated corpus and a rigorous domain ontology. The legal expert does not “hallucinate” case law; it retrieves verifiable precedents from a legal KG [Koenecke et al., 2025]. • Hierarchical efficiency: Routine tasks are handled by small, efficient models, whereas energy-intensive reasoning is reserved for complex integration. This reduces the aggre-gate energy cost per task by orders of magnitude compared to using a frontier model for everything [Institute for Energy Research, 2025]. 

11.2.2 Interoperability and the Internet of agents 

For a Society of Mind to function, agents must share a common language for negotiation and data exchange. Standardized protocols are emerging that act as the “USB-C for AI.” The MCP and A2A standards enable diverse agents—built by different vendors, running on different clouds or devices— to discover each other, advertise their capabilities, and collaborate on tasks [World Economic Forum, 2025]. These protocols enable an Internet of Agents where a healthcare agent from a hospital can securely query an insurance agent from a payer and a scheduling agent from a patient’s phone, orchestrating a complex medical appointment without a single central platform owning the entire interaction [Chen et al., 2024b]. This decentralization supports a competitive ecosystem that prevents vendor lock-in and promotes “antifragility”: if one model fails, the network can route around it. 

11.3 The Pocket Sage: Smartphone-resident intelligence 

A natural endpoint of DSS is the Pocket Sage : a domain-specific expert that resides entirely on a user’s smartphone, requiring no cloud connectivity, no subscription fees, and no data exfiltration. As detailed in Section 10, recent progress in edge hardware and model compression makes this technically feasible. 

11.3.1 From cloud dependency to edge sovereignty 

Modern smartphone processors—such as Apple’s A18 Pro and Qualcomm’s Snapdragon 8 Elite— now include dedicated NPUs capable of running 7-13B-parameter models at interactive speeds while consuming only mW of power [Notebookcheck, 2024, XDA Developers, 2024]. Combined with the quantization and distillation techniques, a high-quality DSS can be compressed to fit within the memory constraints of a typical smartphone (8-16 GB RAM) while retaining 95%+ of its reasoning capability. This decouples intelligence from connectivity. Farmers in rural Kenya do not need a 5G connection to a distant data center; a Maize Agronomy DSS trained on local soil types, pest patterns, and weather cycles can run entirely on their device. Factory technicians in a subterranean facility—where connectivity is impossible—can consult a Hydraulic System DSS trained on equipment manuals and failure modes. This eliminates the “capability tax” imposed by cloud subscriptions and bandwidth requirements. 41 11.3.2 The energy arithmetic of edge intelligence 

The energy economics strongly favor edge deployment. As discussed in Section 10, a cloud-based query to a frontier reasoning model consumes approximately 33 Wh: enough to fully charge a smartphone. In contrast, running an equivalent query on an on-device SLM consumes approximately 0.001-0.01 Wh—roughly 1,000-10,000 × smaller [A-bots, 2025]. This efficiency gap means that a smartphone battery could power thousands of expert consultations before requiring a recharge. The “Stargate” becomes unnecessary when intelligence is distributed to the edge. Edge deployment also eliminates the water consumption associated with data center cooling, carbon emissions from long-haul data transmission, and privacy risks inherent in sending sensitive queries to third-party servers. By design, Pocket Sage is sustainable, private, and under user control. 

11.4 The neurosymbolic bridge: Restoring reliability with knowledge graphs 

The technical foundation of DSS is neurosymbolic AI (as discussed in Section 6): the fusion of linguistic fluency of neural networks (“System 1”) with the logical rigor of symbolic systems (“System 2”). Neural networks excel at intuition and generating plausible text; symbolic systems provide the verifiable, step-by-step logic required for high-stakes reasoning [Colelough and Regli, 2025]. 

11.4.1 GraphRAG: Beyond the vector 

GraphRAG improves on standard vector-based RAG. Vector databases retrieve isolated chunks of text based on fuzzy semantic similarity; GraphRAG instead retrieves structured relationships from a KG [Peng et al., 2024a]. In the DSS context, a KG is the “ground truth.” It explicitly models entities (e.g., “protein,” “drug,” “statute”) and their relationships (e.g., “inhibits,” “is_precedent_for”). When an expert agent receives a query, it does not merely scan for keywords; it traverses the graph to find causal chains. For instance, in a pharmaceutical context, GraphRAG enables multi-hop reasoning: “Drug A targets Protein B. Protein B is upregulated in Disease C. Therefore, Drug A may treat Disease C” [Edge et al., 2024]. Benchmarks indicate that GraphRAG significantly outperforms vector methods in complex reasoning tasks, particularly where schemas and precise constraints are involved [Ahmad et al., 2025]. 

11.4.2 Proof of reasoning and audit trails 

In high-stakes domains, “black box” decisions are legally and ethically unacceptable. A medical DSS cannot simply output a diagnosis; it must provide a “proof of reasoning”: an immutable, cryptographic log of the logical steps taken to reach its conclusion [Calo and Lo, 2026]. This proof might look like: 1. Observation: Patient exhibits symptoms X, Y, Z. 2. Retrieval: KG query identifies three potential conditions that match symptoms. 3. Exclusion: Condition A is excluded due to Lab Result W (based on rule #421 in Medical Ontology). 4. Conclusion: Diagnosis is Condition B with 95% confidence. This audit trail enables human professionals to verify AI logic, making the user an active auditor of reasoning rather than a passive consumer of outputs [Institute for Energy Research, 2025]. This “glass box” approach is essential for regulatory compliance and for keeping professionals accountable in the decision loop. 

11.5 Augmenting productivity of every worker: DSS as a productivity multiplier 

DSS is designed to amplify what workers can do rather than replace them, giving professionals access to expert-level guidance in their domain regardless of location or socioeconomic status. This applies to both white-collar knowledge workers and blue-collar skilled tradespeople. 42 11.5.1 White-collar productivity augmentation: Cognitive offloading with accountability 

For knowledge workers, DSS is an epistemic backstop : a second opinion that is always available, never fatigued, and grounded in verifiable knowledge. 

Medical professionals. A “clinical DSS” resident on a physician’s tablet can cross-reference patient symptoms against the latest diagnostic criteria, flag potential drug interactions, and retrieve relevant case studies from the medical literature. In rural clinics with limited specialist access, this provides a diagnostic capability approaching that of a tertiary research hospital. The DSS does not replace the physician’s judgment; it supplements it with instant access to the medical knowledge encoded in the KG [Prenosil et al., 2025]. 

Legal professionals. A “judicial DSS” can assist judges and lawyers by retrieving relevant prece-dents, identifying analogous cases across jurisdictions, and flagging potential procedural issues. Where a general-purpose LLM might hallucinate citations, the legal DSS is grounded in a veri-fied ontology of case law, statutes, and regulations, making every citation traceable to its source document [Koenecke et al., 2025]. 

Financial analysts. A “portfolio DSS” can synthesize market data, regulatory filings, and macroe-conomic indicators to provide real-time risk assessments. By grounding its analysis in structured financial ontologies, it avoids the speculative “predictions” that plague general-purpose chatbots. 

11.5.2 Blue-collar empowerment: Multimodal procedural guidance 

For skilled trades, DSS operates through multimodal grounding : combining visual recognition, sensor data, and procedural manuals to provide real-time, context-aware guidance. 

Factory technicians. A “maintenance DSS” trained on equipment manuals, failure mode databases, and repair histories can diagnose machine errors via camera input and provide step-by-step repair instructions overlaid on an augmented reality (AR) display. A technician facing an unfamiliar error code no longer needs to wait for a specialist; the DSS provides immediate, verified guidance. The target metric is the reduction of mean-time-to-repair and the elimination of catastrophic errors caused by procedural mistakes [Tulip, 2026]. 

Agricultural workers. A “precision agriculture DSS” running on a farmer’s smartphone can analyze soil samples (via camera-based spectroscopy apps), cross-reference local weather forecasts, and recommend optimal planting schedules, irrigation timing, and pest management strategies. This DSS is trained not on generic agricultural data, but on a KG of local conditions: specific crop varieties suited to the region, endemic pest species, and traditional farming practices that have proven effective over generations [Netafim, 2025, A-bots, 2025]. 

Construction workers. A “building codes DSS” can verify that construction practices comply with local regulations, flagging potential violations before they become costly rework. By integrating with building information models, it can provide spatial guidance for complex installations. 

Electricians and plumbers. Specialized “trade DSS” agents trained on wiring diagrams, plumbing schematics, and safety codes can guide tradespeople through unfamiliar installations, reducing the risk of code violations and safety hazards. 

11.5.3 The human-in-the-loop guardrail 

DSS systems are designed as recommenders , not deciders . For irreversible physical actions—cutting a wire, administering a medication, filing a legal motion—the system enforces mandatory “human-in-the-loop” verification steps. The worker remains the active agent; the DSS provides information but the human bears final responsibility. This design prevents “automation bias” and keeps professional judgment at the center of decision-making [Parseur, 2025]. 43 11.6 Education: Restoring abstraction as the pedagogical center 

A direct application of DSS is in education. As argued in Section 1, intelligence begins by forming structured mental models (abstractions). By grounding AI tutors in domain-specific KGs, we restore abstraction as the prerequisite for learning, creating personalized experiences that combine the rigor of structured curricula with the adaptability of one-on-one instruction. 

11.6.1 Every textbook as a knowledge graph 

In the DSS paradigm, every textbook, manual, and corpus of domain knowledge is accompanied by an explicit KG that encodes its conceptual structure. This graph represents facts and, more importantly, the relationships between concepts: prerequisites, implications, analogies, and common misconceptions. For example, a KG for introductory physics might encode: • Concept: Newton’s Second Law ( F = ma )• Prerequisites: concepts of force, mass, and acceleration • Implications: conservation of momentum, projectile motion, orbital mechanics • Common misconceptions: confusing mass with weight; assuming F = ma applies only to constant forces • Abstraction level: intermediate (requires algebra, no calculus) This structured representation enables the AI tutor to diagnose gaps in understanding by traversing the prerequisite graph. If a student struggles with projectile motion, the tutor can identify whether the root cause is a misunderstanding of Newton’s Second Law, a gap in vector algebra, or a misconception about gravitational acceleration. 

11.6.2 Unlimited high-quality question-answer-reasoning trace generation 

A practical advantage of KG-grounded education is the ability to generate essentially unlimited high-quality question-answer-reasoning trace triplets. Unlike static question banks, a DSS tutor can dynamically construct novel problems by: 1. Sampling a target concept from the KG. 2. Instantiating the concept with novel parameters (e.g., different masses, velocities, contexts). 3. Generating a question that tests understanding of the concept. 4. Producing a step-by-step reasoning chain that derives the answer from first principles. 5. Creating plausible distractors based on common misconceptions encoded in the KG. This capability is useful to both test-givers (educators, certification bodies) and test-takers (students). Educators gain access to an inexhaustible supply of valid, calibrated assessment items. Students gain access to unlimited practice problems, each accompanied by a detailed explanation: not a canned solution but a reasoning trace that teaches the underlying logic and rules of abstraction. 

11.6.3 Teaching abstractions: Key to deep learning 

The DSS approach emphasizes abstraction as the core skill to be cultivated. Rather than memorizing facts or procedures, students learn to recognize patterns, generalize from examples, and apply principles to novel situations. The KG explicitly encodes abstraction hierarchies: showing how specific examples instantiate general principles and how general principles are derived from even more fundamental axioms. This is DL in the pedagogical sense: understanding that F = ma is not just an equation to be memorized, but an instance of the broader principle that changes in motion require external influence. A DSS tutor trained on such a KG can guide students up and down the abstraction ladder, ensuring that procedural fluency is always grounded in conceptual understanding [Aho and Ullman, 2022]. 44 11.6.4 The AI tutor as a world expert 

The long-term goal of DSS in education is broad access to high-quality instruction. Today, the quality of education a student receives depends heavily on geography and socioeconomic status. A student in a well-funded suburban school may have access to expert teachers; a student in an under-resourced rural school may not. DSS can narrow this gap: A “mathematics DSS” trained by leading mathematicians and grounded in a rigorous mathematical ontology can provide Socratic tutoring to any student with a smartphone. This does not replace human teachers; it frees them. Educators can shift from routine drill-and-practice to mentorship and creativity, where human judgment matters most. 

11.7 Breaking the data wall: Synthetic curricula and “textbooks” 

To overcome the data wall and the threat of model collapse, DSS adopts a new paradigm for model training: Synthetic Curricula . Rather than relying on the noisy, unstructured web, we can use KGs and high-performing teacher models to programmatically generate vast amounts of high-signal training data [Institute for Energy Research, 2025]. 

11.7.1 The “textbooks are all you need” paradigm 

The efficacy of this approach was demonstrated by Microsoft’s Phi [Abdin et al., 2024] series of models. By training on “textbook-quality” synthetic data—carefully curated to teach reasoning principles rather than just rote facts—the 1.3B-parameter Phi-1 outperformed models 100 × its size on coding benchmarks [Gunasekar et al., 2023]. DSS extends this “Textbooks Are All You Need” philosophy to every domain. An “organic chemistry DSS” would be trained not on scraped PDFs, but on a synthetic curriculum generated from a rigorous chemistry ontology. This curriculum would systematically cover every reaction type, molecular structure, and bonding rule, presenting them in diverse contexts and difficulty levels [Qin et al., 2025]. This “high-signal” training enables SLMs to reach expert-level depth in their specific niche without the massive parameter count required to “memorize the Internet.” This method also mitigates model collapse. Because the synthetic data are generated from a structured, verifiable ontology, they provide a “genetic template” that preserves variance and factual integrity [Ma et al., 2025b]. The KG ensures that the model trains on the logic of the domain, not just the statistical artifacts of previous model outputs. 

11.7.2 Connecting to training efficiency 

As detailed in Section 10, training efficiency is not merely an economic concern but an ethical imperative. The DSS approach substantially reduces training costs through three mechanisms: 1. Smaller models: By focusing on narrow domains, DSS requires models with 1-13B parameters rather than hundreds of billions, reducing training compute by 10-100 ×.2. Higher-quality data: Synthetic curricula generated from KGs provide higher information density than web scrapes, meaning fewer training tokens are needed to achieve equivalent capability. 3. Transfer learning: Domain-specific models can be fine-tuned from efficient base models (e.g., Phi, Llama), rather than trained from scratch, further reducing energy expenditure. The result is that training a world-class DSS for a specific domain requires a fraction of the energy, water, and capital needed to train a frontier generalist model. This opens AI development to a wider range of institutions: universities, professional associations, and individual enterprises can create expert systems tailored to their needs. 

11.8 Economic restructuring: From top-down dystopia to bottom-up opportunity 

A dominant narrative in public discourse frames AI as an engine of mass unemployment: a technology that will automate white-collar jobs. This “top-down dystopia” envisions a future where a small 45 techno-elite controls omniscient AGI systems while the majority of workers are rendered economically superfluous. We argue this framing is a consequence of the scaling paradigm. The bottom-up trajectory of DSS offers a different path, one that favors productivity augmentation over replacement, job creation alongside displacement, and distributed over concentrated economic gains. 

11.8.1 Historical precedent: Productivity as the engine of progress 

Throughout modern history, increases in productivity have been the primary driver of improvements in living standards. The DSS framework is designed to continue this pattern by raising the productivity floor across all occupations, rather than concentrating gains among a small elite. When every worker is empowered by a domain-specific expert on the phone, the aggregate wealth of society increases, creating demand for new goods and services. 

11.8.2 New labor market: Roles in the DSS ecosystem 

The deployment of DSS networks creates demand for new categories of labor: jobs that draw on human expertise in ways that scale-driven AI cannot replicate. We identify three tiers of emerging roles: 

Entry level: Data curators (high school graduates). The DSS ecosystem requires vast quantities of high-quality, domain-specific data. Unlike the “clickwork” of simple image labeling, data curation 

for DSS demands contextual awareness and domain familiarity. These roles provide meaningful employment for workers without advanced degrees, with wages significantly above minimum wage due to the specialized knowledge required [O-Mega AI, 2025]. 

Intermediate level: Synthetic data engineers (college graduates). Creating the synthetic curricula that train DSS requires a new profession: the “synthetic data engineer.” These professionals design the templates, verification protocols, and quality metrics that govern curriculum generation. They work at the intersection of domain expertise and machine learning, translating expert knowledge into structured formats. 

Advanced level: Ontology architects (Ph.D students and researchers). At the apex of the DSS labor market are “ontology architects”: the experts who define the conceptual structure of KGs. These are domain masters—physicians, lawyers, scientists—who encode their tacit knowledge into explicit representations. This is demanding work that commands high compensation, as it directly determines the quality and reliability of the resulting AI system [SignalFire, 2025, Built In, 2025]. 

11.8.3 Data dignity and the data-centric vision 

The shift to DSS requires a fundamental restructuring of the data economy. As noted in Section 1, good reasoning depends on high-quality, well-curated data. This data-centric view establishes a new social contract based on data dignity [RadicalxChange, 2026]. In the DSS economy, structured, verifiable expertise is more valuable than raw compute. Companies like Surge AI are already paying PhD-level experts hundreds of dollars per hour for specialized data curation. This establishes a reasoning wage premium, shifting economic value back to the human creators of knowledge who provide high-quality curation that models need to generalize. 

11.8.4 Data coalitions and collective bargaining 

To operationalize data dignity, we envision the rise of data coalitions: collective bargaining entities that pool the data of professionals and negotiate licensing terms with AI developers [RadicalxChange Foundation, 2023]. A radiologist data coalition might license a high-quality dataset of annotated scans, so that the doctors receive a royalty stream. This creates a self-reinforcing cycle: Experts are incentivized to maintain the high-quality KGs that power the DSS, which in turn preserves the system’s long-term epistemic integrity. 46 11.9 Global equity: Sovereign AI and the compute divide

11.10 Autonomous discovery and self-driving laboratories 

DSS may have its largest impact on the pace of scientific discovery. The “brain” of the A-Lab is a DSS composed of specialized agents: one to scour the literature and generate hypotheses (hypothesis agent), one to plan the synthesis recipe (experiment agent), and one to control the robotic arms and analyze the results (execution agent) [Zhou et al., 2025]. With KGs, these agents can reason over the entirety of published chemical literature, finding connections and “dark data” that no single human can synthesize. This approach could accelerate progress in clean energy materials, drug discovery, and climate mitigation while maintaining epistemic integrity. The DSS does not just guess a new material; it produces a verifiable synthesis recipe and an audit trail of its reasoning, enabling human scientists to validate and build upon the machine’s work [Clarivate, 2026].

# 12 Conclusion 

DSS is a shift from black-box scaling to structured, verifiable reasoning. It takes the physical limits of energy and water, the finite supply of high-quality training data, and the economic value of human expertise seriously. By distributing intelligence across specialized agents rather than concentrating it in monolithic models, DSS embeds expert-level reasoning into daily workflows. The practical vision is concrete: a doctor in a remote village with diagnostic capability approaching that of a research hospital; a farmer with access to agronomic knowledge tailored to local conditions; a factory technician who can consult an expert system for any piece of equipment; a student working with an AI tutor that provides grounded explanations. GW-scale data centers like Stargate may serve frontier research, but the Pocket Sage —an efficient, verifiable expert running on a phone—is the form factor that scales to billions of users. DSS grounds intelligence in verifiable reasoning, makes it accessible without cloud connectivity, and aligns it with environmental sustainability and human expertise. The history of computing repeatedly demonstrates that monolithic systems give way to modular, optimized architectures once deployment scale increases. AI appears to be approaching a similar transition point. Scaling laws enabled rapid capability gains during the pre-deployment phase, but as systems become infrastructural, energy efficiency, economic sustainability, and deployment constraints reshape the optimization landscape. The next phase of AI development will be defined not by the largest models, but by the most efficient ones: the disciplined allocation of computational resources toward bounded, high-value tasks under real-world constraints.
