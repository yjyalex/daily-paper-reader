## Abstract 

Building facade defect inspection is fundamental to structural health monitoring and sustainable urban maintenance, yet it remains a formidable challenge due to extreme geometric variability, low con-trast against complex backgrounds, and the inher-ent complexity of composite defects (e.g., cracks co-occurring with spalling). Such characteristics lead to severe pixel imbalance and feature am-biguity, which, coupled with the critical scarcity of high-quality pixel-level annotations, hinder the generalization of existing detection and segmen-tation models. To address gaps, we propose Fa-cadeFixer , a unified multi-agent framework that treats defect perception as a collaborative reason-ing task rather than isolated recognition. Specif-ically, FacadeFixer orchestrates specialized agents for detection and segmentation to handle multi-type defect interference, working in tandem with a generative agent to enable semantic recompo-sition. This process decouples intricate defects from noisy backgrounds and realistically synthe-sizes them onto diverse clean textures, generating high-fidelity augmented data with precise expert-level masks. To support this, we introduce a com-prehensive multi-task dataset covering six primary facade categories with pixel-level annotations. Ex-tensive experiments demonstrate that FacadeFixer 

significantly outperforms state-of-the-art (SOTA) baselines. Specifically, it excels in capturing pixel-level structural anomalies and highlights generative synthesis as a robust solution to data scarcity in in-frastructure inspection. Our code and dataset will be made publicly available.

## 1 Introduction 

Automated assessment of building facade integrity is essen-tial for ensuring structural reliability and optimizing main-tenance strategies, particularly as aging infrastructure leads to progressive deterioration [Faqih and Zayed, 2021; Zhang 

et al. , 2025a]. Such damage not only imposes surpassing 4% of annual construction costs, but also poses significant public safety and social implications [de Freitas, 2013; Shi and Ergan, 2025]. Despite the proliferation of deep learning in structural health monitoring (SHM), autonomous, high-fidelity inspection remains a formidable challenge, plagued by several intrinsic complexities: due to the intrinsic topolog-ical variability and semantic co-occurrence of facade patholo-gies [Yang et al. , 2022a]. First, facade pathologies exhibit vast morphological diver-sity and topological variability, ranging from fine-grained, elongated crack filaments to irregular, patch-like spalling. The lack of consistent geometric priors across these hetero-geneous defect types complicates the convergence of gener-alized perception models [Goo et al. , 2025; Xu et al. , 2025]. Additionally, a severe scale imbalance persists where thin cracks with the extreme aspect ratio may occupy only a few pixels in width while stretching across thousands [Zhu et al. , 2023; Zhou and Tiong, 2025]. This extreme conditions often lead to biased training and significant miss rates for sparse defects. Moreover, unlike isolated anomalies, facade defects frequently manifest as composite entities with dif-fuse boundaries. Pathologies such as rust stains and plaster degradation often co-occur or serve as precursors to structural cracks[Zhang et al. , 2025a; Faqih and Zayed, 2021]. This inherent semantic complexity significantly hinders the gener-alization of existing models, most of which are confined to isolated visual paradigms such as patch-level classification, bounding-box detection and pixel-level segmentation[Yang et al. , 2022a]. This task isolation has led to a systemic imbal-ance in the research landscape. More geometrically irregular and visually diffuse defects(e.g. spalling and rust staining), remain critically under-investigated due to the scarcity of in-tegrated multi-task benchmarks.

Recent advances in large-scale foundation models, partic-ularly Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs) such as Gemini3, Qwen-VL, and GPT-4o, have demonstrated re-markable capabilities in zero-shot generalization and com-plex visual reasoning [Carion et al. , 2025; Wang et al. , 2025]. However, their performance in building facade inspection re-mains constrained by a lack of domain-specific structural pri-ors. General-purpose models frequently exhibit feature ambi-guity when encountering the diffuse boundaries and semantic interference characteristic of architectural defects [Zhang et al. , 2025c; Wu et al. , 2025]. To bridge these gaps, we propose FacadeFixer , a unified multi-agent framework that reimagines defect perception as a collaborative reasoning task rather than isolated recogni-tion. By orchestrating specialized expert models alongside powerful foundation models, FacadeFixer interactively re-solves multi-type defect interference through an adjudication mechanism, significantly enhancing the robustness and gen-eralization of the inspection pipeline for building pathology inspection. To support this framework, we collect a compre-hensive multi-task dataset acquired via Unmanned Aerial Ve-hicles (UAVs), covering six primary defect categories with pixel-level annotations. Furthermore, to alleviate the critical bottleneck of data scarcity and the labor-intensive nature of manual annotation, the framework integrates a Defect Memory Bank and a Gen-erative Recomposition Agent. These components facilitate a novel Semantic Recomposition process, which first de-couples intricate pathologies from noisy architectural back-grounds via semantic inpainting to establish a defect-free baseline. Subsequently, the system executes a mask-guided retrieval and generative blending mechanism, enabling the autonomous synthesis of high-fidelity augmented imagery that maintains expert-level semantic fidelity. This provides a scalable and high-quality training resource, effectively ex-panding the data distribution to support robust defect inspec-tion in building health monitoring. Therefore, our contribu-tions are three-fold: • We propose FacadeFixer , a unified multi-agent frame-work that leverages a collaborative reasoning pipeline and MLLM-based adjudication to resolve semantic in-terference in facade defects. • We present a comprehensive multi-task dataset featuring six primary defect categories with precise pixel-level la-bels for detection, segmentation and restoration. • We introduce a semantic recomposition mechanism driven by a generative agent and a defect memory bank, which enables the restoration of defect-free backgrounds via inpainting and the automated synthesis of high-fidelity training samples that maintain expert-level se-mantic fidelity. The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Sec-tion 2 reviews related work on implicit content detection and multimodal analysis. Section 3 introduces the building defect dataset. Section 4 describes the proposed Facade-Fixer framework, detailing the collaborative reasoning of per-ception agents and the semantic recomposition mechanism. Section 5 outlines the experimental setup, while Section 6 presents the results and comprehensive analyses. Finally, Section 7 concludes the paper and discusses potential direc-tions for future research. 

## 2 Related Work 

Building facades inevitably degrade over time due to factors such as environmental exposure, material aging, improper us-age, and insufficient maintenance [Faqih and Zayed, 2021; Zhang et al. , 2025a]. While facade inspection predominantly relies on periodic manual surveys assisted by non-destructive technologies [Yang et al. , 2022a; Faqih et al. , 2020], such practices are inherently subjective, labor-intensive, and prone to human error. There is a burgeoning interest in developing automated frameworks to enhance the efficiency and objec-tivity of structural health monitoring. 

2.1 Conventional Vision-based Inspection 

The first stream of conventional vision-based defect inspec-tion primarily relied on explicit keywords, predefined rules, and handcrafted visual features. Early image processing tech-niques, such as edge detection and local entropy threshold-ing, were manually designed to capture defect patterns [Chen 

et al. , 2021; German et al. , 2012]. However, these methods struggled to effectively model the vast stochastic variations of real-world defects, which often exhibit complex and un-predictable visual characteristics [Ma et al. , 2021]. The resurgence of deep learning has significantly shifted the paradigm toward end-to-end feature learning [LeCun et al. , 2015; Chen et al. , 2023]. Rather than relying on hand-crafted priors, [Cha et al. , 2017; Cha et al. , 2018] suc-cessfully implemented CNNs for direct defect identification. [Gao and Mosalam, 2018] introduced Structural ImageNet, demonstrating that transfer learning from pre-trained VG-GNet architectures could significantly alleviate overfitting is-sues in structural health monitoring. These studies revolves around three hierarchical paradigms: classification, detection, and segmentation [Yang et al. , 2022a; Zhang et al. , 2025b; Yang et al. , 2022b]. However, many existing models con-tinue to focus on single-task solutions, which limit their abil-ity to address the full complexity of facade inspection. Also, even advanced deep learning models face significant compu-tational and data constraints when confronted with the topo-logical complexity and class imbalance inherent in building facade inspection n, such as topological irregularities, diffuse boundaries, and the semantic co-occurrence [Wu et al. , 2025]. Such limitations frequently lead to poor generalization and a performance bottleneck when models are confronted with the extreme class imbalance and intricate facade pathologies found in complex urban environments. 

2.2 Foundation Models and Multi-modal Reasoning 

The emergence of foundation models has marked a piv-otal milestone in artificial intelligence, characterized by ex-traordinary zero-shot generalization without the need for task-specific pre-training [Achiam et al. , 2023; Mohamed and Omaisan, 2025]. Architectures such as the SAM have redefined image segmentation through promptable visual guides[Carion et al. , 2025]. In the context of industrial in-spection, recent adaptations such as DefectSAM leverage Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) and few-shot learning to ex-tract robust priors for anomaly detection [Wu et al. , 2025]. Similarly, IIIM-SAM explores internal information mining to enable zero-shot texture anomaly detection without the need for external prompts [Zhang et al. , 2025c]. Additionally, multi-agent systems (MAS) have emerged as a promising paradigm for orchestrating complex, multi-task workflows [Liu et al. , 2025b; Pan and Wu, 2025; Zuo 

et al. , 2025]. For instance, InfraGPT streamlines infrastruc-ture monitoring by coupling YOLO-based architectures with VLMs, transforming detections into context-aware diagnostic summaries[Mohamed and Omaisan, 2025]. Similarly, [Zhao 

et al. , 2025] proposed BiomedParse, which integrates SAM-2 and GPT-4 to jointly perform segmentation, detection, and recognition across diverse medical imaging modalities. How-ever, despite these advancements in multi-modal and multi-agent reasoning, a comprehensive multi-agent system for fa-cade defect inspection remains largely unexplored. A critical bottleneck is the pervasive scarcity of high-fidelity datasets that encompass diverse defect morphologies and multi-level annotations (spanning patch, bounding-box, and pixel scales) [Yang et al. , 2022a]. To address these limitations, we intro-duce FacadeFixer , a unified multi-agent framework that har-monizes defect perception, semantic restoration, and genera-tive data augmentation. By orchestrating specialized agents through a collaborative reasoning pipeline, FacadeFixer not only overcomes the data scarcity constraint but also estab-lishes a new paradigm for expert-level facade defect annota-tions. 

## 3 Datasets 

To facilitate comprehensive research in facade defect under-standing, we established a high-fidelity, multi-task dataset. This benchmark supports classification, detection, and seg-mentation tasks across six primary defect categories: con-crete crack, spalling, rust stain, degraded plaster, vegetation, and contaminants. 

3.1 Data Acquisition and Pre-processing 

The raw data were captured via UAVs across 112 residen-tial buildings,with each entry georeferenced by precise times-tamps and spatial coordinates. Each entry is georeferenced with precise timestamps, coordinates, and altitudes. High-resolution facade images were extracted from video streams via frame sampling. To ensure data quality and visual di-versity, the initial corpus was refined through a three-stage automated filtering pipeline: • Semantic Deduplication: To minimize data redundancy, we utilized the CLIP model to extract high-dimensional embeddings for each image. Images exhibiting high co-sine similarity were pruned to ensure visual diversity. • Contextual Noise Filtering: Leveraging the vision-language alignment of CLIP, we applied negative prompts (e.g., ”pipes,” ”windows,” ”air conditioners”) to exclude images with excessive background clutter. 3.2 Tiered Expert-Level Annotation Protocol 

The annotation process followed a rigorous two-stage work-flow to ensure diagnostic precision. Initially, a profes-sional annotation team was commissioned to perform com-prehensive bounding-box detection labeling for the entire dataset. Building upon these detection priors, a hybrid semi-automated strategy was implemented for the pixel-level seg-mentation task, synergizing expert models with human-in-the-loop verification. First, to prioritize structural safety and mitigate the risk of false negatives, we employed an ensem-ble union strategy for crack segmentation. By invoking multi-ple specialized models trained on open crack datasets includ-ing Unet VGG16 [Ha, 2023], YOLOv8-crack-seg[OpenSis-temas, 2023], and Segformer[Xie et al. , 2021], we computed the pixel-wise union of their outputs. This ensemble approach maximizes recall, ensuring that fine-grained structural fila-ments are not omitted. Two civil engineering ex-perts independently performed pixel-level refinement on the model-generated results, ensuring the final annotations meet the rigorous requirements for structural diagnostic tasks. ## 4 Method 

4.1 Task Definition 

The goal of FacadeFixer is to achieve autonomous facade health monitoring and high-fidelity data synthesis through a multi-agent collaborative paradigm. Formally, given a raw fa-cade observation Iraw ∈ RH×W ×3 and a user-specified con-textual profile P, the framework aims to transform abstract intentions into a sequence of verifiable inspection states. We define the problem as a multi-stage transformation process coordinated by an Orchestration Agent. 

Collaborative Defect Perception 

Let P = {k1, k 2, . . . , k n} denote the set of active task tog-gles (e.g., localization, segmentation) defined in the Instruc-tion Configuration. The Orchestrator Agent parses P and dispatches specialized expert agents accordingly. For each defect instance i belonging to category c ∈ { 1, . . . , 6}, the perception task aims to produce the following outputs. For bounding boxes: 

B = {bi | bi = ( x, y, w, h, p i)},

where (x, y ) denotes the top-left coordinate, (w, h ) repre-sents the width and height, and pi is the classification confi-dence. In terms of instance masks: 

M = {mi | mi ∈ { 0, 1}H×W },

providing pixel-level geometric constraints. The final perception output is governed by the employed MLLM, which performs a high-level justification based on multi-modal visual cues. Therefore, the process can be for-mulated as an adjudication function A over the candidate set of N expert models: 

Pfinal = A({E1(I), E 2(I), . . . , E N (I)}, C text )

where I is the raw facade image, Ej (I) represents the visual output (bounding boxes or masks) from the j-th expert, and 

Ctext denotes the contextual semantic prompt. 

Knowledge Storage and Retrieval 

The Defect Memory Bank serves as a unified repository K for cross-task semantic information, acting as a structural knowl-edge bridge between the perception and generative agents. Each entry in K is defined as a multi-modal tuple: 

⟨Icrop , m i, c, T ⟩ 

where Icrop denotes the local defect image patch, mi is the corresponding high-fidelity mask, c is the defect category, and T represents the textual description of the defect’s mor-phology. The system continuously restores newly detected in-stances into K and retrieves historical background templates 

Ibg or target masks Mtarget to support downstream synthesis tasks. 

Generative Recomposition 

The Generative Recomposition task aims to synthesize se-mantically consistent synthetics Iaug to mitigate data scarcity. Given a background template Ibg obtained via semantic in-painting and a retrieved defect mask Mret , the Generative Re-composition Agent performs the following mapping: 

Iaug = Ψ( Ibg ⊕ M ret | θ) ,

where Ψ denotes the generative backbone parameterized by θ,and ⊕ represents a semantic blending operation that ensures both textural harmony and label consistency. 

4.2 FacadeFixer Framework 

The FacadeFixer is architected as an autonomous multi-agent system that bridges user intentions with low-level visual per-ception and generative synthesis. As illustrated in Fig.1, the system comprises three pivotal components: the Instruction Configuration, the Orchestration Agent , and a suite of Spe-cialized Functional Agents integrated with a Defect Memory Bank. 

Orchestration Agent 

The Orchestration Agents erves as the central reasoning en-gine, transforming the Instruction Configuration P into an ex-ecutable Task Planning Pipeline. To ensure high efficiency and logical fluidity, this process leverages SOTA MLLM as the primary engine due to its advanced linguistic compre-hension and reasoning capabilities. The orchestration un-folds through three core modules: Firstly, User Profile Inter-preter characterizes the specific inspection context and trans-lates user-selected toggles into domain-specific knowledge, establishing the semantic foundation. Then, utilizing MLLM-based reasoning, this component parses complex user intents (e.g., defect localization or generative recomposition) and de-composes them into granular, interdependent sub-tasks, span-ning from initial detection to final semantic synthesis. These sub-tasks are dispatched the structured sub-tasks to the appro-priate downstream specialized agents. 

Detection and Segmentation agents 

The framework employs a multi-expert collaborative paradigm to achieve high-fidelity defect identification, transitioning from coarse-grained localization to fine-grained pixel-level extraction. Upon receiving images dispatched by the Orchestration Agent, the Detection Agent activates a suite of SOTA specialized models. To overcome the limitations of individual detectors, we implement an ensemble detection strategy (union strategy and intersection stratedy) where multiple experts generate candidate bounding boxes for the same input. These results are visualized and fed into a high-capacity MLLM. Acting as a ”domain expert,” the MLLM evaluates the spatial logic and visual evidence of various detection hypotheses, selecting the most plausible candidates as the optimal output. This arbitration mechanism ensures that the framework prioritizes both recall and structural consistency. The subsequent Segmentation Agent processes these optimized detection results through a category-aware refinement process. For structural cracks—characterized by elongated and fine-grained topologies—the agent orches-trates multiple crack-specific segmentation experts. The final mask is derived via a Union Strategy to ensure connectivity and minimize omissions. For other defect categories (e.g., spalling, efflorescence), the agent leverages the SAM3 archi-tecture. To enhance segmentation accuracy, we implement a Semantic Prompt Mapping mechanism, where textual prompts are refined into domain-specific concepts before being fed into the model. 

Generative Recomposition Agent 

The Generative Recomposition Agent serves as a high-fidelity data synthesis engine, transforming static defect records into diverse, realistic training samples. This process is executed through a structured pipeline of decoupling, re-trieval, and synthesis. Upon receiving defect-laden images, the agent first performs Semantic Inpainting to fill the defect regions based on surrounding textures. This step effectively decouples the defect morphology from its original noisy en-vironment, generating ”clean” contextual anchors. Utilizing these anchors, the agent queries the Defect Memory Bank to retrieve topologically compatible defect masks and corre-sponding clean background templates. ## 5 Experiments 

5.1 Experimental Setup 

The FacadeFixer framework is instantiated with a hetero-geneous ensemble of foundation models, each strategically selected to optimize the functional synergy of specialized agents. We employ GPT-4o as the core reasoning engine for the Orchestration Agent , leveraging its superior linguistic comprehension and multi-modal reasoning to transform high-level user intentions into structured task pipelines [Achiam 

et al. , 2023]. For the Detection and Segmentation Agents ,

Gemini-3-Flash is utilized as a Visual Adjudicator . Its SOTA visual grounding capabilities allow it to arbitrate outputs from multiple expert models and determine optimal defect bound-aries during the ensemble adjudication process [DeepMind, 2025]. The Generative Recomposition Agent is powered by 

Seedream-4.5 [Seedream et al. , 2025], which facilitates both semantic inpainting for background decoupling and stochas-tic recomposition for synthesizing expert-fidelity samples us-ing historical priors from the Defect Memory Bank . All foundation models are operated with default parameters to maintain performance consistency. The framework is im-plemented in PyTorch 2.9.1 and CUDA 12.8 , providing a modern computational backbone. Hardware deployment was conducted on a high-performance server equipped with an 

NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4090 Ti GPU. To facilitate repro-ducibility, we adopt a modular design where specialized ex-pert models (e.g., SAM2/3, YOLO-v10) are integrated as plug-and-play components. 

5.2 Baselines and Evaluation Protocols 

To rigorously evaluate the performance of FacadeFixer in addressing the topological complexities and semantic co-occurrence of facade pathologies, we integrate a diverse ex-pert model zoo as the baseline for comparison. Our objective is to demonstrate that the collaborative adjudication of the agentic framework transcends the performance of individual models and isolated tasks. For the Detection Agent, We incorporate a diverse expert zoo, including YOLOv11-M, YOLOv12-M, Faster R-CNN [Wu et al. , 2019], and RT-DETR [Lv et al. , 2024]. Further-more, traditional ensemble strategies (Union and Intersec-tion) with an Intersection-over-Union (IoU) threshold of 0.6

are employed as direct competitors to our MLLM-based ar-bitration. For structural cracks, we utilize specialized models with distinct inductive biases, such as SegFormer-b0/b4 [Xie 

et al. , 2021], UNet-VGG16, YOLOv8-crack-seg, and the state-of-the-art Mamba-based SCSegamba[Liu et al. , 2025a]. For non-crack defects, we evaluate the zero-shot capabili-ties of SAM-3 under three prompting modalities: coordinate points, bounding boxes, and textual descriptions. To mitigate the performance degradation caused by the se-mantic ambiguity of abstract pathology names, we imple-ment a Domain-Specific Concept Alignment strategy. Specif-ically, we map technical defect categories onto visually de-scriptive prompts that better resonate with the model’ pre-trained knowledge.Specifically, we re-map technical defect categories into visually descriptive prompts that better res-onate with the model’s pre-trained knowledge base. For in-stance, the abstract classes Vegetation and Contaminant are translated into the more common visual concepts of Plant and 

Rubbish , respectively. Similarly, Degraded Plaster is mapped to Peeling , while Rust-stain is simplified to Stain . For com-plex pathologies like Concrete Spalling , which exhibit multi-modal visual features, we employ a composite mapping that includes Hole , Peeling , or Stain to provide more precise local-ized cues. To optimize pixel-level accuracy and reduce false positives, the framework enforces a spatial anchor constraint, where segmentation is exclusively conducted within the high-confidence bounding boxes predicted by the Detection Agent. The core tasks are evaluated on a curated test set contain-ing 300 high-resolution images with expert-verified ground truth. Furthermore, to validate the Semantic Fidelity and label consistency of the generative recomposition, we generated a synthetic dataset of 281 images. These samples underwent rigorous manual secondary-annotation to measure the align-ment between the generated pathologies and the intended se-mantic masks. We assess the performance of FacadeFixer on both real-world and synthetic distributions to demonstrate the framework’s capacity for high-fidelity data augmentation and knowledge transfer. 

## 6 Results and Analysis 

6.1 Performance for Detection and Segmentation 

As demonstrated in Table 2, we evaluate the FacadeFixer Detection Agent against several SOTA expert baselines and traditional ensemble heuristics. Our proposed Facade-Fixer framework establishes a new performance benchmark, achieving a high precision of 0.7026 and an F1-score of 0.6423, outperforming both individual expert baselines and traditional ensemble heuristics. Notably, while the RT-DETR model yields the highest Recall (0.6622), it struggles with a relatively high false positive rate in complex architectural environments. In contrast, FacadeFixer maintains a robust balance between detection sensitivity and accuracy, achiev-ing a leading mAP 50:95 of 0.5678. Additionally, a naive Union of expert outputs introduces excessive false positives, significantly degrading precision. Conversely, while the In-tersection Strategy is traditionally viewed as overly restric-tive, our results show that it achieves a commendable balance across all metrics, with performance levels closely approach-ing those of the FacadeFixer. However, by leveraging multi-modal adjudication, FacadeFixer identifies the optimal con-sensus among experts, effectively filtering stochastic errors that persist even in intersection results, while preserving crit-ical structural information that might otherwise be omitted. To evaluate the robustness of FacadeFixer amidst the mor-phological complexities of facade pathologies, we conduct comparative experiments across diverse segmentation tasks, distinguishing between fine-grained crack segmentation and broader defect categories.
