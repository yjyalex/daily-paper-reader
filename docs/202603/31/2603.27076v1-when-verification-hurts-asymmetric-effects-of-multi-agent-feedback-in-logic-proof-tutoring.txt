Abstract 

Large language models (LLMs) are increas-ingly used for automated tutoring, but their reliability in structured symbolic domains re-mains unclear. We study step-level feedback for propositional logic proofs, which require precise symbolic reasoning aligned with a learnerÔÇÖs current proof state. We introduce a knowledge-graphÔÇôgrounded benchmark of 516 unique proof states with step-level anno-tations and difficulty metrics. Unlike prior tutoring evaluations that rely on model self-assessment or binary correctness, our frame-work enables fine-grained analysis of feedback quality against verified solution paths. We evaluate three role-specialized pipelines with varying solution access: Tutor (partial solution access), Teacher (full derivation access), and Judge (verification of Tutor feedback). Our re-sults reveal a striking asymmetry: verification improves outcomes when upstream feedback is error-prone (<70% accuracy), but degrades per-formance by 4ÔÇô6pp through over-specification when feedback is already reliable (>85%). Crit-ically, we identify a shared complexity ceil-ing; no model or pipeline reliably succeeds on proof states exceeding complexity 4ÔÇô5. These findings challenge the assumption that adding verifiers or richer context universally improves tutoring, motivating adaptive, difficulty-aware architectures that route problems by estimated complexity and upstream reliability. 

1 Introduction 

Propositional logic is foundational to computer science education, underpinning formal reasoning in programming, algorithms, and digital systems (Mayer and Baraniuk, 2025). Yet mastering opti-mal proof strategies remains challenging for stu-dents due to limited access to timely and personal-ized tutoring (Inamdar et al., 2025). Intelligent tutoring systems (ITS) achieve learn-ing gains comparable to human tutoring through 

step-level feedback , guiding each inference rather than evaluating only final answers (VanLehn, 2011). However, traditional ITS relies on template-based feedback that poorly adapts to diverse miscon-ceptions (Zerkouk and Chikhaoui, 2025). Large language models (LLMs) offer scalable alterna-tives, yet their tendency to hallucinate risks incor-rect feedback undermining learning (Macina et al., 2023). Effective step-level tutoring requires more than surface correctness: feedback that is partially cor-rect or misaligned with a studentÔÇÖs proof state can reinforce misconceptions and anchor flawed rea-soning even when not strictly wrong (VanLehn, 2011). Without external grounding, even capable LLMs risk generating feedback that appears cor-rect but misguides student reasoning, motivating the need for verification mechanisms (Dhuliawala et al., 2024). Role-specialized feedback pipelines, where a dedicated verifier agent evaluates another agentÔÇÖs output, offer a potential safeguard distinct from self-correction (Huang et al., 2024). Yet the condi-tions under which verification helps, or hurts, is not well studied. A key factor also overlooked in prior work is the degree of solution information access 

for the feedback agent. Greater access may enable more precise verification, but risks prematurely re-vealing answers and undermining scaffolding (Da-heim et al., 2024). Moreover, how information ac-cess shapes verification effectiveness, what failure modes emerge, and whether automated accuracy metrics capture the underlying pedagogical char-acteristics remain largely unexplored. We address these gap in our work with the following research questions: 

RQ1: How does the degree of solution information access shape step-level tutoring feedback effective-ness across models of varying capability? 

RQ2: Under what conditions does role-specialized verification improve tutoring feedback, and when does it degrade performance?

RQ3: What systematic failure modes emerge across feedbacks in each pipelines, and whether automated metrics alone capture their pedagogical impact? We answer these RQs through step-level feed-back for propositional logic proofs, where students apply inference rules (e.g., Modus Ponens, Dis-junctive Syllogism) to a set of premises (logical statements assumed to hold) to derive a target con-clusion through intermediate derivations or steps. At each proposed step a tutoring agent must as-sess the derivation and provide scaffolded guidance without revealing the answer. For our investigation, we developed three role-specialized agents (Fig. 1): Tutor (access only to the correct next step), Teacher (full solution access), and Judge (verification of Tutor feedback along with full solution access), op-erationalizing solution information access as a con-trolled variable. Unlike prior evaluations relying on binary correctness, we perform a fine-grained eval-uation against a knowledge graph (KG), encoding the complete derivation space, enabling analysis of 

how steps are derived, not only whether they are valid. Our contributions are as follows: 

Logic Tutoring Benchmark: A novel dataset of 516 real student propositional logic proof states with a KG-encoded multi-path solution space and instance-level difficulty metrics, enabling step-level evaluation beyond binary correctness and sup-porting the first controlled study to the best of our knowledge on information access effects in logic proof tutoring. 

Asymmetric Verification Effect: Empirical evi-dence that the verification benefit is conditional on upstream feedback reliability, improving outcomes when feedback is error-prone, but degrading perfor-mance through over-specification when feedback is already reliable. 

Failure Mode Taxonomy: Four systematic fail-ure modes, echo effects, partial correctness val-idation, premature revelation, and verification over-specification, confirmed by human evaluation across four pedagogical dimensions, demonstrat-ing that automated accuracy metrics systematically overestimate feedback quality. 

2 Related Work 

2.1 LLM-based scaffolding for tutoring systems 

Scaffolding theory (Vygotsky and Cole, 1978) un-derlies intelligent tutoring systems (ITS), where step-by-step hints must be specific enough to ad-vance reasoning yet scaffolded enough to pre-serve productive struggle (Wood et al., 1976; Bel-land, 2017). This balance is especially difficult to achieve in structured symbolic domains where each inference step constrains all subsequent reasoning. LLMs show promise for generating adaptive feedback across diverse educational contexts, with prior work demonstrating gains in diagnosing stu-dent errors, and generating pedagogical feedback in college algebra tutors (Reddig et al., 2025), fos-tering active reasoning and self-reflection in ad-vanced computer science courses (Kumar et al., 2024), and supporting adaptive scaffolding in pro-gramming contexts (Scholz et al., 2025). However, two persistent challenges limit their effectiveness in intelligent tutoring systems: LLMs often reveal answers instead of scaffolded guidance (Macina et al., 2023, 2025), and their feedback frequently hallucinates without external grounding (Liu et al., 2025; Jia et al., 2024). Moreover, LLMs cannot reliably locate reasoning errors in student solutions even with access to reference solutions (Srivatsa et al., 2025; Tyen et al., 2024; Est├®vez-Ayres et al., 2025). Yet these limitations remain unaddressed in structured symbolic domains where step-level feedback alignment is especially consequential. 

2.2 Role-specialized Feedback Pipelines 

Role-specialized pipelines have shown promise in educational AI, where role separation ensures feed-back meets both accuracy and pedagogical stan-dards (Daheim et al., 2024; Phung et al., 2024), addressing over-praise and over-inference errors (dos Santos and Cury, 2025; Chudziak and Kostka, 2025). Prior work shows that role-specialized multi-agent systems improve instructional design (Zhang et al., 2025), and that educational evalua-tion benefits from incorporating multiple pedagog-ical roles (Chen et al., 2025). EducationQ further demonstrates that studentÔÇôteacherÔÇôevaluator triads promote effective scaffolding and align with human pedagogical judgment (Shi et al., 2025). Yet these limitations remain unaddressed in structured sym-bolic domains where step-level feedback alignment is especially consequential. Beyond educational context, prior work shows that self-correction without external grounding can degrade reasoning performance (Huang et al., 2024). Intrinsic self-correction sometimes wors-ens performance across diverse reasoning tasks, in-cluding arithmetic reasoning, code generation, and question answering (Kamoi et al., 2024), and that

2.3 Logic Reasoning Benchmarks 

Existing benchmarks for symbolic or logical rea-soning primarily evaluate end-to-end proof genera-tion using single-reference solutions, with limited or no annotations for intermediate reasoning steps (Saparov and He, 2022). ProofWriter (Tafjord et al., 2021) assesses only final proof validity, obscur-ing whether models apply inference rules correctly at each step. FOLIO (Han et al., 2024) provides expert-authored first-order logic problems but pe-nalizes semantically valid alternative derivations, while ProntoQA (Saparov and He, 2022) enables controlled evaluation through synthetic reasoning chains but cannot capture branching decision points where multiple inference rules may apply. In contrast, step-level logic tutoring requires dis-tinguishing incorrect rule use, suboptimal strate-gies, and state misalignment (steps inconsistent with the current proof state), that existing bench-marks collapse into binary outcomes obscuring in-termediate reasoning errors (Saparov and He, 2022; Tafjord et al., 2021; Chi and Wylie, 2014). These limitations motivate our KG-grounded framework (Section 3), which encodes not only whether a step is derivable but how ; capturing the inference rule and parent statements justifying each derivation, enabling evaluation that existing single-reference benchmarks cannot support. 

3 Task and Dataset 

3.1 Task Formulation 

A propositional proof problem is a tuple (P, C ),where P = {p1, . . . , p n} is a set of premises which are logical statements assumed to hold, and C is the target conclusion. A proof state ¤â = ( P, I, C )

represents a studentÔÇÖs progress, where I is the or-dered set of intermediate statements derived so far by applying inference rules r Ôêê R (e.g., Modus Po-nens, Disjunctive Syllogism; complete rule set in Appendix A). Each r derives a new statement from parent statements in P Ôê¬ I, with I = Ôêà initially. Given ¤â = ( P, I, C ), the task is to predict the optimal next step s, a statement derivable in exactly one rule application from P Ôê¬ I, that minimises re-maining derivation distance to C, preferring shorter proofs that reflect more direct reasoning paths. We focus on single-step prediction, as each feedback round targets exactly one student decision point. A representative proof instance is in Appendix B. 

3.2 Dataset Construction 

We draw on interaction logs from a propositional logic tutoring system used in an undergraduate dis-crete mathematics course, at a large public U.S. uni-versity. Further details and illustrations of the tutor are present in Appendix C. The tutoring system comprises seven levels: a diagnostic assessment level (pre-test), five practice levels, which include on-demand context-specific hints along with im-mediate feedback on correctness, and a summative assessment level (post-test). Our dataset comprises 516 unique proof states ,defined at the (P, I, C ) triple level, from 32 prob-lems across five practice levels of varying difficulty from Spring 2023. Each proof state instance repre-sents a distinct student proof state, from minimal configurations with only three premises to complex states with up to 12 intermediate statements. Ta-ble 4 provides the complete distribution of proof states in the dataset, where unequal instance counts across levels reflect natural student activity.

4 Role-Specialized Tutoring Pipelines 

We distinguish three role-specialized pipelines that vary in solution information access: Student-Tutor, Student-Teacher, and Student-Tutor-Judge. In each pipeline, agents operate sequentially in a fixed or-der with no bidirectional communication as illus-trated in Fig 1. We operationalize solution information access as a controlled variable across three agents: ÔÇó Tutor : receives only correct next step s.ÔÇó Teacher : receives correct next step s with com-plete derivation context (inference rule, parents). ÔÇó Judge : receives complete derivation context as the Teacher along with Tutor feedback. This asymmetric grounding isolates (i) information access (Tutor vs. Teacher) and (ii) verification ef-fects (Tutor vs. Judge) as independent variables controlling for task difficulty. 

4.1 Student 

The Student agent simulates an undergraduate learner with no solution access following (Shi et al., 2025). Given a proof state ¤ât the agent predicts the next state ¤ât+1 . To this end, it generates (i) 2ÔÇô3 candidates with rationales , (ii) select the optimal 

¤ât+1 along reasoning , (iii) next step s, (iv) infer-ence rule , and (v) parent statements . This multi-candidate approach reflects least-to-most prompt-ing (Zhou et al., 2022) and pedagogical chain-of-thought (Jiang et al., 2024), externalizing reasoning that tutoring agents can reference when scaffolding. After feedback, the Student agent revises their response only if errors are identified ensuring mea-sured improvements reflect genuine feedback ef-fects rather than forced changes. Student agent prompts and a sample student response are shown in Figs 6, 7, and 11. The Student agent revises only when errors are identified, ensuring measured improvements re-flect genuine feedback effects rather than forced changes. 

4.2 Tutor 

The Tutor models a teaching assistant (TA) who knows the correct answer but not the derivation, a more constrained setting than the restricted-teacher condition in Shi et al. (2025). Tutor receives only the optimal next step s without derivation context (rule, parent statements), requiring inference of both the applicable rule and reasoning (Kochmar et al., 2022). It classifies student responses as op-timal or invalid and provides scaffolded feedback guided by the shared scaffolding constraints (┬º4.5). This tests whether correctness-only grounding suf-fices for effective tutoring. Tutor prompt and a sample response are shown in Fig 8 and Fig 12. 

4.3 Teacher 

The Teacher models an instructor with full solution access, the standard assumption in ITS (Corbett, 2001; Barnes and Stamper, 2008) representing the upper bound on information availability. It receives the complete derivation ¤ât+1 (rule, parents, optimal next step s), enabling precise evaluation and feed-back, which may risk answer revelation (Daheim et al., 2024), a limitation that we discuss in sec-tion 6.4. Comparing with Tutor, Teacher isolates the effect of solution access on feedback quality. Teacher prompt and sample responses are shown in Fig 9 and Fig 13. 

4.4 Judge 

The Judge models a quality-assurance reviewer, fol-lowing verifier-in-the-loop designs in educational AI (Phung et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2025). It re-ceives full derivation context ¤ât+1 and Tutor feed-back, to evaluate both the studentÔÇÖs step and the TutorÔÇÖs feedback for correctness and answer leak-age. Based on this, it either enhances or overrides the tutorÔÇÖs feedback, addressing over-praise and answer-leakage failures common in single-agent systems (Gonnermann-M├╝ller et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025). Judge prompt and sample responses are given in Fig 10, Fig 16, and 15. 

4.5 Shared Constraints 

All feedback agents adhere to scaffolding princi-ples from learning science, guide discovery without revealing answers (Belland, 2017; Van de Pol et al., 2010), employ Socratic questioning over directives (Chen, 2023), reference student candidates for rele-vance (Serban et al., 2020), and acknowledge cor-rect reasoning before addressing errors (Shi et al., 2025). Feedback is limited to 2-3 sentences. 

4.6 Interaction Protocol 

Each interaction consists of a single feedback round to isolate pipeline effects. To ensure fair compari-son, initial Student responses were generated once and reused across all pipelines; Tutor feedback from the Student-Tutor pipeline was reused in the Student-Tutor-Judge pipeline. All agents communi-cate via structured JSON format, where fewer than 3% of instances required a retry due to malformed JSON output. Implementation details and quality validation constraints across dialogue generation appear in Appendix H. 

5 Experimental Setup and Evaluation 

5.1 Evaluation Metrics 

Building on the KG evaluation framework, we de-fine six metrics that operationalize pipeline perfor-mance at the proof-state level. 

Task Performance: We report Pre (student-only) and Post (after feedback) accuracy, each evaluated over all N =516 proof states. It is computed as the proportion of Optimal predictions by classi-fying predicted next steps as Optimal or Invalid per next-step classification described in (Sec 3.3). The Learning gain Ôêå = Post ÔêÆ Pre is reported in percentage points (pp). 

Tutor Rule Accuracy: measures how often the Tutor correctly predicts the inference rule for the optimal step, given only s as input, serving as a proxy for upstream feedback reliability. 

Reasoning Difficulty Analysis: Step Complexity 

quantifies the symbolic difficulty of a proof step as a weighted function of logical operators and nesting depth (Appendix E), providing a model-independent characterization of instance difficulty. We report Mean Complexity , the average Step Com-plexity of proof states where each pipeline im-proves student performance, to characterize which difficulty regimes benefit from each condition. 

Verification Effects: To isolate verificationÔÇÖs ef-fects beyond full solution access, we compute the 

Unique Improvement Count (UIC) , the number of proof states where only the Judge pipeline succeeds and the Teacher pipeline fails on identical student inputs. Since both pipelines share full derivation access, any Judge improvement over Teacher is attributable solely to verification rather than infor-mation access. 

Feedback Quality (Human Annotation): To iden-tify failure mechanisms that automated metrics can-not capture, three domain experts with at least two years of undergraduate Discrete Structures teach-ing experience independently annotated feedback responses across all pipelines on four dimensions: 

Mistake Identification , Correctness , Answer Reve-lation , and Actionability (KrippendorffÔÇÖs ╬▒ ÔëÑ 0.65 ;full protocol in Appendix J). Three systematic fail-ure modes emerge. 

5.2 Experimental Setup 

We evaluate seven LLMs spanning reasoning-augmented models (GPT-o3, Gemini-1.5-Pro, DeepSeek-R1), standard instruction-tuned models (GPT-4.1, Qwen-3-32B), and open-source models (Mistral-Large, LLaMA-3.3-70B). This selection covers diverse capability levels and architectural families, enabling analysis of whether pipeline ef-fects are capability-dependent (Sec 6). Each model serves as both a student and a feedback agent (Tutor, Teacher, Judge) across all pipelines, generating 516 ├ù 7 ├ù 3 = 10 ,836 dia-logues (516 proof states ├ù 7 models ├ù 3 pipelines). All experiments use temperature 0.0 via APIs listed in Appendix I for reproducibility. Model specifica-tions and sample dialogues are presented in Appen-dices I and G. 

6 Results 

We organize our findings around three RQs: how information access shapes feedback (RQ1), when does role-specialized verification improves or de-grades the feedback (RQ2), what failure modes emerge in feedback across pipelines and whether automated metrics capture the pedagogical impact (RQ3). We begin with baseline performance, then address each RQ in turn, complimenting the eval-uation metrics with expert human evaluation of feedback, and discussing the pedagogical aspects. 

6.1 Baseline (Student Reasoning Performance) 

As shown in Table 2, baseline performance varies markedly across models. DeepSeek and Gemini achieve the highest pre-feedback accuracy (56% and 54.5%), followed by Qwen (46%), while GPT variants and Mistral perform more modestly (20ÔÇô25%) and LLaMA performs weakest (17%). Examining baseline accuracy alongside Tutor rule accuracy reveals two model groups. We refer to Gemini and DeepSeek, which consistently exhibit high performance on both measures, as strong models . In contrast, GPT models, Mistral, Qwen, LLaMA show lower performance on both dimen-sions and are referred to as weak models in this paper.

6.4 Human Evaluation - Failure Mode Analysis (RQ3) 

Automated accuracy metrics reward correct out-comes but cannot penalize pedagogically flawed reasoning paths. Human evaluation across four dimensions (┬º5.1) reveals four systematic fail-ure modes. Echo Effect (Tutor). Weak Tutor agents, lacking derivational context, anchor on the studentÔÇÖs incorrect rule rather than correcting it (Fig 15). Human evaluation confirms this system-atically: Tutor feedback scores lowest on Mistake Identification among weak models, indicating in-sufficient derivational grounding rather than ran-dom noise. Verification mitigates this through KG-grounded override (┬º6.3). 

Partial Correctness Validation (Tutor). When students predict the correct step via flawed rea-soning, Tutor agents validate the faulty logic, re-inforcing misconceptions. Correctness scores re-main moderate even when post-feedback accuracy improves, confirming that accuracy overestimates genuine reasoning repair (Kamoi et al., 2024; Tyen et al., 2024). 

Premature Revelation. Teacher feedback fre-quently discloses the correct step or over-specifies derivational hints (Fig 14), leading student agents to anchor on the disclosed answer without inte-grating it into their reasoning. Human evaluation confirms that Teacher feedback scores highest on 

Answer Revelation while Actionability scores re-main moderate, indicating that disclosed answers produce surface-level correctness rather than trans-ferable reasoning improvement. 

Verification Over-Specification. Although Judge feedback improves Correctness and Mistake Identification for weak models, human evaluation reveals a distinct failure mode for strong models: when the Judge overrides already reliable Tutor feedback, the resulting guidance becomes overly abstract, reducing Actionability scores below those of the original Tutor feedback. This pattern mirrors the quantitative degradation observed for Gemini and DeepSeek ( ÔêÆ5pp) and suggests that verifica-tion introduces a specificity cost when upstream feedback is already well-grounded: the Judge im-poses derivational constraints that narrow rather than scaffold the studentÔÇÖs reasoning space. Human evaluation confirms the four failure modes, demonstrating that automated metrics over-estimate feedback quality by rewarding correct out-comes without penalizing flawed reasoning paths or answer leakage. 

7 Conclusion 

We present a controlled study of multi-agent LLM tutoring for propositional logic using a knowledge-graphÔÇôgrounded benchmark of 516 proof states. Our central finding is that verification benefit is 

asymmetric : it improves outcomes by +10ÔÇô14pp when upstream feedback is error-prone (<70% ac-curacy), but degrades performance by 4ÔÇô6pp when feedback is already reliable (>85%). This asymme-try is governed by the proof-state Complexity. Ver-ification helps in intermediate regimes but cannot overcome a shared reasoning ceiling (complexity >4ÔÇô5) beyond which no pipeline reliably succeeds. We identify three systematic failure modes: echo effects, partial correctness validation, and prema-ture revelation that verification mitigates only under limited conditions. These findings challenge the assumption that adding verifiers or solution access improves tutoring. Instead, effective LLM tutoring requires adaptive pipelines that estimate upstream reliability and route problems by complexity, rather than defaulting to more agents or richer context. 

Limitations 

This study has several limitations. First, our evalu-ation is restricted to propositional logic proof tutor-ing, and the findings may not directly generalize to domains with weaker symbolic constraints or richer semantic structure. While our primary metrics as-sess the intrinsic properties of feedback indepen-dently of the receiver, LLM students may respond differently from human learners; therefore, human-in-the-loop validation would be necessary to sub-stantiate claims about learning outcomes. Second, while our knowledge-graph-based metrics enable fine-grained analysis of step-level reasoning, they rely on a predefined solution space and do not cap-ture open-ended or creative reasoning strategies. Third, our experiments focus on single-step feed-back and do not model long-horizon studentÔÇôtutor interactions or learning gains over time. Moreover, our qualitative analysis is limited to a small expert-reviewed sample (10 responses per model), and broader manual analysis could reveal additional failure modes. Finally, although we analyze multi-ple large language models, results may vary with future architectures or training regimes.

H Implementation Details 

Response Constraints. We standardize infer-ence rule references using abbreviations (MP, MT, DS, HS, DeM, Impl) for consistent evaluation. The complete list appears in Table 3. 

Token Limits. Rather than hard limits, we em-ploy soft constraints via prompts (2ÔÇô3 sentences). 

Quality Validation. Two automated mechanisms ensure data integrity: (1) zero-token outputs trig-ger automatic retry, and (2) responses missing re-quired JSON fields or violating constraints trig-ger re-prompting. Persistent failures after three attempts are flagged for manual review. Across 516 instances on 7 models, fewer than 3% required retry.

J Manual Evaluation Protocol 

To complement our automated metrics and bet-ter understand qualitative differences in feedback behavior across pipelines, we conducted a man-ual evaluation of a representative subset of model responses. This analysis focuses on pedagogical properties of feedback that are difficult to capture through accuracy-based metrics alone, such as scaf-folding quality and answer revelation. Three do-main experts independently annotated 10 problem instances per feedback pipeline (Student, Tutor, Teacher, and Judge), resulting in a total of 40 re-sponses per model along four pedagogical dimen-sions. We use a minimally sufficient sampling strat-egy until inter-rater agreement exceeded a moder-ate KrippendorffÔÇÖs ╬▒ threshold ( ╬▒ ÔëÑ 0.65 ). 

J.1 Annotators 

Three expert annotators participated in the study. All annotators had at least two years of experience as teaching assistants for undergraduate Discrete Structures and had previously served as researchers for the logic tutoring system used to generate the dataset. This ensured familiarity with both proposi-tional logic proof strategies and pedagogical objec-tives of step-level tutoring. 

J.2 Sample Selection 

We randomly sampled 10 problem instances per feedback pipeline (Tutor, Teacher, and Judge), re-sulting in a total of 40 annotated responses per model. Sampling includes a mix of correct, sub-optimal, and incorrect student attempts. Following best practices for qualitative analysis in educational NLP, we used a minimally sufficient sampling strat-egy, increasing the sample size until inter-annotator agreement reached a stable, moderate level, Krip-pendorffÔÇÖs ╬▒ ÔëÑ 0.70 across all annotation dimen-sions. 

J.3 Annotation Dimensions 

Each feedback response was independently anno-tated along four pedagogically motivated dimen-sions: 

Evaluation Rubrics. Feedback was annotated along four dimensions capturing both reasoning accuracy and pedagogical effectiveness: ÔÇó Mistake Identification : Whether the feedback correctly identifies errors or suboptimality in the studentÔÇÖs proposed next step, including incorrect rule use, invalid derivations, or deviations from the optimal reasoning path. ÔÇó Correctness : Whether the feedback itself is logi-cally sound and consistent with the correct solu-tion, providing guidance that does not introduce new errors or misleading reasoning. ÔÇó Answer Revelation : Whether the feedback ex-plicitly or implicitly reveals the correct next step, inference rule, or parent statements, thereby re-ducing the need for student reasoning. ÔÇó Actionability : Whether the feedback provides clear, concrete, and usable guidance that a stu-dent could plausibly act on to improve their next attempt. Due to the cognitive complexity of step-level logic feedback and the time cost of expert annotation, we adopt a coarse 3-point ordinal scale (1ÔÇô3) for all rubrics, with 1 = poor, 2 = partial, 3 = strong. This scale captures meaningful distinctions while maintaining annotation reliability and consistency. 

J.4 Annotation Procedure 

Annotators were provided with the problem con-text (givens, intermediates, and conclusion), the studentÔÇÖs response, and the corresponding feedback generated by the pipeline under evaluation. An-notators worked independently and were blinded to model identity and quantitative performance re-sults. Inter-annotator agreement was measured us-ing KrippendorffÔÇÖs ╬▒, which is suitable for ordinal data and multiple annotators. Agreement exceeded a moderate reliability threshold across all four di-mensions ( ╬▒ ÔëÑ 0.65 ), indicating consistent inter-pretation of the rubric. 

J.5 Analysis 

Although qualitative and limited in scope, this analysis reveals consistent patterns that align with our quantitative results. Tutor feedback is typ-ically vague but provides scaffolding, encourag-ing reasoning without explicit solution disclosure, whereas Teacher feedback often encodes the cor-rect inference more directly (e.g., naming rules or premises). Judge feedback, when applied to noisy or overly revealing Teacher responses, tends to over-correct or abstract guidance, reducing in-structional specificity, mirroring the conditional effectiveness of verification observed in our experi-ments. 

K Design Implications for Education 

Our findings have direct implications for the design of LLM-based tutoring systems in formal reasoning domains. First, verification should be condition-ally deployed, not treated as a universal safeguard. Systems should estimate upstream tutor reliabil-ity and invoke verification primarily when error rates are high, rather than indiscriminately. Sec-ond, solution information access must be adaptive. Full derivational access expands coverage to harder states but increases the risk of premature revelation; lightweight access is preferable in low-complexity settings. Third, step-level reasoning alignment is critical: feedback that is locally valid but mis-aligned with the learnerÔÇÖs current proof state can anchor incorrect reasoning and undermine learn-ing. Finally, our results reveal a complexity ceiling beyond which no automated feedback strategy is re-liable, suggesting the need for escalation strategies such as human intervention or extended symbolic reasoning support in highly complex cases. 

L Future Directions 

Several directions remain for future research. First, validating these findings with human learners is essential to assess learning gains, engagement, and transfer beyond LLM-based student simulations. Second, extending the framework to multi-turn tu-toring dialogues may reveal different verification dynamics, including delayed correction or cumu-lative anchoring effects. Third, applying our ap-proach to broader formal reasoning domains, such as first-order logic or program verification, would test its generality. Finally, developing adaptive rout-ing mechanisms that dynamically select tutor roles, verification depth, and solution access based on student state and problem difficulty represents a promising direction toward robust, scalable educa-tional AI systems.
