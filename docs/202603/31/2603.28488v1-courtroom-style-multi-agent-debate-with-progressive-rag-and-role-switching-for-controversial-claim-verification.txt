## Abstract 

Large language models (LLMs) remain unreliable for high-stakes claim verification due to hallucinations and shallow reasoning. While retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) and multi-agent debate (MAD) address this, they are limited by one-pass retrieval and unstructured debate dynamics. We propose a courtroom-style multi-agent framework, PROC LAIM , that reformulates verification as a structured, adversarial deliberation. Our approach integrates specialized roles ( e.g. , Plaintiff, Defense, Judge) with Progressive RAG (P-RAG) to dynamically expand and refine the evidence pool during the debate. Furthermore, we employ evidence negotiation, self-reflection, and heterogeneous multi-judge aggregation to enforce cali-bration, robustness, and diversity. In zero-shot evaluations on the Check-COVID benchmark, PROC LAIM achieves 81.7% accuracy, outperforming standard multi-agent debate by 10.0 percentage points, with P-RAG driving the primary performance gains (+7.5 pp). We ultimately demonstrate that structural deliberation and model heterogeneity effectively mitigate sys-tematic biases, providing a robust foundation for reliable claim verification. 

## 1 Introduction 

LLMs have demonstrated strong zero-shot performance on reasoning-intensive tasks, yet their reliability in high-stakes domains, such as claim verification, remains limited. Despite fluent generation, LLMs frequently exhibit hallucinations, shallow reasoning, and overcon-fident predictions when evaluating evidence-dependent claims (Huang et al., 2025). RAG partially addresses these issues by grounding responses in external corpora (Lewis et al., 2020). Still, standard pipelines rely on static, single-pass retrieval (Gao et al., 2024) and lack mechanisms for iterative reasoning, often resulting in incomplete or biased conclusions. To improve reliability, recent work has explored Multi-Agent Debate (MAD), where multiple LLM instances iteratively argue and refine answers (Du et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2024; Han et al., 2025). While promising, prior studies show that unstructured debate often leads to premature convergence, shared bias reinforcement, and limited evidence exploration (Smit et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2025). In particular, agent agreement is frequently misinterpreted as correctness, even when grounded in insufficient or biased evidence. In this work, we propose a courtroom-style multi-agent deliberation framework PRO-CLAIM (Progressive Retrieval Orchestrated multi-agent framework for Claim verification) that reformulates claim verification as a structured, adversarial reasoning process. Inspired by legal systems and recent agent-based simulations (Chen et al., 2025), our approach introduces explicit roles (Plaintiff, Defense, Judge, Critic, and Expert Witness), evidence admission protocols, and multi-stage deliberation. Central to the framework is Progres-sive Retrieval-Augmented Generation (P-RAG), which performs iterative, query-adaptive retrieval to continuously expand and refine the evidence pool during debate.

2.2 Initial Evidence Retrieval 

Relevant evidence is retrieved from COVID-19 PubMed abstracts (2020–2024) corpus, con-structed for this study, using dense vector embeddings to measure semantic similarity (Gao & Callan, 2021). Claims and abstract chunks are mapped into a 384-dimensional embedding space using the all-MiniLM-L6-v2 bi-encoder (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019), and the most semantically similar chunks are selected as the seed evidence pool with source and year pre-served for provenance. The detailed embedding normalization and similarity computation are provided in Appendix L. 

2.3 Evidence Negotiation and Judicial Arbitration 

Inspired by the pre-trial discovery phase in legal proceedings, this stage constructs an admissibility-weighted evidence pool before the debate begins, drawing on recent works (Jeong et al., 2026; Hu et al., 2025b), proceeding in four steps. 

Premise-grounded shared retrieval: The FAISS index is queried once per decomposed premise, producing a shared pool of retrieved evidence. 

Stance-conditioned retrieval: An LLM generates two distinct queries — one optimised to surface supporting evidence (for Plaintiff Counsel) and one for challenging evidence (for Defense Counsel) — yielding a proponent pool and opponent pool respectively. This asymmetric retrieval prevents the “Echo Chamber” effect common in Top-K RAG, where the engine retrieves only the most statistically frequent papers, often ignoring marginalized but critical counter-evidence (Wu & Saxena, 2025), ensuring the evidence pool is adversarially balanced from Round 0. 

Negotiation injection: Both sides are exposed to each other’s discovery pools via struc-tured LLM prompts, simulating the disclosure and challenge process. Rather than merely retrieving more data, agents actively refine the search space based on their legal strategy — a form of metacognitive evidence curation. 

Admissibility scoring: Each candidate is scored on two dimensions: Relevance r ∈ [0, 1 ],the degree to which the evidence pertains to the specific claim, and Credibility c ∈ [0, 1 ],the scientific rigour and source quality. The admissibility weight is w = r × c. Items with 

w > 0.5 are admitted, 0.1 < w ≤ 0.5 are flagged as disputed , and w ≤ 0.1 are discarded. Admitted items are ranked in descending order of w and passed to the debate orchestrator (see Appendix H.2). This mechanism operationalises a principle from both legal theory and fact-checking re-search: evidence quality and provenance matter beyond surface-level relevance, especially under conflicting sources (Ge et al., 2025). It mirrors the Daubert Standard (Legal Infor-mation Institute, 2024) used in real-world expert testimony, excluding “scientific hearsay” before it can pollute agent deliberation. 

2.4 Progressive Retrieval-Augmented Generation (P-RAG) 

Static, one-time retrieval is ill-suited to adversarial debate as new evidential gaps emerge as arguments evolve. Unlike prior iterative approaches that query solely from the previous gen-eration (Shao et al., 2023; Trivedi et al., 2023), P-RAG concatenates three sources: (i) the last four messages of the rolling debate context, (ii) the agent’s self-identified evidential gap, and (iii) reflection-driven discovery needs from the prior round (Section 2.6) —into one prompt with the Judge refining each query before execution (see App. I for full logic and prompts). 2.5 Multi-Agent Debate (MAD) Orchestration 

The multi-agent debate is the central reasoning engine of the pipeline involving five adjudication roles—Plaintiff Counsel, Defense Counsel, and three Judges—each assigned to a different underlying model (Table 2). Heterogeneous assignment serves to prevent collusion from shared representations, introduce authentic disagreement, and improve argument diversity across rounds, as homogeneous agents cannot reliably improve over majority vote, while diversity in agent capabilities is critical for effective deliberation (Zhang et al., 2026; Zhu et al., 2026). Each debate round executes five steps: (1) Evidence discovery: Both agents independently identify an evidential gap and propose a targeted query, which the Judge refines before execution via P-RAG, with novel results admitted to the shared pool. (2) Argument genera-tion: Each agent produces a structured legal argument grounded in the current evidence pool and debate history. (3) Expert witness testimony: Either side may request a domain expert; if granted by the Judge, a domain-specific persona is dynamically generated to pro-vide targeted testimony. This mechanism enables access to specialized knowledge without predefining expert domains. (4) Self-reflection: Each agent critiques its own performance, with identified gaps injected into the next round’s P-RAG query (Section 2.6). (5) Critic evaluation: The Critic Agent independently scores both sides and signals early termination if all key premises have been addressed. The debate runs for up to max rounds = 10 but terminates early when any of the following conditions are met: (i) Reflection plateau: |∆ total reflection score | < 0.05 for two consec-utive rounds; (ii) Critic resolution: debate resolved=True ; (iii) Novelty exhaustion: average novelty < 0.10 over two consecutive P-RAG calls; or (iv) Judicial signal: the Court agent affirms readiness to deliberate. 

2.6 Self-Reflection and Critic Evaluation Per-agent self-reflection. After each round, each counsel performs a structured self-critique (Madaan et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2023) across three dimensions: logic l ∈ [0, 1 ] (inferential validity), novelty n ∈ [0, 1 ] (new information introduced), and rebuttal b ∈ [0, 1 ] (engagement with the opposing side). Logical validity is weighted highest (0.4) as the core requirement for inferential integrity, with novelty and rebuttal balanced equally (0.3 each) to maintain adversarial accountability without compromising factual grounding. They’re aggregated as: 

sref = 0.4 l + 0.3 n + 0.3 b, (2) and mapped to a symmetric confidence adjustment used in the final verdict (Section 2.8): 

δref = ( sref − 0.5 ) × 0.6 ∈ [−0.30, +0.30 ]. (3) The 0.6 multiplier scales the centered reflection score to a range of ±0.30. This allows the winning counsel’s self-critique to exert a significant, yet non-dominant, influence on the baseline consensus confidence ( cbase ≈ 0.8), maintaining the supremacy of the judicial panel’s verdict. The self-reflection output also surfaces a discovery need —a description of missing evidence—which is included with the subsequent round’s P-RAG query. 

Independent Critic Agent. An independent Critic Agent, distinct from both debating agents, evaluates each round, leveraging prior findings that dedicated critics outperform 4Preprint. It scores both sides on logic, evidence coverage, and rebuttal; identifying unaddressed premises from Section 2.1; providing actionable recommendations; and issuing the debate resolved signal. Using an architecturally distinct model prevents both debaters from converging on a shared but potentially erroneous position. 

2.7 Role-Switching Consistency Test 

To probe whether arguments are genuinely evidence-grounded rather than position-anchored, we introduce a role-switching consistency test . Unlike prior work that uses role-switching to mitigate bias (Jin et al., 2025) or elicit uncertainty (Liu et al., 2025), we employ it as a consistency diagnostic. After the primary debate, Plaintiff and Defense Counsel swap roles, with the orchestrator resetting all state and re-running the full debate under the switched configuration. This is analogous to requiring legal counsel to argue the opposing brief: if arguments are evidence-driven, a capable agent should construct comparably strong arguments from either position. A separate LLM then analyses both transcripts, assessing whether the logic in the switched debate contradicts the agent’s original position and whether evidence is marshalled con-sistently or opportunistically, producing a consistency score for each agent. The score is incorporated into the final confidence weighting (Section 2.8). 

2.8 Judicial Panel Evaluation and Final Verdict 

The complete case record—both debate transcripts, admitted evidence, P-RAG retrieval histories, critic evaluations, and per-round self-reflection scores—is submitted to a three-member Judicial Panel of independent LLM judges, each assigned a different model to prevent correlated errors (Verga et al., 2024). Each judge independently evaluates the case through six stages: (1) case reconstruction, (2) evidence Strength, (3) argument Validity, 

(4) source Reliability, (5) discovery rigour and P-RAG query evolution, and (6) a final verdict of S UPPORTED , N OT SUPPORTED , or I NCONCLUSIVE . 4.1 Main Pipeline Performance 

This framework achieves a aggregate majority-vote accuracy of 81.7% across three inde-pendent runs, with an oracle ceiling of 95.8% confirming that near-optimal performance is structurally attainable under favourable initialisation. Inter-judge agreement remains stable across all runs (mean κ: 0.429, 0.549, 0.474), indicating coherent panel deliberation regardless of outcome. The observed variability across runs is primarily attributable to stochastic effects in sequen-tial agent interactions. Run-1’s lower κGT (0.372) does not translate to equivalent accuracy loss (0.817), as its errors remain class-balanced. Run-2, however, exhibits class-asymmetric degradation: retrieval bias introduced during stochastic premise decomposition suppresses REFUTE recall and propagates through deliberation, dropping κGT to 0.384 while inter-judge 

κ holds stable at 0.474 — confirming the panel converged confidently on systematically biased verdicts rather than breaking down structurally.

4.5 Judicial Panel: Reliability and Negativity Bias 

Inter-judge agreement ( κmean = 0.468, 48.9% unanimous) indicates heterogeneous panels produce genuine disagreement in 51.1% of cases, underscoring the need for diversity. Indi-vidual judges exhibit distinct biases: DeepSeek-R1 aggressively refutes (high REFUTE recall but false-refutes SUPPORT), Hermes-3-405B cautiously abstains (frequent I NCONCLUSIVE 

on SUPPORT), and Qwen3-235B-A22B remains most calibrated (highest SUPPORT recall, balanced abstention). All overproduce REFUTE or I NCONCLUSIVE , reflecting structural negativity bias from epistemic caution induced by training corpora, yet their errors (com-mission, omission, calibration) differ such that disagreements correct rather than compound mistakes. Consequently, the majority-vote panel achieves balanced judge–GT agreement (κGT ∈ [0.431, 0.468 ], mean 0.450), confirming that heterogeneous voting improves reliability over homogeneous panels by mitigating shared biases.

## 5 Conclusion 

PROC LAIM shows that courtroom-style deliberation yields a claim verification system that is accurate, interpretable, and auditable. The framework establishes that reliability in multi-agent LLM systems emerges from deliberative architecture rather than individual model capability, and that the courtroom provides a principled, empirically validated template for building such systems. Despite strong empirical performance, the pipeline has several limitations. Computational cost is high due to argument mining, multi-round debate, role-switching, and repeated judicial evaluations, and run-level variance remains notable despite majority voting. KS stability relies on final confidence scores as a proxy for per-round distributions, limiting insight into intra-claim dynamics. ## A Related Work 

Retrieval-Augmented Generation. RAG enhances factual grounding in large language models by incorporating external knowledge at inference time (Lewis et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2024). Recent work improves retrieval quality through structured pipelines such as coarse-to-fine retrieval (Zhao et al., 2025) and retrieval with reasoning (Park et al., 2025), helping mitigate hallucinations (Huang et al., 2025). Hybrid approaches further embed retrieval into multi-agent and iterative reasoning systems, including debate-augmented RAG (Hu et al., 2025b), Tool-MAD (Jeong et al., 2026), R-Debater (Li et al., 2025c), CircuitLM 13 Preprint. Under review. (Hasan et al., 2026), and multi-source retrieval frameworks (Gong et al., 2026), underscoring the need for adaptive evidence acquisition under conflicting or noisy conditions (Ge et al., 2025). However, most approaches rely on pre-collected evidence pools or limited iterative mechanisms, lacking retrieval continuously driven by structured deliberation. Recent work also shows that claim decomposition’s benefits in fact-checking depend on how sub-claims are constructed (Hu et al., 2025a), motivating its reuse as a means of extracting premise-level units to guide targeted retrieval. 

Multi-Agent Debate. MAD has emerged as an effective paradigm for improving reasoning, factuality, and robustness in LLM outputs (Du et al., 2024), with variants spanning group-based discussions (Liu et al., 2024), efficiency-oriented architectures (Fan et al., 2025), and fact-checking frameworks (Han et al., 2025; Ma et al., 2025; He et al., 2025). Extensions incorporating credibility scoring and evidence aggregation further improve performance in high-stakes verification tasks (Dong & Ito, 2026; Li et al., 2025a; Rahman et al., 2025). By enabling agents to critique and refine each other’s reasoning, debate reduces hallucinations and improves consensus quality. However, controlled analyses reveal persistent limitations including premature convergence, conformity bias, and sensitivity to agent configuration (Wu et al., 2025; Zhu et al., 2026; Smit et al., 2023), motivating more structured debate protocols. 

Agent Coordination and Learning Dynamics. To address these limitations, recent work explores improved coordination mechanisms in multi-agent systems, including dynamic role assignment (Zhang et al., 2026), uncertainty-aware role switching (Liu et al., 2025), and self-reflection frameworks such as Self-Refine (Madaan et al., 2023) and MAR (Ozer et al., 2025; Siddique et al., 2025). Diversity and reasoning coverage are further improved through persona-based debate (Hu et al., 2025c;d) and divergent thinking strategies (Liang et al., 2024), while knowledge-enhanced and tool-augmented systems incorporate external signals to strengthen reasoning (Wang et al., 2025; Jeong et al., 2026). Despite these advances, existing methods treat these components as independent objectives, whereas PROC LAIM 

repurposes them within a unified pipeline — where role-switching serves as a consistency diagnostic and self-reflection actively drives subsequent evidence retrieval. 

Structured Deliberation and Courtroom Simulation. Structured debate frameworks in-troduce stronger inductive biases to stabilize multi-agent reasoning. Courtroom-style simulations such as AgentCourt (Chen et al., 2025) and AgenticSimLaw (Chun et al., 2026) demonstrate the effectiveness of explicit roles and adversarial interaction in high-stakes decision-making, while recent systems combine debate with retrieval for multi-round verifi-cation (Wang et al., 2023b; Hu et al., 2025b). While these approaches improve robustness, they typically lack tightly integrated, dynamic retrieval mechanisms that evolve alongside the debate process, limiting their applicability to open-domain fact verification. Overall, prior work largely treats retrieval, debate, and coordination as separate or loosely coupled components. PROC LAIM advances this line of research by tightly integrating progressive retrieval, structured courtroom-style deliberation, and multi-level judicial evalu-ation within a single unified framework, where evidence acquisition is continuously guided by the evolving argumentation process, addressing key limitations in evidence evolution, reasoning stability, and decision reliability.

## C The ‘Burden of Refutation’ Standard 

The framework’s structural decision to formally classify ‘Inconclusive‘ judicial panel verdicts as ‘SUPPORT‘ is derived directly from the foundational legal concept of the burden of proof ,which functions analogously to the presumption of innocence (“innocent until proven guilty”). In a traditional courtroom, a defending party does not need to absolutely guarantee their innocence to survive a trial; rather, the prosecution bears the strict burden of definitively proving guilt. If the prosecution’s evidence is ambiguous, controversial, or broadly insuffi-cient, the presiding jury is legally obligated to return a “Not Guilty” verdict, even if they remain partially unsure. Our multi-agent debate architecture mirrors this exact decision-making protocol. When ver-ifying controversial claims, the refuting agent mathematically functions as the prosecution. If the refuting agent fails to successfully furnish enough concrete, high-quality evidence to convince the judicial panel to issue a definitive ‘Refute‘ consensus, the panel will naturally return an ‘Inconclusive‘ stance. Under the burden of proof, this ‘Inconclusive‘ ruling means the prosecution completely failed to meet its evidentiary threshold. Consequently, the original claim legally and logically survives the adversarial trial, defaulting to ‘Supported.‘ This standard is a core necessity of the framework’s design. It actively prevents the multi-agent system from stalling on highly ambiguous cases and faithfully operationalizes the adversarial mechanics utilized to evaluate the Check-COVID dataset.

## I P-RAG Query Construction 

To address the challenge of merging heterogeneous sources into a coherent search strategy, P-RAG does not issue multiple separate queries. Instead, it concatenates the three sources into a single directed prompt, formulates an initial query via a lightweight LLM, and passes it to the presiding Judge for rigorous refinement. This guarantees a single, highly targeted natural-language query is executed against the vector corpus per agent, per round. 

Step 1: Gap Proposal (Counsel) 

Each counsel identifies a discovery need from the current debate state:               

> As {job title },analyze the current proceedings and identify a critical gap in the available exhibits. What specific evidence do you need to request to strengthen your case or challenge the opposition? Context: {debate context }
> Propose exactly one specific evidence need (1 sentence):

Concatenation of Sources: The agent’s newly identified evidential gap (Source 2) and any unresolved discovery need generated during the prior round’s self-reflection (Source 3) are concatenated into a single agent request string:  

> {gap proposal }. Focus also on: {reflection discovery need }

This combined agent request is then injected alongside the last four messages of the debate history ( debate context , Source 1) into the formulation prompt below.

## N Consensus Edge-Cases 

Consensus Edge-Cases. While the multi-agent panel primarily operates on a standard majority-vote consensus, a statistically rare edge-case may occur when all three evalu-ating judges return completely separate and distinct verdicts (e.g., one agent concludes ’SUPPORT,’ another ’REFUTE,’ and the third ’INCONCLUSIVE’). In such highly disputed scenarios, the framework structurally defers to the judge DeepSeek-R1. DeepSeek-R1 was strategically designated as the Chief Justice because its specialized capabilities in deep Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning provide unparalleled fidelity in tracing complex logical deductions and identifying fallacies during debate evaluation. Because it is established as the most analytically rigorous model on the panel, its initial independent evaluation is granted ’Chief Justice’ priority-weighting to automatically break the deadlock. This ensures that in moments of complete ambiguity, the final determination safely relies on the panel’s highest-quality reasoning trajectory without forcing an arbitrary or redundant meta-review cycle. 

## O Sycophancy and Argument Integrity Metrics 

To rigorously evaluate the framework’s susceptibility to sycophancy—where agents prema-turely yield to opposition, abandon their persona, or collude without sufficient evidence (Malmqvist, 2025)—we track four quantitative metrics from the execution logs. 

1. Role-Play Consistency (0–10) 

During the role-switching consistency test (Section 2.7), an independent consistency ana-lyzer evaluates whether an agent successfully argues the opposing position using identical evidence without logically contradicting its prior arguments. The score reflects adherence to the persona constraints on a 10-point scale; lower scores indicate “leakage” or positional sycophancy where an agent is unable to fully adopt the adversarial stance. 

2. Concession Rate 

We programmatically track explicit linguistic markers of concession and conversational yielding (e.g., “I concede,” “you make a good point,” “I partially agree” ) within the counsel transcripts. To normalize for varying debate lengths, the metric is reported as the frequency of such triggers per 1,000 generated words. A near-zero rate indicates high adversarial retention.

Auditability has a value that accuracy alone does not capture. A black-box verdict offers no mechanism for human reviewers to interrogate why a claim was accepted or rejected, which evidence was admitted, whether the opposing case was meaningfully considered, or whether the system’s confidence is calibrated. PROC LAIM produces a structured case record—comprising admitted evidence with admissibility weights, per-round argument transcripts, self-reflection trajectories, critic evaluations, role-switch consistency scores, and a six-stage judicial opinion for each judge—that directly supports post-hoc audit. In regulated domains such as healthcare, finance, or legal proceedings, this deliberative traceability is not merely desirable but is increasingly mandated by emerging AI governance frameworks. 

Trajectory instability as a reliability signal. Section 4.2 documents that incorrect predictions exhibit oscillating self-reflection trajectories , a behavioural signature absent from confident correct predictions. This instability signal is entirely invisible in single-call pipelines, where confidence is reported as a single scalar that correlates poorly with calibrated accuracy (ECE = 0.18 for na ¨ıve averaging vs. 0.034 for PROC LAIM ; Appendix J). The multi-round deliberation process is thus a prerequisite for generating the per-round evidence from which this diagnostic is derived, meaning the additional token cost is precisely what enables reliability estimation beyond the final answer. 

Heterogeneous adjudication and the value of diversity. Section 4.5 establishes that het-erogeneous judicial panels produce genuine disagreement in 55.8% of cases, and that disagreements correct rather than compound individual judge errors. A homogeneous single-judge panel sacrifices 3.3 pp of accuracy by amplifying shared biases (e.g., the struc-tural negativity bias documented for DeepSeek-R1, which over-produces R EFUTE verdicts). The additional inference cost of three independent judges is thus the mechanism by which systematic model bias is suppressed—an effect that cannot be replicated by calling a single, larger model. 

P.2 Pathways to Cost Reduction 

PROC LAIM ’s deliberative architecture is not inherently incompatible with cost efficiency; rather, its current implementation prioritises correctness and interpretability over through-put. As noted in Section 5, several avenues exist to reduce token usage without sacrificing the core deliberative properties of the framework. Early-exit mechanisms beyond the current reflection plateau could further reduce average rounds; retrieval filtering via tighter admis-sibility thresholds could shrink evidence pools; and model distillation could replace large-parameter role assignments (e.g., Hermes-3-LLaMA-405B) with smaller task-specialised models. 33
