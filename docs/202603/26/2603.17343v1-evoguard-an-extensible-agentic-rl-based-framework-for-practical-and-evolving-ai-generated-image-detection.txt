Abstract. The rapid proliferation of AI-Generated Images (AIGIs) has introduced severe risks of misinformation, making AIGI detection a crit-ical yet challenging task. While traditional detection paradigms mainly rely on low-level features, recent research increasingly focuses on lever-aging the general understanding ability of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) to achieve better generalization, but still suffer from limited extensibility and expensive training data annotations. To better address complex and dynamic real-world environments, we propose Evo-Guard, a novel agentic framework for AIGI detection. It encapsulates various state-of-the-art (SOTA) off-the-shelf MLLM and non-MLLM de-tectors as callable tools, and coordinates them through a capability-aware dynamic orchestration mechanism. Empowered by the agent’s capacities for autonomous planning and reflection, it intelligently selects suitable tools for given samples, reflects intermediate results, and decides the next action, reaching a final conclusion through multi-turn invocation and reasoning. This design effectively exploits the complementary strengths among heterogeneous detectors, transcending the limits of any single model. Furthermore, optimized by a GRPO-based Agentic Reinforce-ment Learning algorithm using only low-cost binary labels, it eliminates the reliance on fine-grained annotations. Extensive experiments demon-strate that EvoGuard achieves SOTA accuracy while mitigating the bias between positive and negative samples. More importantly, it allows the plug-and-play integration of new detectors to boost overall performance in a train-free manner, offering a highly practical, long-term solution to ever-evolving AIGI threats. Source code will be publicly available upon acceptance. 

Keywords: AI-Generated Image Detection · Multimodal Large Lan-guage Models · Agent

## 2 Related Work 

2.1 AI-Generated Image Detection 

For years, AIGI detection has largely relied on identifying synthetic artifacts [5], e.g., generator fingerprints in spatial/frequency domain features [17, 56, 76] or upsampling-induced textures [70]. However, such cues often overfit to specific generators and are fragile to real-world degradations [47]. More fundamentally, this paradigm treats authenticity as a sink class and ties the decision boundary to particular forgery domains [50], exposing significant generalization deficiencies with the rapid advancement of generative models. Recent studies explored leveraging pre-trained visual encoders to incorporate high-level semantics, representing a significant improvement over purely low-level cues-based methods and enabling the capture of artifacts requiring global rea-soning [34]. For example, Effort [88] preserves pre-trained knowledge while learn-ing forgery patterns by freezing principal components. Other methods combine CLIP [58] with other feature extractors [10, 43, 87]; UnivFD [50] measures real– fake distances in the CLIP feature space. Some works exploit SAM [35] and DINO [51] for visual representations [12, 54]. Several innovative paradigms have also been proposed, such as comparing test samples against visual priors derived from authentic images [39,46], leveraging diffusion-reconstruction errors [78,80], or mitigating bias induced by generation-agnostic artifacts [4, 6, 8, 20, 59]. The rapid evolution of generative models has rendered single detectors in-creasingly inadequate for diverse real-world scenarios. A classic remedy is Mix-tures of Experts (MoE), either by training fusion networks [2, 16, 36, 48] or by directly combining outputs from multiple detectors [7]. Another line of work adapts detectors by augmenting training data with images from newly emerged generators [14,52]. 2.2 MLLM in Visual Forensics 

MLLMs offer new perspectives for visual forensics through their general visual understanding capabilities [30], and their textual outputs can provide explana-tory justifications. However, studies indicate MLLMs’ weak sensitivity to fine-grained cues limits forensic performance [32,40,94]. This largely reflects their re-liance on high-level semantics, which benefits robustness and generalization [74], yet lacking low-level visual backbones to capture pixel-level artifacts [23, 92]. MLLM-based detector paradigms can be broadly categorized into three types. (1) Enhance fine-grained artifact perception and reasoning, via tailored reasoning procedure [26, 69, 79] and artifact taxonomies [57, 71, 93], or box-guided visual reasoning [31]. (2) Capture low-level image features through dedicated modules [18, 65, 85, 96] then directly feed them into MLLM or preprocess features before it [44, 67, 73, 90], or using more low-level-sensitive encoders [49]. This typically requires substantial training to align LLM with newly added features. (3) Build MLLM-centric pipelines by passing specialist detector outputs as text [9, 55] or images [66], or having MLLMs generate special tokens for classification or localization [25,27,28,38,83,92]. Besides, some studies employ MLLMs as routers to select the most suitable detector for a given image [24]. However, constructing explanatory or reasoning data presents a significant challenge for Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT). Early works relied on GPT-4o [18, 28, 29, 44, 86] or human annotation [31, 34]. Subsequent approaches increas-ingly adopted cross-validation across multiple MLLMs [49, 71, 96]. ## 3 Methodology 

3.1 Overview 

MLLMs primarily comprehend images via high-level semantics and can capture phenomena requiring global reasoning, such as logical inconsistencies [34]. This perspective offers strong generalization while complementing low-level feature-based methods [7], motivating many recent AIGI detectors built around MLLMs. However, both improving MLLM explanatory ability and injecting low-level fea-tures via structural modifications require large-scale fine-grained training data. Since AIGI detection remains challenging for pre-trained MLLMs [32], using MLLMs for data annotation could be unreliable and may induce a risky distil-lation setup [92]. EvoGuard 7

We aim to address the above challenges effectively and further unlock the potential of MLLMs for AIGI detection. To this end, we propose a novel agen-tic paradigm with three key principles: (1) Instead of building a single strong detector, we encapsulate diverse detectors as tools and construct an MLLM-based agent that autonomously plans tool usage through action and reflection, forming a dynamic and intelligent detection process that exploits the synergistic strengths of diverse tools; (2) Exploiting agent’s general understanding ability to support heterogeneous tools with varying output formats, and ensures seamless extensibility to incorporate future tools; and (3) Training the agent via Agentic RL using only binary labels, bypassing the need for fine-grained annotated data. Following the above principles, we present EvoGuard. As shown in Fig. 2, it mainly consists of two parts: Capability-Aware Selection and Dynamic Or-chestration. The former encapsulates existing detectors as tools and establishes a tool profile system which is used to selects appropriate tools for an input image (Sec. 3.2). The latter enables the agent to autonomously plan task com-pletion by, at each round, analyzing outcomes from prior tool calls and deciding whether to call additional tools or to summarize conclusions based on tools’ output (Sec. 3.3). Under this paradigm, we train EvoGuard using the GRPO algorithm with specially designed reward functions. Notably, this requires only image-level binary labels, without relying on fine-grained annotations (Sec. 3.4). Finally, we discuss how this design leaves room for future extensibility (Sec. 3.5). 

3.2 Capability-Aware Selection 

The first key to the design of EvoGuard lies in the intelligent invocation of ap-propriate tools to obtain effective results while mitigating the negative influence of suboptimal outputs. To this end, it is essential to recognize the disparities among different tools and establish a paradigm for matching suitable tools. We therefore design a profile for each tool to characterize its capabilities, thereby representing the differences. Subsequently, we encourage the agent to select tools by integrating the input image with the tool profiles. 

Tool Profile Design. The profile comprises two layers: structure , which specifies the dimensions used to characterize a tool, and content , which provides concrete descriptors within each dimension. We define four structure aspects: 

Overall Profile , Strengths , Weaknesses , and Conflict Hints . The Overall Profile 

summarizes behavioral tendencies (e.g., models trained to detect generative ar-tifacts tend to be more conservative, often overlooking unseen artifacts [46]). 

Strengths and Weaknesses indicate data types where the tool performs particu-larly well or poorly. Conflict Hints support decision-making under disagreement by emphasizing a tool’s relative advantage on the given sample type rather than its overall performance. Together, these dimensions capture both holistic and fine-grained characteristics, covering absolute and relative capabilities, thereby establishing a comprehensive framework for tool description. The content layer selects criteria to distinguish images so that capability differences are exposed; it should be neither too coarse nor too granular. We use 8 Zhu. et al. 

three tag dimensions— Subject , Quality , and Style —and constrain the candidate tag set of each dimension to facilitate profile generation and tool selection. 

Tool Profile Generation. We generate tool profiles based on two prin-ciples. 1) Conciseness . We only describe cases where metric values (accuracy, false negative rate, and false positive rate) are notably high or low on spe-cific data types, omitting ordinary cases, by considering that excessive context length degrades performance [13] and increases inference costs. 2) Ambiguity .Rather than specifying exact numerical values, we use linguistic expressions to convey the magnitude of metrics. This is because the training set distribution inevitably differs from real-world applications, and LLM-driven agents possess inherent reasoning capabilities; thus, maintaining ambiguity enables dynamic decision-making based on actual sample characteristics. In practice, we generate tool profiles using the training set. We employ the same underlying MLLM of EvoGuard as a tagger to assign tags to each image. Then for each tool, we compute its metrics under each tag. Based on these metrics, we first use a LLM to produce an initial tool profile and then manually refine it. 3.3 Dynamic Orchestration 

Capability-Aware Selection decides which tool(s) to invoke at each round. Though we can constrain the interaction to a single round, it may not be a good idea. For example, two tools may produce conflicting conclusions in the previous round, or a tool may return a low-confidence prediction; in such cases, invoking addi-tional tools for cross-validation is preferable. We therefore introduce a Dynamic Orchestration mechanism to better leverage the agent’s ability to autonomously plan actions. After each tool invocation, the agent reflects on the current evi-dence and decides whether to call more tools or to conclude the final answer. Specifically, at round n, given the context cn−1 and the tool output from round n−1, denoted as On−1, EvoGuard analyzes the current state and produce an action an ∈ { stop , continue }. The context cn is also updated by incorporat-ing On−1 and the analysis: 

an = Analyze( x, g (x), c n−1, O n−1, P) , cn = {cn−1, O n−1}. (2) Afterwards, if an = continue , we return to tool selection (see Eq. (1)) and proceed to the next round with the new context; otherwise, we summarize the current evidence to produce the final prediction Answer( x):

Answer( x) = Conclude( x, g (x), c n, P) . (3) EvoGuard 9

Enabling reflection and additional actions essentially improves final perfor-mance on complex cases via test-time scaling [64,82]. Compared with selecting a single tool [24] or ensembling all tools [16], our approach preserves the benefits of multi-tool collaboration while mitigating interference and adaptively controlling cost based on the sample-specific runtime behavior. Notably, the general under-standing capability of the MLLM is crucial for this design: it enables fusion of heterogeneous outputs from different detectors, including numerical scores, text, and images. This is very important for extending to future tools, meanwhile such auxiliary signals beyond binary labels can further help the MLLM make more informed decisions. 

3.4 Agentic RL Training 

Under this new paradigm, training focuses on using tools effectively rather than improving the performance of a single detector. This leads us to Agentic Rein-forcement Learning (Agentic RL), which reframes LLMs from passive sequence generators into autonomous, decision-making agents embedded in complex, dy-namic worlds [91]. GRPO was initially used to improve reasoning ability [61], and it is now one of the most commonly used policy optimization algorithms in Agentic RL. It evaluates multiple candidate responses collectively by comparing their relative rewards. This design makes it particularly suitable for tasks with limited supervision or where outcome quality is best assessed relatively. Agentic RL paradigms and the GRPO algorithm align closely with our task setting: in practice, reasoning and tool-use trajectories are highly diverse, making it difficult to assert that any single trajectory is unequivocally superior. Mean-while, SFT datasets that capture detailed intermediate processes are challenging to obtain at low cost. Moreover, models trained via reinforcement learning ex-hibit stronger generalization [11] and can more naturally extend to future model variants. Therefore, we adopt GRPO training that allows the agent to explore autonomously. For the critical reward design in GRPO, in addition to the commonly used outcome-correctness Reward acc and format-compliance Reward format rewards [21], we introduce an explicit thinking reward Reward analysis that encourages the agent to produce its analysis both before and after each tool invocation, which is inspired by prior studies [33, 81] suggesting that moderately increas-ing the number of reasoning steps can improve performance. Additionally, we empirically observe performance degradation when the agent fails to engage in sufficient deliberation. Therefore, in Reward analysis , we penalize cases where the agent produces fewer than 10 tokens of output before each tool invocation. We do not explicitly constrain the form of reasoning; instead, we let the model ex-plore it autonomously. Accordingly, our reward function R( ·) is formulated as:

3.5 Extend to Future Tools 

EvoGuard is inherently designed to extend to additional detectors. First, we encapsulate heterogeneous detectors as tools under a unified schema, so adding or removing tools only requires updating the prompt. This enables true plug-and-play integration. Second, we leverage an MLLM to interpret tool outputs, which naturally supports diverse return formats. For newly added tools, our experiments show that a lightweight profile de-scribing only overall behavior (drafted from prior knowledge) is already capable of enhancing the performance (Sec. 4.3). While a comprehensive profile would evaluate the tool on a given dataset, this still avoids retraining and redeploying the agent, making the extension substantially more convenient. 

## 4 Experiments 

To validate the effectiveness and advantages of the proposed method, we design our experiments to answer three questions: (1) Can EvoGuard more effectively detect AIGI than existing SOTA methods (Sec. 4.2)? (2) Can EvoGuard be easily extended to future tools? This extensibility is crucial for rapidly responding to newly emerging real-world threats (Sec. 4.3). (3) What are the individual contributions of the components in EvoGuard (Sec. 4.4)? We first introduced the setup of experiments, and then presented results and analysis. 

4.1 Experiment Setup Tools Set. EvoGuard is designed to leverage the strengths of different tools. Therefore, the selected tools should differ in architecture and methodology to provide complementarity. We select four recently published SOTA methods. Ef-fort [88] builds a CLIP-based detector and prevents overfitting by freezing the principal components of the feature space. FakeVLM [79] improves VLM-based forgery detection through fine-grained artifact-explanation supervision. MIR-ROR [46] uses DINO [51] to encode reality priors that approximate the real-image manifold, and then evaluates forgery based on the perplexity and residual of manifold-consistent references constructed from the input image. AIDE [87] combines DCT-based frequency information with CLIP-based semantic informa-tion for detection. These four distinctive tools provide a strong foundation for EvoGuard, as well as highly competitive reference baselines. 

Datasets. The training set should be different from those used by its tools, and should be rich and diverse in content to simulate in-the-wild detection sce-narios for the tools. Therefore, we construct a mixed training set of 8,000 images as follows: using a random seed (42), we sample the first 1,000 real and 1,000 fake images from each of the MMFR training set, MMFR test set [18], and DDA-COCO [8]; and the first 2,000 fake images from SynthScars [34]. For evaluation, we use LOKI [89], Bfree [20], and CommunityForensic [52]. LOKI is a recent comprehensive benchmark for synthetic-content detection, con-taining images from multiple sources with broad content diversity. The Bfree test EvoGuard 11 

set contains different versions of the same images collected from social networks, making it suitable for evaluating robustness to artifacts unrelated to forgery. CommunityForensic includes fake images generated by thousands of generators. Because its evaluation set is excessively large, we sample a subset of it. Together, these three datasets form a strong benchmark for effectively assessing the per-formance of EvoGuard and baseline methods in complex and diverse in-the-wild settings. 

Implementation Details. EvoGuard uses Qwen3-VL-4B-Instruct [3] as the base model. We use the AgentLoop module in the VeRL framework [63] to conduct GRPO training and evaluation with multi-round tool calls. We set the learning rate to 1 × 10 −6 and the KL loss coefficient to 0.001. Note that only the agent is trained, while all tools remain frozen. 

Comparative Methods. To ensure a comprehensive comparison, the eval-uated methods include four existing tools (Effort, FakeVLM, MIRROR, and AIDE), EvoGuard framework with pretrained MLLMs (Qwen3-VL-2B/4B/8B-Instruct), and other SOTA methods (FakeShield [86], SIDA [28], FakeReason-ing [18], and Forensic-MOE [16]). For all these competing methods, we utilize the officially released checkpoints provided by their authors. Together, these methods form a diverse and comprehensive benchmark for comparison. 

Metrics. We employ four commonly used evaluation metrics to assess per-formance, including Real Accuracy (R-Acc), Fake Accuracy (F-Acc), Balanced Accuracy (B-Acc), and F1 score.

These results indicate that the proposed capability-aware dynamic orchestration not only improves overall performance, but also helps mitigate the bias of individual detectors. 4.3 Extensibility to Future Tools 

The rapid evolution of generative models and forgery methods requires detectors to continuously evolve as well. A detector that can improve efficiently and con-tinuously would be highly valuable. An important design goal of EvoGuard is to scale to more tools without training. In this way, when new detection tools emerge in the future, EvoGuard can incorporate them directly, thereby improv-ing its ability to handle novel forgeries. To investigate the effectiveness of this design, we train EvoGuard using a subset of tools and evaluate whether providing additional tools at inference time can improve performance. For newly introduced tools, we only supply the Overall Profile , mimicking a realistic setting where we may only have coarse information about a new tool. Since the number of possible subsets is large, we construct tool combinations according to two principles: (1) selecting heterogeneous detec-tors to exploit complementarity, and (2) simulating the introduction of newer, stronger detectors. Concretely, we exclude the best-performing detector on the test set, namely the MIRROR, and instead form three-tool combinations con-sisting of FakeVLM and two other detectors. The results are shown in Fig. 3. 

Train-free Extensibility. As shown, agents trained on each tool subset consistently benefit from having more tools available at test time. This training-free tool extension yields substantial gains, bringing performance close to that of an agent trained with the full tool set. 4.4 Ablation & Mechanism Analysis 

Although the experiments have already demonstrated the superior performance of EvoGuard, in this section, we further investigate whether the design ratio-nale of its core capability-aware dynamic orchestration of heterogeneous detec-tors mechanism is sound, and whether its actual behavior fulfills our design vision. We conduct ablation studies along multiple dimensions and compare against purely routing-based and other ensemble methods, with results sum-marized in Tab. 3. 

Effectiveness of Capability-Aware Selection. We first consider the vari-ant where tool profile is not provided (w/o Tool Profile), which leads to a clear performance drop, indicating that tool profile provides effective guidance for decision-making. Additionally, we tested an approach that directly matches the three tools with the highest accuracy using the three-dimensional tags of a given image, followed by a majority vote (Match Best Tools). Theoretically, this should identify the best tool in the most precise way, but it performed poorly in prac-tice, mainly because the distribution of test set does not fully match the training set. This also shows that our strategy of maintaining a certain level of ambiguity when constructing tool profile helps mitigate this distribution shift. 

Effectiveness of Dynamic Orchestration. We first construct variants where EvoGuard is forced to invoke all tools and to select a single tool; both settings lead to performance degradation. Moreover, EvoGuard also outperforms methods that integrate different models via MOE [62] or directly combine re-sults from heterogeneous detectors with a logic OR operation [7], validating the effectiveness of our designed mechanism, which requires EvoGuard to perform cross-verification and reasoning through autonomous, plan-driven actions. 14 Zhu. et al.

## 5 Discussion and Conclusion 

Conclusion. We present EvoGuard, a pioneering agentic framework designed to address the complex and ever-changing challenges of AIGI detection. Un-like monolithic detectors, EvoGuard encapsulates heterogeneous detectors as callable tools. Powered by capability-aware dynamic orchestration mechanism, it autonomously selects tools and fuses their outputs via multi-round planning and reasoning. Trained exclusively with weak supervision via Agentic RL, Evo-Guard achieves SOTA detection accuracy across various benchmarks while bet-ter balancing accuracy between positive and negative classes. Most importantly, EvoGuard supports plug-and-play integration of new tools without retraining, enabling continual evolution to counter emerging threats. 

Discussion. While EvoGuard offers a practical and sustainable solution, there remains room for improvement. First, MLLM agent introduces additional overhead beyond the detectors themselves. Second, its performance is bounded by the underlying toolset; if all tools fail together, the agent’s reasoning may be misled. 

Future Work. To further advance this work, we identify three future di-rections: (1) Build task-specific small MLLMs via distillation and related tech-niques [84]. (2) Establish a continual optimization pipeline that collects real-world samples to improve the underlying tools and update tool profiles. (3) Extend EvoGuard to broader multi-modal tasks, exploring the potential of this paradigm in wider visual forensics domains. EvoGuard 15
