Abstract 

While large language model-based agents demonstrate great potential in collaborative tasks, their interactivity also introduces secu-rity vulnerabilities. In this paper, we propose and model group collusive attacks, a highly destructive threat in which multiple agents coordinate via sociological strategies to mis-lead the system. To address this challenge, we introduce GroupGuard, a training-free de-fense framework that employs a multi-layered defense strategy, including continuous graph-based monitoring, active honeypot inducement, and structural pruning, to identify and isolate collusive agents. Experimental results across five datasets and four topologies demonstrate that group collusive attacks increase the attack success rate by up to 15% compared to individ-ual attacks. GroupGuard consistently achieves high detection accuracy (up to 88%) and ef-fectively restores collaborative performance, providing a robust solution for securing multi-agent systems. 

1 Introduction 

As large language model (LLM)-based agents demonstrate remarkable capabilities in solving complex tasks (Achiam et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024), they integrate functions such as planning, memory, and tool use, enabling interaction and learning from external environments to address more practical problems (Schick et al., 2023; Song et al., 2023; Zhong et al., 2024). However, this au-tonomy and high interactivity introduce new secu-rity challenges (Li et al., 2023b; Boiko et al., 2023; Ruan et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2024), especially in multi-agent systems (MAS) where collaboration and information propagation among agents expand potential attack surfaces and expose more complex security vulnerabilities (Deng et al., 2025). These attacks contaminate the decision-making environment, erode group trust, and ulti-mately cause collective decision failure, exhibiting greater stealth and destructiveness than individ-ual attacks. Although sociology has long studied the mechanisms behind group behaviors (Sawyer, 2003), current MAS security research has yet to sufficiently consider sociologically driven group attack mechanisms. Traditional defense methods rely on analyzing the behaviors of individual agents, making it dif-ficult to detect attacks arising from covert col-laboration among agents in MAS (Zhang et al., 2024b; Xiang et al., 2024). Consequently, recent research has begun to explore interaction topol-ogy analysis among agents. A representative work is G-Safeguard (Wang et al., 2025a), which em-ploys graph neural networks for anomaly detection but depends heavily on large amounts of training data that are difficult to label. Meanwhile, Xie et al. (Xie et al., 2025) analyze malicious behaviors from the perspectives of psychology and intention concealment but still overlook the effects of group coordination among agents. Therefore, there is an urgent need to develop a training-free, real-time defense framework capable of countering group coordinated attacks. To bridge these research gaps, we first formal-ize Group Collusive Attacks and subsequently develop GroupGuard , a multi-layered defense framework. Specifically, our work addresses the following three key questions: 

How to model Group Collusive Attacks? To fill the gap in existing studies on group attack be-haviors, we propose new group attack strategies inspired by sociological theories. We define three types of group attacks: false consensus, targeted suppression, and group exclusion. The essential characteristic of these attacks is the coordinated collaboration among multiple malicious agents, who manipulate group dynamics through strategies such as cyclic endorsement, concentrated question-ing, or collective exclusion to achieve malicious goals. 

How to detect Group Collusive Attacks? 

To efficiently identify group collusive attacks, we design GroupGuard , a multi-layer framework. First, the continuous monitoring layer dynamically constructs social interaction graphs to capture real-time abnormal group behaviors, flagging agents that exceed a suspicion threshold for secondary detection. Next, the honeypot detection layer uti-lizes Bayesian optimization to generate discrimi-native inducement tasks, forcing suspicious agents to reveal their attack intentions. Finally, the prun-ing layer structurally isolates confirmed malicious nodes from the communication graph, cutting off propagation paths to prevent further spread. 

How generalizable are the attacks and Group-Guard? Given the diversity of communication topologies in MAS, we evaluate the generalizabil-ity of both group collusive attacks and the Group-Guard framework across four representative topolo-gies and five benchmark datasets. Experimental results demonstrate that both the proposed attacks and GroupGuard maintain their effectiveness with-out topology-specific adjustments. This highlights the universal threat of group collusion and the ro-bustness of our defense in complex, dynamic multi-agent environments. 

2 Related Work 

2.1 Graph-based MAS 

Modeling multi-agent interactions as graphs has become a mainstream and efficient research paradigm (Hua et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Qian et al., 2024). In the field of MAS collaboration, researchers widely leverage graph structures to fa-cilitate inter-agent communication, optimize task allocation, and enhance cooperation efficiency (Wu et al., 2024; Chan et al., 2023). Furthermore, some studies further improve performance by optimizing the communication topology (Zhuge et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2025b). In the field of MAS security, this graph-based paradigm has also begun to show its potential. For example, NetSafe (Yu et al., 2024) investigates toxicity prop-agation in MAS under various topological attacks. Inspired by these works, this study also adopts a graph structure to analyze and detect collaborative behavioral patterns among agents.

3 Methodology 

We model multi-agent interactions as a social inter-action graph GS = ( V, E S , T , W ), where V is the set of agents, ES is the edge set, T maps edges to social semantics (SUPPORT, QUESTION, INFO), and W measures interaction intensity. We specif-ically focus on support edges Esupport = {e ∈

ES | τ (e) = SUPPORT } for cooperative rela-tionships and attack edges Eattack = {e ∈ ES |

τ (e) = QUESTION } for adversarial interactions. This structure forms the basis for subsequent mod-eling and defense. Based on GS , GroupGuard establishes a three-layer defense framework. As shown in Figure 2: (1) a continuous monitoring layer for graph-based anomaly detection (Sec. 3.2); (2) a honeypot detec-tion layer for active intent probing (Sec. 3.3); and (3) a pruning layer for structural isolation(Sec. 3.4). These layers work collaboratively to identify and mitigate group collusion attacks in real time. 

3.1 Group Collusive Attack Modeling 

This section models coordinated attacks launched by a set of several malicious agents Vatk ⊂ V .We assume malicious agents can only observe the public conversation history to coordinate strategies, without access to the internal parameters or private prompts of benign agents. Furthermore, attacks are restricted to the communicative level, meaning that agents can only mislead the group via natural language and lack the authority to alter system code or historical records.We divide coordinated attacks into three categories: 

False consensus: The attackers’ goal is to create a false atmosphere of opinion unanimity in order to exert conformity pressure. At the be-havioral level, attackers Vatk form an opinion alliance, with members highly endorsing each other’s views through repeated endorsements and other means, and collectively and some-times questioning external members who hold different opinions. 

Targeted suppression: The attackers’ goal is to concentrate resources to discredit a specific 3target vtarget ∈ V \ Vatk . At the behavioral level, attackers continuously express doubts or make aggressive statements toward vtarget ,regardless of whether the target’s views are correct, with the ultimate goal of systemat-ically weakening its credibility within the group. 

Group exclusion: The attackers’ goal is to dis-mantle group trust and paralyze the decision-making process. At the behavioral level, at-tackers Vatk form a highly cohesive in-group, whose members indiscriminately question and deny all views from out-group members 

V \ Vatk , thereby creating opposition and chaos and undermining the decision-making environment. 

3.2 Continuous Monitoring Layer Based on Interaction Graph Analysis 

The continuous monitoring layer is executed by an Auditor Agent , whose core task is to dynam-ically construct the social interaction graph GS

and identify potential sociological attack behaviors. This process includes two stages: type mapping and graph aggregation. The auditor first contin-uously listens to the raw communication stream and maps each message o(t) 

> i

to a sociological inter-action type τ ∈ { SUPPORT , QUESTION , INFO }.It then performs temporal aggregation over multi-round communications to generate GS with agents as nodes. Based on this graph structure, the au-ditor detects three specific attacks and computes suspicion scores using the following metrics. 

False Consensus Detection This attack mani-fests as high internal consistency within the group and pressure exerted outwardly. In GS , this corre-sponds to a subset of nodes where internal support edges Esupport are dense and attack edges Eattack 

are directed outward. The auditor first extracts the subgraph Gsupport = ( V, E support ) composed solely of support edges, as the primary characteris-tic of colluders is high-frequency mutual endorse-ment among themselves. Next, the overlapping community detection algorithm (Palla et al., 2005) is utilized on Gsupport to identify a set of topolog-ically tight candidate communities C. For each potential community C ∈ C , we define a suspicion score:        

> Sconsensus (C) = α·Sloyalty (C) + (1 −α)·Saggression (C),

(1) where Sloyalty (C) measures the collaborative con-sistency within the community by calculating the weighted density of support edges between internal nodes, and Saggression (C) is defined as the average intensity of attack edges initiated by community members towards external dissenting nodes. False consensus exhibits significant targeting: colluders do not attack randomly, but rather concentrate their efforts to suppress specific external nodes that chal-lenge their false consensus. Therefore, we intro-duce Saggression to focus on capturing the intensity of attacks against dissenters. 

Targeted Suppression Detection This attack aims to weaken the credibility of a specific tar-get vtarget by concentrating attacks. To accurately identify such attacks and reduce the false positive rate, the auditor adopts a comprehensive judgment mechanism based on multi-dimensional features. Specifically, the system marks a node v as a victim only when it simultaneously satisfies the following three characteristics: (1) Abnormal attack intensity: The weighted in-degree Dattacked (v) of node v on attack edges Eattack is significantly higher than the population average (i.e., exceeding the mean by k

standard deviations). (2) Trust collapse: The trust score T (v) of the node exhibits a sharp downward trend within a short period. We define the trust score T (v) as the proportion of effective support received by the node relative to all interactions: 

T (v) = Dsupport (v)

Dtotal (v) (2) where Dsupport (v) denotes the weighted in-degree of node v on support edges, and Dtotal (v) rep-resents the total weighted in-degree of the node across all types of interaction edges. If ∆T (v) falls below a threshold δτ , it indicates that the attack has caused substantial damage to its reputation. (3) Concentration of attack sources: Attacks are highly concentrated in a specific subset of nodes rather than being randomly distributed. This is quantified by the attack source entropy Hsrc . A candidate community is flagged when Hsrc < δ H . Once a victim target is identified, the system traces back the attack sources and marks nodes contributing the primary attack weights as suspicious. 

Group Exclusion Detection This attack is char-acterized by high in-group cohesion, accompanied by widespread and indiscriminate rejection of ex-ternal nodes. The auditor quantifies exclusion by 4evaluating the distribution breadth of attacks:        

> Sexclusion (C) = β·Sloyalty (C) + (1 −β)·Sdispersion (C),

(3) where Sloyalty (C) is similarly used to measure the degree of internal solidarity, while Sdispersion (C)

measures the coverage of attacks, i.e., the propor-tion of external nodes that have been attacked by this community relative to the total number of ex-ternal nodes. Unlike false consensus, the core so-ciological characteristic of group exclusion is in-discriminate hostility, referring to a generalized pattern of rejection and negation directed at all non-members, regardless of their specific views or identities. Attackers tend to refuse effective inter-action with any non-internal members, attempting to isolate themselves from the system. Therefore, we introduce the dispersion metric Sdispersion to quantify the breadth of attacks rather than mere intensity. Detailed formulations of Sloyalty (C),

Saggression (C), Sdispersion (C) and Hsrc along with hyperparameter configurations are provided in Appendix C and D.

To minimize the high cost of evaluating f (q), we combine semantic embeddings with Bayesian opti-mization by mapping discrete questions into a con-tinuous space and employing the Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE) (Watanabe, 2023) for effi-cient posterior sampling. To avoid local optima, the Proposer LLM acts as an exploration mechanism: with probability ϵ = 0 .1, it dynamically generates new candidate questions based on high-score sam-ples to expand Q. This approach ensures search efficiency while enabling continuous exploration of the highly discriminative problem space. 

3.4 Pruning Layer 

Once the honeypot layer confirms the malicious identity of an agent, the system immediately exe-cutes an outgoing edge pruning strategy. Specif-ically, it permanently removes from the underly-ing communication graph all outgoing connections originating from malicious nodes. This mecha-nism can physically block the propagation paths of malicious information while maintaining the con-nectivity of the benign subgraph, thereby achieving immediate isolation of the threat source.

4.3 RQ2: Defense Effectiveness 

We evaluate the defense effectiveness of our frame-work by comparing the detection accuracy of GroupGuard with AutoDefense and G-safeguard. Based on Table 2, we make the following observa-tions: 

Obs3. Improvement in detection accuracy. 

Experimental results show that GroupGuard con-sistently outperforms both AutoDefense and G-safeguard in terms of average accuracy (Avg.) across most datasets. For instance, when detecting False Consensus on Deepseek-V3.1, GroupGuard achieves an average accuracy of 83.20%, surpass-ing AutoDefense (64.76%) and slightly exceeding G-safeguard (82.44%). Although AutoDefense and G-safeguard exhibit competitive performance in specific individual tasks, GroupGuard demonstrates a higher perfor-mance floor and broader task coverage across di-verse scenarios. This indicates that, compared to AutoDefense, which relies on single-text se-mantic analysis, and G-safeguard, which trains a GNN model for detection, GroupGuard can more precisely capture the structured anomalous behav-iors characteristic of collusion attacks. By model-ing a more comprehensive social interaction graph among agents, GroupGuard effectively identifies complex inter-dependencies that are often over-looked by existing GNN-based or text-based detec-tion methods.

5 Conclusion 

In this study, we propose Group Collusive Attacks in MAS and develop the GroupGuard for their de-fense. By integrating sociological theories with graph-based interaction analysis, GroupGuard ef-8fectively identifies complex malicious behaviors, that are difficult for traditional methods to cap-ture. Evaluation across four topologies demon-strates that GroupGuard not only significantly out-performs the baseline in detection accuracy but also maintains a high performance floor and broad task coverage. Furthermore, the pruning mecha-nism enables the system to recover collaborative performance to near-original levels. We hope our work provides valuable insights into building se-cure and trustworthy LLM-based MAS. 

Limitation 

Although GroupGuard demonstrates strong robust-ness in addressing the three proposed group col-lusive strategies, our threat model is primarily de-rived from established sociological theories. In real-world adversarial environments, malicious agents may adopt more adaptive camouflage strate-gies, such as simulating benign interaction logic or introducing noise data to evade graph-based monitoring. Although we have validated its effec-tiveness across various topologies and tasks, future research still needs to explore the defensive per-formance against unknown, heterogeneous, and dynamically evolving attack strategies to further extend the defensive boundary of GroupGuard.
