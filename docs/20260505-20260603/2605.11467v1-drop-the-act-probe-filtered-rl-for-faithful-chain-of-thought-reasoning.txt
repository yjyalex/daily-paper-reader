# Drop the Act: Probe-Filtered RL for Faithful Chain-of-Thought Reasoning 

Swapnil Parekh 

Intuit 

swapnil_parekh@intuit.com 

Abstract 

Reasoning models post-hoc rationalize answers they have already committed to internally, producing chains of reasoning theater : deliberative-looking steps that contribute nothing to correctness. This wastes inference tokens, pollutes interpretability, and obscures what the model actually computed. We introduce 

ProFIL (Pro be-Fil tered Reinforcement Learning) to reduce theater, increase chain-of-thought faithfulness, and shrink chain length in a single, drop-in extension to Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO). A multi-head attention probe is trained once on the frozen base model to detect post-commitment steps from internal activations alone; during GRPO, rollouts whose probe score exceeds a threshold have their advantage zeroed. Our central finding is that a probe trained on a frozen base, with verifier-derived labels and no human annotation, provides a stable signal that suppresses theater while resisting the RL-obfuscation failure mode predicted by prior work. Across four reasoning domains (GSM8K, LiveCodeBench, ToolUse, MMLU-Redux) and two model architectures (Llama-8B, Qwen-7B), ProFIL reduces post-commitment theater by 11–100% , raises faithful-fraction (e.g., +24 pp on LiveCodeBench under an independent Claude 3.7 Sonnet judge), and shortens chains by 4–19% , all while preserving or improving task accuracy. ProFIL also beats a matched length-penalty GRPO baseline, isolating the gain as semantic commitment-detection rather than chain compression. Probe weights, training configurations, and rollouts are released across all four domains. 

1 Introduction 

While scaling up reasoning with reinforcement learning has produced remarkable results [ 6, 22 , 12 ], a converging body of evidence reveals a systematic gap between what these models appear to do and what they actually do. Biased prompts steer final answers while leaving the chain of thought unchanged [ 20 ]; models recover correct answers from truncated chains far earlier than their length implies [ 14 ]; large-scale audits of production reasoning models find post-hoc rationalization to be widespread [ 4 ]. The pattern is consistent: reasoning models commit to an answer internally, then continue generating steps that look deliberative but contribute nothing. We call this reasoning theater 

(Figure 1). Theater carries a real cost. Post-commitment steps pollute interpretability, introduce spurious signal into process-reward training [ 21 , 16 ], and inflate token cost in agentic deployments where inference budget matters. Prior work has measured this gap carefully but largely treated it as a diagnostic. Our goal is to reduce it : to suppress theater, raise faithfulness, and shrink chains for inference efficiency, while preserving or improving task accuracy. Faithfulness is non-verifiable: unlike answer correctness, no ground-truth label is available at the end of a rollout, and standard GRPO reward shaping cannot reach behaviors the verifier never sees. Our key observation is that the verifier we need is already inside the model. We introduce ProFIL

2 Related Work 

Chain-of-thought faithfulness. Turpin et al. [20] flip final answers via biased prompts while leaving the chain unchanged; Lanham et al. [14] introduce the forced-answering protocol we adopt for label generation (§4); Chen et al. [4] report large-scale faithfulness audits on production reasoning models. We refer to the post-commitment elaboration these studies measure as reasoning theater . This thread is diagnostic; ProFIL acts on the same signal to reduce it during training. 

Probes as reward signals. Probes recover latent knowledge [ 3, 2] and steer behavior at inference [ 15 , 18 ]. Goodfire’s RLFR (Reinforcement Learning from Feature Rewards; 8) pushed multi-head attention probes from inference into RL training. (1) Forced-answering yields verifier-derived step-level performativity labels (no human annotation): the first step whose forced answer is correct defines the commitment point ; later steps are performative. (2) A gated multi-head attention probe (RLFR-style; 8 ) is trained once on activations of the frozen base at layers ℓ1, ℓ 2, then never updated. The probe boundary is therefore stable throughout RL, and the GRPO update itself is unchanged outside this filter. key departure is that we probe the frozen base, not the policy being trained. Inference-time steering methods such as Contrastive Activation Addition (CAA; 18 ) and Inference-Time Intervention (ITI; 15 ) suppress behaviors at the forward-pass level but leave policy weights untouched, so the model regenerates the suppressed behavior on the next call. ProFIL eliminates theater durably by changing what the policy learns. 

Process reward models. Process Reward Models (PRMs; 21 , 16 ) score step correctness ; ProFIL scores step commitment . A step can be correct and performative simultaneously: a PRM rewards it, ProFIL filters the rollout containing it. The two are complementary. 

Length control as the closest auxiliary signal. Brevity and length-control penalties [ 12 , 1] target the same surface phenomenon (over-long chains) but operate on token count rather than commitment. Our LiveCodeBench ablation (§5.3) shows they worsen theater, isolating commitment-detection as the operative signal. 

3 Background 

GRPO. GRPO [ 19 , 6, 12 ] samples K rollouts per prompt, scores each with a verifier (a binary correctness checker, e.g., string match for GSM8K, test-case execution for LiveCodeBench), and updates the policy via group-relative advantage. Because the verifier cannot score non-verifiable behaviors like faithfulness, ProFIL gates which rollouts contribute to the update (§4). We define our two metrics, the performativity ratio (fraction of post-commitment steps) and the faithful fraction 

(fraction of chains with ratio ≤ δ), formally in §4.

4.1 Preliminary: Performativity, commitment, and faithful fraction 

A reasoning policy πθ produces a chain y = ( y1, . . . , y T ) of reasoning steps for input x, followed by an answer a. Let a⋆(x) denote the correct answer. We define the commitment point c(y; x) ∈{1, . . . , T } as the smallest index t such that the forced answer from prefix y1: t equals a⋆(x); if none exists, the chain is uncommitted . Labels are constructed without human annotation: at every step boundary we insert a forced-answer prompt and greedy-decode the answer; the first step whose answer matches the verifier’s ground truth becomes the commitment point, and later steps are labeled performative (App. B). 

4.2 Frozen-base attention probe 

We train a gated multi-head attention probe in the style of RLFR [ 8 ]: H=8 heads with learned queries 

qh ∈ Rdh ; keys and values come from per-layer LayerNorm-projected residual-stream activations of the frozen base πbase at layers ℓ1, ℓ 2; each head has a sigmoid gate, and the outputs are combined through a scalar output projection. The probe is trained with binary cross-entropy (BCE) on forced-answering labels using frozen-base activations only, then frozen. Training AUROC exceeds 0.92 on all four domains (Table 1). Running the probe on πbase rather than on the policy being trained keeps the decision boundary stable throughout RL (analogous to the target network in DQN; 17 ), so the probe needs no recalibration. 

4.3 The probe filter 

GRPO [ 19 ] samples K rollouts per prompt, scores each with verifier reward r(k), and updates πθ via group-relative advantage ˆA(k) = (˜ r(k) − μ)/σ . We introduce a probe filter : compute mean per-step performativity ¯p(k) via πbase and the probe; if ¯p(k) ≥ τ , zero the reward and the advantage: 

˜r(k) = r(k) · 1[¯ p(k) < τ ], ˆA(k) ← ˜r(k) − μσ · 1[¯ p(k) < τ ].

Filtered rollouts contribute zero to the gradient. Their reward is set to zero before GRPO’s group normalization, so they enter the μ/σ computation as zero-reward rollouts rather than being dropped. The threshold τ is the only ProFIL hyperparameter; sparse-reward regimes call for a smaller τ to avoid over-filtering the few correct rollouts. 5.2.1 ToolUse: Chain-Level Theater and a 5× Faithful-Fraction Gain 

On ToolUse, ProFIL increases the faithful fraction by 5× (0.029 → 0.147 , Table 1). The theater signal here is chain-level rather than per-step: each tool-call step is a well-formed JSON object with no room for post-hoc elaboration, so theater appears as additional planning blocks generated after the agent has already committed its first complete tool plan (commitment heuristic in App. D). 

ProFIL increases single-tool-call resolutions 8×. The faithful-fraction gain has a concrete mecha-nistic source: single-thinking-block resolutions rise from 1/68 to 8/68 . In these rollouts the model identifies the right tool and acts on it without generating a second planning block to re-examine its choice. The filter is selective: it suppresses post-commitment elaboration when the first plan is sufficient, while preserving genuine multi-step reasoning. 

5.3 Length Compression Is Not the Operative Mechanism 

A matched GRPO + length-penalty baseline on LiveCodeBench (Table 2; full reward form in App. B) achieves performativity 0.374 , higher than the unpenalized baseline ( 0.267 ) and well above ProFIL’s 0.076 . ProFIL’s gain holds within every length quintile (App. A, Figure 8), and the ranking ProFIL < baseline < length-penalty is stable across all detection thresholds we tested (App. A.3). Faithfulness and brevity are therefore byproducts of the same mechanism: chains shrink when the rollout filter targets commitment, not when the reward penalizes characters.

6 Discussion and Conclusion 

Self-correction is preserved by construction. The commitment point is defined as the first step whose forced answer matches ground truth, so any earlier reasoning, including wrong intermediate answers and the correction moves that fix them, sits in the pre-commitment region and is never filtered. Two GSM8K case studies in App. G make this concrete: in both, the model produces an 8incorrect answer at an early step, recovers, and arrives at the correct answer; ProFIL retains every step of that correction trajectory and only filters post-commitment elaboration that follows. 

Limitations and future work. We evaluate on the DeepSeek-R1-Distill family, the de facto open-weight standard for reasoning-RL research, across two architectures (Llama-8B, Qwen-7B); scaling up to larger checkpoints and other RL-trained reasoners is a natural next step. Two ambitious extensions follow from the recipe. First, ProFIL composes with process reward models in a single GRPO update: a PRM rewards correct steps while the probe filters rollouts whose reasoning concludes after 

commitment, combining local correctness with global faithfulness. Second, replacing the verifier with an LLM-judge-derived commitment signal lifts ProFIL to open-ended domains (writing, dialogue, agentic web tasks) that lack a ground-truth checker. 

Conclusion. ProFIL targets the three things that make reasoning theater costly: it suppresses post-commitment elaboration, raises chain-of-thought faithfulness, and shortens chains for inference efficiency, while preserving or improving task accuracy. Across four reasoning domains and two model architectures the trained model commits and stops, with a probe boundary that survives RL intact and a learned policy whose chain-of-thought more closely reflects the computation that produced the answer. More broadly, the result demonstrates that a model’s own internal representations, read once on the frozen base, can supply a stable, annotation-free training signal for behaviors that the verifier alone cannot see. This turns the verifier blind spot into an asset and points to a general recipe for non-verifiable objectives in post-training, with faithfulness as the first concrete instance.

G Self-Correction Case Studies 

We present two GSM8K rollouts from the probe-trained model in which the forced-answer oracle confirms a wrong answer at an early step and a correct answer at a later step. In both cases, ProFIL assigns the commitment point at the first correct step; the intermediate correction steps are pre-commitment and are never labeled performative or filtered. 

Example 1: Javelin vs. Dragon (ground truth: 200 feet). The problem asks how far outside the dragon’s reach Polly can stand. At step 1 the model computes the gem-enhanced range (1200 feet) and, if stopped there, answers 1200 , mistaking an intermediate quantity for the final answer. At step 2 it subtracts the dragon’s reach ( 1200 − 1000 = 200 ) and correctly answers 200 . The commitment point is assigned at step 2; step 3 (a restatement) is post-commitment. ProFIL filters step 3 if it is performative, but steps 1–2 (including the correction) are untouched. 

Example 2: Post-it Notes (ground truth: 163 notes). The problem requires setting up and solving 

80 + x = 243 . At step 0 the model reads off the initial count (80) and would answer 80 if stopped; this is a premature answer before the equation is established. Steps 1–5 build the equation; the model cannot yet commit. At step 6 it solves x = 163 and the forced-answer oracle returns 163 : the commitment point. The six-step correction path (steps 0–5) is entirely pre-commitment and is never filtered.

I Broader Impacts 

ProFIL produces models whose chain-of-thought more closely reflects the computation that led to the answer. The primary benefit is auditability: a reasoning model whose chain reflects its computation is more amenable to oversight, step-level interpretability, and honest disagreement. The symmetric concern is that a method suppressing “performative” filler could in principle target any reasoning pattern a probe is trained to recognize, including reasoning a model should express (safety considerations, uncertainty, refusal preambles). Since our probe targets behavior identified by forced-answering correctness, the immediate risk is low; the general lesson is that probes are dual-use and the choice of probe target is a value judgment.
