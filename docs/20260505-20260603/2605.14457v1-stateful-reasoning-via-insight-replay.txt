# Stateful Reasoning via Insight Replay 

Bin Lei 1, Caiwen Ding 1, Jiachen Yang 2, Ang Li 2 and Xin Eric Wang 2 

> 1University of Minnesota, 2Simular AI

Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning has become a foundation for eliciting multi-step reasoning in large language models, but recent studies show that its benefits do not scale monotonically with chain length: while longer CoT generally enables a model to tackle harder problems, on a given problem, accuracy typically increases with CoT length up to a point, after which it declines. We identify a major cause of this phenomenon: as the CoT grows, the model’s attention to critical insights produced earlier in the trace gradually weakens, making those insights progressively less accessible when they are most needed. Therefore, we propose InsightReplay, a stateful reasoning approach in which the model periodically extracts critical insights from its reasoning trace and replays them near the active generation frontier, keeping them accessible as the reasoning scales. Extensive experiments on a 2×3×4 benchmark grid, covering model scales {8B , 30B }, model families {Qwen3.5 , DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen , Gemma-4 }, and reasoning benchmarks {AIME , HMMT , GPQA Diamond , LiveCodeBench v5 }, show that 3-round InsightRe-play yields accuracy gains across all 24 settings, with an averaged improvement of +1.65 points over standard CoT, and a largest single-setting gain of +9.2 points on R1-Distill-32B’s LiveCodeBench v5 subset. Our results suggest that the effectiveness of test-time scaling depends not only on how much a model reasons, but also on whether critical intermediate insights remain accessible throughout long reasoning trajectories. 

# 1. Introduction 

Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning is a key mechanism for eliciting reasoning in large language models. By explicitly generating intermediate steps, CoT decomposes difficult objectives into smaller sub-problems and has become a foundation for test-time scaling: increasing reasoning-token budgets often improves performance on difficult tasks [1, 2, 3, 4]. However, recent work shows that the benefits of additional reasoning tokens do not scale monotonically. On fixed-difficulty problems, accuracy typically follows an inverted-U relationship with CoT length (see Figure 1): performance first improves as the model reasons longer, reaches a peak, and then declines as the trajectory becomes excessively long [ 5 , 6 , 7]. Harder problems shift this peak rightward, requiring longer reasoning before degradation appears. What drives this decline? Prior work has identified that not all reasoning tokens contribute equally: a small subset of sentences disproportionately determines the final answer, and certain attention heads consistently focus on them [ 8 ]. We complement this line of work by asking a different question: how does the model’s access to such critical insights evolve as the reasoning chain grows? 

In Section 2.2, we directly measure the pre-softmax attention from answer tokens to critical insights as a function of token distance, and find that it decays monotonically. As a result, later reasoning is conducted with progressively degraded access to the very intermediate conclusions that matter most. This observation suggests a broader view of test-time scaling: increasing reasoning depth alone is insufficient unless important reasoning state can also remain accessible over time. This echoes a classical challenge in sequence modeling: preserving long-range information across time. The main contributions of this paper are as follows: 

• We empirically study critical insights in CoT and identify two key properties: (I) critical insights and the surrounding reasoning trace are complementary; (II) attention to critical insights decays as CoT grows, making them progressively less accessible to the model. 

• Building on these observations, we propose InsightReplay , a stateful reasoning approach that periodically extracts critical insights and replays them near the active generation frontier. We further provide a theoretical analysis showing that InsightReplay can shift the optimal reasoning length rightward and raise the achievable peak accuracy. 

• We validate InsightReplay in a purely inference-time setting and show that replaying critical in-sights yields consistent improvements across a broad range of models and reasoning benchmarks, without any additional training. 

• We further reinforce this reasoning pattern through post-training, demonstrating that InsightRe-play improves both training stability and reasoning performance over vanilla CoT under same training settings.

# 2. Properties of Critical Insights in Long CoT 

2.1. Critical Insights and the Reasoning Trace are Complementary 

Related Work. Recent work provides evidence that information in CoT is not uniformly distributed: 

compression methods show that many reasoning tokens can be pruned with minimal accuracy loss [ 10 , 11 , 12 , 13 ]; summarize-and-restart methods first generate a reasoning segment, then summarizes it into a compact form, discards the original segment, and resumes thinking from the summary [ 14 , 15 , 16 ]; and importance-attribution methods identify a small number of sentences or tokens that disproportionately influence the final answer [ 8, 17 ]. What remains underexplored is the relationship between the original insights produced within the CoT and the surrounding reasoning trace that produced them. We seek to verify and quantify whether the two are substitutable or complementary.

2.2. Critical Insights Become Less Accessible as Reasoning Chains Grow 

Related Work. While prior work has identified important reasoning steps and the heads that attend to them [ 8], and while positional encodings such as RoPE [ 19 ] are known to bias attention toward nearby tokens, empirical attention patterns are ultimately shaped by learned query-key interactions rather than positional geometry alone [ 20 ]. Whether attention to critical insights actually decays with distance therefore requires direct measurement. We take critical insights as the unit of analysis and measure how attention from answer tokens to these insights varies with token distance. 

Experimental Setting. On 60 AIME problems with Qwen3-8B and Gemma-4-31B-it , the model first generates a CoT trace, after which Qwen3.5-397B-A17B identifies 5–7 critical insights, each localized to a token span 𝑐 ending at position 𝑝 𝑒 . We use a stronger extractor here because attention measurement requires precise token boundaries that smaller models do not produce reliably. 3.1. Method Overview

As the trajectory progresses, the sequence of insights {𝐼 𝑡 } acts as a continuously updated, compressed abstraction of what has been concluded so far, kept near the active generation frontier so it remains accessible to subsequent reasoning. # 4. Pure Inference Experiments 

We evaluate InsightReplay across a 2 × 3 × 4 experimental grid: two model scales, three model families, and four reasoning benchmarks. Full implementation details are provided in Appendix D. 

4.1. Experimental Setup 

Models. We evaluate 6 open-weight reasoning models, organized into a 2 × 3 grid of scales and fam-ilies: (1) Qwen3.5-9B and Qwen3.5-35B-A3B [21 ], where the latter is a mixture-of-experts model with 3B active parameters; (2) Gemma-4-E4B and Gemma-4-31B-it [ 22 ], dense models that inter-leave local sliding-window attention ( 1024 tokens) with global full-context attention and apply partial-rotary RoPE on the first quarter of head dimensions in global layers; (3) R1-Distill-Qwen-7B and 

R1-Distill-Qwen-32B [3], distilled from DeepSeek-R1 onto Qwen2.5 backbones. 

Benchmarks. We evaluate on four reasoning benchmarks: AIME 2025/2026 [ 18 ] (60 math prob-lems with integer answers), HMMT [ 23 ] (60 Harvard–MIT competition math problems), GPQA Diamond [ 24 ] (198 graduate-level science MCQs), and LiveCodeBench v5 [ 25 ] (62 code problems uniformly sampled from the full benchmark; see Appendix D for the sampling procedure). 

Variants. We compare four inference variants: (1) Base : standard thinking-mode CoT; (2) VO 

(Verify-Only ): the model is prompted to continue thinking after its initial trace via a budget-forcing cue ( "Wait, let me double-check..." ), but performs no insight extraction or replay; (3) IR1 

/ (4) IR3 : InsightReplay with 1 or 3 rounds of extract-then-replay. The contrast between IR 𝑘 and VO isolates the contribution of the InsightReplay mechanism beyond extended thinking alone. 

Sampling and metrics. For each problem, we draw 16 completions with temperature 𝜏 = 1 and top-𝑝 = 0.95 . The model generates until it produces an EOS token or reaches its native context length. We report accuracy as the primary metric, with average completion tokens as a cost measure.

# 6. Limitation 

Although InsightReplay can deliver substantial absolute gains where the base model has substantive room to grow, with the largest single-cell improvement reaching +9.2 points on R1-Distill-32B /LiveCodeBench v5 subset(lifting 25 .8% to 35 .0% , +35 .7% relative), the gains are bounded by each benchmark’s avail-able headroom. On near-saturated settings the marginal improvement is modest: on Qwen3.5-35B-A3B /HMMT, IR3 improves over an 88 .5% baseline by only +0.1 points. This pattern is consistent with our theo-retical framework, in which Φ(𝑑 0) relocation cannot exceed the ceiling imposed by 𝑝 (𝑁 ) itself. We therefore expect InsightReplay to provide the largest practical value on hard reasoning tasks where the underlying model is far from saturation. 

# 7. Conclusion 

We introduced InsightReplay, a stateful reasoning approach that periodically extracts critical insights from the CoT trace and replays them near the active generation frontier. By mitigating the attention de-cay that critical insights suffer in long trajectories, InsightReplay reshapes the inverted-U between CoT length and accuracy, shifting the peak rightward and lifting it upward, thereby expanding the regime in which extended reasoning translates into higher accuracy. We support this through both theoretical analysis and empirical validation: positive gains across all 24 cells of our scale × family × benchmark grid in the pure-inference setting, and consistent improvements in training stability and peak accuracy when used as a rollout protocol for post-training.

# D. Pure Inference Experiment Details 

This appendix expands on the experimental setup of Section 4, covering inference infrastructure, decoding configuration, prompting templates, evaluation protocol, and reproducibility notes that were omitted from the main text for brevity. 

D.1. Hardware and Inference Stack 

All inference experiments are run on a single node with 8× NVIDIA H200 141GB GPUs interconnected via NVLink. Models are served through vLLM [ 30 ] v 0.19 .0 via its OpenAI-compatible HTTP endpoint, with one server instance per model and all 8 GPUs visible to that instance. The parallelism layout depends on model size: the 30B-tier models (Qwen3.5-35B-A3B, Gemma-4-31B-it, R1-Distill-Qwen-32B) use tensor parallelism TP = 2 with data parallelism DP = 4, while the 8B-tier models (Qwen3.5-9B, Gemma-4-E4B-it, R1-Distill-Qwen-7B) use TP = 1 with DP = 8. KV-cache memory utilization is capped at 0.92 . The maximum context length is set per model family at the largest value the KV budget can hold under the chosen DP : 200 ,000 tokens for Qwen and Gemma, and the native 131 ,072 

tokens for the R1-distill family. We pass –trust-remote-code so that custom model code – e.g. Gemma’s interleaved local/global attention with sliding-window size 1024 – loads with its released configuration; no custom attention modification is applied on our side. 

D.2. Decoding Configuration 

We sample with temperature 𝜏 = 1.0 and nucleus top-𝑝 = 0.95 ; all other vLLM sampling parameters (e.g. top_k , repetition_penalty , presence/frequency penalties) are left at their library defaults so

the comparison is not biased by ad-hoc decoding tweaks. We pass skip_special_tokens=False so that thinking-block markers ( ⟨think ⟩ / ⟨/think ⟩ for Qwen and R1-distill, ⟨| channel ⟩thought / ⟨channel |⟩ 

for Gemma) appear in the raw response; downstream extraction relies on these markers to split the thinking trace from the post-think tail. The 𝑘 = 16 samples per problem are produced by issuing 𝑘

D.3. Prompting Templates 

User-turn header. The user-turn content is a dataset-specific header concatenated with the format-ted question; the chat template then wraps it into a model-specific prompt. The header explicitly specifies the answer-format the grader keys on: 

• AIME : “ Solve the math problem. Final answer must be a single non-negative integer in ⟨Answer ⟩. . . ⟨/Answer ⟩.”

• GPQA Diamond : “ Select the best answer. Final answer must be a single letter (A, B, C, or D) in

InsightReplay injection. InsightReplay is a single post-hoc edit of the baseline response: once the baseline emits the thinking-close marker, we (i) ask the model to enumerate the important conclusions of its own thinking (ii) splice a self-verification block back in before the thinking-close marker; and (iii) let the model continue from that point until it re-emits a thinking-close marker and writes a fresh final answer. The block is phrased in the model’s first person so that, embedded inside the thinking block, it reads as a continuation of the internal monologue rather than an external instruction. Concretely the block contains: 

• an opening line “ Wait, before I commit to a final answer, let me restate what’s being asked and double-check the key conclusions I’ve been relying on ”; 

• a one-line restatement of the user’s header and the question, so the model does not drift from the task in a long thinking stream; 

• a numbered list of the extracted insights under “ Key conclusions so far:

D.5. Evaluation Protocol 

The per-sample correct flag is computed by first stripping the model’s thinking block (everything up to and including the last thinking-close marker) and then applying dataset-specific extraction: 

• AIME. The post-think tail is searched for an integer answer using an ordered list of patterns: 

⟨Answer ⟩ 𝑁 ⟨/Answer ⟩, \boxed{N} , \Answer{N} , “ Answer: N” (with markdown bold/italic tolerated around the word “ Answer ” and between the colon and the digit), and a final “open 

⟨Answer ⟩ without a closing tag” fallback. The first pattern that matches wins; ties within a pattern resolve to the last occurrence. Grading is exact integer comparison against the gold answer. 

• GPQA Diamond. The same priority list adapted to single-letter answers A–D. We deliberately do not match “ Option A/B/C/D ” phrases inside the reasoning, which would otherwise silently override the model’s actual final answer with the last-mentioned option. Grading is case-insensitive letter equality. 

• LiveCodeBench v5. The last ``` python ... ``` block in the post-think tail is taken as the candidate solution and executed in a Docker sandbox ( python:3.10-slim , –network none ,

–cpus 1 , –memory 2g ) against the full hidden test suite. The runner walks all test cases serially within a single sandbox invocation under a 20 -second wall-clock budget covering all tests for that candidate; a sample is correct iff every test passes. We grade 8 candidates in parallel via a process pool. Function-style tests call Solution().<func>(*args) ; stdin-style tests pipe the input to 

python solution.py and compare stdout (after stripping leading/trailing whitespace) against the expected output. 

• HMMT. A LaTeX-aware grader is used because gold answers are arbitrary symbolic expressions. We first extract the candidate’s final answer from the post-think tail with a brace-counting scan over the last \boxed{...} (brace counting handles nested {} that a flat regex would mis-balance), with end-of-text fallbacks to $$ ... $$ , \[ ... \] , inline $ ... $ (with a leading “ lhs =” stripped), and finally a bare standalone final line. All fallbacks are restricted to the end of the response so math fragments mid-reasoning cannot win. Equivalence is checked with the math_verify library (HuggingFace MathArena) configured with float_rounding=3 (∼5×10 −4 tolerance) plus two robustness fallbacks: (i) if the candidate contains \pm , we expand it into both signed branches and additionally retry the comma-joined two-solution form, since the prompt asks for multi-solution sets to be packed into a single \boxed{} separated by commas; (ii) if symbolic verification fails, we evaluate gold and candidate to 30 -digit sympy floats and accept on relative tolerance 10 −3,catching cases where a model truncates a decimal just inside the symbolic-rounding cutoff. # F. Length-Sweep Protocol Details 

To jointly characterize the relationship between compute budget and accuracy for the length sweep in Section 4.3, we apply the following bin-based visualization protocol per (model, dataset, method) triple. 

Per-problem binning. For each problem, we independently sample 𝑘 = 16 baseline completions (temperature 1.0, top-𝑝 0.95 ), producing 16 thinking trajectories of varying length. We sort these 16 

samples by completion tokens (ascending) and bin them uniformly into 8 length levels ( 2 samples per level). Crucially, this binning is done per problem , not globally across all problems: since problems differ substantially in intrinsic difficulty, a global token-based binning would conflate length with difficulty (a “short” bin would be dominated by easy problems, while a “long” bin would be dominated by hard problems). Per-problem binning instead identifies, for each problem, its 8 relative length levels (very-short, short, medium, . . . , very-long), so that within each level we compare samples that come from the same set of problems —isolating the length effect from the difficulty effect. Figure 6 illustrates this procedure schematically. 

Sample-level pairing for VO/IR. The corresponding VO / IR1 / IR3 samples strictly inherit the baseline’s token ordering: each VO/IR/IR3 sample is paired to its source baseline sample (sorted by that baseline’s token count). # H. Alternative Training Method 

This appendix documents an earlier attempt to train the multi-round InsightReplay structure directly, rather than inducing it at rollout time with a fixed continuation cue. The pipeline has two stages: we first construct supervised multi-round traces, and then optimize the policy with a training procedure that explicitly mirrors the round-wise visibility pattern used at inference time. 

H.1. Method Overview 

Base model and goal. We start from Qwen3.5-9B-Base [ 21 ]. The goal of this alternative design is to make the multi-round reasoning structure explicit during training: each round exposes only the question, the currently visible reasoning context, and the replayed intermediate insights that should be accessible at that round. 

Stage 1: Supervised multi-round trace construction. To obtain SFT data in the target format, we sample 2,000 math problems from DAPO-Math-17K [ 29 ]. For each problem, a strong teacher model, Qwen3.5-397B-A17B , generates a full solution, which we then decompose into a sequence of reasoning steps and compact intermediate insights. We convert these into multi-round targets where all non-final rounds contain a reasoning segment together with an intermediate insight, and the last round produces the final answer. This procedure yields 1,892 valid problems and 8,102 SFT training entries. A concrete example appears in Appendix H.3. 

Stage 2: Direct multi-round RL training. Our RL training procedure consists of two steps, illus-trated in Figure 8. In Step 1 , we start from the standard causal mask and add a round-level visibility mask over the concatenated multi-round response. This makes the intended computation explicit: at each round, the model can attend only to the original question, the relevant reasoning tokens, and the replayed insights that should be visible in that round, while all other tokens are masked out on top of the causal constraint. Figure 9 summarizes the validation performance of the alternative training method over a unified training budget of 420 steps. By the end of the 420 -step budget, the InsightReplay variant overtakes the baseline on both mean@32 and best@32 , indicating a late advantage in average sample quality and upper-tail performance. However, maj@32 remains consistently below the baseline, suggesting that this training recipe does not improve sample-level consistency in the same way. We also emphasize that these experiments were stopped after exactly 420 steps rather than trained further. In addition, this direct multi-round training mode is substantially more expensive operationally. Each InsightReplay rollout must merge multiple rounds into a single training example, and the prompt grows longer at later rounds because more reasoning and insight tokens are carried forward. As a result, token cost increases over the course of a single rollout. For this reason, despite the late crossover on mean@32 and best@32 , we ultimately did not adopt this training strategy in the main paper.
