Abstract 

Large language models (LLMs) are often evaluated based on their stated values, yet these do not reliably translate into their actions—a discrepancy termed value–action gap . In this work, we argue that this gap persists even under explicit reasoning, revealing a deeper failure mode we call “Pseudo-Deliberation”: the appearance of principled reasoning without corresponding behavioral alignment. To study this systematically, we introduce VALDI , a framework for measuring alignment between stated values and generated dialogue. VALDI includes 4,941 human-centered scenarios across five domains, three tasks that elicit value articulation, reasoning, and action, and five metrics for quantifying value adherence. Across both proprietary and open-source LLMs, we observe consistent misalignment between expressed values and downstream dialogues. To investigate intervention strategies, we propose VIVALDI , a multi-agent value auditor that intervenes at different stages of generation. While interventions targeting intermediate reasoning aim to shape value formation, we find post-hoc auditing and rewriting of final dialogues yields more robust alignment gains. These findings suggest that value-action alignment in LLMs may not emerge from deliberative reasoning alone, and highlight the need for action-level alignment strategies at the point of generation 1.

1 Introduction 

Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly entrusted with decisions that shape human lives – advising clinicians on diagnoses, guiding students through their education, and screening candidates in hiring pipelines [1, 2, 3]. As these systems migrate from controlled benchmarks to consequential conversations, a fundamental question becomes urgent [ 4]: do the values an LLM professes actually govern the actions it generates? 

Recent work has identified the “value–action gap [ 5],” where LLMs’ stated value preferences diverge from the actions they recommend. However, this gap has primarily been evaluated in static multiple-choice settings [ 6, 5]. In practice, LLMs act through situated dialogue, where advice is delivered conversationally and shaped through interaction. This raises a critical, underexplored issue: if a model endorses a value in principle but abandons it under generation pressure, alignment evaluations based on stated preferences alone are dangerously misleading. 

A natural hope is that deliberative reasoning closes this gap. Chain-of-thought and structured reasoning [ 7] have become the dominant levers for aligning LLM behavior, and the appeal of these methods rests on a tacit assumption: that explicit reasoning over values yields actions consistent with those values. 2 VALDI Framework: Measuring Situated Value-Action Gaps in Dialogues 

The key components of our framework involve a value-driven dataset (§2.1) stated value and dialogue generation (§2.2), a measurement tool for extracting model’s stances through text §2.3, and a suite of metrics to quantify alignment and decompose reasoning formulation (§2.4). 22.1 DAISY Dataset Generation 

We introduce DAISY (Decisions AI Steers for You), a dataset of 4,941 guidance-seeking dilemmas designed to evaluate value–action alignment in realistic decision-making contexts. The dataset spans diverse domains (e.g., social, ethical, and personal tradeoffs) and is constructed from a mixture of 6 social dialogue datasets [ 8 , 9 , 10 , 11 , 12 , 13 ] and controlled generation. A human annotator first filters scenarios according to predefined criteria. To increase value diversity of selected scenarios, we generate paraphrases using GPT-4o ( T = 0 .7, max_tokens=500), producing multiple formulations of the same dilemma with varied surface realizations [ 14 ]. Crucially, all scenarios are rewritten to exclude explicit mentions of values, requiring models to implicitly infer and balance competing values during generation. This design enables a controlled evaluation of value–action gaps under naturalistic conditions, where value expression must emerge from behavior rather than prompting. Additional details regarding DAISY’s construction and content are provided in Appendix D. 

2.2 Three Tasks for Stated Values and Situated Actions 

We define three tasks to assess LLMs’ value expression across stages of generation: (1) stating value inclinations, (2) generating immediate dialogue, and (3) producing deliberative dialogue following structured reasoning, enabling evaluation of whether values expressed in isolation are preserved in downstream interaction. While we leverage Shalom Schwartz’s Theory of Basic Values to evaluate our model, our framework is extendable to other value orientations [15]. 

Task 1: Stated Value Inclinations. For each scenario, the LLM evaluates the extent to which each of the 10 Schwartz values should be encouraged when advising the user, using a 3-point Likert scale: 

Encourage , Neutral , or Discourage , which we map to {+1 , 0, −1} respectively. This yields a signed value vector representing the model’s stated value preferences. 

Task 2: Fast Dialogue Generation. Given the scenario and value definitions, the LLM generates a response directly to the user without explicit reasoning. This setting captures intuitive, immediate behavior and reflects standard prompting conditions. 

Task 3: Slow (Deliberative) Dialogue Generation. The LLM is prompted to produce a structured reasoning trace (four steps) prior to generating its final response. As in Task 2, we provide value definitions in the system prompt, but allow the model to implicitly select and express values during reasoning and in the final dialogue. 

2.3 Detecting and Scoring Values in Text 

Prior work has shown success in multi-stage prompting for complex LLM tasks [ 16 ]. We use a two-stage multiprompt pipeline to construct an LLM-based ValueJudge that extracts value signals from text. In the first stage, the model identifies which Schwartz values are present. In the second stage, it assigns a stance label to each detected value: +1 (action endorsement for value), −1 (action discouragement for value), or 0 (neutral discussion of value). Values not detected in the first stage are labeled as “irrelevant” and represented as ∅.To assess the reliability of our annotation scheme, we report human–human agreement using Cohen’s 

κ for both value detection and stance scoring, following our two-pass ValueJudge setup. For detection, we compute binary agreement over value presence ( ∅ vs. −1, 0, 1), yielding κ = 0 .7483 between two annotators. For stance scoring, we compute agreement over the full label space ∅, −1, 0, +1 , yielding 

κ = 0 .8077 between two annotators. These results indicate substantial agreement, supporting reliable measurement of value identification and stance in text. In our final ValueJudge , we leverage a two-stage setup with GPT-4.1-mini for value detection and GPT-4.1 for stance scoring [ 17 ], achieving Cohen’s κ ≈ 0.62 on a sample of N = 240 annotated examples, indicating substantial agreement with human annotators. Specific prompts and details on ValueJudge can be found in Appendix F.

3 Experimental Setup 

We conduct our primary full-scale analysis on GPT-4o [ 14 ], covering prompt optimization, value extraction, and all alignment metrics. To ensure reproducibility, we shuffle DAISY with a fixed seed (seed = 42) before human evaluation and model inference. To assess generality, we replicate the experiments on a uniformly sampled 10% subset using Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct [ 18 ], Qwen3-8B [ 19 ], and Gemini-3-Flash [ 20 ]. All models use the same prompts, evaluation protocol, and deterministic decoding (temperature = 0). Proprietary models are accessed through their APIs, while open-weight models are served with vLLM [21] on NVIDIA RTX 4090 cloud GPU instances. For Task 1 , we evaluate stated value inclinations across prompt variants using GPT-4o-mini and observe high robustness, with 92.27% consistency, allowing us to fix a single prompt for subsequent experiments [ 14 ]. For Tasks 2 and 3 , we generate dialogue responses and reasoning chains under deterministic decoding, with GPT-4o using seeded generation (seed = 42). We also compare expert-designed prompts against prompts optimized with DSPy/MIPROv2 on GPT-4o-mini. Expert prompts achieve higher alignment in Task 2 and match DSPy-optimized prompts in Task 3 [ 22 , 23 ]; therefore, we use expert prompts throughout. Additional prompt robustness details are provided in Appendix E.

4.1 Value-Action Alignment Rates and Distances. 

A natural hypothesis is that reasoning should improve value–action alignment, as step-by-step reasoning allows the model to reflect on its stated values before producing a response. Contrary to this expectation, we observe a consistent degradation in alignment under Slow Thinking. GPT-4o achieves an alignment rate of 0.4511 under Fast Thinking, which decreases to 0.4219 under Slow Thinking. This pattern is also reflected in alignment distance: Fast Thinking yields a lower distance (0.1685) compared to Slow Thinking (0.1785), indicating that responses generated without explicit reasoning more closely track the model’s stated value profile. At the individual-value level, we observe substantial heterogeneity and a mismatch between value endorsement and execution. As shown in Figure 2, highly endorsed values such as Self-Direction, Security, and Benevolence in the stated distribution (Figure 2a) do not necessarily achieve high action alignment (Figure 2b). For example, Benevolence and Self-Direction remain near the random baseline in F1 despite frequent endorsement, suggesting that these values are not reliably preserved in generated responses. Conversely, values such as Achievement and Tradition, which have more class-balanced or neutral stated distributions, achieve higher alignment. Thus, alignment is not simply driven by endorsement strength, but by whether values are consistently preserved during generation. Across nearly all values, Slow Thinking further reduces alignment relative to Fast Thinking, reinforc-ing that deliberative reasoning does not improve, and may actively degrade, value–action consistency. These results reveal a systematic gap in which models fail to align with their own stated value commitments during response generation. 

4.2 Reasoning Decomposition. 

To identify where misalignment arises, we decompose Slow Thinking into intermediate reasoning and final dialogue, and analyze how value signals transition between two stages. As shown in Figure 3, 5Figure 3: Reasoning decomposition metrics across values, showing rates of survival, suppression, and emergence of GPT-4o. We find that self-oriented values are deliberated on but rarely acted on in the final response while prosocial values are more consistent in translating from reasoning to dialogue. the model exhibits high value suppression for several self-oriented values such as Stimulation, Power and Achievement, where the model invokes these values in reasoning, but fails to carry a stance in dialogue. In contrast, values such as Security and Universalism display relatively higher survival rates, indicating they are more consistently carried into the final response. Emergence remains relatively low overall, but is more pronounced for values such as Self-Direction and Security, suggesting that certain value dimensions are more likely to be introduced during response generation. In contrast, other values exhibit near-zero emergence, indicating strong dependence on prior reasoning signals. This asymmetry suggests that the model does not uniformly translate its reasoning into action, but instead selectively filters which values are expressed. In particular, values associated with individual preference or self-enhancement are more likely to be suppressed, while socially normative or safety-aligned values are more likely to persist. One plausible explanation for this suppression effect lies in post-training procedures such as instruction tuning and reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), which shape how models express their outputs. While pretraining equips the model with the ability to recognize a broad range of value-relevant considerations, post-training may impose constraints that favor socially acceptable, safe, or normative responses. 

4.3 Benchmarking Models. 

As shown in Table 1, all models perform well above their uniform random baseline under both Fast and Slow thinking, indicating that LLMs encode non-trivial value representations in dialogue. However, introducing deliberation consistently reduces value alignment across most models. With the exception of Gemini-3-Flash, all models exhibit negative deltas, suggesting that this degradation is directionally consistent across model families. We assess these Fast–Slow differences using paired permutation tests over 1,000 random permutations and report the resulting p-values in Table 1. The magnitude of these declines is non-trivial (e.g., −0.0378 for GPT-4o and −0.0432 for Qwen3-8B), indicating that deliberation can meaningfully reduce value–action alignment. Gemini-3-Flash is the only model that exhibits a positive delta, suggesting that the impact of deliberation on alignment is not uniform across models. Additional results are in Appendix L. 

5 VIVALDI : Gap Mitigation with the Multi-Agent Value Alignment System 

Motivated by the suppression of value signals between reasoning and final dialogue, we developed VIVALDI system with three agentic intervention strategies: • VIVALDI -R : A reasoning-centric repair approach that iteratively identifies and corrects inconsis-tencies within intermediate reasoning, and generates the final dialogue from the revised reasoning. 6Figure 4: Overview of V IVALDI -D’s multi-agent dialogue auditor. We create a system with Val-ueExtractor to evaluate distorted values, rubricate them, and make a plan to correct the output. VIVALDI -R&D performs iterative reasoning-level repair to improve alignment, and applies dialogue-level repair when alignment fails to propagate to the final output. • VIVALDI -R&D : A reasoning-centric repair approach that identifies and corrects inconsistencies at each reasoning step, and generates the final dialogue from the revised reasoning. When alignment fails to propagate, a final dialogue-level audit can be applied. • VIVALDI -D : A dialogue-centric repair approach that applies corrections directly to the final response without modifying intermediate reasoning. 

VIVALDI -R&D and V IVALDI -R Architecture. Our multi-agent architecture (Figure 4) enforces value alignment through iterative audit and repair. Given a scenario, a generator first produces intermediate reasoning and a final dialogue. A ValueExtractor , implemented via our two-stage 

ValueJudge , identifies the presence and stance of each value. During the iterative phase, an auditor agent operates on the reasoning, comparing extracted values against a target value profile to identify discrepancies (e.g., missing, overexpressed, or conflicting values). A planning module translates these discrepancies into structured repair instructions, which are executed by a rewriting agent to produce revised reasoning. A new dialogue is then generated from the repaired reasoning and evaluated using a verifier based on exact-match alignment with a fixed threshold. If alignment fails to meet this threshold after a fixed number of iterations, a final fallback stage directly audits and rewrites the dialogue. This design allows us to additionally consider VIVALDI -R ,a variant that performs reasoning-level repair without the final dialogue-level fallback. This isolates the effect of reasoning-centric interventions on alignment. Additional details in Appendix I. 

VIVALDI -D Architecture. Our multi-agent system in Figure 4 performs value steering directly at the level of final dialogue, without modifying intermediate reasoning. 7 Related Work 

Value–Action Alignment in LLMs. The belief–behavior gap, long studied in social science [ 24 , 25 ], has recently been examined in LLMs via frameworks such as ValueActionLens [ 5] and ValueBench [6], which quantify discrepancies between expressed values and model decisions. However, their reliance on value-primed prompts and multiple-choice formats limits their ability to capture value–action gaps in open-ended dialogue. Additionally, the rise of post-training methods has made study of this gap increasingly important. Alignment approaches such as reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) [ 26 ] and constitutional AI [ 27 ] steer outputs toward human preferences. However, they operate at the level of individual responses and do not assess consistency between reasoning and subsequent actions. A more holistic lens is needed to understand how LLMs socially reason and whether such methods contribute to value misalignment between assertions and behavior. 

Reasoning and External Deliberation Mechanisms. The dual-process model distinguishes fast, intuitive reasoning from slower, deliberative reasoning [ 28 ], motivating methods such as chain-of-thought prompting [ 7 ]. Prior work in social reasoning emphasizes mental state inference [ 29 ] and interpersonal relationships [ 30 ], rather than value propagation in dialogue. Additionally, recent work demonstrates that generated reasoning may not reflect underlying decision processes [ 31 ]. We leverage this paradigm to examine whether reasoning reduces the value–action gap. Beyond internal reasoning, ensemble methods such as multi-agent systems introduce collaboration, debate, and self-critique to improve reasoning [ 32 , 33 ], with recent work extending these ideas to alignment by studying value propagation across agents [ 34 ]. In contrast, we propose a multi-agent auditing system that verifies and steers value consistency across both reasoning and dialogue. 

8 Conclusion 

We introduced V ALDI , a framework for measuring value–action gaps in realistic dialogue settings. Across nearly 5,000 scenarios and multiple LLMs, we find that models’ stated values often fail to carry over into generated responses. More importantly, explicit chain-of-thought deliberation does not reliably close this gap and can even worsen it—a failure mode we term pseudo-deliberation . Our analysis shows that models often recognize relevant values in reasoning but suppress or alter them in final dialogue, suggesting a failure of value expression rather than value recognition. To mitigate this, we proposed V IVALDI , a multi-agent auditing framework that verifies and repairs outputs after generation. Dialogue-level repair improves alignment more reliably than reasoning-level intervention, indicating that value-consistent behavior must be checked and corrected at the level of action, not assumed to emerge from reasoning alone. B Limitations 

Our study has several limitations. First, our evaluation relies on automatic value extraction through 

ValueJudge . Although ValueJudge shows substantial agreement with human annotations, value inter-pretation is inherently subjective and context dependent. Implicit, culturally specific, or pragmatically conveyed values may therefore be missed or simplified by the annotation pipeline. Second, the same underlying value-extraction pipeline is used both inside the V IVALDI repair loop, where we refer to it as ValueJudge , and in final post-hoc assessment, where we refer to it as ValueEvaluator . Although we include human evaluation as a complementary check, some observed improvements may partly reflect optimization toward evaluator-specific criteria rather than broader value alignment. Future work should test robustness with larger-scale human studies, independent evaluators, and alternative value-detection methods. Third, DAISY focuses primarily on advice-giving scenarios. Value–action alignment may behave differently in other settings, such as collaborative problem solving, negotiation, education, healthcare, or long-term human–AI interaction. Finally, we operationalize values using Schwartz’s 10-value framework. While this provides broad psychological coverage, it does not capture all dimensions of moral, cultural, or social reasoning. Future work should examine whether our findings generalize across domains, cultures, and value ontologies. 

C Broader Impacts 

This work studies value–action misalignment in LLMs, with the goal of improving the reliability of LLMs in advice-giving and other socially situated dialogue settings. A positive societal impact of this work is that V ALDI and V IVALDI may help researchers and deployers identify cases where models state socially desirable values but fail to preserve them in generated responses. This could support more reliable auditing of LLMs used in domains such as education, clinical triage, workplace support, and other settings where value-sensitive advice may influence user decisions. At the same time, the work has potential negative impacts. First, value alignment is inherently normative and culturally dependent; systems that optimize toward a fixed value ontology may overrepresent some moral or cultural perspectives while underrepresenting others. Second, automated value auditing could create a false sense of safety if used as a substitute for human oversight, especially in high-stakes settings. Finally, because our evaluation focuses on advice-giving scenarios, deployment in other domains may produce harms not captured by our benchmark. To mitigate these risks, we frame V ALDI and V IVALDI as research tools for auditing and diagnosis rather than as sufficient safeguards for deployment. We recommend using them alongside human evaluation, domain-specific review, alternative value ontologies, and independent evaluators. We also encourage transparent reporting of the target value framework, known failure cases, and the limits of automatic value extraction when applying these tools in practice.

E Prompt Variants and Optimizations 

E.1 Task 1 Prompt Variants Prompt Decomposition. To systematically study how prompt design influences value articulation, we decompose our prompting strategy into three orthogonal components: context framing , value 

21 definition , and response scale . Rather than treating prompts as monolithic, this decomposition allows us to isolate how different semantic and structural choices affect the model’s stated value profile. • Context framing controls the role and perspective of the model. We vary between: 

– Neutral : a generic assistant with minimal framing, 

– Advisor : an explicitly decision-support role, 

– First-person : a more immersive framing where the model imagines itself in the scenario. • Value definition determines how abstract values are presented: 

– Canonical : textbook-style definitions of Schwartz values, 

– Actionary : operationalized definitions grounded in observable behavior (e.g., “the advice reflects...”). • Response scale specifies how value stance is expressed: 

– 3-class : {− 1, 0, +1 } (discourage, neutral, encourage), 

– 5-class : {− 2, −1, 0, +1 , +2 } (allowing finer-grained intensity with "strongly agree"). These components are programmatically composed to generate prompt variants, resulting in a controlled grid of configurations (e.g., 3 × 2 × 2 combinations of context, definition, and scale). Each prompt asks the model to map a given scenario to a structured JSON object over the ten Schwartz values. The implementation explicitly enforces output structure and deterministic decoding (temperature = 0 ), ensuring consistency across runs. 

Prompt Agreement and Scale Normalization. To quantify the consistency of value elicitation across prompt variants, we introduce a prompt agreement metric. Because our prompt configurations use both 3-class and 5-class response scales, we first normalize all outputs into a shared semantic space before computing agreement. 

Scale normalization. We map all responses onto a unified 5-point ordinal scale: 

{0, 1, 2, 3, 4} ≡ { strongly discourage , discourage , neutral , encourage , strongly encourage }.

Specifically, 3-class outputs {− 1, 0, +1 } are mapped to {1, 2, 3}, while 5-class outputs 

{− 2, −1, 0, +1 , +2 } are mapped to {0, 1, 2, 3, 4}. We further collapse this representation into a shared 3-class scale: 

{0, 1} → discourage , 2 → neutral , {3, 4} → encourage ,

Final Prompt Choice. Based on this analysis, we use the canonical Schwartz value definitions with a 3-class response scale {− 1, 0, +1 } for downstream experiments. E.2 Task 2 and 3 Prompt Optimization with DSPy (MIPROv2) 

To optimize the fast-thinking policy (Task 2) and slow-thinking policy (Task 3), we use DSP Y and its teleprompting module MIPRO V2, which performs automated prompt search under a user-defined objective. 

Setup. We define a structured generation program that maps a scenario to action-oriented advice. The base prompt encodes (i) a value ontology (Schwartz’s ten values), (ii) behavioral constraints (decisive, non-neutral), and (iii) formatting requirements. This serves as the initial policy prior to optimization. 

Objective. We optimize prompts using a task-specific alignment metric. For each generated response, we apply V ALUE JUDGE to extract value stances and compare them to the model’s stated values. We compute macro-F1 over the label space {− 1, 0, +1 }:Score = MacroF1 (Vstated , A dialogue ) (3) where Vstated denotes the stated value vector and Adialogue denotes the values inferred from the generated advice. Missing values are mapped to 0.

Optimization Procedure. MIPRO V2 performs iterative prompt search by: 1. Sampling candidate prompt variants, 2. Evaluating each candidate on a minibatch using the alignment metric, 3. Selecting high-performing prompts and refining them over multiple trials. Given C candidates and T trials, the procedure evaluates O(C × T ) prompt variants and retains the best-performing program. 

Training Protocol. We use a held-out tuning setup with N = 25 examples for optimization and 

N = 25 for evaluation (nested prompt on DAISY with first seed=50 then seed = 42). No in-context demonstrations are used (i.e., zero-shot optimization), ensuring improvements arise solely from prompt search. 

Implementation Details. We use GPT-4o-mini as both the prompt and task model with temperature 

0. Optimization is run with 10 candidates and 12 trials, using minibatches of size 4. 23 Evaluation. We compare the original hand-written prompt and the optimized prompt on held-out data using macro-F1. This isolates the effect of prompt optimization independent of downstream interventions such as MAS. 

Prompt Optimization Results. Based on the results in Tables 12 and 13, we use the original prompts for both fast (Task 2) and slow (Task 3) generation in all subsequent experiments. For fast generation, the manually designed prompt clearly outperforms the DSPy-optimized variant, achieving higher Macro F1 (0.4273 vs. 0.3848) and lower alignment distance (0.180 vs. 0.194), indicating both stronger value alignment and reduced deviation from the target value profile. For slow generation, the DSPy-optimized prompt yields only a negligible improvement in Macro F1 (0.3762 vs. 0.3759), well within the observed variance, and does not provide a meaningful advantage in alignment quality. Taken together, these results suggest that automated prompt optimization does not reliably improve performance in this setting and, in the case of fast generation, can even degrade alignment. We therefore adopt the original prompts as a stable and stronger baseline for all downstream evaluations.

We use GPT-4.1-mini for value detection, as it achieves comparable performance to GPT-4.1 on this task, and GPT-4.1 for stance scoring, where we observe improved reliability. K Human Validation of V IVALDI Performance 

We constructed the blind human evaluation set by uniformly sampling 60 scenarios (seed=123) from the 500-scenario evaluation subset used in 4. For each scenario, we created pairwise comparisons between each V IVALDI variant and its corresponding baseline: V IVALDI -D was compared against Fast, while V IVALDI -R and V IVALDI -R&D were compared against Slow. For each comparison, the order of the two responses was randomly assigned to Response A or Response B, and method 33 identities were hidden from annotators. Each annotation row contained the scenario, the target stated-value profile, two anonymized responses, and an empty preference column. A private key stored the mapping from Response A/B to the underlying systems and was used only after annotation to compute win rates. The final annotation set was shuffled and split into two Google Sheets for annotation. To assess our multi-agent system from human standpoint, we provide further details on our human evaluation paradigm. Blind pairwise human evaluation was conducted using a Google Sheets annotation interface. Each row corresponded to one pairwise comparison and contained a pair identifier, the target stated-value profile, two anonymized model responses, and an annotator decision column. Responses were shown as response_A and response_B ; model identities and method labels were hidden from annotators. Annotators were asked: “Which demonstrates the stated values better?” They selected A or B, with C used for ties. We instructed annotators to use the tie option sparingly, only when both responses preserved the stated values similarly well or when neither response was clearly preferable. For blind pairwise human evaluation, annotators selected which of two anonymized responses better preserved the target stated-value profile, with a tie option. For each comparison, we counted a win if the selected response corresponded to the evaluated V IVALDI method and a loss if it corresponded to the baseline. Ties were excluded from win-rate computation but reported separately as a tie rate. Thus, for each method, human win rate is computed as WinRate = #VIVALDI wins 

#VIVALDI wins + # baseline wins .
