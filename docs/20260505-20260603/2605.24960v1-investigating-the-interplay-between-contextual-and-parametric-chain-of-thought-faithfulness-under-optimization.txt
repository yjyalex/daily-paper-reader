Abstract 

Chain-of-Thought (CoT) faithfulness, i.e., whether CoTs genuinely reflect large language models’ (LLM) underlying behavior, is typi-cally evaluated under two disjoint paradigms: 

contextual faithfulness , measured by perturbing the input or CoT trace, and parametric faith-fulness , assessed by intervening on a model’s parametric knowledge. Yet prior work com-pares them only descriptively. We fill this gap by proposing FaithMATE , a unified preference-alignment interface for optimizing models to-wards either faithfulness paradigm. 1 It enables us to investigate the interplay between the two paradigms, examining whether and to what ex-tent faithfulness gains generalize within and across paradigms. Across three models, two datasets, and six faithfulness metrics, we find that the two paradigms are positively coupled, yet asymmetric: optimizing towards paramet-ric faithfulness yields consistent gains across both paradigms, whereas the contextual coun-terpart delivers more variable gains. Within the contextual paradigm, faithfulness gains on one metric do not consistently transfer to others, implying that existing contextual metrics cap-ture disjoint facets of faithfulness and exposing inherent trade-offs. These findings imply that CoT faithfulness is not a monolithic objective and therefore requires multifaceted optimiza-tion and evaluation. 

1 Introduction 

CoT has proven effective for improving LLMs’ per-formance on various reasoning tasks (Kojima et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022). Alongside this success, however, questions have been raised about CoT faithfulness, i.e., whether CoTs accurately reflect the model’s actual reasoning process. In practice, CoTs can appear plausible to users while being weakly coupled with a model’s internal computa-

tions (Agarwal et al., 2024; Arcuschin et al., 2025; Paul et al., 2024; Zaman and Srivastava, 2026). Existing work evaluates CoT faithfulness primar-ily through two paradigms. Contextual faithfulness 

intervenes on the input and reasoning trace in vari-ous ways, e.g., by inserting an error (Lanham et al., 2023), or injecting answer hints into the context (Turpin et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2025), and deems a CoT faithful if the intervention is truly reflected. However, such measures only consider contextual consistency instead of model internals. Parametric faithfulness , in contrast, intervenes on a model’s parameters rather than its context: it deems a CoT step faithful if unlearning the knowledge underly-ing that step changes the answer (Tutek et al., 2025). While this perspective targets model internals di-rectly, it is limited to the knowledge explicitly ver-balized by the CoT, neglecting information that shapes CoT generation but remains unexpressed. Although the two paradigms have largely been studied in isolation, they can yield divergent faith-fulness conclusions for the same CoTs (Zaman and Srivastava, 2026), raising the question of whether they capture a shared notion of faithfulness or re-ward different properties of CoTs. As methods for improving CoT faithfulness proliferate, observa-tional comparisons on fixed CoTs cannot reveal what each faithfulness paradigm rewards under op-timization or whether and to what extent optimizing one transfers to the other. To this end, we introduce FaithMATE 

(Faith fulness Metric Alignment and Transfer 

Evaluation), a unified framework for investi-gating CoT faithfulness under optimization. It constructs faithfulness-targeted preference pairs, allowing models to be optimized towards either contextual or parametric faithfulness (Figure 1). This unified optimization interface enables an optimization-based investigation of the interplay between the two paradigms, while also allowing us to compare what each rewards under optimization. 1

Experiments on three models, two datasets, and six faithfulness metrics reveal the following findings. First, across faithfulness paradigms, contextual and parametric faithfulness exhibit a positively coupled, yet asymmetric relationship (§5.1): opti-mizing for parametric faithfulness delivers more consistent improvements across both paradigms, while contextual optimization, although occasion-ally larger in magnitude, is less consistent. This positions parametric faithfulness as the more reli-able target for broad generalization. 

Second, within the contextual faithfulness paradigm, faithfulness gains from optimizing one contextual metric do not consistently transfer to others, e.g., between Adding Mistake and Para-phrasing (§5.2). This suggests that contextual faith-fulness metrics are better understood as a family of related but distinct properties, rather than inter-changeable measurements of a single latent quan-tity. Thus, we call attention to potential tensions among contextual faithfulness metrics. Additional experiments show that task-arithmetic merging of models, each optimized for a specific faithfulness target, can partially mitigate this inconsistency and amplify the faithfulness improvements. Third, we qualitatively characterize what dif-ferent metrics actually reward under optimization (§5.4). We find that contextual metric-shared gains are typically driven by improved alignment be-tween reasoning and final answer, whereas con-textual metric-specific gains are mostly prompted by the mitigation of post-hoc rationalization 2. By contrast, parametric gains arise from more explicit grounding in task-relevant facts and knowledge. 

This suggests that evaluating and reporting gains on a single faithfulness metric can be misleading, as improvements may reflect distinct underlying behaviors that are not interchangeable. 

2 Related Work 

CoT Faithfulness Metrics. Prior work has mainly studied contextual faithfulness , which inter-venes on the CoT or input and checks whether the intervention is reflected in the answer. Corruption-based methods perturb the CoT, and measure whether the model’s answer changes (Lanham et al., 2023; Zaman and Srivastava, 2025). Attribution-based methods measure the alignment between input contributions to CoT generation and those to answer prediction (Parcalabescu and Frank, 2024; Admoni et al., 2026). Another line of work uses Biasing Features, which inject answer hints and check whether the CoT verbalizes them (Turpin et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2025; Chua and Evans, 2025; Young, 2026). Recent work further proposes 

parametric faithfulness , which intervenes on a model’s parametric knowledge rather than on the CoT. Tutek et al. (2025) unlearn the knowl-edge involved in individual CoT steps from the

2model parameters and measure whether the answer changes after unlearning. Given that contextual and parametric metrics target different aspects of CoT faithfulness, we evaluate both families within our unified FaithMATE framework under optimization. 

Meta-Evaluation of CoT Faithfulness. Parcal-abescu and Frank (2024) compare multiple con-textual faithfulness metrics and find that they of-ten assign divergent faithfulness verdicts to the same CoTs, suggesting that these metrics are bet-ter understood as measures of output-level self-consistency. Zaman and Srivastava (2025) use model editing to construct faithful–unfaithful CoT pairs and evaluate whether the target faithfulness metrics rank the faithful CoTs higher. More re-cent work extends metric comparison to parametric faithfulness. Zaman and Srivastava (2026) show that contextual and parametric faithfulness met-rics can yield different conclusions on the same CoTs. Such observational comparisons, however, leave open how the two paradigms interact when models are explicitly optimized for faithfulness. 

FaithMATE fills this gap with a unified interface for optimizing either paradigm, enabling controlled study of faithfulness improvement transfers across metrics and paradigms. 

CoT Faithfulness Improvement. Several meth-ods have been proposed to improve CoT faithful-ness. Nevertheless, they are designed to optimize only one specific type of faithfulness . FRODO trains the model to prefer correct reasoning chains over counterfactual ones (Paul et al., 2024). Li et al. (2025) use integrated-gradient attribution to recall question information missing from the CoT, and weight sampled CoTs by their information gain when voting for the final answer. FRIT optimizes towards faithful CoTs identified via causal impor-tance – whether interventions on reasoning steps change the answer (Swaroop et al., 2025). Höth et al. (2026) leverage differentiable attention ma-nipulation to learn faithful reasoning through re-inforcement learning. In comparison, FaithMATE 

does not focus on better results in individual faith-fulness metrics; rather, it serves as a universal pref-erence optimization framework applicable to both 

contextual and parametric faithfulness, facilitating the study of how the two paradigms interact.

4 Experimental Setup 

4.1 Datasets 

Consistent with prior CoT faithfulness evaluation studies (Paul et al., 2024; Tutek et al., 2025; Zaman and Srivastava, 2025), we select two widely used reasoning datasets (see examples in App. B.1): 

OpenbookQA (Mihaylov et al., 2018) is a ques-tion answering dataset designed to test scientific reasoning. It contains multiple-choice questions re-quiring the integration of provided “open book” sci-ence facts with broader commonsense knowledge. 

LogiQA (Liu et al., 2021) is a dataset designed to evaluate logical reasoning in natural language understanding. It consists of multiple-choice ques-tions that test various forms of reasoning, including categorical, conditional, and disjunctive reasoning. 

4.2 Models 

In our experiments, we employ three widely used open-source instruction-tuned models from dis-tinct families with varying parameter sizes (Ap-pendix B.2): Gemma3-4B (Team et al., 2025), 

Qwen2.5-7B (Yang et al., 2025) and Llama3.1-8B 

(Grattafiori et al., 2024).

5 Results 

We analyze two key questions: (1) whether a model’s faithfulness on one specific metric is im-proved by optimizing towards another, i.e., faith-fulness gain transfer δ (§3.5), especially when they are from distinct faithfulness paradigms (§5.1); and (2) whether this faithfulness gain transfer δ re-mains positive across metrics within the contextual paradigm (§5.2). We also examine how these trends vary across base models and training paradigms, and how task performance is affected under faith-fulness optimization (§5.3). 9 Finally, we conduct a qualitative analysis to examine what each faithful-ness paradigm enhances (§5.4).

5.2.2 Between Corruption-based and Attribution-based Metrics 

We next ask whether transfer holds across funda-mentally different operationalizations of contextual faithfulness, by including CC-SHAP (§4.3.1), in contrast to the corruption-based metrics studied above. Prior work reports moderate correlation between CC-SHAP and intervention-based contextual metrics (Parcalabescu and Frank, 2024); our results extend this correspondence from measurement-time corre-lation to optimization-time transfer. This provides preliminary evidence that contextual faithfulness gains may transfer beyond a single operationaliza-tion paradigm, at least for CC-SHAP.

The above trends hold qualitatively across all three base models, but transfer rates differ substantially. 5.4 Qualitative Analysis 

Table 1 displays that, in the pre-alignment CoT, the model selects the correct option yet gives rea-soning that undermines the choice, suggesting reasoning-answer inconsistency. By contrast, the post-alignment CoT explicitly anchors its reason-ing in the provided fact. Further inspection shows that contextual faithfulness improvements arise primarily from improved reasoning-answer con-sistency and mitigation of post-hoc rationaliza-tion (Appendix F). Most enhancements are shared across multiple metrics and are substantially at-7tributed to better reasoning-answer consistency. By contrast, metric-specific gains result from the mit-igation of post-hoc rationalization. A plausible explanation is that reasoning-answer consistency functions as a foundational property of contextual faithfulness, such that its improvement yields broad gains across all metrics; whereas post-hoc rational-ization mitigation addresses more localized reason-ing deficiencies that are only captured by metrics specifically sensitive to those phenomena. By con-trast, the parametric top-shift cases in Table 14 exhibit a less uniform surface signature: some be-come more explicitly grounded in the provided fact, while others show only modest visible CoT change. Their minimal overlap with the contextual top-shift cases suggests that contextual and parametric faith-fulness track different aspects of CoT behavior .

6 Does Model Merging Help? 

Motivation and Setup. Optimizing towards a single contextual faithfulness metric yields gains that do not consistently transfer across other con-textual metrics (§5.2), suggesting that each metric captures a complementary and partial notion of faithfulness. This observation naturally motivates a strategy that comprises these complementary strengths. Model merging is well-suited to this goal: it can combine the parameters of multiple spe-cialized models into a single model without addi-tional training, potentially integrating their respec-tive advantages (Tang et al., 2024; Song and Zheng, 2026). 7 Conclusion 

We presented FaithMATE , a unified framework for CoT faithfulness optimization that enables a con-trolled investigation of the connection between con-textual and parametric faithfulness. Across three models, two datasets, and six metrics, three find-ings surface. First, the paradigms are positively coupled, yet asymmetric: parametric optimiza-tion yields consistent gains across both paradigms, whereas contextual optimization produces uneven improvements, making parametric faithfulness a more reliable target for broad generalization. Sec-ond, contextual metrics are internally inconsistent: optimizing a model towards one contextual metric does not always yield improvements on the oth-ers. Third, the two paradigms act through largely disjoint mechanisms: contextual faithfulness gains stem from improved reasoning–answer consistency and reduced post-hoc rationalization, while para-

Limitations 

We acknowledge several limitations, stated as fol-lows. 

Lack of User Study. Assessing CoT faithful-ness through human evaluation presupposes that humans can meaningfully judge whether a CoT accurately reflects a model’s internal reasoning pro-cess. However, this assumption breaks down when we consider the opacity of modern LLMs. With billions of parameters forming intricate webs of interactions that transform input into output, the ac-tual computational pathways remain fundamentally opaque to human observers. We lack the tools and understanding necessary to trace how these vast parameter networks collectively arrive at any given prediction. Consequently, human evaluation of CoT faithfulness, while intuitively appealing, may be methodologically flawed given our current lim-ited insight into LLM internals, consistent with Ja-covi and Goldberg (2020); Parcalabescu and Frank (2024). 

Online vs. Offline Learning. Given the high inference time required for almost all faithfulness metrics, as indicated in Section 3.4, only offline learning is feasible in our setting. Consequently, online-learning approaches such as GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) are not applicable. Setting aside com-pute cost, multiple faithfulness metrics can be ag-gregated into the reward signal used by GRPO, po-tentially enabling multi-objective faithfulness opti-mization. Though worth exploring, it is currently out of the scope of this paper. 

Scope of Experiments. Although we conducted experiments with two datasets, three models, and six faithfulness metrics, extending the scope of these experiments, i.e., including additional datasets and models, is non-trivial, given the large number of possible configurations and exception-ally high inference time required to assess CoT faithfulness using the selected faithfulness metrics (§4.3). Furthermore, our experimental setup is lim-ited to English language Multiple-Choice Question Answering (MCQA) tasks. Due to the inherent lim-itations of parametric faithfulness, code and math reasoning is conceptually challenging (Tutek et al., 2025), while current faithfulness metrics are only applicable to closed-ended reasoning. Therefore, consistent with Tutek et al. (2025); Zaman and Srivastava (2025), we focus on two widely used MCQA datasets in our experiments. 

Metrics as Proxies for Faithfulness. The met-rics we employ are proxies for faithfulness rather than direct measures of it. In the absence of a ground truth for faithfulness, no degree of agree-ment among metrics can establish their validity for measuring CoT faithfulness. This challenge is partly mechanistic: a model’s answer may depend on latent or unspoken factors that are not fully ex-pressed in the CoT, and it remains unclear whether LLMs have a single explicit reasoning process that any textual trace could recover. The metrics stud-ied here are therefore best viewed as proxies, each capturing useful but partial facets of faithfulness. 

FaithMATE contributes by making the relationships among these proxies explicit, especially regarding where gains transfer, and why multi-metric evalua-tion is necessary. Stronger validation may require mechanistic-interpretability-based ground truth or counterfactual metrics that probe decision-relevant but unspoken factors; absent such ground truth, CoT faithfulness may be better treated as a family of related but distinct properties rather than as one universal score.

D Faithfulness Metric Implementation Details 

D.1 Prompt Templates for CoT Corruptions 

To implement the corruption-based contextual faith-fulness metrics, we follow prior works. Specially, for Early Answering , we truncate one-third of the CoT explanation. For Filler Tokens , we re-place each character with “...”, following Zaman and Srivastava (2025); Parcalabescu and Frank (2024). For Adding Mistake and Paraphrasing ,we use Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct as a helper model to generate corrupted CoTs, using prompt tem-plates adapted from Zaman and Srivastava (2025); Parcalabescu and Frank (2024). The prompts are shown in Tables 8 and 9. 

D.2 Parametric Faithfulness Implementation 

For the parametric faithfulness metric, we fol-low Tutek et al. (2025) and implement FUR with NPO+KL unlearning. We segment each generated CoT into reasoning steps, unlearn one step at a time with KL regularization on a retain set sampled from other CoT steps in the same dataset, and then measure whether the model’s answer changes. We use the default NPO+KL hyperparameters from Tutek et al. (2025): β = 0 .1, npo_coeff =1.0, 

KL_coeff =1.0, ref_policy =fine_tuned , 5 train-ing epochs, and no warmup. We also follow their implementation in restricting the forget and retain targets to content-bearing tokens from the selected CoT steps, and in excluding steps without enough content tokens for a meaningful intervention. All CoTs are generated and evaluated in greedy/deterministic decoding mode, i.e., without sampling, in the evaluation phase.

F.3 Summary 

This qualitative case study suggests different sur-face signatures for contextual and parametric faithfulness improvements. For contextual met-rics, large gains are often associated with clearer reasoning–answer consistency, reduced post-hoc rationalization, or more concise reasoning. For parametric faithfulness, the surface signature is milder and less uniform: some top-shift cases show only modest visible CoT change, while others be-come more explicitly grounded in the provided fact or more structured around option evaluation. Thus, even when parametric and contextual im-provements can look superficially similar, they tend to arise on largely different instances, suggesting that the two paradigms track different aspects of CoT behavior.
