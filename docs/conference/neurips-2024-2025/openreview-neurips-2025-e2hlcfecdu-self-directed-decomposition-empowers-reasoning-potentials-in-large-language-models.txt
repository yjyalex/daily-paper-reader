## Abstract 

Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable advancements 1

in natural language processing and reasoning tasks, yet often struggle with logi-2

cal coherence during problem-solving. This paper introduces Self-Directed De-3

composition (SD), a novel prompting strategy enabling LLMs to autonomously 4

decompose reasoning problems into manageable sub-tasks without human inter-5

vention, allowing models to determine their own approach with adaptive flexibility 6

across diverse reasoning domains. Experiments across seven reasoning tasks reveal 7

that this methodology particularly enhances performance on deductive, inductive, 8

mathematical, commonsense, and scientific reasoning tasks, while showing more 9

modest benefits for abductive and causal reasoning tasks, achieving 62.26% overall 10 

median accuracy compared to 49.64% and 46.43% for zero-shot and zero-shot 11 

Chain-of-Thought (CoT) approaches, respectively. Error and statistical analy-12 

sis demonstrates that SD significantly transforms reasoning patterns by reducing 13 

wrong selection errors but increasing process mistakes for simpler variants, with 14 

only SD1 maintaining optimal balance. We discover a counterintuitive negative 15 

correlation between token consumption and accuracy ( R2 = 0 .162 , p = 0 .004 ), 16 

challenging conventional resource-performance assumptions. Abductive reasoning 17 

demonstrates critical vulnerability to decomposition strategies, showing significant 18 

perspective errors increase ( R2 = 0 .66 ). These findings explain why SD1 outper-19 

forms other variants: it balances different error types effectively while avoiding the 20 

complexity-accuracy trade-off that affects simpler decomposition strategies. 21 

## 1 Introduction 22 

Large Language Models (LLMs) have recently made significant progress in natural language pro-23 

cessing (NLP), decision-making, and cognitive learning tasks (16; 18). During the LLMs evolution, 24 

zero-shot Chain-of-Thought (CoT) is a widely used prompt engineering technique to enhance LLM 25 

efficiency by instructing the model with “ Let’s think step-by-step ” to break down tasks into intermedi-26 

ate steps ( 13 ; 30 ). This approach addresses complex tasks that cannot be resolved through single-step 27 

procedures into smaller, tractable step-by-step components. A significant advantage of this strategy 28 

is that it operates without necessitating pre-defined exemplars or explicit instructional guidance in 29 

the prompts. However, the main limitations of this strategy are that LLMs can still make semantic 30 

misunderstanding errors or skip intermediate steps during reasoning ( 24 ), consequently yielding 31 

erroneous conclusions and limiting their generalization capabilities. 32 

Afterwards, based on the CoT’s success, various prompting techniques have been developed 33 

to further enhance LLMs’ reasoning capabilities. Including Least-to-Most prompting( 36 ), self-34 

consistency( 26 ), Logical CoT (Logi-CoT)( 15 ), Tree of Thoughts (ToT)( 35 ), Thread of Thought 35 

(ThoT)( 37 ), Chain of Table( 29 ), System 2 Attention (S2A)( 33 ), Graph of Thoughts (GoT)( 2 ), Task 36 

Dynamic Decomposition( 28 ). However, these approaches typically rely on predefined exemplars 37 

Submitted to 39th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NeurIPS 2025). Do not distribute. or researcher-designed algorithms that constrain the model’s autonomy, rather than allowing it to 38 

independently select the best analytical approaches. Researchers have also explored emotional 39 

prompting strategies that engage LLMs through emotional cues( 14 ). Most emotional prompts require 40 

specific social contexts or additional psychological cues, except for emotional stimuli EP02, “ This is 41 

very important to my career ”, which stands out as more generic. 42 

To enhance the autonomous functioning of LLMs, this research introduces a novel prompting tem-43 

plate, Self-Directed Decomposition (SD), which facilitates the resolution of diverse tasks without 44 

extraneous guidance. This approach enables LLMs to systematically decompose complex problems 45 

into constituent, more tractable sub-tasks based on their internal representations. To evaluate the 46 

effectiveness of SD strategies, we compare them with zero-shot, zero-shot CoT, and emotional stimuli 47 

EP02 as baseline prompts across seven reasoning datasets. 48 

Through comprehensive experiments across multiple reasoning domains, we analyze how different 49 

SD formulations affect performance and error patterns, providing insights into the mechanisms 50 

underlying autonomous decomposition in LLMs. The remainder of this paper is structured as follows: 51 

Section 2 outlines the definitions of SD strategies. Section 3 presents experimental setups across 52 

the different datasets of reasoning tasks and the evaluation standards for the mistakes made in the 53 

processes. Section 4 shows experimental results for different prompting strategies and discusses 54 

the main reason why SD strategies perform differently with error and statistical analysis. Finally, 55 

Sections 5 and 6 summarize the main limitations and conclude this approach’s implications for future 56 

research and applications and beyond. 57 

## 2 Self-Directed Decomposition Prompting Strategies 58 

This paper developed a series of prompting techniques called Self-Directed Decomposition (SD). 59 

These SD prompts enable LLMs to autonomously decompose complex reasoning problems into 60 

manageable sub-tasks, with varying levels of structural guidance to determine optimal decomposition 61 

complexity. SD1 includes explicit decomposition instructions using modal operators (" should ") and 62 

structured terminology (" sub-tasks "), providing comprehensive guidance for the decomposition 63 

process. SD2 shortens the instruction while maintaining the core decomposition directive. SD3 adds 64 

a politeness marker (" please "), which may affect model behavior similar to emotional stimuli( 14 ). 65 

SD4 adopts a minimal wording approach(9; 22; 38). 66 67

## 3 Experiments 81 

3.1 Datasets 82 

These experiments aim to measure how SD strategies affect LLM performance compared to existing 83 

techniques, quantify their impact on error patterns, and identify which reasoning tasks benefit most 84 

from autonomous decomposition. The reasoning categories and datasets include:(1) Deductive 85 

Reasoning: A top-down approach. Starting with a general principle or assumption to conclude 86 

specific answers ( 8). Datasets: bAbI (task 15) and EntailmentBank (E-Bank) ( 5; 32 ); (2) Inductive 87 

Reasoning: Derives general principles based on the given observations ( 7; 10 ). Datasets: CLUTRR 88 

and bAbI (task 16) ( 21 ; 32 ); (3) Mathematical Reasoning: Using mathematical or logical principles 89 

to find the solution to problems ( 20 ). Datasets: Mathematics (Math) and SVAMP ( 17 ; 19 ); (4) 90 

Scientific Reasoning: Similar to mathematical reasoning, but using a set of collegiate-level scientific 91 

problems from calculus, chemistry and physics domains. Dataset: Scibench ( 27 ). (5) Commonsense 92 

Reasoning: Based on humans’ daily knowledge and shared understanding, interpret and navigate 93 

rules and situations ( 1). Dataset: CommonsenseQA (CommonQA), PiQA and Pep-3K ( 4; 23 ; 25 ); 94 

(6) Abductive Reasoning: Infers the most likely explanation for the given observation, focusing on 95 

plausible hypotheses rather than deriving necessarily true conclusions ( 31 ). Datasets: αNLI and ART 96 

( 3; 7 );and (7) Causal Reasoning: Focuses on identifying and understanding the cause-and-effect 97 

relationships among the given facts or observations ( 11 ). Datasets: E-care and Balanced-COPA 98 

(B-COPA)(6; 12). 99 100 

3.2 Implementation 101 

This paper used the GPT-3.5-turbo model via APIs as the backbone model for conducting the above 102 

datasets. The purpose of using this model rather than larger parameter models such as the GPT-4o 103 

series is to demonstrate that effective reasoning can be achieved through improved prompting strate-104 

gies rather than simply increasing model size. This approach emphasizes that empowering a model’s 105 

potential through strategic prompting may be more efficient than relying on larger architectures to 106 

overcome reasoning limitations. Each API call is dedicated to testing exactly one prompting strategy 107 

(SDs, zero-shot, zero-shot CoT, or emotional EP02) to avoid cross-contamination of information. 108 

The temperature is set to 0 throughout the experiment. From each dataset, a representative sample 109 

comprising 10 tasks was manually extracted through random selection procedures (with an expanded 110 

sample of 40 tasks from the Scibench dataset), prioritizing methodological feasibility and assessment 111 

efficiency for generalization capabilities rather than task-specific optimization paradigms. Through-112 

out the experimental procedures, each task was presented precisely once per prompting strategy to 113 

maintain methodological consistency. Accuracy was determined based on whether the response from 114 

LLMs provided the correct answer. All statistical procedures were implemented using Python’s SciPy 115 

and statsmodels libraries, with visualization support from Matplotlib and Seaborn packages. In all 116 

box plots presented in this paper, the box boundaries represent the first ( Q1 ) and third ( Q3 ) quartiles, 117 

the middle line shows the median ( Q2 ), whiskers extend to the most extreme data points within 118 

1.5 times the interquartile range ( IQR ) from the box boundaries, and individual points beyond the 119 

whiskers indicate outliers. All the tokens were calculated via OpenAI Tokenizer. 120 

3.3 Evaluation Metrics 121 

3.3.1 Error Analysis 122 

To facilitate a more comprehensive evaluation of the performance parameters and inherent limitations 123 

of the proposed methodology, this investigation implements a systematic error analysis framework 124 

3predicated on the five-category error taxonomy established by Xu et al. ( 34 ). This error analysis is 125 

to reveal how SD strategies influence the model’s reasoning process and provides insights into the 126 

underlying mechanisms through which SD affects model reasoning. The error taxonomy categorizes 127 

mistakes into five distinct types: (1) Wrong Selection (WS) : At the beginning of the reasoning 128 

process, LLMs choose the wrong facts or ignore the necessary facts to generate the answer. (2) 129 

Hallucination (HA) : LLMs incorporate information elements or factual assertions that lack empirical 130 

verification or explicitly contradict the established contexts. (3) No Reasoning (NR) : LLMs listed 131 

the given facts without reasoning from the facts to conclude an answer. (4) Perspective Mistake 132 

(PPM) : LLMs started from an incorrect or irrelevant point of view to process the answer. (5) Process 133 

Mistake (PM) : LLMs started from the correct perspective but made mistakes during reasoning. 134 

3.3.2 Statistical Analysis 135 

To establish statistically rigorous evaluation protocols for the accuracy and error analysis, this in-136 

vestigation employs multiple complementary statistical frameworks that quantify the relationships 137 

between dependent variables (error proportions, accuracy) and independent variables (prompting 138 

methods). The primary statistical metrics utilized in this investigation include: Coefficient of Deter-139 

mination ( R2): The explanatory power of linear regression models is quantified using R2 values, 140 

which measure the proportion of variance in error rates attributable to the independent variables. 141 

Higher R2 values (approaching 1.0) indicate stronger predictive relationships, while values closer to 142 

zero indicate minimal association. Statistical Significance ( p-value) : All reported correlations and 143 

regression models are subjected to hypothesis testing with a significance threshold of α = 0 .05 . The 144 

p-values reported alongside regression analyses indicate the probability that observed relationships 145 

could occur by random chance, with lower values conferring higher confidence in the validity of the 146 

findings. Regression Coefficients : Linear regression equations provide quantitative measures of 147 

effect size, where the slope coefficient represents the expected unit change in error proportion per 148 

unit increase in the independent variable. These coefficients facilitate direct comparisons of effect 149 

magnitudes across different error types and reasoning categories. Distributional Metrics : Box plots 150 

visualize the central tendency (median), dispersion (interquartile range), and outlier characteristics of 151 

error distributions across methods and reasoning types. The positional differences in median lines and 152 

box heights quantify both the absolute performance differences and the variability in performance 153 

across experimental conditions. ANOVA : Analysis of variance tests determine whether observed 154 

differences in error rates across categorical variables (methods, reasoning types) reflect statistically 155 

significant patterns rather than random variation. This framework enables systematic comparisons 156 

across multiple experimental conditions while controlling for familywise error rates. 157 

## 4 Results & Discussion 158 

4.1 SD1 Achieves Superior Decomposition Performance with Optimal Error Balance 159 

Figure 2: Overall reasoning accuracy vs. prompting methods. 4As shown in Figure 2, SD1’s structured decomposition approach eliminates the trade-off between 160 

accuracy and consistency that plagues other methods, achieving both superior performance (62.26%, 161 

p < 0.05 ) and tight error bounds (50%-70% IQR). This dual advantage stems from explicit decompo-162 

sition instructions using modal operators (" should ") and structured terminology (" sub-tasks "), which 163 

provide sufficient guidance without the excessive complexity that increases process errors 164 

Zero-shot CoT’s high variability (Q1 at 30% vs. median at 45%) exposes its fundamental limitation: 165 

the generic " step-by-step " instruction fails to adapt to domain-specific reasoning requirements. For 166 

instance, mathematical tasks require intermediate variable tracking and equation manipulation (e.g., 167 

solving for unknowns across multiple steps), while deductive reasoning benefits from direct premise-168 

to-conclusion mapping without intermediate computations. This mismatch between CoT’s uniform 169 

step-wise approach and domain-specific cognitive requirements explains its unstable performance 170 

across reasoning types. 171 

The systematic performance decline from SD1 to SD4 (from 62.26% to 47.26% in median accuracy) 172 

reveals a critical threshold effect in decomposition granularity. SD2-SD4’s simplified instructions 173 

lack essential structural elements—particularly modal operators and explicit task terminology—that 174 

prevent models from maintaining reasoning coherence during problem breakdown. This explains 175 

why SD2-SD4 fail: they trigger decomposition without providing the scaffolding necessary for 176 

maintaining logical consistency; instead, SD2-SD4 introduce extra information in the prompt that is 177 

meaningless for reasoning. 178 

4.2 Token-Accuracy Paradox: More Computational Resources Don’t Guarantee Better 179 

Performance 180 

Figure 3: Overall reasoning accuracy vs. tokens. Computational resource allocation paradoxically harms reasoning performance: higher token 181 

consumption correlates with lower accuracy ( R2 = 0 .162 , p = 0 .004 , slope = -0.056). This 182 

negative correlation directly contradicts the intuition that more detailed processing leads to better 183 

results—each additional token decreases accuracy by 0.056%, with statistical significance despite 184 

weak overall explanatory power (16% variance). 185 

This paradox emerges from the mechanics of decomposition-induced error accumulation. Increased 186 

token usage typically results from excessive reasoning steps that introduce process mistakes (PM), as 187 

shown by the strong positive correlation between tokens and PM errors ( R2 = 0 .405 , p < 0.001 ). 188 

While longer outputs reduce wrong selection errors (WS) through better fact identification, they 189 

simultaneously create more opportunities for calculation mistakes, logical inconsistencies, and 190 

coherence failures during multi-step reasoning chains. 191 

SD1 instances span a wide token range (100-350 tokens) while maintaining consistently low PM 192 

error rates (< 0.3), demonstrating unique resilience to the typical token-PM error correlation. In 193 

contrast, SD2-SD4 methods exhibit the expected positive correlation more dramatically, with PM 194 

errors escalating substantially at higher token counts. This differential response reveals that SD1’s 195 

5prompt design includes inherent safeguards that prevent decomposition-induced coherence failures. 196 

Mathematical reasoning demonstrates this clearly: as shown in Figure 4, SD1 maintains PM errors 197 

below 15% despite increased complexity, while SD2-SD4 show exponential PM growth (from 198 

30% to 35%) due to insufficient structural scaffolding. This reveals why minimal prompting fails 199 

systematically: without explicit semantic constraints, models cannot distinguish between productive 200 

decomposition (breaking coherent logical units) and counter-productive fragmentation (arbitrary text 201 

splitting). The 30% performance gap between SD1 and SD4 in mathematical tasks directly quantifies 202 

the cost of removing structural guidance mechanisms. 203 204

Critically, SD variants differ in how well they manage this trade-off: SD1 maintains superior accuracy 209

The PPM analysis reveals fundamental limits of universal decomposition approaches. Abductive 273

Future decomposition frameworks should incorporate domain-specific guards against PPM errors, 276 

particularly for inference tasks requiring simultaneous consideration of multiple explanatory hypothe-277

## 5 Limitations 285 

While this investigation demonstrates SD1’s potential for enhancing autonomous problem-solving ca-286 

pabilities, with notable improvements in deductive reasoning (95% accuracy on deductive reasoning) 287 

and up to 25% gains over baseline approaches, several important limitations warrant consideration. 288 

These constraints contextualize our findings and highlight opportunities for future research. 289 

The error analysis reveals domain-specific limitations, particularly in PPM error patterns for abductive 290 

reasoning and the systematic trade-off between WS and PM errors. While SD1 maintains an optimal 291 

balance, the substantial performance decline in SD2-SD4 variants suggests that decomposition strate-292 

gies are sensitive to structural prompt modifications. Moreover, the negative correlation between 293 

token consumption and overall accuracy ( R2 = 0 .162 , p = 0 .004 ) indicates that computational 294 

efficiency does not uniformly translate to performance gains across reasoning types. 295 

Our analysis reveals a fundamental tension where increased token consumption reduces WS errors 296 

but increases PM errors. This relationship suggests inherent limitations in current decomposition 297 

frameworks, where the very mechanisms designed to improve fact selection create conditions fa-298 

voring process mistakes. The high variance in performance across reasoning types indicates that 299 

domain-specific optimizations may be necessary rather than universal decomposition strategies. 300 

The performance ceiling observed in complex domains like scientific reasoning (37.5% maximum 301 

accuracy) suggests that SD strategies remain bounded by the underlying LLMs’ knowledge and 302 

architectural limitations. The inconsistent patterns in abductive reasoning across datasets (e.g., SD2’s 303 

100% accuracy on ART versus poor performance of 20% accuracy on αNLI) further highlight the 304 

limitations of current decomposition approaches for certain reasoning structures. 305 

These limitations suggest several promising avenues: investigating domain-adaptive decomposition 306 

strategies that balance WS-PM error trade-offs, developing hybrid approaches for reasoning types 307 

sensitive to PPM errors, and exploring the integration of SD methods with other prompting techniques. 308 

Additionally, robustness testing under varied real-world conditions—including input noise, domain 309 

variations, and assumption violations—represents a critical area for future investigation. 310 

86 Conclusion 311 

The Self-Directed Decomposition (SD) methodology enhances LLMs’ autonomous reasoning ca-312 

pabilities by instructing models to decompose complex problems independently, without relying 313 

on human intervention or exemplar-based prompting. Unlike previous approaches that constrained 314 

models to predefined problem-solving trajectories, SD enables models to autonomously develop 315 

problem-solving strategies based on their understanding of tasks. 316 

Experimental results across seven reasoning domains reveal that SD1 achieves superior performance 317 

by maintaining an optimal balance between error types, while subsequent variants (SD2-SD4) exhibit 318 

deteriorating performance despite reduced structural complexity. The comprehensive error analysis 319 

uncovers a fundamental trade-off in decomposition reasoning: while SD strategies systematically 320 

reduce wrong selection (WS) errors through improved fact selection, they simultaneously introduce 321 

process mistakes (PM) that increase with computational resource allocation. SD1’s exceptional 322 

performance stems from its resilience to this token-error paradox, maintaining consistent error control 323 

across varying token consumption levels. 324 

The investigation further reveals domain-specific error patterns, particularly the concentration of 325 

perspective mistakes (PPM) in abductive reasoning, highlighting the limitations of universal de-326 

composition prompting strategies. The negative correlation between overall token consumption 327 

and accuracy ( R2 = 0 .162 , p = 0 .004 ) demonstrates that increased computational resources do not 328 

uniformly improve performance, challenging the intuitive assumption that more detailed processing 329 

leads to better results—a relationship our findings directly contradict. 330 

These findings contribute to a nuanced understanding of decomposition-based prompting, revealing 331 

both its transformative potential and inherent limitations. The work demonstrates that effective prompt 332 

engineering requires careful calibration between methodological sophistication and error control, with 333 

different reasoning types demanding specialized optimization strategies. Future research should focus 334 

on developing adaptive frameworks that can dynamically adjust decomposition complexity based on 335 

domain characteristics, error risk profiles, and the fundamental token-error trade-offs identified in 336 

this investigation. 337
