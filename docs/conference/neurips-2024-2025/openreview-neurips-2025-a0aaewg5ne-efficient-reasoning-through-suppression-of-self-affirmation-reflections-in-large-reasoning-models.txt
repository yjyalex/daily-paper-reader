# Efficient Reasoning Through Suppression of Self-Affirmation Reflections in Large Reasoning Models

## Abstract 

While recent advances in large reasoning models have demonstrated remarkable 1

performance, efficient reasoning remains critical due to the rapid growth of output 2

length. Existing optimization approaches highlights a tendency toward "overthink-3

ing", yet lack fine-grained analysis. In this work, we focus on Self-Affirmation 4

Reflections : redundant reflective steps that affirm prior content and often occurs 5

after the already correct reasoning steps. Observations of both original and op-6

timized reasoning models reveal pervasive self-affirmation reflections. Notably, 7

these reflections sometimes lead to longer outputs in optimized models than their 8

original counterparts. Through detailed analysis, we uncover an intriguing pattern: 9

compared to other reflections, the leading words (i.e., the first word of sentences) in 10 

self-affirmation reflections exhibit a distinct probability bias. Motivated by this in-11 

sight, we can locate self-affirmation reflections and conduct a train-free experiment 12 

demonstrating that suppressing self-affirmation reflections reduces output length 13 

without degrading accuracy across multiple models (R1-Distill-Models, QwQ-32B, 14 

and Qwen3-32B). Furthermore, we also improve current train-based method by 15 

explicitly suppressing such reflections. In our experiments, we achieve length 16 

compression of 18.7% in train-free settings and 50.2% in train-based settings for 17 

R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B. Moreover, our improvements are simple yet practical and 18 

can be directly applied to existing inference frameworks, such as vLLM. We believe 19 

that our findings will provide community insights for achieving more precise length 20 

compression and step-level efficient reasoning. 21 

## 1 Introduction 22 

Recent advancements [ 23 , 43 , 2 , 17 ] in large language models have delivered remarkable performance 23 

across various tasks [ 10 , 4 ], yet they still struggle with complex reasoning tasks demanding advanced 24 

mathematical [ 15 , 22 ] and formal logical deduction [ 35 ] abilities. Works [ 18 , 17 , 12 , 38 , 36 ] like 25 

Deepseek-R1 [ 12 ] have driven progress in reasoning models via reinforcement learning. However, 26 

current large reasoning models are troubled by high token usage and computational costs due to 27 

generating lengthy reasoning chains. Researchers have been delving into different technical strategies 28 

to enhance reasoning efficiency, like refining latent representations [ 14 , 33 , 7] and combining small 29 

models [ 27 , 11 ]. In order to directly optimize the model itself, several approaches have emerged as 30 

promising methods, using supervised fine-tuning (SFT) [ 41 , 13 , 25 ], direct preference optimization 31 

(DPO) [32], and reinforcement learning (RL) [3, 24, 3, 1, 44]. 32 

Although these methods have yielded impressive results, such as producing more semantically 33 

efficient or direct solutions, we observed that redundant reflection persists in the optimized models 34 

[ 3, 24 ]. Rather than attributing this phenomenon to the more general "overthink" issue (defined in 35 

Submitted to 39th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NeurIPS 2025). Do not distribute. work [ 34 ] as the generation of excessively detailed or unnecessary lengthy reasoning steps by large 36 

reasoning models during inference, which undermines reasoning efficiency), we conducted a more 37 

detailed examination of these examples (for more details, refer to Section 3 ). As illustrated in Figure 38 

1, we have identified a common phenomenon: affirming prior content typically occurs following 39 

correct steps, which we refer to as Self-Affirmation Reflection. In some cases, this superfluous 40 

reflection even leads to longer answers for certain problems in the optimized models. 41

with shorter lengths receiving higher rewards. Our method complements this strategy by explicitly 81

the feasibility and significance of the findings in this paper. 84 

Our work offers three key benefits: (1) We provides the first focused analysis of the Self-Affirmation 85 

Reflection phenomenon and provides actionable insights for improving reasoning models . (2) 86 

We present a simple yet practical intervention strategy : suppressing crucial leading tokens. 87 

This directly lessens Self-Affirmation Reflection, achieving efficient output compression without 88 

modifying the model architecture or training process. (3) Extensive experiments across diverse 89 

models and datasets in both train-free and train-based settings demonstrate that mitigating Self-90 

Affirmation Reflection can effectively reduce output length while preserving or even enhancing 91

The first category of methods [ 13 , 42 , 20 ] aim 99 

to obtain some information from the input to 100 

estimate the cost in advance. Some methods 101 

[ 13 , 42 , 20 ] choose to estimate the reasoning 102 

cost. For example, TokenBudget [ 13 ] instructs 103 

models to estimate the token count required to 104 

solve a problem upfront, compelling them to 105 

generate concise responses within a predefined 106 

budget. Other methods [ 27 , 5 , 9, 8] estimate 107 

the model size. Approaches like RouterLLM 108 

[ 27 ] and Sot [ 5] pre-train classifiers to route 109 

problems to the most suitable LLM based on 110 

task complexity. In contrast, router-free methods 111 

such as Self-ReF [ 9] and Seek-Router [ 8 ] predict 112 

the necessity of invoking additional LLMs by 113 

analyzing internal uncertainty scores. However, 114 

how to accurately determine the optimal cost is 115 

still a problem. 116 

Recent advances in the second category of methods have focused on refining the output of optimization 117 

models. As demonstrated in [ 28 ], simply adding intermediate "thinking" tokens, or even nonsensical 118 

fillers like "......", can lead to satisfactory performance. Subsequent works, such as those in [ 14 , 33 , 7], 119 

have explored treating the last layer hidden states of LLMs as "continuous thinking" to replace 120 

traditional discrete markers. These approaches optimize latent representations through training. 121 

However, such methods may render parts of the thought chain invisible, potentially introducing 122 

security risks. In addition to optimizing the output sequence, there are also methods to choose to 123 

switch the model during output, such as RSD [ 21 ] integrating multiple LLMs to achieve dynamic 124 

reasoning. These methods demand precise determination of when to switch models and which model 125 

to switch to. 126 

The third category of methods centers on fine-tuning LLM itself to directly address redundancy in 127 

the output of the original model. Generally, these methods [ 24 , 3, 3, 1, 44 ] first obtain responses of 128 

varying lengths via model self-sampling. Then, they design a length-based reward conditioned on 129 

correctness, with shorter correct answers receiving higher rewards. However, balancing accuracy 130 

and output length remains a significant challenge. Although these methods reduce average response 131 

length of the entire datasets, they do not guarantee shorter outputs across all instances. Specifically, 132 

the figure 2 highlights that at the instance level, some outputs (depicted in blue) remain longer than 133 

those from the original model. Our train-based experiments in Section 5.2 is closely related to these 134 

methods but differs in focus. Rather than focusing on reward function design, our objective is to 135 

directly optimize sampling outcomes, thereby refining the quality of the constructed data pairs and 136 

achieving more succinct responses. 137 

## 3 Observation 138 

Our observation begins with experiments evaluating two representative open-source works [ 24 , 3]. 139 

We tested R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B [ 12 ] and QwQ-Preview [ 37 ] on the MATH500 dataset [ 22 ] alongside 140 

their compressed counterparts (EfficientReasoning [ 3] and O1-Pruner [ 24 ]). For each question, three 141 

responses were sampled, with average response length recorded. 142 

As shown in Figure 2, results exhibit a counterintuitive trend: certain ratio of instance-level questions 143 

elicit longer responses, yet most of these extended responses remain correct (e.g., 91.6% in QwQ-144 

Preview settings). This pattern persists across varying compression levels. For instance, the parameter 145 

3α in EfficientReasoning[ 3] balances brevity and accuracy. Larger α values shorten outputs at the cost 146 

of reduced accuracy. As shown in Figure 2, even at mild compression ( α = 0 .05 ), 6% of questions 147 

generated longer responses. Aggressive compression ( α = 0 .4) still failed to resolve this behavior, 148 

though the trade-off led to a significant drop in accuracy. 149 

To gain deeper insights into the optimized models, we conducted a manual examination and analyzed 150 

their qualitative behaviors, as shown in Figure 3. Two key characteristics consistently emerge: (1) the 151 

model tends to produce more concise steps during the problem-solving phase. For instance, the brown 152 

text in Figure 3 illustrates how the optimized model succinctly summarizes the problem background. 153 

(2) certain questions have been solved using more direct approaches. This arises because, during the 154 

self-sampling process, the model generates multiple potential problem-solving methods. By favoring 155 

shorter solution ideas, the model discards superfluous strategies. 156 

However, we also observe that the model frequently engages in prolonged reflections even after 157 

executing correct reasoning steps or arriving at the correct answer, resulting in excessively lengthy 158 

outputs. Figure 3 illustrates this issue: compared to the baseline R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B model [ 12 ], 159 

the optimized EfficientReasoning model ( α = 0 .05 ) [ 3 ] achieves the correct solution more rapidly 160 

but produces lengthier responses due to increased self-reflections. These reflections often show a 161 

tendency to affirm the previous content. Quantitative results in Figure 2 further confirm that this 162 

pattern is prevalent across current efficient reasoning models [ 3, 24 ]. Overall, these findings suggest 163 

that naively optimizing for solution-level simplicity is not enough to effectively compress the model’s 164 

outputs. This indicates that existing frameworks may still have opportunities to enhance output 165 

simplicity by suppressing step-level self-affirmation reflections. 166 

Notably, while this phenomenon was widely summarized as an overthink problem in previous works 167 

[29 , 39 , 34 ], its persistence in optimized reasoning models underscores the need for targeted solutions. 168 

Therefore, we formally define this behavior as self-affirmation reflection, as introduced in Section 169 

1, and focus on it in this paper. To address this issue fundamentally, we analyze how to locate and 170 

suppress self-affirmation reflection in the original model, as detailed in the subsequent Section 4. 171

## 4 Analysis 172 

4.1 Locate Self-Affirmation Reflections 173 

To investigate the self-affirmation reflection, we first randomly sampled 500 training instances from 174 

the MATH dataset. For each problem, we generated a single solution using R1-Distill-1.5B [ 12 ]. The 175 

generated reasoning steps were split using the " \n\n" delimiter. 176 

4Then, we utilized the Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct [ 43 ] to classify each step as either a reasoning reflection 177 

or non-reflective reasoning. To improve the accuracy, we further combine the previous method [ 6] to 178 

assist in judgment. For steps identified as reasoning reflections, we invoked the Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct 179 

again to determine whether the reflection affirmed the preceding reasoning, i.e., whether it constituted 180 

a self-affirmation reflection. The specific prompts used for classification are detailed in Appendix A.3. 181 

To validate this automated classification, we manually annotated self-affirmation reflections in all 182 

responses of 20 problems to form a test set. Evaluation revealed the Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct achieved 183 

an accuracy of 80.6%. This is primarily because while most self-affirmation reflections contain 184 

explicit signal words such as "that’s correct" in Figure 3, some self-affirmation reflections merely 185 

repeat previous conclusions to indicate affirmation of prior content, making these more challenging 186 

to detect. 187

spectives to identify patterns in self-affirmation 190 

reflection occurrence. First , prior researches 191 

[ 31 , 26 ] has established that language patterns 192 

are critical in determining whether models ac-193 

tivate specific reflection mechanisms during 194 

decision-making. Therefore, we sampled 20 195 

self-affirmation reflections and other reflection 196 

types, then performed multiple rollout iterations 197 

from their contextual positions. We hope to 198 

explore whether models can elicit distinct be-199 

havioral responses by producing diverse leading 200 

words. As shown in Figure 4, we found that 201 

self-affirmation reflections exhibit significantly 202 

greater diversity in behavior-guiding continua-203 

tion patterns compared to other reflection types. 204 

Specifically, during the initial reflection (Self-205 

Affirmation Reflection), the model attempts to verify its understanding of the problem context. 206 

Examining the top 8 predicted word continuations, we observe the model’s indecision between 207 

further reflection and progressing toward a solution, as evidenced by suggestions such as "break it 208 

down". In the second reflection (Other Reflection), the trajectories of the model show a high degree 209 

of consistency. Here, the top 8 predicted words uniformly advocate for continued reflection, such 210 

as "double-check". The final reflection (Self-Affirmation Reflection) occurs after the model has 211 

5already provided the correct answer. Similar to the first reflection, we observe the model confronting 212 

uncertainty, contemplating whether to terminate the task or initiate another round of reflection. 213 

Second , we conducted an additional analysis of the quantity and probability of the leading words 214 

across all reflections. It is worth noting that, for simplicity, we only selected the first word as leading 215 

words, but a typical leading word is sometimes not necessarily a single word. For example, when 216 

sentences start with "But", we found that the next word is "wait" in 38% of self-affirmation reflections. 217 

In this case, "But wait" is more suitable to be used as a unique leading word. During statistical 218 

processing, we excluded repetitive tail-end reflections from model outputs. Because self-affirmation 219 

reflections sometimes exhibited recurring patterns where incremental reinforcement of leading-word 220 

probabilities led to closed-loop generation. These loops could skew statistical accuracy. While this 221 

phenomenon warrants further study, it falls outside the scope of this work. For an illustrative example 222 

of such looping behavior and comprehensive results including looped sentences, refer to Appendix 223 

A.4. 224

reflection. To our surprise, the confidence level of leading words in self-affirmation reflection appears 226 

to be lower than that in other Reflections. This finding echoes the results from the Figure 4, indicating 227 

that when the model is uncertain about its actions, the output leading words also lack confidence. 228 

4.2 Suppress Self-Affirmation Reflections 229 

Figure 6: The distribution of "Wait" in the two types of reflections. Up to now, our analysis highlights a critical insight: 230 

leading tokens in self-affirmation reflections exhibit 231 

significantly lower generation probabilities compared 232 

to other reflection types. Our objective is to sup-233 

press self-affirmation reflections in order to achieve 234 

shorter outputs while maintaining performance. Con-235 

sequently, we propose a straightforward intervention: 236 

when the model assigns a low probability to gener-237 

ating leading words, we set their probability to zero 238 

to suppress self-affirmation reflections. Given the 239 

statistical significance of the "Wait" token, we first 240 

focus on it in this paper. 241 

To validate our method, we analyze the probability 242 

density of the "Wait" token across two reflection types in Figure 6. Though their distributions exhibit 243 

partial overlap, they remain distinguishable through thresholding. We acknowledge that suppressing 244 

low-probability "Wait" instances may inadvertently filter out other reflections. However, empirical 245 

results show that even when such instances are suppressed, other high-probability tokens can still 246 

facilitate emergence of necessary reflections via compensatory mechanisms, as exemplified by the 247 

second reflection in Figure 4. We also recognize the limitation of exclusively targeting the "Wait" 248 

token, which relates to balancing interventions across multiple leading words. In practical results, 249 

we found that since "wait" frequently follows "But", suppressing this token also partially mitigates 250 

self-affirmation reflections. Consequently, we intervene on both "Wait" and "wait" (collectively 251 

referred to as the "wait" tokens hereafter). More discussions of other interfered tokens appear in 252 

Appendix A.5. 253 

Our results also demonstrate that suppressing low-probability "wait" tokens maintains performance 254 

integrity and produces shorter outputs. In train-free settings (e.g., R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B in Table 255 

1), output lengths shorten while performance improves when thresholds decrease below 0.5. For 256 

training-based scenarios, ablation studies identify optimal performance at a threshold of 0.3. These 257 

findings curiously align with the probability distributions in Figure 6, suggesting that thresholds ≤ 0.3258 

effectively suppress self-affirmation reflections while having minimal impact on other reflection 259 

types. 260 

## 5 Experiments 261 

We first assess the impact of removing Self-Affirmation Reflections in Section 5.1. Subsequently, in 262 

Section 5.2, we integrate a representative train-based approach to verify whether explicitly suppressing 263 

self-affirmation reflections can lead to improved results. 264 

65.1 Train-free Experiments 265 

5.1.1 Settings 266 

We hypothesize that removing low-probability self-affirmation reflection will not degrade model 267 

performance. To test this hypothesis, we conducted experiments across several prominent reasoning 268 

models: R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B/7B/32B [ 12 ], QwQ-32B [ 37 ] and Qwen3-32B [ 38 ]. We also addition-269 

ally compared Underthink [ 40 ], which intervened with logits within a certain window length. For 270 

more details and our discussion on this work, please refer to Appendix A.2. Similar to the previous 271 

approaches [ 6, 3 , 24 , 32 ], our experiments are conducted on four datasets: (1) MATH500 [ 22 ], 272 

comprising 500 problems across seven mathematical domains (algebra, geometry, number theory, 273 

etc.) that challenge both humans and LLMs; (2) AIME24, featuring 2024 American Invitational 274 

Mathematics Examination problems to test advanced mathematical competition problem-solving 275 

skills; (3) AMC23 [ 15 ], with problems from the 2023 American Mathematics Competitions covering 276 

secondary school mathematics; and (4) GSM8K [ 10 ], containing 8.5K linguistically diverse elemen-277 

tary school math problems to evaluate simple arithmetic reasoning. Regarding the results of the 278 

out-of-domain dataset, please refer to the Appendix A.6. 279 

For AIME24 and AMC23, we sampled eight responses per question. For MATH500 and GSM8K, we 280 

collected one response per problem. The maximum allowed output length was 32k tokens. In order 281 

to evaluate Qwen3-32B [ 38 ], we utilized the latest vLLM implementation [ 19 ] in zero-shot inference 282 

settings. We report the average accuracy (Acc) and average token count (LEN) per response across 283 

all datasets. All experiments were conducted on NVIDIA L20 GPUs. 284 

5.1.2 Results 285 

As shown in Table 1, we evaluated the impact of different thresholds on performance. Our results 286 

indicate that an appropriate threshold can effectively reduce output length without compromising 287 

performance, and in some cases, even enhances performance with shorter outputs, which is consistent 288 

with our previous analysis in Figure 6. However, as the threshold increases, high-probability "wait" 289 

tokens are also filtered out, leading to a noticeable decline in performance due to the removal of some 290 

necessary reflections. Despite its simple implementation, our method achieves a significant reduction 291 

in output length with almost no performance loss. Specifically, it reduces average length by 18.7%, 292 

14.3%, 11.1%, 9.1% and 8.4% for R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B (Threshold-0.9), R1-Distill-Qwen-7B 293 

(Threshold-0.7), R1-Distill-Qwen-32B (Threshold-0.7), QwQ-32B (Threshold 0.7) and Qwen3-32B 294 

(Threshold 0.9), respectively. Moreover, we were surprised to find that the compression ratio adapts 295 

dynamically across different datasets. For instance, AIME24 demonstrates a lower compression ratio 296 

than MATH500. This aligns with the intuition that AIME24, being more challenging, inherently 297 

possesses less compressible structure compared to MATH500. 298

## 6 Conclusion 344 

Our paper delves into the distinctive features of self-affirmation reflections in the distribution of 345 

leading words and their correlation with confidence. Leveraging these insights, we introduce a strategy 346 

to suppress critical leading words. Experimental results demonstrate that this method effectively 347 

reduces output length in both training-free and training-based scenarios while sustaining or even 348 

elevating model performance. While we acknowledge certain limitations (see Appendix A.1), we aim 349 

for this study to offer a fresh viewpoint on achieving more precise length compression at step-level 350 

efficient reasoning and to improve the efficiency of large reasoning models. 351

A.1 Limitations 472 

Two core challenges persist. First, methods for systematically identifying candidate tokens for 473 

intervention remain underdeveloped. Second, developing principles to balance dependencies among 474 

multiple intervened words represents an open research question. Consequently, our intervention 475 

framework in this work focuses solely on the most salient specific tokens. Future research will explore 476 

strategies to generalize token intervention approaches, addressing both identification scalability and 477 

multi-word coordination. 478 

We further recognize that the foundational mechanisms governing the model’s reasoning process, 479 

as well as the emergence of redundant reflections during generation, are not yet fully understood. 480 

These knowledge gaps persist within the large reasoning model community. As the field matures, we 481 

anticipate opportunities to integrate insights from complementary work and conduct more in-depth 482 

analyses of these phenomena in subsequent studies. 483

already correct steps) or after the completed exploration (self-affirmation reflection after outputting 503 

correct answers). Notably, while this work provides a preliminary analysis of underthinking, our 504 

work offers a more in-depth and specific analysis of self-affirmation reflections, presenting a fresh 505 

perspective on efficient reasoning area. 506

A.6 Results of out-of-domain dataset 561 

We additionally evaluate our model on out-of-domain test data using the GPQA-Diamond benchmark 562 

[30 ]. GPQA-Diamond serves as a rigorous evaluation dataset designed to assess models’ capacity for 563 

deep reasoning and domain expertise. This dataset represents the highest-quality resource within the 564 

GPQA series, comprising 198 graduate-level or competition-level multiple-choice questions. The 565 

questions primarily focus on core STEM disciplines including biology, physics, and chemistry, pre-566 

senting complex problems that require sophisticated reasoning abilities. We sampled four responses 567 

for each question and restricted the output length to 32k tokens. We report the average accuracy 568 

(Acc) and average token count (LEN) per response. As shown in Table 7, our method generates 569 

significantly shorter outputs on out-of-domain dataset while maintaining competitive performance. 570
