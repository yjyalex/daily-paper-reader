## Abstract 

Large Reasoning Models (LRMs) achieve strong performance on complex tasks by lever-aging long Chain-of-Thought (CoT), but of-ten suffer from overthinking, leading to ex-cessive reasoning steps and high inference la-tency. Existing CoT compression methods struggle to balance accuracy and efficiency, and lack fine-grained, step-level adaptation to re-dundancy and reasoning bias. Therefore, we propose State-Aware Reasoning Compression with Knowledge Guidance (STACK), a frame-work that performs step-wise CoT compression by explicitly modeling stage-specific redun-dancy sources and integrating with a retrieval-augmented guidance. STACK constructs online long-short contrastive samples and dynamically switches between knowledge-guided compres-sion for uncertain or biased reasoning state and self-prompted compression for overly long but confident state, complemented by an answer-convergence-based early stopping mechanism to suppress redundant verification. We fur-ther propose a reward-difference-driven train-ing strategy by combining Proximal Policy Op-timization (PPO) and Direct Preference Opti-mization (DPO), enabling models to learn state-conditioned compression strategies. Experi-ments on three mathematical reasoning bench-marks show that STACK achieves a superior accuracy-efficiency balance, reducing average response length by 59.9% while improving ac-curacy by 4.8 points over existing methods. 

## 1 Introduction 

Driven by reinforcement learning (RL) with verifiable rewards, Large Reasoning Models (LRMs) (Jaech et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2025) have developed long Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reason-ing patterns (Wei et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2025). By leveraging deep logical deductions, extensive exploration, and mechanisms for reflection and self-

It induces a qualitative shift from shallow pattern matching toward structured and verifiable reasoning in challenging tasks such as mathematical proofs, complex programming and logical analysis. However, long CoT reasoning also introduces an overthinking phenomenon: re-gardless of task complexity, the model consistently follows a deep reasoning pattern, leading to ex-tended reasoning steps that substantially increase response latency and computational cost (Chiang and Lee, 2024; Sui et al., 2025). To address this issue, CoT compression tech-niques (Liu et al., 2025; Qu et al., 2025) aim to improve reasoning efficiency while preserving per-formance, primarily through output or model op-timization, as shown in Figure 1. Output opti-mization typically relies on prompt design to en-courage concise reasoning (Xu et al., 2025; Han et al., 2025). However, it may compromise reason-ing completeness and coherence, and is inherently constrained by performance ceilings. In contrast,

by introducing reward functions or penalty terms within RL, to train the models to adaptively decide when to engage explicit reasoning (Zhang et al., 2025a; Hou et al., 2025). This type of methods are effective in reducing reasoning length at the cost of reasoning accuracy. However, in long CoT, the redundancy arises from heterogeneous sources, leading to varied reasoning states at different stages of reasoning, such as repetitive elaboration under high confidence and excessive verification under uncertainty . Although existing model optimiza-tion methods adopt online learning strategies, they primarily adjust compression intensity according to task-level complexity (Yan et al., 2025a; Fang et al., 2025). The absence of a finer-grained, step-wise modeling of redundancy sources prevents the existing methods from dynamically selecting the appropriate compression strategies required for dif-ferent reasoning states along the reasoning chain. Moreover, recent studies show that long CoT may aggravate hallucinations (Lu et al., 2025): early-stage local biases are propagated and ampli-fied through reflection and self-persuasion, leading to error accumulation that undermines reasoning reliability and accuracy. Retrieval-augmented gen-eration (RAG) is a widely used approach to tackle the problem. However, given the extended rea-soning chains of LRMs, the external knowledge introduced as contextual input tends to be gradu-ally dominated by internal inference as reasoning unfolds. This observation motivates a step-wise integration of COT compression and knowledge guidance, enabling timely correction of reasoning deviations while suppressing redundancy. To address these challenges, we propose State-Aware Reasoning Compression with Knowledge Guidance (STACK), a unified framework that dy-namically regulates step-level CoT compression while integrating with a retrieval-augmented guid-ance. During sampling, STACK constructs online contrastive long–short CoT samples and adaptively switches between different compression strate-gies for different reasoning states: specifically, a knowledge-guided compression for uncertain or biased reasoning state and a self-prompted com-pression for overly long but confident state, to ad-dress stage-specific sources of redundancy. This would enable the model to learn when to incorpo-rate external knowledge for correction and when to condense redundant reasoning. In addition, we introduce an early stopping mechanism based on the convergence of answer distribution to identify and suppress redundant verification steps, further improving CoT conciseness. At the training stage, STACK combines the re-ward modeling capability of Proximal Policy Op-timization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017) with the lightweight contrastive learning paradigm of Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023). By driving policy updates through reward differences, the framework enhances the model’s ability to autonomously learn state-conditioned optimal compression strategies, while leveraging high-quality external evidence to guide inference under the uncertain states, thereby effectively miti-gating reasoning bias and error accumulation. Our work claims the following key innovations: 1. We propose STACK, a state-aware framework that dynamically switches targeted compres-sion strategies to address step-level sources of redundancy. 2. We introduce a knowledge-guided con-trastive decoding mechanism with an answer-distribution-based early stopping mechanism, enabling controllable suppression of redun-dant reasoning while correcting uncertain or biased paths. 3. Extensive experiments on three mathemati-cal reasoning benchmarks demonstrate that STACK consistently outperforms existing CoT compression methods, achieving a su-perior balance between reasoning accuracy and efficiency across different model scales. 

## 2 Related work 

Chain-of-Thought Compression. Existing CoT compression strategies to mitigate overthinking pri-marily fall into three classes: prompt engineer-ing (Xu et al., 2025; Yan et al., 2025b), which constrains reasoning length or format via explicit prompts; reasoning output optimization (Shen et al., 2025b; Cheng and Van Durme, 2024), which com-presses reasoning steps into a latent space or ad-justs strategies during inference; and model opti-mization (Shen et al., 2025a; Luo et al., 2025a), which enhances reasoning efficiency via online RL or offline fine-tuning. While online RL en-courages concise reasoning through reward design or penalty item (Team et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025b), and offline fine-tuning uses variable-length CoT data (Munkhbat et al., 2025). However, these methods overlook step-level redundancy causes, and frequently compromise accuracy by ignoring Figure 2: Framework of State-Aware Reasoning Compression with Knowledge Guidance. 

the influence of the model’s intrinsic biases. Our work addresses this by dynamically switching com-pression strategies at the step level and introducing external knowledge to correct reasoning biases. 

Model Hallucination and Contrastive Decod-ing. Hallucination remains a key challenge for LRMs (Huang et al., 2024; Tonmoy et al., 2024). Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), which incorporates real-time external knowledge, has emerged as a mainstream approach to address hal-lucinations (Gao et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2024). To ensure models incorporate external knowledge in long-context inputs, contrastive decoding is pro-posed to subtly strengthen context guidance in rea-soning (Li et al., 2023). For instance, context-aware decoding (Shi et al., 2024) suppresses hallucina-tions by contrasting outputs distributions with and without external context, but assumes perfectly re-liable knowledge. Recent work integrates both relevant and irrelevant knowledge to improve ro-bustness in conflict scenarios (Zhao et al., 2024). By masking retrieval heads to trigger hallucina-tions, some approaches dynamically contrast the original and masked model outputs via conditional entropy to mitigate hallucinated responses (Gema et al., 2024). In contrast, our Knowledge-Guided Contrastive Decoding adaptively integrates exter-nal evidence with parametric knowledge, with the aim of improving output accuracy while mitigating redundancy. 

## 3 State-Aware Reasoning Compression with Knowledge Guidance (STACK) 

3.1 Overview 

As illustrated in Figure 2, STACK builds upon the DPO paradigm to achieve adaptive chain-of-thought compression via online long–short con-trastive sampling, avoiding the training instabil-ity of PPO and the high computational cost of GRPO. For each input, the original reasoning chain is treated as the long sample, while the short sample is generated online through step-wise segmentation and fine-grained compression. STACK enables step-wise compression by mon-itoring local information entropy to identify hesita-tion states that indicate high reasoning uncertainty. When such states are detected, a knowledge-guided compression strategy employs contrastive decoding to steer generation toward evidence-supported rea-soning. If absent but the response is overly long, a self-prompted compression strategy removes redun-dant or repetitive steps. An answer-convergence-based early stopping mechanism further suppresses unnecessary verification once reasoning stabilizes. The resulting compressed reasoning chains are paired with their original counterparts to form contrastive samples. STACK then evaluates both chains with a reward function and integrates the reward difference into the DPO loss, enabling effi-cient reasoning compression while preserving accu-racy. Subsequent sections detail each component. 

3.2 Detection of hesitation state 

Existing research addressing the overthinking is-sue in LRMs typically relies on fine-tuning with offline-constructed datasets or implements coarse-grained compression based on problem-level com-plexity (Zhang et al., 2025b), which limits the flex-ibility and adaptability of such approaches. In prac-tice, redundant reasoning primarily stems from two sources: first, the presence of substantial expres-sions that convey little substantive information; sec-ond, the model’s low confidence in the intermediate reasoning outcome, necessitating iterative verifica-tion to confirm correctness (Wang et al., 2025). We refer to the latter as the hesitation state. To enable fine-grained compression, we intro-duce local entropy during sampling to track the model’s reasoning state at each step, allowing dy-namic, targeted compression based on the source of redundancy. 3.3 Step-level compression strategy Knowledge-Guided Compression. When the model enters a hesitation state, relying solely on self-guided reasoning tends to amplify local errors or perpetuate incorrect reasoning trajectories, ne-cessitating a knowledge-guided mechanism that simultaneously corrects reasoning direction and suppresses redundancy verification. To this end, we propose Knowledge-Guided Contrastive Decod-ing (KGCD). KGCD treats retrieved information as a factual anchor and dynamically regulates gen-eration by contrasting the distributional differences between the model’s parametric knowledge and external evidence. Self-Prompted Compression. When the model is in a high-confidence reasoning state but the re-sponse exceeds the predefined maximum length of 2000 tokens(Zhao et al., 2025), STACK activates the self-prompted compression strategy. Under the constraint of lightweight prompt (e.g., “Summarize the key reasoning steps, maintaining logical com-pleteness and removing redundant elaboration."), LRMs are encouraged to generate reasoning steps that are both logically complete and concisely ex-pressed based on the current reasoning context. This approach significantly reduces token usage while avoiding information loss caused by coarse-grained global compression. 

3.4 Early stopping mechanism 

Given that LRMs exhibit introspection and self-verification capabilities, they may continue to gen-erate redundant confirmatory reasoning even after reaching a final answer, resulting in unnecessary computational overhead (Qiao et al., 2025). This observation motivates the incorporation of an early stopping mechanism that explicitly detects informa-tion saturation during online reasoning, enabling timely termination of redundant steps and facili-tating more effective CoT compression as well as more informative training signals. To this end, we design an early stopping mech-anism based on convergence at the answer level. Effective reasoning steps are expected to contribute substantive information toward deriving the final answer. In contrast, when subsequent steps serve only as redundant verification, the model’s prob-ability distribution over candidate answers tends to stabilize. ## 4 Experiments 

4.1 Setup 

We employ DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B and DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B (202, 2025) as the base models and adopt STACK as the training framework. During training, the learning rate and batch size are set to 2e-6 and 64, respectively. The experiments are conducted using the PyTorch framework on 4×NVIDIA A100-80GB GPUs. Fur-thermore, to provide the model with external knowl-edge, following the approach of Search-o1 (Li et al., 2025), we utilize the Bing Web Search API and col-lect the top-5 retrieved results. Considering that LRMs lack the generation capability for short CoT and knowledge-guided logical reasoning, we per-form a cold-start fine-tuning using annotated data to prevent mode collapse and training instability in subsequent DPO optimization. Additional training details are provided in the appendix A. 

Datasets Given data requirements of the task and the base model’s strong mathematical per-formance, we employ three math datasets of in-creasing difficulty: GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), MATH500 (Hunter et al., 2023), and AIME24. Ac-curacy (ACC), response length (Len) and inference latency (Lat) are used as evaluation metrics. We also adopt Token Efficiency ( TE = Acc Len ∗ 100 ) to assess the method’s balance between accuracy and efficiency (Yan et al., 2025a).

4.2 Performance Comparison on Reasoning Benchmarks 

Table 1 reports the performance of various CoT compression methods across LRMs of different scales. Overall, STACK consistently achieves the best balance between reasoning efficiency and ac-curacy. Compared with the baseline, STACK re-duces the average reasoning length by over 59.9% while improving average accuracy by 4.8 points, yielding the highest token efficiency and the low-est inference latency. Moreover, the distribution of reasoning lengths across datasets with varying difficulty indicates that STACK enhances adaptive reasoning behavior. On the simpler GSM8K bench-mark, the 1.5B model adaptively generates shorter reasoning chains (684 tokens on average). In contrast, prompt-based compression methods exhibit limited effectiveness, reducing inference latency by only approximately 1 second and failing to adequately support complex reasoning. Despite incorporating external knowledge, they incur a 1.1-point accuracy drop on AIME2024 using the 1.5B model. As reasoning chains grow longer, model generation becomes increasingly dominated by in-ternal inference, thereby weakening the influence of external evidence. STACK addresses these is-sues via step-level fine-grained compression and explicit knowledge reinforcement at the output dis-tribution. This advantage persists on larger models: on the 7B model, STACK improves average accu-racy by 2.7 points while reducing inference latency to 6.73s (vs. 7.23s for the 1.5B model). Neverthe-less, significant performance gaps remain among compression strategies. ConCISE and TokenSqueeze significantly im-prove reasoning efficiency by constructing high-quality long–short sample pairs offline and fine-tuning models via preference optimization or con-trastive learning. However, their statically con-structed training data introduces a growing distribu-tion shift as model capabilities evolve, which limits further performance gains. By contrast, STACK constructs contrastive samples online, enabling its compression strategy to adapt dynamically to vari-ations across inputs and reasoning stages, resulting in superior overall performance. MuTIS also adopts an online intervention paradigm and demonstrates strong performance across multiple settings. However, it merely fo-cuses on structural-level compression strategy. In contrast, STACK continuously monitors step-level reasoning states and dynamically switches be-tween self-prompted and knowledge-guided com-pression, enabling it to address both stage-specific redundancy and reasoning bias. Experiments on challenging benchmarks such as MATH500 and AIME2024 show that this state-aware and knowledge-guided design substantially improves reasoning accuracy and token efficiency, underscor-ing the robustness of STACK in complex tasks. 4.5 Hyperparameter analysis 

Empirically, we find that the contrastive decoding coefficient μ and the compression reward weight 

α are crucial for balancing reasoning accuracy and chain compactness. Specifically, μ controls the magnitude of the penalty applied when the model’s generation deviates from external evidence, while 

α determines the scaling factor of compression gains during training. Values that are too small make compression ineffective, while values that are too large cause premature truncation or reduced reasoning quality as shown in Figure 4. Across different model scales, μ = 0 .25 and α = 0 .5

consistently achieve the best balance between pre-serving accuracy and compressing reasoning. 

## 5 Conclusion 

In this work, we propose STACK, a State-Aware Reasoning Compression framework with Knowl-edge Guidance, which innovatively integrates a retrieval-augmented mechanism into the COT com-pression process and enables dynamic switching of online compression strategies to address diverse sources of redundancy at different steps along the reasoning chain. The introduction of early stopping mechanism based on answer-level convergence en-sures thorough reasoning while further enhancing output conciseness. Moreover, by incorporating the reward differences into the DPO optimization objective, STACK achieves a superior balance be-tween accuracy and reasoning length, attaining a state-of-the-art performance across different mod-els and datasets. 

## 6 Limitations 

Despite the effectiveness of STACK in dynami-cally compressing reasoning chains and leverag-ing retrieval-augmented guidance, several limita-tions remain. First, the incorporation of online contrastive sampling and retrieval-based strategies introduces additional computational overhead, lead-ing to increased training time and resource con-sumption. Second, the current retrieval mecha-nism does not guarantee fully accurate or rele-vant external knowledge, which may propagate suboptimal guidance during reasoning; joint opti-mization of the retrieval component and the model could further enhance performance. Finally, our framework currently relies solely on textual knowl-edge for external augmentation, while other modal-ities or external reasoning tools, such as structured databases, symbolic solvers, or multi-modal knowl-edge sources, represent promising directions for further extension and improvement.

## A Training Detail 

We train our models on the DeepScaleR dataset (Luo et al., 2025b), which comprises 40,000 mathematical problems sourced from AIME (1983–2023), AMC, Omni-Math (Gao et al., 2024), and STILL (Min et al., 2024). In addition, con-sidering that LRMs are not inherently equipped to generate short Chains of Thought (Short CoT) or perform knowledge-guided logical reasoning, di-rectly applying DPO to an unadapted model would require the new policy πθ to simultaneously learn the two challenging objectives of knowledge uti-lization and logical compression. This often results in a substantial divergence from the reference pol-icy πref , leading to KL divergence explosion and training instability. To address this, we introduce a knowledge-guided cold-start stage designed to pre-align the reference policy distribution. 

Data Construction & Mixing Strategy. Dur-ing the cold-start phase, we perform supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on the base model using a 5,000 sample dataset, composed of two parts mixed at an 80:20 ratio: 1. Task-Specific Data (80%). To enable the model to acquire compressed reasoning pat-terns conditioned on retrieved knowledge, we construct pairs of the form (x, y teacher ), where the input x = [ Q, I ] consists of the question and retrieval context, and the output yteacher 

is a high-quality, knowledge-integrated, com-pressed Chain of Thought generated by a teacher model (GPT-4o). 2. General Regularization Data (20%). To pre-vent catastrophic forgetting caused by over-fitting to specific compression patterns, we incorporate general QA and reasoning data (e.g., standard CoT), preserving the model’s fundamental language and logical generaliza-tion capabilities. The primary objective of this phase is to con-struct a high-quality reference policy πref . By pre-adapting πref to the conditional distribution P (y | Q, I ), we constrain the exploration space in the subsequent reinforcement learning phase from unguided trial-and-error to knowledge-grounded refinement, ensuring that the KL divergence con-straint acts as a benign “language competence safe-guard” rather than a barrier to effective knowledge injection. 

## B Analysis of Hesitation State 

Due to the inherently iterative and self-reflective na-ture of long chain-of-thought reasoning, reflection tokens appear with high frequency across reasoning steps. Figure 5 visualizes the corresponding distri-butions of token-level and step-level information entropy, illustrating the variation in model uncer-tainty throughout the reasoning process. Compared to single reflection token-based detec-tion, our local-entropy-based method significantly reduces unnecessary knowledge retrievals while maintaining or slightly improving reasoning accu-racy. Specifically, as shown in figure 6, across long reasoning chains, entropy-based detection triggers only 3 retrievals per chain on average, achieving 82.2% accuracy with a mean response length of 3,190 tokens. In contrast, relying on individual reflection tokens leads to 7 retrievals per chain, with slightly lower accuracy (81.5%) and longer responses averaging 3,650 tokens. In our opinion, this seemingly counterintuitive result arises because excessive external knowledge retrieval does not necessarily improve reasoning outcomes and can even degrade DPO training per-formance. Frequent retrievals introduce redun-dant or low-relevance information, which increases context length and disperses the model’s attention across less critical tokens. During DPO optimiza-tion, the model is guided by reward differences be-tween compressed and uncompressed chains; if the context is filled with repetitive or non-informative knowledge, the reward signal for effective com-pression becomes noisy. Consequently, the policy may struggle to correctly attribute which reasoning steps are truly valuable, leading to reduced accu-racy and less efficient chain compression. By con-trast, local-entropy-based detection selectively in-troduces external knowledge only when the model exhibits genuine hesitation, ensuring that retrieved evidence is relevant and timely. This targeted ap-proach not only preserves reasoning fidelity but also stabilizes the training signal, resulting in more accurate and compact reasoning chains.
