## Abstract 

Reinforcement learning (RL) for LLM post-training faces a fundamental design choice: whether to use a learned critic as a baseline for policy optimization. Classical theory favors critic-based methods such as PPO for variance reduction, yet critic-free alternatives like GRPO have gained widespread adoption due to their simplicity and competitive performance. We show that in sparse-reward settings, a learned critic can inject estimation noise that exceeds the state signal it captures, increasing rather than reducing advantage variance. By casting baseline selection as a Kalman filtering problem, we unify PPO and GRPO as two extremes of the Kalman gain and prove that explained variance (EV), computable from a single training batch, identifies the exact boundary: positive EV indicates the critic reduces variance, while zero or negative EV signals that it inflates variance. Building on this insight, we propose Explained Variance Policy Optimization (EVPO ), which monitors batch-level EV at each training step and adaptively switches between critic-based and batch-mean advantage estimation, provably achieving no greater variance than the better of the two at every step. Across four tasks spanning classical control, agentic interaction, and mathematical reasoning, EVPO consistently outperforms both PPO and GRPO regardless of which fixed baseline is stronger on a given task. Further analysis confirms that the adaptive gating tracks critic maturation over training and that the theoretically derived zero threshold is empirically optimal. q

## 1 Introduction 

Reinforcement learning (RL) has become a central paradigm for LLM post-training, driving advances in mathematical reasoning [ 10 , 27 , 21 , 33 ], instruction following [ 37 , 6, 8 ], and interactive decision-making [ 26 , 31 , 3]. A prominent instantiation is reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards 

(RLVR) [ 10 ], where a sparse reward is assigned only at the terminal step via an automatic verifier. A fundamental algorithmic choice in this setting is whether to employ a critic (a learned value function) as a baseline for policy optimization. Classical RL theory holds that a critic reduces policy-gradient variance by providing a state-dependent baseline [ 28 , 24 ], forming the foundation of actor-critic methods including PPO [ 23 ]. In practice, however, critic-free methods such as GRPO [ 25 , 10 ] have gained widespread adoption, as they are cheaper and can match or even surpass PPO [ 11 ]. Rather than treating the critic as a binary design choice, we ask: 

In sparse-reward LLM post-training, when does a learned critic help and when does it hurt? 

To answer this, we identify three progressive sub-questions. Q1: Is the boundary observable? Critic quality fluctuates during training, and we need to measure its impact on policy optimization in a controlled setting. Q2: Where exactly is the boundary? In sparse-reward LLM generation, the critic may inject more estimation noise than the state signal it captures. We need a precise characterization of when this happens. Q3: Can this boundary be exploited online? If the helpful-vs-harmful regime can be detected in real time, we can build an algorithm that adapts rather than committing to a fixed choice. We adopt explained variance (EV), the fraction of return variance accounted for by the critic, as a real-time proxy for critic usefulness. Empirically ( Q1), EV remains negative during the early stage of training, and controlled interventions (suppressing the critic or injecting noise) confirm that performance degrades once EV falls to zero or below, suggesting that its sign reliably indicates whether the critic helps or hurts (Section 2.2). To pinpoint the boundary ( Q2), we cast baseline selection as a Kalman filtering problem [ 14 ] in which the critic and the batch-mean return are two competing estimators of the true state value, with PPO and GRPO corresponding to the two extremes of the Kalman gain (Section 2.3). We prove that the sign of EV, computable from a single batch, is sufficient to determine which extreme is preferable (Theorem 1), placing the boundary at EV = 0 .Exploiting this boundary online ( Q3), we propose Explained Variance Policy Optimization (EVPO) , which implements this adaptive hard gating at each training step. EVPO maintains a standard PPO-style critic alongside the policy, and at each iteration computes batch-level EV as a scalar indicator. When EV is positive, the critic captures meaningful state-dependent signal, and EVPO uses its predictions as the baseline (the PPO mode); when EV falls to zero or below, EVPO discards the critic and falls back to the batch-mean return (the GRPO mode). We prove that this switching rule yields advantage-estimator variance no greater than the better of PPO and GRPO at every step, so EVPO never underperforms either fixed choice. We evaluate EVPO on four tasks spanning classical control, interactive decision-making, and math-ematical reasoning. Across all tasks, EVPO achieves the best final performance and maintains its advantage throughout training, regardless of whether PPO or GRPO is the stronger fixed baseline on a given task. Further analysis confirms that the adaptive gating tracks critic maturation over training and that the theoretically motivated zero threshold is empirically optimal. Our main contributions are: 1. Indicator and theory. We unify PPO and GRPO as two extremes of a Kalman baseline-selection spectrum and prove that the sign of explained variance is the exact boundary separating the variance-reducing from the variance-inflating critic regime (Theorem 1). 2. Algorithm. We propose EVPO, a lightweight method that switches between critic-based and critic-free advantage estimation via batch-level EV, with a provable guarantee of no greater variance than the better of PPO and GRPO at every step. 3. Empirical validation. EVPO consistently outperforms fixed PPO and GRPO across four tasks spanning classical control, agentic interaction, and mathematical reasoning. Analysis experiments confirm that the zero threshold is empirically optimal and that the adaptive gating reflects critic maturation across training stages. 22 Preliminaries

2.1 RL for LLM Post-Training 

We model autoregressive text generation as a token-level Markov Decision Process [ 18 , 29 , 36 ]. Given a prompt x, the policy πθ generates a response y = ( y1, . . . , y T ) token by token, where the state at step t is st = ( x, y <t ) and the action is at = yt ∼ πθ (· | st).In the reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR) setting [ 10 , 31 ], a scalar reward is assigned only at the terminal step: rT = R(x, y ), rt = 0 for t < T . With discount factor γ = 1 ,the return from any state equals the terminal reward: Gt = rT ≜ G. The state-value function is 

V π (s) = Eπ [G | st = s], and the observed return G is a stochastic realization whose variance arises solely from the randomness of future actions under π.

In the following subsection, we formalize this intuition through a Kalman filtering lens. 

2.3 A Kalman Filtering Perspective on Baseline Selection 

To precisely locate the boundary ( Q2), we observe that choosing a baseline is fundamentally a 

state-estimation problem: both the critic ˆVϕ(s) and the batch mean ¯GB are noisy estimates of the true value V π (s), and the best baseline is the one with lower estimation error. This is exactly the setting addressed by the Kalman filter [ 14 , 19 ], which optimally fuses two noisy estimates by weighting each inversely to its error variance. 

Setup. The observed return decomposes as G = V π (s)+ ϵ with sampling noise R ≜ E[Var( G | s)] .The two competing baselines have distinct error structures: (i) the critic ˆVϕ(s) = V π (s) + δ(s)

with estimation error variance PA ≜ Var( δ), and (ii) the batch mean ¯GB whose deviation from any specific state value has variance PB ≜ Var s(V π (s)) . The minimum-variance linear combination of these two estimators assigns the critic a weight of 1−K∗ with the optimal gain K∗ = PA/(PA +PB )

(see Appendix A.1 for the full derivation). 

Connection to explained variance. The EV metric (Eq. (3) ) directly encodes the relative reliability of the two estimators. Substituting the variance decomposition into the definition of EV yields: 

ev = PB − PA

PB + R . (4) 4Since the denominator PB + R is always positive, the sign of EV is determined solely by PB − PA.This means ev = 0 marks the exact boundary where PA = PB , i.e., where the critic’s estimation noise equals the state signal it captures. Above this boundary ( ev > 0), the critic outperforms the batch mean; below it ( ev ≤ 0), critic noise dominates. The sampling noise R compresses the magnitude of EV but preserves its sign, keeping this boundary robust under high stochasticity. 

## 3 Methodology 

Building on the Kalman filtering framework of Section 2.3, we now formalize when a critic helps or hurts (Section 3.1), unify PPO and GRPO as two extremes of the Kalman gain (Section 3.2), and derive the EVPO algorithm (Section 3.3). 

3.1 The Performance Collapse Boundary 

From Eq. (4), the sign of EV is governed by PB − PA. We formalize this as: 

Theorem 1 (Performance Collapse Boundary) . Let ˆVϕ(s) = V π (s) + δ(s) with δ ⊥ ϵ, and let 

PA = Var( δ), PB = Var s(V π (s)) , K = PA/(PA + PB ). Then the following are equivalent: 

ev ≤ 0 ⇐⇒ PA ≥ PB ⇐⇒ K ≥ 12 . (5) In words, the critic inflates advantage variance whenever its estimation noise ( PA) exceeds the state signal it captures ( PB ), and ev = 0 is the exact tipping point. Since the sign of EV is computable from a single batch, this boundary is directly observable during training. 

Computing EV from a training batch. Replacing the population variances in Eq. (3) with sample variances over a batch of M rollouts B = {(ym, G m)}Mm=1 from the same prompt gives the batch-level EV: 

bev B = 1 − Var B(Gm − ˆVϕ(sm)) Var B(Gm) , (6) computed from the critic values already obtained for advantage estimation, with no additional forward passes. 

3.2 PPO and GRPO as Kalman Extremes 

Under RLVR conditions ( γ=λ=1 , terminal-only reward), PPO and GRPO can be unified into the form ˆAt = G − b(st), differing only in the choice of baseline b(st) (see Appendix A.2 for the detailed derivation). This difference maps directly onto the Kalman gain K:• PPO (critic mode, K=0 , full critic trust): b(st) = ˆVϕ(st). The baseline is entirely model-driven; the Kalman filter places zero weight on the evidence-driven estimator. • GRPO (batch-mean mode, K=1 , no critic trust): b(st) = ¯GB. (Since 1/σ (GB) does not affect the gradient direction.) The baseline is entirely evidence-driven; the critic is discarded and the batch mean serves as a constant baseline for all tokens. In other words, PPO and GRPO are not fundamentally different algorithms but rather two extreme operating points of the same Kalman baseline-selection spectrum. PPO implicitly assumes PA ≪ PB

(the critic is always reliable), while GRPO implicitly assumes PA ≫ PB (the critic is never worth using). Neither assumption holds uniformly across training, motivating an adaptive approach.

## 4 Experiments 

Our experiments aim to validate that the per-step variance reduction of EVPO (Eq. 9) translates into consistent performance gains over fixed baselines (Section 4.2). We further analyze whether EV serves as a reliable indicator of the PA-vs-PB balance throughout training (Section 4.3) and whether the theoretically derived collapse boundary ev = 0 (Theorem 1) coincides with the empirically optimal switching threshold (Section 4.4).

## 5 Related Work 

Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards for LLMs. RL-based LLM post-training has evolved from actor-critic architectures such as PPO [ 23 ], which rely on a learned value function for variance reduction via GAE [ 24 ], toward critic-free alternatives that bypass the value network entirely. GRPO [ 25 , 10 , 30 ] replaces the learned baseline with intra-group reward normalization and has become the dominant paradigm; REINFORCE++ [ 11 ] further stabilizes this family via global batch normalization, and similar estimators like RLOO [ 1] and DAPO [ 35 ] further demonstrate that a critic is often dispensable for single-turn, verifiable tasks. Yet critic-free methods face two systematic challenges: group-relative advantages introduce statistical bias, systematically underestimating hard prompts and overestimating easy ones [ 12 , 16 ], and uniform trajectory-level credit assignment degrades severely in long-horizon settings lacking early termination [ 4, 7], compelling multi-turn frameworks such as RAGEN [ 31 ] to reintroduce the critic for stable training. All existing approaches resolve this tension between critic-free simplicity and critic-based credit assignment [ 15 ] through a static, a priori architectural commitment that either always uses or always discards the critic, regardless of its current predictive quality. 

Adaptive Baselines and Explained Variance. Reducing policy-gradient variance without detri-mental bias is a foundational RL challenge [ 32 , 28 ]. State-dependent baselines are theoretically unbiased but rely on the assumption that the value network is accurate; in LLM post-training, the vast state space and sparse rewards frequently violate this assumption, causing the baseline subtraction to inject more noise than it removes. Prior work addresses this through two complementary strategies: redesigning the baseline structure itself [ 2 ], or using critic quality as a signal to adapt hyperparameters such as λ in GAE [17] or to filter uninformative transitions [9]. However, these methods treat critic quality as a signal to tune hyperparameters or curate data while preserving the actor-critic architecture. EVPO departs from both strategies by elevating EV from a passive diagnostic to a architecture selection criterion that determines whether the critic is used at all, switching to critic-free estimation when the value network becomes mathematically harmful. 

## 6 Conclusion 

We presented EVPO, an adaptive RL method for LLM post-training that uses explained variance to switch between critic-based and batch-mean advantage estimation at each training step. By casting baseline selection as a Kalman filtering problem, we proved that the sign of EV is the exact boundary separating the variance-reducing from the variance-inflating critic regime (Theorem 1), and that EVPO’s per-step switching yields advantage variance no greater than the better of PPO and GRPO at every step. Across four tasks spanning multi-turn planning, agentic interaction, and mathematical reasoning, EVPO consistently outperforms both fixed baselines, regardless of which one is stronger on a given task. The gating analysis confirms that EVPO’s adaptation tracks critic maturation over training, and the threshold sensitivity study validates that the theoretically derived zero boundary is empirically optimal. 9Limitations 

Population vs. batch EV. Theorem 1 characterizes the collapse boundary in terms of population EV, whereas EVPO switches on batch EV computed from finite samples. We do not provide finite-sample concentration bounds for the switching rule, though the threshold sensitivity analysis (Section 4.4) suggests that zero is a robust practical threshold. 

Resource overhead. EVPO maintains a critic network throughout training, including iterations where the batch-mean mode is selected, and therefore retains the memory footprint of PPO rather than matching fully critic-free methods. 

Task coverage. Our evaluation spans four tasks across three domains. Tasks with dense intermediate rewards, learned reward models, or substantially different rollout lengths may exhibit different EV dynamics.

A.1 Detailed Kalman Filtering Analogy 

We provide here the full state estimation analogy and derivation that motivates the EV-based switching criterion introduced in Section 2.3. 

State estimation analogy. We cast baseline construction as a scalar state estimation problem. The hidden state to be estimated is the true value function x = V π (s). For each sample in a training batch, the observed return decomposes as 

G = V π (s) + ϵ, ϵ ∼ (0 , R ), R ≜ E[Var( G | s)] , (10) where R is the irreducible sampling noise arising from the stochasticity of future actions under π.Two competing estimators of V π (s) are available at each training step: 15 • Model-driven prediction (Critic). ˆxA = ˆVϕ(s) = V π (s) + δ(s), where δ(s) denotes the critic’s estimation error with variance PA ≜ Var( δ). This parallels the state prediction in a Kalman filter: it exploits learned state features but carries modelling noise whose magnitude depends on the critic’s current training quality. • Evidence-driven observation (Batch mean). ˆxB = ¯GB, the empirical mean return over the batch, which approximates E[V π (s)] . Its error relative to any specific state s has variance PB ≜ Var s(V π (s)) , paralleling the observation noise in a Kalman filter: it reflects how much true state values vary within the batch, and is large when the batch covers heterogeneous states. The sampling noise R affects both estimators symmetrically and controls the sensitivity of the EV diagnostic without altering its sign .

Optimal fusion and the Kalman gain. The Kalman filter combines the two estimators via a linear combination b(s) = (1 − K) ˆ xA + K ˆxB that minimizes the mean squared error E[( b(s) − V π (s)) 2].The optimal Kalman gain is:

## B Tasks 

We evaluate on four tasks spanning multi-turn grid-world control, interactive web agents, and single-turn mathematical reasoning. All training code is based on the RAGEN2 framework [ 31 ], which wraps 

verl for PPO-style LLM post-training and provides multi-turn agent environments. 16 Sokoban. We use the CoordSokoban variant of the RAGEN2 Sokoban environment [ 13 ]. Each episode instantiates a 6 × 6 grid with one box and one target, procedurally generated from an envi-ronment seed. The observation at each step is a symbol grid together with zero-indexed coordinates of the player, the box, and the target. The action space is {UP, DOWN , LEFT , RIGHT }; the policy is prompted to emit an action sequence of length up to two per turn, separated by “ || ” and enclosed in 

<answer>...</answer> tags. A trajectory consists of up to 10 executed actions across up to 5 turns. The reward is terminal and binary: +1 if all boxes end on targets, 0 otherwise, with an additional format penalty of −0.1 for malformed outputs. 

FrozenLake. We use the CoordFrozenLake variant wrapping the Gymnasium FrozenLake-v1 

environment [ 5]. The map is a 4 × 4 grid with randomly placed holes, the start tile, and the goal tile; the map layout is resampled per environment seed. Transitions are stochastic ( is_slippery=True ): the commanded action succeeds with probability 1/3, and with probability 2/3 the agent slips perpendicular to the intended direction. The observation is again a symbol grid plus coordinates of start, goal, player, and all holes. The action space is {LEFT , DOWN , RIGHT , UP}, with up to 10 

actions per trajectory across up to 5 turns. The reward is terminal and binary: +1 if the player reaches the goal, 0 if the player falls into a hole or exhausts the action budget; a format penalty of −0.1 is applied for malformed outputs. 

WebShop. WebShop [ 34 ] is an interactive web-navigation task in which the agent must locate and purchase a product matching a natural-language instruction. We use the webshop-minimal fork with the small product catalogue shipped with RAGEN2. At each turn the agent observes the current page (search box, product list, or product detail) together with the list of clickable elements, and produces exactly one action drawn from search[ keywords ] or click[ element ], again wrapped in <answer>...</answer> . A trajectory consists of up to 9 actions across up to 9 turns. The reward is the built-in WebShop matching score in [0 , 1] , delivered only at the terminal click[buy now] .

MATH. We use DAPO-Math-17k [ 35 ] (17,267 competition-level problems) for both training and validation: the full training set is used for RL updates, and a fixed subset of 32 problems , held out from training, is used for evaluation. Each problem is a single-turn reasoning task ( max_turn=1 ): the policy is prompted with the problem statement and must emit a <think>...</think> block followed by the final numerical answer inside <answer>...</answer> . The reward is terminal and binary: +1 if the final answer matches the ground truth (after normalization), 0 otherwise. 

## C Baselines and Hyperparameters 

We compare EVPO against a Base-LLM reference and four RL baselines that span both critic-based and critic-free families. • Base LLM. Instruction-tuned Qwen2.5 without any RL post-training, prompted zero-shot with the same task template as the RL runs. • PPO [ 23 ]. Standard clipped-surrogate PPO with GAE advantages. Under our RLVR setting (γ=λ=1 , terminal-only reward) this reduces to the PPO advantage of Eq. (1) with a learned, state-dependent critic. • StarPO-S [ 31 ]. A multi-turn PPO variant introduced by RAGEN that filters the lowest-variance rollouts before each update (rollout_filter_ratio = 0.25 ,

rollout_filter_metric =reward_variance) to stabilize long-horizon agentic training. • GRPO [25 ]. Critic-free; the baseline is the response-level batch mean, normalized by the batch standard deviation. • DAPO [ 35 ]. A modern critic-free variant built on GRPO with (i) dynamic sam-pling that discards groups whose rewards are all identical and resamples up to 

max_num_gen_batches =5 times until enough informative groups are collected; (ii) 

clip-higher asymmetry (εlow , ε high ) = (0 .2, 0.28) ; (iii) token-level loss aggregation (loss_agg_mode =token-mean); and (iv) no std normalization of advantages and no in-reward KL term.
