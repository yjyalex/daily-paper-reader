Abstract 

Agentic retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems enable large language models (LLMs) to solve complex tasks through multi-step in-teraction with external retrieval tools. How-ever, such multi-step interaction often involves redundant search steps, incurring substantial computational cost and latency. Prior work limits search depth (i.e., the number of search steps) to reduce cost, but this often leads to underexploration of complex questions. To ad-dress this, we first investigate how search depth affects accuracy and find a minimal sufficient search depth that defines an accuracy-efficiency trade-off, jointly determined by question com-plexity and the agent’s capability. Furthermore, we propose AutoSearch , a reinforcement learn-ing (RL) framework that evaluates each search step via self-generated intermediate answers. By a self-answering mechanism, AutoSearch identifies the minimal sufficient search depth and promotes efficient search by rewarding its attainment while penalizing over-searching. In addition, reward mechanisms are introduced to stabilize search behavior and improve an-swer quality on complex questions. Exten-sive experiments on multiple benchmarks show that AutoSearch achieves a superior accuracy-efficiency trade-off, alleviating over-searching while preserving search quality.

2 Related Work 

2.1 Agentic RAG System with RL 

Agentic RAG methods (Gao et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2024) have been proposed to enhance reasoning by integrating external knowledge retrieval. Motivated by the effectiveness of RL (Sun et al., 2026; Tu et al., 2025b; Sun et al., 2025a; Lu et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2025), recent studies (Guo et al., 2025; Jaech et al., 2024) employ RL to train agents to strategi-cally manage retrieval. Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025) utilizes an RL framework with outcome-based re-wards, enabling LLMs to interleave self-reasoning with real-time search. R1-Searcher (Song et al., 2025a) proposes a two-stage RL method to improve the reasoning process for answer generation. How-ever, these RL-based approaches struggle with com-plex tasks due to sparse reward signals. To mitigate this, recent works use process rewards to provide informative feedback in RL. StepSearch (Zheng et al., 2025) introduces step-wise rewards that leverage information gain and penalize redundancy. Atom-Searcher (Deng et al., 2025) further decom-poses reasoning into atomic thoughts and provides atomic-level process rewards. Although prior ap-proaches improve search performance, they over-look the issue of over-searching, leading to high computational cost. 

2.2 Efficient Agentic RAG 

To reduce redundant retrievals, recent studies de-sign reward functions that penalize excessive re-trieval. R1-Searcher++ (Song et al., 2025b) re-wards the use of internal knowledge and caches re-trieved content to prevent repetition, while another study (Wang et al., 2025) proposes a trajectory-level reward that compares the current number of tool calls with an estimated optimal number to improve tool efficiency. Other methods guide re-trieval based on uncertainty or external judgment. Search Wisely (Wu et al., 2025) uses a confidence threshold to reward high-certainty search decisions. ReARTeR (Sun et al., 2025b) employs a trustwor-thy process reward that combines a precise process reward model with an explanation-driven refine-ment model. HIPRAG (Wu et al., 2026) uses an external LLM to supervise the necessity of each search step. However, these approaches (Yue et al., 2026; Qian et al., 2025; Shen et al., 2024) ignore how search depth should vary with problem complexity and model capability. In contrast, our method dynamically adjusts search depth accord-ingly, achieving an accuracy-efficiency trade-off.

However, what is the appropriate search depth that balances accuracy and efficiency remains under-explored. To investigate this, we analyze the im-pact of search depth on answer accuracy and over-searching behavior. Specifically, we prompt the Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct model to perform a fixed number of search steps for a given question. Ex-periments are conducted on the single-hop QA dataset Natural Questions (NQ) and the multi-hop QA dataset Bamboogle. We measure answer accu-racy using Exact Match (EM) and define an over-searching ratio as the proportion of samples where the agent could answer correctly before the final search step. As shown in Fig.2(a), NQ reaches near-optimal performance with a single search step, while additional steps provide little benefit. In con-trast, Bamboogle requires up to three steps to peak, after which further searches reduce performance. Fig. 2(b) further shows that exceeding the near-optimal depth leads to a notable increase in the over-searching ratio for both datasets, with the effect being more pronounced on the more complex Ban-boogle dataset. These results indicate that the ap-propriate search depth is task-dependent, with a minimal sufficient depth achieves near-optimal accuracy while mitigating over-searching. 3.2 What Affects Appropriate Search Depth? 

Different LLMs exhibit varying reasoning and re-trieval capabilities, which in turn affect their answer accuracy in agentic RAG systems. This raises a natural question: does the agent’s capability also influence the appropriate search depth? To inves-tigate this, we conduct controlled experiments on the Bamboogle dataset using Qwen-3B, Qwen-7B, and Qwen-14B models, each performing a range of search steps to answer the same set of questions. As shown in Fig. 2(c), the 3B model steadily im-proves with increasing search depth, achieving its peak performance at the final search step within the evaluated range. In contrast, the 7B and 14B models reach their optimal performance at the sec-ond and third search steps, respectively, after which additional searches lead to performance degrada-tion. Fig. 2(d) further shows that over-searching becomes increasingly pronounced as search depth grows, with stronger models exhibiting more pro-nounced degradation. Taken together, these results from the previous and current subsections indicate that the minimal sufficient search depth, which balances accuracy and efficiency, is jointly deter-mined by question complexity and the agent’s capability .

4.1 Base reward 

We introduce a base reward to ensure stable multi-step retrieval and accurate answers, comprising a 

format reward to enforce structured actions and an 

outcome reward to encourage correct answers. The format reward enforces a structured action format that separates reasoning, retrieval, and final answer-ing. During retrieval steps, an action is considered invalid if it violates the prescribed format. Addi-tionally, repeated retrieval of previously observed information is also treated as invalid, discourag-ing redundant search behavior. At the final step, only the format of the produced answer is evalu-ated. Rather than assigning a single reward to the entire trajectory, we compute the validity of each step independently to enable fine-grained credit as-signment. Formally, for the i-th question at step t,we define a binary indicator valid i,t ∈ { 0, 1}, indi-cating whether the action at step t is well-formed and non-redundant. 4.2 Search Efficiency Reward 

To improve search efficiency while maintaining an-swer quality, we propose a search efficiency reward that adaptively determines search depth based on the agent’s capability. To ensure answer quality, we generate intermediate answers at each retrieval step to identify the capability-aware optimal search depth, defined as the minimal number of retrieval steps required for the agent to answer correctly. Based on this depth, we design a reward function that encourages the agent to reduce its search depth while penalizing redundant retrievals, thereby im-proving search efficiency.

Baselines We compare AutoSearch against sev-eral RL-based search baselines, including Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025), StepSearch (Zheng et al., 2025), and HiPRAG (Wu et al., 2026). 

Evaluation Metrics We assess answer quality using the canonical Exact Match (EM) and word-level F1 scores. To evaluate search cost and ef-ficiency, we report Search Depth (SD) , which records the number of search steps, and Search Efficiency (SE) (SE = EM /SD ), quantifying the accuracy gained per search step. We also intro-duce the Over-Searching Ratio (OSR) , the pro-portion of samples correctly answered before the final search step, to quantify over-searching. 

Training and Evaluation We conduct experi-ments on two models from the Qwen series: Qwen-2.5-3B-Base and Qwen-2.5-7B-Base. 5.2 Main Results AutoSearch Achieves Superior Performance. Ta-ble 1 reports the performance of AutoSearch and baseline methods across six QA datasets. Across both in-domain and out-of-domain settings, as well as single-hop and multi-hop QA tasks, AutoSearch consistently outperforms the baselines, achieving higher EM and F1 scores and demonstrating robust accuracy across diverse scenarios. 

AutoSearch Balances Accuracy and Efficiency. 

The search depth results show that Search-R1 and StepSearch achieve strong answer accuracy but rely on deeper searches, resulting in higher computational cost. In contrast, HiPRAG adopts fewer search depth on most tasks, which reduces search cost but leads to noticeably lower accuracy. Compared to baselines, AutoSearch consistently achieves higher accuracy with fewer search steps across most tasks, demonstrating balanced trade-off between answer accuracy and search efficiency. 

AutoSearch Adaptively Adjusts Search Depth. 

AutoSearch does not simply minimize search depth. On single-hop datasets, it maintains relatively shal-low searches, while on multi-hop datasets, it per-forms noticeably more search steps. The search efficiency metric shows that, compared to baseline methods, AutoSearch achieves higher efficiency across most datasets, indicating its ability to adap-tively adjust search depth based on question com-plexity and the agent’s capability. 

AutoSearch Effectively Suppresses Over-Searching. Table 2 shows that Search-R1 and StepSearch exhibit high over-searching ratios, particularly on multi-hop tasks, indicating substan-tial redundant searches in prior methods. While HiPRAG reduces over-searching by leveraging external models, this comes at the cost of lower answer accuracy. In contrast, AutoSearch effec-tively suppresses over-searching while maintaining strong performance, leveraging the agent’s own capability to adaptively control search depth.

6 Conclusion 

In this work, we propose AutoSearch , a RL frame-work that adaptively selects the search depth based on question complexity and the agent’s capability. By analyzing how search depth affects answer per-formance, we identify a minimal sufficient depth that balances accuracy and efficiency. AutoSearch leverages intermediate answers at each step to adap-tively reach this depth, ensuring efficient search without compromising accuracy. Further reward mechanisms are employed to stabilize the search process and improve answer quality on complex questions. Empirical results on multiple bench-marks demonstrate that AutoSearch achieves a fa-vorable accuracy-efficiency trade-off. Limitations 

Although AutoSearch achieves an accuracy-efficiency trade-off by adaptively selecting search depth, this study focuses on relatively low maxi-mum search steps. Investigating the balance be-tween search depth and accuracy under a broader range of search depths remains an interesting direc-tion for future work.

A.1 Agentic RAG System with RL 

Large language models (LLMs) face challenges such as outdated knowledge and factual inaccura-cies, making it difficult to rely on them for up-to-date or contextually correct responses. To mitigate these limitations, Agentic Retrieval-Augmented Generation (Agentic RAG) systems have emerged as an effective solution. These methods enhance the reasoning capabilities of LLMs by integrating external knowledge retrieval directly into their rea-soning process (Gao et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2024). While early works in this area (Trivedi et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2022; Shi et al., 2024)primarily relied on prompt engineering to enable agents to invoke search engines, they often struggled with ensuring that these agents could effectively use and interact with external tools. In contrast, recent studies have begun utiliz-ing reinforcement learning (RL) to train agents to strategically manage the retrieval process. For instance, Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025)introduces a reinforcement learning framework that enables LLMs to dynamically interleave self-reasoning with search, improving both the accuracy and ef-ficiency of responses. R1-Searcher (Song et al., 2025a)proposes a two-stage RL method, where the first stage trains the agent to use external re-trieval effectively, and the second stage integrates retrieval seamlessly into the reasoning process for more accurate answers. While these methods have demonstrated success in simpler tasks, they face limitations when applied to complex, multi-hop question answering (QA) tasks, due to sparse and delayed reward signals. This often leads to ineffi-ciencies in utilizing the retrieved knowledge and may negatively impact performance on more chal-lenging queries. 

A.2 Advancements in RL-Based Agentic RAG Systems 

Building upon the initial successes of Agentic RAG systems, recent studies have introduced novel methods to improve the performance of RL-based agents. To address the issue of sparse rewards, which can hinder the agent’s learning process, sev-eral approaches have been proposed. For exam-ple, StepSearch (Zheng et al., 2025)introduces step-wise rewards that aim to provide more infor-mative feedback by considering information gain and penalizing redundant retrieval steps. Atom-Searcher (Deng et al., 2025)takes this further by decomposing reasoning tasks into smaller, atomic thoughts, enabling more detailed process rewards at the atomic level. This approach enhances the agent’s ability to reason more efficiently. De-spite these improvements, RL-based approaches often struggle with balancing the trade-off between search cost and reasoning quality, particularly when dealing with complex, multi-hop tasks where the retrieval process may need to be more dynamic and adaptive. 

A.3 Efficient Agentic RAG Systems 

To address the problem of inefficient search be-havior, several studies have focused on designing reward functions that can reduce unnecessary re-trievals. R1-Searcher++ (Song et al., 2025b)offers a solution by rewarding the effective use of in-ternal knowledge and memorizing previously re-trieved content to avoid repetition. This helps re-duce the need for repeated searches, improving efficiency. Similarly, studies such as (Wang et al., 2025)propose trajectory-level rewards that com-pare the number of retrieval steps taken by the agent to an estimated optimal number, further improving search efficiency. Other methods, such as Search Wisely (Wu et al., 2025), guide retrieval decisions based on the agent’s confidence, rewarding high-certainty search actions. While these techniques reduce redundant retrieval, they often overlook the dynamic nature of retrieval depth, which should vary depending on task complexity and the capabil-ities of the model. In contrast, our method dynami-cally adjusts the retrieval depth, which allows it to better manage trade-offs between performance and efficiency.

G Case Study 

To demonstrate the effectiveness and efficiency of AutoSearch, we present qualitative analyses across five representative cases, comparing its reasoning and retrieval behavior against three strong base-lines: Search-R1, StepSearch, and HiPRAG.
