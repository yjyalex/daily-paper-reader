# Abstract 

Tabular data remains prevalent in high-stakes do-mains such as healthcare and finance, where pre-dictive models are expected to provide both high accuracy and faithful, human-understandable rea-soning. While symbolic models offer verifiable logic, they lack semantic expressiveness. Mean-while, general-purpose LLMs often require spe-cialized fine-tuning to master domain-specific tab-ular reasoning. To address the dual challenges of scalable data curation and reasoning consistency, we propose ReSS , a systematic framework that bridges symbolic and neural reasoning models. ReSS leverages a decision-tree model to extract instance-level decision paths as symbolic scaf-folds. These scaffolds, alongside input features and labels, guide an LLM to generate grounded natural-language reasoning that strictly adheres to the underlying decision logic. The resulting high-quality dataset is used to fine-tune a pre-trained LLM into a specialized tabular reasoning model, further enhanced by a scaffold-invariant data augmentation strategy to improve general-ization and explainability. To rigorously assess faithfulness, we introduce quantitative metrics in-cluding hallucination rate, explanation necessity, and explanation sufficiency. Experimental results on medical and financial benchmarks demonstrate that ReSS-trained models improve traditional de-cision trees and standard fine-tuning approaches up to 10% while producing faithful and consistent reasoning.

There are two key challenges to be addressed: (i) how to curate high-quality reasoning data for fine-tuning a LLM; and (ii) how to ensure that the reasoning traces generated by the learned model are both faithful and explainable. Cu-rating high-quality reasoning data for high-stakes domains such as healthcare and finance typically requires domain experts and is therefore expensive, time-consuming, and difficult to scale. Moreover, while current LLMs are capa-ble of producing chain-of-thought (CoT)-like outputs, these explanations are not guaranteed to be faithful and causal to the tabular data prediction (Kambhampati et al., 2025; Barez et al., 2025). To address these challenges, we propose ReSS , a system-atic framework that curates high-quality reasoning data via symbolic scaffolds and trains efficient reasoning models for tabular prediction. ReSS first trains a decision tree on tabular data and extracts instance-level decision paths from sam-ples that are correctly predicted by the decision tree, which serve as explicit symbolic scaffolds capturing the model’s underlying decision logic. These scaffolds, together with input features and labels, are used to guide an LLM to gener-ate grounded, step-by-step natural-language reasoning that strictly adheres to the logical constraints encoded in the deci-sion paths, yielding a reasoning dataset that is both faithful to the original decision process and interpretable by hu-mans. To further enhance generalization and explainability, ReSS incorporates a scaffold-invariant data augmentation strategy that expands the reasoning dataset and introduces out-of-distribution examples by perturbing input features while preserving the symbolic decision paths and labels. The results demonstrate the effectiveness of ReSS and the scaffold-invariant data augmentation, and validate the faithfulness and causal impact of the generated rationales. 

# 2. Related work 

While tabular data prediction has long been dominated by tree-based ensemble models, recent years have seen the emergence of new approaches based on DL and LLMs. 

Deep Learning Methods for Tabular Prediction. Early DL approaches for tabular prediction typically rely on generic architectures such as MLPs, which lack induc-tive biases aligned with the structure of tabular decision manifolds and often struggle to match the performance of tree-based methods (Arik & Pfister, 2020). To overcome this limitation, subsequent work introduced tabular-specific inductive biases into neural architectures. TabNet (Arik & Pfister, 2020) injects inductive bias through sequential, instance-wise feature selection, enabling competitive per-formance with tree-boosting models on a wide range of benchmarks. DNF-Net (Abutbul et al., 2020) incorporates a logical inductive bias by aligning model structure with dis-junctive normal form, encouraging localized and rule-like decision behaviors. TabTransformer (Huang et al., 2020) leverages self-attention to model complex feature interac-tions, demonstrating that transformer-based architectures can match or surpass gradient-boosted trees in tabular pre-diction tasks. TabPFN (Hollmann et al., 2023) formulates tabular prediction as in-context inference with a prior-data fitted transformer network and achieves competitive or supe-rior performance on small to medium-sized datasets. Inter-preTabNet (Si et al., 2024) attempts to provide intrinsic in-terpretability through instance-wise feature selection mech-anisms, by employing an LLM to map from the learned fea-ture mask onto natural language text describing the learned signal. Despite these advances, they do not aim to produce explicit, human-understandable reasoning processes. 

LLMs for Tabular Prediction. Recent work has explored applying LLMs to tabular prediction by serializing tab-ular records into natural-language descriptions, allowing LLMs to operate in their native textual space. Depending on whether model parameters are updated, these approaches can be broadly categorized into inference-only and fine-tuning–based methods. TABLET (Slack & Singh, 2023) studies inference-only prediction by prompting pretrained LLMs such as Flan-T5 (Chung et al., 2022), GPT-J (Black et al., 2022), and ChatGPT, but reports that simple tree-based baselines consistently outperform LLMs under com-parable settings. Fine-tuning–based approaches, including LIFT (Dinh et al., 2022) and TabLLM (Hegselmann et al., 2023), demonstrate that language pretraining and mean-ingful feature semantics can improve tabular prediction, particularly in low-shot regimes. However, these gains are inconsistent across datasets, and overall performance still struggles to reliably surpass strong tree-based models. over, most existing methods focus solely on final predictions and do not generate explicit reasoning. 

Large Reasoning Models. Recently, Large Reasoning Mod-els (LRMs) trained by RL have demonstrated strong capabil-ities in solving complex tasks (Guo et al., 2025; Team et al., 2025), positioning them as a promising paradigm for tabular prediction. Beyond generating final predictions, LRMs can produce explicit reasoning chains. Many RL algorithms have been developed including, GRPO (Guo et al., 2025), REINFORCE++ (Hu et al., 2025), DisCO (Li et al., 2025), to name just a few. Two recent works, including TabRea-son (Xu et al., 2025) and TabR1 (Cai et al., 2025) have applied RL directly to a base model for tabular prediction. However, reasoning capabilities of existing LRMs do not readily transfer to tabular reasoning, which requires both semantic and numerical understanding (Cai et al., 2025). As a result, directly generating reasoning traces from existing LRMs without fine-tuning on the tabular reasoning data may lead to hallucinations, undermining the faithfulness and trustworthiness of the outputs. Werling et al. (2025) considered medical question-answering tasks and proposed an approach for curating reasoning traces from tabular data by constructing contrastive triplets of an example including the sample itself, the nearest samples with the same and dif-ferent labels, and prompting an generalist LLM to transform the triplets into contrastive decision processes, which are then used to supervise fine-tuning of a target LLM. 

Faithfulness of Reasoning Models. The reasoning traces generated from LRMs, although provide explicit explana-tion for the final prediction, could be unfaithful (Turpin et al., 2023; Arcuschin et al., 2025; Barez et al., 2025). These works show that the reasoning chains of a model can be mis-leading or suffer from subtle biases that are not reflected in the traces, hampering the trustworthiness of the prediction. Thus, to ensure that the answer is trustworthy, evaluating the faithfulness of model reasoning explanation is imperative for any task, including tabular prediction. An important quality of a faithful reasoning is consistency, where impor-tant sentences or features in the reasoning trace have strong causal impact (Paul et al., 2024; Atanasova et al., 2023) and replacing them with their counterfactual version should greatly affect the output of the model. However, existing works that use LRMs in tabular prediction (Xu et al., 2025; Cai et al., 2025; Werling et al., 2025) only focus on evalu-ating prediction accuracy, lacking a comprehensive test to quantify the faithfulness quality of their models’ reasoning.

3.1. Notations and Baselines 

We consider classification problems on tabular data. Each sample can be represented by a tuple (x, y ), where x is a feature vector x = ( x1, . . . , x d)⊤ ∈ Rd and y ∈ Y is the class label. The features in x may be numerical, ordinal, or categorical. Given an example x, our goal is to fine-tune an LLM that given input features x predicts the label y,along with a reasoning trace z = ( z1, . . . , zk) to support and explain the final prediction, where each zk represents a reasoning step consisting of a sequence of reasoning tokens. To input each tabular row into an LLM, we serialize the feature vector x into a sequence of natural-language tokens. 3.2. Using Decision Tree Paths as Symbolic Scaffolds Motivation of Using Symbolic Scaffolds. A consequence of the direct curation approach discussed above is that the generated rationale may contain many non-useful features. An example given in Figure 9 shows that the rationale gen-erated by ChatGPT could iterate through all eight features on the diabetes dataset. To explain its limitation and moti-vate the proposed approach, we consider a simple example below. Let us consider the input data x ∈ { 1, −1}2d, which contains a sequence of {1, −1} tokens. Suppose the y is given by y = Qdi=1 xP (i), where P is a fixed (but unknown) permutation of {1, . . . , 2d}. Hence, y is just the parity of d

fixed tokens in x. The existing work (Wies et al., 2023) has shown that if we directly learn a model πw(y|x) to predict 

y given x, the learning requires an exponential number of samples in the order of O(exp( d)) . Hence, it is impossible to learn the correct model for predicting y given x with limited data. The challenge of the problems lies that finding the correct permutation P out of all permutations is expo-nentially hard. However, if we tell the learner that the final prediction y only relies on the features in xP (1) , . . . , x P (d),it could dramatically simplify the learning process. This motivates the proposed approach of using symbolic scaffolds to guide the feature selection and decision process. A symbolic scaffold refers to an explicit external structure such as rules, diagrams, decision trees, or step-by-step proce-dures that humans use to organize and guide their reasoning processes. In developmental psychology (Vygotsky, 1978), symbolic scaffolds are known to support problem solving by breaking complex tasks into interpretable intermediate steps, thereby reducing cognitive load and enabling systematic in-ference. Rather than replacing reasoning, these symbolic aids shape how reasoning unfolds by constraining the space of possible actions and making intermediate states explicit. It is important to note that the concept of a symbolic scaf-fold differs from that of chain-of-thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022) in reasoning models. CoT concerns how rea-soning is expressed, whereas a symbolic scaffold determines how the reasoning process is structured and constrained. To construct a symbolic scaffold for a tabular data, it is nat-ural to use a decision tree model. To this end, we first train a decision tree with a grid search of its depth on the given tabular data. For each input instance x, the tree induces a unique root-to-leaf symbolic decision path, which we for-malize as an instance-specific symbolic scaffold denoted by 

S(x). In particular, the scaffold S(x) is an ordered set of feature-level logical constraints that characterizes the suffi-cient conditions leading to the prediction for x. Importantly, 

S(x) is induced in a data-dependent manner: both the se-lected features and their associated thresholds arise from the empirical data distribution. As a result, the scaffold provides a compact, verifiable constraint space that can be directly used to guide rationale generation. An example of a scaffold 

S(x) from the decision tree is given in Figure 1, “age >26.5, BMI >9.625, ... ”. Similar as before, we serialize S(x) into a sequence of tokens.

To this end, we prompt a generalist LLM (e.g., ChatGPT) by asking it to provide the rationale how the label y is predicted from the input features x following the symbolic decision path S(x) that leads to the correct label. Generalist LLMs encode rich domain knowledge acquired from large-scale pretraining, which can be leveraged to translate symbolic rules into coherent natural language explanations. In our setting, we use a generalist LLM as a constrained genera-tor that injects domain knowledge while strictly adhering to symbolic constraints as specified in the symbolic scaf-fold. 3.4. Scaffold-Invariant Data Augmentation 

One might note that we only use the data that is correctly predicted by the decision tree model. This is important for LLM to learn consistent decision rules. However, it will shrink the size of training data for SFT. Below, we present an effective data augmentation strategy. A data augmentation is usually performed by perturbing the input features. However, a major challenge in data aug-mentation for tabular prediction is ensuring the consistency between perturbed features and the label, as unconstrained perturbations may cause an instance to no longer correspond to its original label. Our framework addresses this issue by enforcing scaffold invariance during augmentation. Specif-ically, for each correctly predicted training instance by the decision tree model, its symbolic scaffold S(x) character-izes the decision logic leading to its label. This scaffold defines a conjunction of feature-level constraints and serves as an explicit invariance during data augmentation. To generate an augmented sample, we randomly sample some features (e.g., by flipping a coin for each feature) and for each feature we randomly perturb its value such that it does not violate the decision rule specified by the scaffold 

S(x). Perturbed values are sampled within a feature-specific valid range. To improve the out-of-distribution generaliz-ability, we also allow for a certain probability (e.g., 0.2) to perturb a sampled feature that do not appear in the deci-sion path to the value unknown . This not only simulates out-of-distribution data but also facilitates the evaluation of explainability presented in next section. As a result, all perturbations satisfy the original decision-path constraints, ensuring that the symbolic scaffold remains unchanged. By preserving the decision scaffold while allowing controlled feature-level variation, our augmentation procedure gener-ates diverse yet label-scaffold consistent samples. # 4. Evaluation of Faithfulness and Explainability 

Since the ReSS-trained reasoning model is derived from an explainable and faithful decision tree model, a natural ques-tion is whether the learned reasoning model preserves the faithfulness and explainability of the underlying decision tree. To answer this question, we need to evaluate whether the model-generated reasoning is faithful and explainable. Without systematic evaluation, reasoning traces may appear plausible while relying on nonexistent features or implausi-ble feature values, and including artificial rationales that are not causal for the final predictions. To this end, we consider the following three complementary criteria: hallucination, explanation sufficiency, and explanation necessity, which together characterize whether the reasoning is grounded in the input, complete, and causally relevant to the prediction. 

Hallucination. We measure hallucination by identifying reasoning steps that violate the input evidence. Specifically, we consider three types: (i) feature existence hallucination (FEH) , where the reasoning refers to features absent from the input; (ii) feature value hallucination (FVH) , where incorrect or unsupported feature values are asserted; and (iii) 

comparison hallucination (CH) , where invalid numerical or categorical comparisons are made. The hallucination rate is computed at the step level: for each reasoning step, we mark it as hallucinated if it contains a violation, and report the fraction of hallucinated steps among all steps. 

Explanation Sufficiency. To assess explanation sufficiency, we mask features that are not referenced in the generated reasoning traces by setting their values to unknown in the input, and prompting the model to re-generate reasoning and prediction, and evaluating the prediction accuracy. Specifi-cally, we progressively mask the number of such features from k = 1 to 4. If the accuracy remains stable under these perturbations, the original reasoning traces are considered to be sufficient.

5.2. Comparison with Baselines 

Table 2 summarizes the classification accuracy of differ-ent methods across four tabular datasets. We can observe that Direct SFT generally exhibits weaker performance than traditional methods. Introducing additional reasoning super-vision (DRC+SFT) improves performance on some datasets but yields inconsistent gains, indicating the limitations of di-rect unconstrained reasoning curation. In contrast, with sym-bolic scaffold informed reasoning boosted by the scaffold-invariant data augmentation, ReSS consistently outperforms all baselines, with particularly notable gains on Diabetes and AD datasets. We also see that Direct RL approach shows high variance across datasets, reflecting the difficulty of optimizing reasoning behavior without explicit struc-tural guidance. These results collectively demonstrate that benefits of structured reasoning supervision grounded in symbolic scaffolds, underscoring the importance of high-quality, structure-aligned reasoning data for effective tabular decision-making. 

5.3. Evaluation of Faithfulness and Explainability 

As discussed in Section 4, we evaluate faithfulness and ex-plainability on our ReSS models trained with the augmented datasets from three complementary perspectives: hallucina-tion , explanation sufficiency , and explanation necessity .

Hallucination Analysis. Table 3 reports the hallucination rates across four datasets. Across all datasets, FEH and FVH are consistently zero, indicating that the model does not invent non-existent features or incorrect feature values in its reasoning. Comparison Hallucination occurs only rarely, with rates below 2% on all datasets. These results demon-strate that the generated explanations are highly faithful to the underlying input features, with only occasional errors arising from incorrect relational comparisons. 

Explanation Sufficiency and Necessity. Figure 2 illustrates the results of explanation sufficiency and necessity evalua-tion by progressively masking features with an unknown 

token. We observe that across all datasets, masking unused features in reasoning has only a marginal impact on perfor-mance, indicating that the explanations are sufficient: they already capture the core information needed for prediction. In contrast, masking used features in reasoning leads to a substantial and monotonic drop in accuracy, where perfor-mance degrades sharply as more explanatory features are removed. This clear divergence between the two curves demonstrates that the explanations rely on features that are genuinely necessary for the model’s decision, providing strong evidence of both explanation sufficiency and neces-sity. We further analyze explainability under the absence of symbolic guidance by comparing ReSS with DRC+SFT, and observe a substantial degradation in explanation necessity without symbolic scaffolds; detailed results are provided in Appendix E.4.

5.5. Ablation studies Domain Knowledge Matters. To quantify the role of do-main knowledge, we conduct an ablation study that delexi-calizes all feature names and values during reasoning data curation. Specifically, we preserve the original input struc-ture—including the input features, symbolic scaffolds, and the label—but anonymize all feature names and categorical values into abstract placeholders (e.g., Feature A , Category B). The LLM is strictly instructed to follow the symbolic scaffold while being prohibited from providing any seman-tic or domain-specific explanations, resulting in reasoning that purely imitates the symbolic structure without introduc-ing external domain knowledge. Prompts for reasoning data curation and fine-tuning, as well as curated reasoning exam-ples, are provided in Appendix A.3 and B.3, respectively. Figure 13 reports the results. Models trained on delexical-ized reasoning achieve performance nearly identical to the decision tree baseline across all datasets, but consistently underperform ReSS. This result indicates that delexicalized reasoning mainly captures the symbolic structure of the de-cision tree, while the additional performance gains of ReSS arise from domain knowledge introduced by a general LLM. We further include a cherry-picked analysis to illustrate how domain knowledge contributes to the performance gains of our ReSS-trained model over decision trees (Appendix E.1). 

SFT vs RL. Since RL is usually regarded as an effective ap-proach to improve the generalization of SFT, we conduct RL on ReSS-trained models. To this end, we conduct RL based on DisCO from the model initialized from ReSS-trained model without augmentation. We compare this approach, denoted by ReSS (w/o aug) + RL, with the direct RL ap-proach in Figure 14, which shows that performing RL after ReSS consistently outperforms directly applying RL to the base model across all datasets, demonstrating that RL bene-fits substantially from structured reasoning supervision. We also compare the approach ReSS (w/o aug) + RL with ReSS and ReSS (w/o aug.) in Figure 15. We can observe while RL generally improves its SFT counterparts, it is not necessarily better than ReSS-trained model on augmented data. This indicates that our scaffold-invariant data augmen-tation is an effective approach for improving generalization. We additionally apply RL on top of ReSS with augmented data. However, this setting does not yield further consis-tent improvements. This suggests that scaffold-invariant augmentation already supplies a strong learning signal, di-minishing the marginal benefits of additional RL. 

# 6. Conclusions 

We proposed ReSS, a framework for learning faithful rea-soning models for tabular data by using decision-tree paths as symbolic scaffolds. ReSS enables scalable reasoning data curation and trains LLMs to produce structured, causally grounded explanations. Experiments on medical and finan-cial datasets demonstrate improved predictive performance and strong faithfulness and explainability. While ReSS shows promising results in financial and medical domains, future work will explore its effectiveness in other domains where LLMs possess strong intrinsic domain knowledge. 8ReSS: Learning Reasoning Models for Tabular Data Prediction via Symbolic Scaffold 

# Impact Statement 

This work focuses on improving the faithfulness and consis-tency of model-generated reasoning for tabular prediction tasks. By grounding natural-language explanations in sym-bolic decision logic, the proposed approach may support greater transparency and interpretability in machine learn-ing systems, particularly in domains where tabular data is prevalent. While our experiments include datasets from healthcare and finance, the proposed method is intended as a decision-support tool rather than an autonomous decision-making system. Potential downstream impacts depend on how such models are deployed in practice. In high-stakes settings, improper use or over-reliance on model outputs could lead to adverse outcomes if not accompanied by appropriate human oversight and domain expertise. This work does not address deployment, governance, or regulatory considerations directly, and we encourage prac-titioners to carefully evaluate domain-specific risks when applying the proposed framework.

# E. Supplementary Experimental Results 

E.1. Knowledge helps our ReSS-trained LLM to correct mistakes of Decision tree 

Decision trees are purely data-driven models and do not encode domain knowledge. As a result, some predictions may reflect dataset-specific artifacts or spurious correlations rather than applying domain-plausible rules (see an example given below). While this can introduce a small fraction of noisy or counterintuitive reasoning examples for training, the majority of decision-tree-derived reasoning paths remain consistent with domain commonsense. When trained on such data, the language model does not simply memorize individual paths, but instead learns implicit explanatory structures and domain-level regularities underlying the paths. Consequently, during inference, the model may generate predictions that deviate from specific decision-tree leaf outcomes while remaining aligned with learned domain-level regularities. To illustrate this phenomenon, we present a qualitative case study on a testing data from the Diabetes dataset shown in Figure 12. Along this decision path, the decision tree assigns a non-diabetic label, which reflects the empirical training label distribution observed in this localized region of the feature space but is wrong. However, every condition along the path corresponds to a well-established risk factor for diabetes according to the domain knowledge. In contrast, our fine-tuned LLM generates a rationale that faithfully follows the same decision path conditions, explicitly referencing each split and providing medically plausible interpretations for them. The generated reasoning emphasizes the cumulative risk implied by high elevated 2-hour plasma glucose levels, extreme obesity, elevated diabetes pedigree function, and high blood pressure, and finally predicts the correct diabetic label.

E.4. Explainability without Symbolic Guidance. 

We further analyze the explainability of the generated reasoning by comparing ReSS, trained without augmented reasoning data, with a DRC+SFT baseline, whose reasoning data is generated without symbolic scaffold constraints. This analysis focuses on explanation sufficiency and necessity, using the same feature-masking protocol as in the main experiments. Figure 16 shows the sufficiency and necessity curves on Diabetes and AD. For ReSS, masking unused features results in only minor accuracy changes, while masking explanation-referenced features leads to a sharp and monotonic performance drop, indicating strong explanation necessity. In contrast, DRC+SFT consistently exhibits substantially weaker necessity. On Diabetes, masking features referenced by the explanation causes a much smaller accuracy degradation compared to ReSS. The issue becomes more pronounced on AD, where the accuracy drop under necessity masking is minimal and, in some cases, even smaller than that observed when masking unused features, revealing a clear violation of explanation necessity. This behavior can be attributed to the absence of symbolic guidance during reasoning data generation. Without symbolic scaffold constraints, the LLM tends to incorporate a large number of input features into the explanation, rather than selectively identifying decision-critical evidence. This over-inclusive behavior is particularly detrimental for high-dimensional datasets such as AD, which contains 25 input features. Under DRC+SFT, the generated explanations reference an average of 14 features, many of which are redundant or only weakly correlated with the prediction outcome. From an explainability perspective, such reasoning fails to distinguish truly influential features from incidental information, leading to substantially degraded explanation necessity.
