# Concept Training for Human-Aligned Language Models 

## Christine Zhang, Dan Jurafsky, Chen Shani 

## Stanford University 

## {chrzhang, jurafsky, cshani}@stanford.edu 

## Abstract 

The next-token prediction (NTP) objective trains language models to predict a single con-tinuation token at each step. In natural lan-guage, however, a prefix can be continued in many valid ways, and even similar meanings may differ in surface form. For example, the sentence “this website is safe to browse ” could plausibly continue with words such as browse, search, visit, surf, or navigate. While standard NTP training treats these alternatives as mutu-ally exclusive targets, we explore a framework that instead predicts concepts, approximated as sets of semantically related tokens. We show that models trained with concept supervision exhibit stronger alignment with human seman-tic similarity judgments on multiple lexical benchmarks. These gains are accompanied by lower perplexity on semantically meaningful words (definition in Section 3.1), and a mod-est increase in global token-level perplexity, reflecting a tradeoff between standard NTP op-timization and concept-level supervision. Our results suggest that concept-level objectives can improve semantic alignment while maintaining competitive language modeling performance. 1

## 1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) generate coher-ent text, answer questions, and perform increas-ingly complex reasoning tasks. Yet, the training objective underlying most state-of-the-art LLMs re-mains strikingly limited: predicting the next token in a sequence. Although the next token prediction (NTP) objective has been successful in practice, it restricts LLMs to learning surface-level token patterns. Specifically, NTP treats different surface realizations as distinct outcomes, even when they convey the same or very similar meaning. In contrast, humans reason and communicate with concepts that may be realized by variations

in phrasing and context. Synonyms such as “dad” and “father” refer to the same conceptual entity, and words that are not exact synonyms can realize the same concept in certain contexts. For instance, “dog” and “pet” can often be interchangeable. As a result, NTP may penalize one valid completion in favor of another, which diverges from the way humans semantically process concepts (Holtzman et al., 2021; Shani et al., 2026). Here, we modify the training objective to predict concepts rather than surface-level tokens , where we approximate concepts as sets of contextual syn-onyms. We introduce a self-contained training pipeline that leverages the model’s own knowledge to make its representations more concept-aware. Our concept-trained models show stronger alignment with human semantic intuition . More-over, despite not being trained to predict exact to-kens, concept models achieve lower perplexity and higher accuracy on content words (definition in Section 3.1) than both pretrained and NTP-trained baselines. At the same time, global token-level perplexity increases, suggesting that concept-level predictions shift models away from mere NTP. 

## 2 Related Work 

NTP’s tendency to reward only exact token matches has been shown to penalize correct concepts, distort token rankings, and produce undesirable behaviors (Holtzman et al., 2021; Shani et al., 2023; Iyer et al., 2026; Liu et al., 2026). Bender and Koller (2020) argue that this surface-level focus limits a model’s ability to learn true semantics, emphasizing the distinction between meaning and form. Empirical work supports this, showing that NTP and teacher-forcing can fail on tasks requiring entailment, plan-ning, or multiple valid continuations (Bachmann and Nagarajan, 2024; Merrill et al., 2024). To-gether, these suggest that fluent generation does not necessarily imply semantic understanding.

Recent work has explored modifying pretrain-ing to address these limitations. A survey by Wy-att et al. (2025) categorizes these approaches into: multi-token prediction (Liu et al., 2024; Ahn et al., 2025), plan-then-generate frameworks (Mahajan et al., 2026; Wei et al., 2025), latent reasoning ap-proaches (Barrault et al., 2024; Tack et al., 2026), continuous generation (Li et al., 2022; Shabalin et al., 2025), and non-transformer architectures (Gu and Dao, 2024; Huang et al., 2026). Others focused on mitigating NTP’s limitations during post-training . Alignment techniques such as RLHF and DPO (Ouyang et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023; Rafailov et al., 2023), instruction tuning, con-trastive learning, and multitask finetuning introduce semantically informed signals during finetuning. For example, semantic augmentations during in-struction tuning improve generalization (Yan et al., 2024), and injecting lexical information improves word sense disambiguation (Levine et al., 2020). The most closely related approaches incorporate concept-level information directly into the training signal. Chen et al. (2025) propose Concept-Aware Fine-Tuning, which augments NTP with auxiliary heads predicting future tokens. Similarly, Iyer et al. (2026) introduce next-synonym and hyper-nym prediction methods that enrich training data with semantically related alternatives. Although these methods improve perplexity and downstream performance, they rely on external resources to define concept sets. In contrast, we recover con-cept structure directly from the model, requiring no external supervision. 

## 3 Training Methods 

We now describe both the process of enriching stan-dard data with concept signal (Section 3.1; Figure 1a), and modifying standard NTP objective func-tion to be concept-aware (Section 3.2; Figure 1b). 

3.1 Concept Dataset Creation 

We used the standard training datasets: 1) Al-lenAI’s C4 (Raffel et al., 2020), and 2) OpenWeb-Text (Gokaslan and Cohen, 2019), an open-source WebText replication. From each corpus, we down-sample 2,000 English text sequences, keeping only sequences with 50 < characters < 2,000. Figure 1a depicts the data generation process. For each sequence, we identify tokens that corre-spond to complete words (rather than subwords) and are nouns, verbs, or adjectives, ensuring that meaningful semantic alternatives exist. We first tokenize each sequence using the Llama 3.1 8B tokenizer and retain only tokens that form com-plete words, determined by verifying that both the current token and the following token begin with a space. We then apply spaCy part-of-speech tagging to identify nouns, verbs, and adjectives. To ensure consistency between tokenizations, we verify that these tokens align between spaCy’s tokenizer and the Llama 3.1 8B tokenizer. Using this procedure, complete-word single-token nouns, verbs, and ad-jectives constitute on average 28% of tokens in each sequence. We name these tokens content words .Then, we use Llama 3.1 8B (Grattafiori et al., 2024) to generate the top 200 most likely NTPs for each of these word positions. These candidates represent potential surface forms of the same under-lying meaning. Next, we filter this pool to identify contextually interchangeable tokens by prompting Llama 3.1 8B Instruct to select which candidates could replace the original token without changing the meaning of the sentence (Appendix A). The resulting subset forms a contextual synonym set, which we treat as a discrete approximation of the concept associated with that token in its context. 

3.2 Concept Training Objective 

We introduce a concept-level loss that rewards se-mantically equivalent completions, rather than a single surface form. Let S denote an input sentence prefix (e.g., “this website is safe to ”), and let T be the original con-tent token (“browse”). We define T ∗ as the set of contextual synonyms for T {browse, search, visit, surf, navigate, access, click, view, ... }. Instead of optimizing the likelihood of predicting the exact to-ken T , the concept objective rewards the model for predicting any token belonging to the conceptual equivalence class T ∗. Formally:

3.3 Combined Training Loss 

We combine the standard NTP loss with the con-cept loss using a weighted linear interpolation: 

Ltotal = (1 − λ) LNTP + λ Lconcept 

where λ ∈ [0 , 1] controls the strength of concept-level supervision. Setting λ = 0 is equivalent to standard NTP training, while larger values place increasing emphasis on concept prediction. To prevent the concept objective from dominat-ing NTP, we introduce a probability mass threshold on the contextual synonym set. If the cumulative probability assigned by the model to tokens in T ∗

exceeds a certain threshold (we used 0.6), we set 

Lconcept = 0 for that training example. This ensures that once the model sufficiently captures the con-cept, additional optimization pressure is removed. In practice, we found that this threshold was only reached less than 1% of the time during training.

## 4 Experiments & Results 

We evaluate the base, NTP, and concept models on alignment with human semantic judgments (Sec-tion 4.1) and NTP perplexity and accuracy on held-out data (Sections 4.2 & 4.3). 

4.1 Increased Semantic Understanding 

We evaluate alignment with human semantic judg-ments using both word pairs and sentence pairs with the following standard benchmarks: 

MEN (Bruni et al., 2014) contains 3,000 word pairs annotated by 50 humans based on relatedness judgments. Participants were not instructed about the difference between similarity and relatedness, where similarity can refer to {“car, automobile”}) and relatedness can be {“wheels, car”}. 

WordSim353 (Finkelstein et al., 2002) consists of 353 word pairs annotated by 13-16 humans. Par-ticipants estimated the relatedness of each pair on a scale from 0 to 10. 

SimLex-999 (Hill et al., 2014) contains 999 word pairs. Unlike MEN and WordSim353, SimLex-999 quantifies similarity rather than association or relatedness. Similarity is defined as synonymy, and each pair is rated by roughly 50 humans. 

STS-B (Cer et al., 2017) consists of 1,500 sen-tence pairs, drawn from news headlines, video and image captions, and natural language inference data. Each pair is human-annotated with a nor-malized similarity between 0 and 1. For all benchmarks, we feed each word or sen-tence through the model and apply mean pooling over the final-layer hidden states, followed by L2 normalization. We then compute cosine similarity between the two vectors in each pair and report Spearman correlation with human judgments. 

Concept-trained models achieve substantially higher Spearman correlation than both base-lines on all semantic similarity tasks (Figure 3). In contrast, NTP-only post-training slightly de-grades semantic alignment relative to the pretrained model, suggesting that continued token-level op-timization alone can weaken semantic structure, whereas concept supervision mitigates this effect. For the SimLex-999 benchmark, we note that the optimal concept weight is 0.75 rather than 1 for the 1B and 8B models. 4.2 New Metric: Content Word Perplexity 

While we deem human alignment to be the most relevant metric, this approach requires manual an-notation and is therefore costly and difficult to scale. In contrast, perplexity remains the dominant auto-matic metric for LLM evaluation due to its simplic-ity and ease of computation. Yet, standard perplexity treats all tokens equally, averaging across function words and content-bearing words. This can obscure meaningful im-provements in semantic modeling. A growing body of work highlights that not all tokens contribute equally to learning or evaluation. Several studies show that prioritizing more informative or chal-lenging tokens can improve model performance on downstream tasks (Bigelow et al., 2025; Thankaraj et al., 2025; Lin et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2025). Consistent with this view, recent work has ques-tioned the practice of computing perplexity by av-eraging across all tokens. For example, Fang et al. (2024) show that perplexity computed only on key tokens correlates better with performance on long-context benchmarks. Similarly, Miralles-González et al. (2026) argue that aggregating token-level met-rics via simple averaging ignores differences in to-ken predictive difficulty, and propose weighting schemes that emphasize more informative tokens. Other work also highlights mismatches between global perplexity and model quality. Luitel et al. (2025) show that lower perplexity does not neces-sarily imply better performance, and Meister and Cotterell (2021) find that low-perplexity models may still fail to capture important linguistic proper-ties. Together, these findings suggest that the stan-dard global perplexity approach, which treats all tokens equally, may obscure meaningful dif-ferences in token importance .Motivated by these observations, we introduce a more informative metric, which we call content word perplexity : standard NTP perplexity com-puted only over whole-word tokens that are nouns, verbs, or adjectives. These tokens typically carry most of the semantic content of a sentence, while less informative tokens such as “is”, “a”, “are”, and “the”, as well as subword fragments, are excluded. Concept models also demonstrate stronger OOD generalization . When models trained on C4 are evaluated on OpenWebText (and vice versa), they achieve lower perplexity and higher accuracy than both baselines (significant under paired boot-strap confidence intervals; see Appendix C). These findings suggest that concept-level supervision im-proves robustness to domain shift and leads to mod-els that generalize more reliably to unseen data. We also observe that increasing concept weight from λ = 0 to λ = 0 .75 yields monotonic improve-ments in content word performance. At λ = 1 ,however, some models plateau or slightly degrade, suggesting that retaining some degree of token-level supervision is beneficial. In practice, com-bining concept-level and token-level objectives yields the strongest overall performance .

4.3 Global Perplexity 

Figure 9 shows that concept models exhibit minor degradation relative to the base model for both in-domain and OOD evaluation, with the λ = 1 model showing the largest drop. This pattern is consistent with our objective: concept training emphasizes semantically meaningful tokens, which are typi-cally harder to predict than the function words that dominate global perplexity (content words make up 28% of tokens in our data). For λ = 1 , the larger degradation likely reflects that the model is trained on fewer tokens, since loss is applied only to content words, while the other models receive NTP supervision on all tokens. We conclude that models with concept weights of 0.25 and 0.5 achieve the best trade-off, improv-ing human alignment while increasing global perplexity by less than one point and reduc-ing content-word perplexity by more than five points (Appendix D). 

## 5 Analysis and Ablations 

We further analyze our concept training manipula-tion. Specifically, we examine how it affects latent space geometry (Section 5.1), how it interacts with model size (Section 5.2), the effect of introducing noise (Section 5.3), and the impact of reducing the number of content words in the data (Section 5.4). 

5.1 Improved Cluster Separation 

To examine the effect of concept training on inter-nal geometry, we analyze hidden states on 1,000 held-out samples. For each sequence, we randomly select one content word and replace it with each synonym in its concept set to construct concept variations. We then extract the last-layer hidden state of the content token for all tokens in the set. We evaluate whether synonyms for the same concept occupy a coherent region in representation space using intra- and inter-concept cosine similar-ity. Intra-concept similarity is the mean pairwise cosine similarity among hidden states sharing a con-cept, while inter-concept similarity is the mean across words belonging to different concepts. The clustering score is the difference between these val-ues. We find that all models have comparable intra-concept similarity, but concept models show no-tably lower inter-concept similarity, indicating that concept loss sharpens decision boundaries rather than collapsing embeddings (Figure 5, left). Thus, concept supervision combined with NTP training preserves representational expressivity while learn-ing more meaningful semantic boundaries. We observe that the 8B model shows smaller clustering improvements than the 1B and 3B mod-els, likely because the base 8B model already ex-hibits stronger semantic understanding and there-fore benefits less from additional concept learning. 5.2 Effect of Model Size 

We examine how model size affects concept train-ing. First, we see a clear scale effect on content word NTP performance (Figure 4), and a more noisy effect for alignment with semantic similarity (much clearer without concept training; Figure 3).

Figure 6 compares the improvement in NTP per-plexity between concept weight 0 and 1, showing that smaller models gain more from concept su-pervision compared to bigger models, consistent with our observation in Section 5.1. For alignment with semantic similarity, we see a similar but much weaker trend (see Figure 10 in Appendix E). These results suggest that concept supervision is most helpful for smaller models, where encouraging ab-straction at the concept level may help allocate limited representational capacity more efficiently. 

5.3 Learning from Noise 

To verify that gains stem from semantically coher-ent synonym sets rather than mere lexical variation (i.e., noise as regularization), we train control mod-els with randomized synonym assignments. We pool all synonyms from the training set and, for each sequence, randomly sample a matching num-ber of words from this pool, replacing every true synonym set with this noise-based control. As representatives of the general trend, we show the in-domain NTP accuracy on content words and the WordSim353 correlation in Figure 7 (all re-sults in Appendix F and Appendix H). We find that even at the weight on the noise “concept” loss, models trained with randomized synonym assign-ments perform substantially worse than both the pretrained and NTP-trained baselines on both NTP and human semantic alignment benchmarks. These results indicate that the improvements from con-cept training are not simply due to additional label variability or regularization . Instead, se-mantically structured concept supervision appears necessary for the observed gains, enabling models to improve performance on both NTP and human alignment tasks relative to all baselines.

We evaluate concept training using 100%, 50%, 25%, and only the last of these content words. For the last content word, we use the last word in each sequence, where we filtered sequences to end in a content word. ## 6 Conclusions and Future Directions 

We introduce a self-supervised method for training LLMs using a concept-based objective that comple-ments standard NTP. We show that concept-level models learn more meaningful semantic bound-aries, align better with human intuition, and better predict semantically informative tokens in both in-distribution and OOD settings. These results sug-gest that incorporating concept signal into training can yield more human-like representations. Our results also highlight the importance of the evaluation metric. Concept models better model content words, but not all other tokens. Because frequent function words dominate global perplex-ity, improvements on meaningful tokens can be obscured. Evaluating content word perplexity bet-ter reflects how models capture semantic structure. Finally, we find that combining concept-level and token-level supervision yields the strongest overall performance , suggesting that concept ob-jectives can serve as a complementary training sig-nal rather than a replacement for NTP. Future work can explore multilingual data, multi-token expressions, and evaluations on additional downstream tasks. More broadly, we suggest that incorporating concept-level training objectives and evaluating models on semantically meaningful pre-dictions can lead to LLMs that better reflect the con-ceptual organization underlying human language. Limitations 

Although we show promising results with concept training, our study has several limitations. First, our experiments focus exclusively on decoder-based models, which limits our ability to evaluate perfor-mance under instruction prompting for downstream tasks. As a result, our evaluation tasks in this work are relatively small in scope and do not capture ways in which concept training could be applied to improve downstream performance. Second, our concept supervision is applied only to nouns, verbs, and adjectives that are a single token and a complete word. This restricts our train-ing paradigm to a narrow prediction setting and may not fully capture the benefits of concept-level learning across longer and more diverse spans of text. Extending supervision to additional tokens within sequences could yield stronger effects. Third, our method for constructing concept sets relies on LLM-generated synonym clusters. While scalable, this approach may not perfectly reflect hu-man semantic organization, and alternative sources, such as human annotations, could provide more reliable or linguistically grounded concept group-ings. Finally, our concept weight 1 model is trained on fewer tokens than all other models (as it is only trained on content words and the other models have some degree of NTP on non-content tokens), which might account for its degradation in global NTP performance and SimLex-999 correlation. 

## Ethical Considerations 

Training models with our concept loss formula-tion could potentially lead to overgeneralizing and overextending concept boundaries. The approach we describe in this proposal involves LLMs creat-ing concept sets without human input, which can be dangerous in amplifying biases and stereotypes present in training data. We advise users to be care-ful of potentially harmful associations generated in the concept dataset creation. As with NTP models, concept-trained models may behave in an unwanted or harmful manner, such as producing hallucinations. In this work, we did not explore using our concept-trained models for unconditioned generation, and thus the sever-ity of these risks is unknown for our models. We caution users to always check model outputs. Disclosure: LLMs were used to assist with cod-ing and refine the text of this paper.

## B Training Details 

We split each dataset into 90% train and 10% val-idation. All models are trained using LoRa (Hu et al., 2022) with a learning rate of 7 × 10 −5 and batch size of 2 for 5 epochs, leading to a training time of approximately 1.5 hours on an NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPU. The loss plateaus by the end of the fifth epoch — with early stopping enabled, we find that models typically stop training around 4

epochs. We limit the contextual synonym set size to a maximum of 10 excluding the content word itself, and we use a probability mass threshold of 

0.6.
