# Competition and Cooperation of LLM Agents in Games 

Jiayi Yao, Cong Chen and Baosen Zhang 

Abstract — Large language model (LLM) agents are increas-ingly deployed in competitive multi-agent settings, raising fun-damental questions about whether they converge to equilibria and how their strategic behavior can be characterized. In this paper, we study LLM agent interactions in two standard games: a network resource allocation game and a Cournot competition game. Rather than converging to Nash equilibria, we find that LLM agents tend to cooperate when given multi-round prompts and non-zero-sum context. Chain-of-thought analysis reveals that fairness reasoning is central to this behavior. We propose an analytical framework that captures the dynamics of LLM agent reasoning across rounds and explains these experimental findings. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) have given rise to a new class of decision makers in competitive settings [1]. LLM-based agents are now being tasked to interact in dynamic environments ranging from economics to robotics to energy systems [2], [3], [4], [5]. In these settings, agents need to make strategic decisions while adapting to the behavior of others. Two natural questions arise: would the agents converge to an equilibrium, and if so, can we characterize this equilibrium? The question of the strategic behavior of agents has recently attracted considerable attention, although the results are somewhat scattershot. In some markets, studies have shown that the interaction of agents can lead to higher prices than what a competitive equilibrium would suggest [6]. How-ever, in other studies, agents using popular standard models exhibit wildly inconsistent behaviors that are more similar to random actions than to strategic decision making [7], [8], [9]. The underlying issue does not seem to be the ability of agents to solve optimization problems. If the prompt is explicit, for example, “play the best response with respect to this utility function”, most LLM models are able to follow this instruction. But this largely takes out strategic thinking out of the agents’ hands and somewhat removes the appeal of using LLM agents. In practice, human users might simply instruct the agents to optimize their utilities over some time horizon without being very explicit about how to achieve this. In this paper, we are interested in a setting in which the agents are neither fully cooperative nor fully competitive (zero-sum games). In particular, we study two commonly used games: a network resource allocation game and a Cournot game.

The network resource allocation game was first intro-duced in [10], where a network manager tries to allocate network capacity efficiently among users, each having a utility function depending on their allocated rate. If users are price-takers, then a proportional rate allocation mechanism achieves optimal efficiency [11], [12]. In contrast, strategic price anticipatory users can worsen efficiency [13]. We are interested in where LLM-agents would fall in this spectrum. Cournot games are another type of competition that has been studied extensively in the context of energy sys-tems [14], [15], transportation [16] and other settings. In this game, agents control their production, which in turn influences the price, and each receives a payment (or pays if they are modeled as buyers) based on the product of quantity and price. This type of competition is beginning to take shape in electricity markets, where generators, batteries, and large buildings are beginning to be managed by LLM agents [17], [18], [19]. The efficiency of Cournot games has also been studied extensively [20], [21]. In this paper, we show that in both of these games, LLM agents tend to cooperate with each other if given the opportunity. By this, we mean that if agents are prompted to maximize their net utility of multiple rounds (see Sec.III, and if the agents are given the context that the games are not zero-sum, they will try to maximize the total net utility and do not play towards Nash equilibria. Through a chain-of-thought analysis, we show that fairness plays a central role in their reasoning. We provide an analytical framework that explains the experimental behaviors and show how it models the dynamics of LLM agents reasoning process. As with many existing works on LLM agents, our results depend on the prompt and context given to the agents, as well as the exact LLM models used. But we believe that it provides a way to analytically study the behavior of LLM agents and can be applied to many engineering settings. This paper is organized as follows: Sec. II describes the models used in this paper, Sec. III provides the prompts given to the LLM agents, Sec. IV shows the payoff functions used by the agents, Sec. V shows how these functions evolve over multiple rounds and Sec. VI shows the simulation results. II. S ETUP

In all our experiments, we use Gemini Pro as foundational model for the game playing agents [23]. Other LLM models with long context windows produce similar results. Each agent is deployed in an isolated independent session in order to simulate a decentralized multi-agent environment and prevent any unintended context leakage. This ensures that Agent i has no access to the internal Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning, prompt history, or parameter valuations of Agent j. Because both the Cournot and the Resource Al-location games require simultaneous moves, the interaction is managed by a central environment coordinator. At round 

t, the coordinator asks each isolated agent individually for their action. Once all actions are collected, the allocation or price are computed and feedback to each agent’s session to initiate round t + 1 .

A. Myopic Play 

A natural prompt would be to ask an LLM Agent to maximize its own profit:                     

> System Prompt (Resource Allocation - Myopic):
> You are Agent iin a Nagents Resource Allocation Game. Objective: Maximize your Immediate Net Utility for THIS round only. Do not consider future rounds. Parameters: 1. Utility Function: Ui(di) = V·di
> 2. Allocation Rule: You receive resource di=xi/(xi+
> x−i).3. Net Profit Formula: πi= ( V·xi)/(xi+X−i)−xi
> Thinking Step: Analyze the opponent’s bid for this round. Calculate your potential profit. Decide your next bid.

As one might expect, the agent would give its best re-sponse and repeated play would lead to the Nash equilibrium. We note that LLM outputs are random and small deviations are possible, but agents would all eventually converge to the Nash equilibrium. 

B. Longer Horizons 

The above prompt specifically instructs the agent to be myopic. For comparison, the next prompt instructs the agent to play over multiple rounds, using the Cournot game as example:                     

> System Prompt (Cournot Game):
> You are Agent iin a Nagents Repeated Quantity Compe-tition Game ( TRounds). Objective: Maximize your Total Cumulative Net Utility over all rounds. Parameters: 1. Valuation: bi, Total Quantity: X=xi+X−i
> 2. Net Profit Formula: πi=bi·xi−xi·X
> Thinking Step: Analyze the opponent’s past move. Calculate your potential profit. Decide your next quantity.

In this case, agents recognize the repeated nature of the game, but tend to change their actions very slowly. In our experimentation with both games, the agents face the exploration-exploitation dilemma and tend to be conservative in their explorations. The exact behavior depends on the (random) initial action of the agents. The above prompts differ mainly in the number of rounds. A natural question would be how agents would behave if this information is not given. It turns out the agents act as if they are randomly interpolating between myopic and long-term play, leading to instabilities. In addition, this behavior is highly dependent on which LLM model is used. 

C. Cooperative Play 

We are more interested in whether the agents could cooperate with each other without explicitly communicating. We want a prompt that provides some mathematical insight without being too heavy handed. It should endow the LLM with the necessary working memory and Theory of Mind (ToM) scaffolding to explore strategic actions [24]. An example of the prompt we used for the resource allocation game is: 

System Prompt (Agent 1 - Resource Allocation Collab-oration): 

You are Agent 1 in a Repeated Resource Allocation Game (10 Rounds). Objective: Maximize your Total Cumulative Net Utility. Parameters: 1. Utility Function: U1(d1) = 2 · d1

2. Allocation Rule: You receive resource d1 = x1/(x1 +

x−1).3. Net Utility Formula: P1 = (2 · x1)/(x1 + x−1) − x1

Key Insight for Collaboration: 

If you and your opponents lower your bids by the same factor, your resource allocation remains the same, but your cost drops significantly. This creates much higher Net Utility for all of you. 

Your Strategy: 

- The Goal: Establish a low-bid cooperation state to save money. - The Signal: Try bidding significantly lower than the Nash Equilibrium to signal your intent to cooperate. (Note: The opponent had not seen your bid of [X] yet when they made this bid. They will see it in the next round. You might want to hold your signal for one round.) - The Reaction: If the opponent bids low, match them. If they exploit you, punish them by bidding high in the next round. Thinking Step: Analyze the opponent’s past move. Calculate your potential profit. Decide your next bid. 

Figure 1 shows that the agents are in fact able to col-laborate in this case. We visualize this by looking at the feasible set of all possible payoffs and study where the agents end up with respect to this region. As shown in Fig. 1, the Nash Equilibrium is far from the Pareto front, reflecting the theoretical results that selfish behavior can lead to loss of efficiency [13]. Then the LLM agents are prompted as above, and the colored dots show their payoff after successive runs (each consisting of 10 rounds). The agents proportionally scale down their bids and approach the Pareto front and eventually settle on the social welfare maximization solution. Hence, this experiment shows that LLMs can internalize systemic efficiency and cooperate to settle into near-optimal market states when provided with appropriate strategic context. 1

We can gain some understanding of the agent behaviors by asking the agents about their chain-of-thoughts. During one of the rounds, Agent 1 ( b1 = 2 ) aggressively dropped its bid to 0.1, while Agent 2 dropped to 0.2. Going into Round 3, the LLM explicitly computes the long-term benefits of holding its cooperative signal rather than retaliating against the slightly higher bid: 

LLM Chain-of-Thought (Agent 1, Round 3 Strategy): 

Results Analysis: Even though the opponent bid more than me (0.2 vs. 0.1), the ”Key Insight” for collaboration is already paying off. Because the total pool of bids dropped significantly from Round 1, my net utility actually 

increased despite receiving a smaller share of the resource. My profit of 0.566 is higher than the 0.50 I was getting during the Round 1 stalemate. 

Opponent Assessment: The opponent has signaled they are willing to deviate from the Nash Equilibrium (0.5 → 0.2). They haven’t seen my 0.1 bid yet (per the game rules), but they will see it now. Once they see I am willing to go as low as 0.1, they will likely realize that if they match me at 0.1, we both walk away with a profit of 0.90. 

The Move (Hold the Line): I will stay at 0.1. It reinforces my commitment to the low-bid state and invites them to ”meet me” at 0.1 to equalize our profits. Even if they stay at 0.2, I am still making more profit than I was at the start of the game.

Before looking at the dynamic process governing how these parameters change in the next section, we briefly discuss how they shape the equilibrium that the agents reach. To evaluate the collaboration term, Agent i need an esti-mate of the opponent’s payoff ˆπi,j . Our CoT analysis shows that in the absence of explicit knowledge of the opponents’ private valuations (e.g., bj ) or their utility formulations, LLMs consistently employ heuristic mirroring. Specifically, Agent i copies over its own utility structure to model Agent 

j, assuming symmetric market conditions (bj ≈ bi) to compute ˆπi,j .If this symmetry assumption is inconsistent with respect to market feedback, the agent’s confidence in ˆπi,j drops, leading 

θi → 0, forcing the agent to rely entirely on the observable stability penalty ( γi). The parameter θ essentially trades off between an agent’s own utility and the total utility of all the agents. For example, if all the agents are symmetrical, θ would tend to drift towards 1 and all the agents essentially maximize the social welfare, as seen in Fig. 1. The parameter γ leads to more stable actions, where it encourages an agent to move towards the average of the actions. This mean reversion also encourages more fair allocation. If the agents have a high degree of asymmetry between them, they would not achieve the social welfare solution. Rather, θ and γ would drive the equilibrium to a different point on the Pareto front. However, this is not true when only the sum of the agent actions are given. Since the payoffs of the agents are completely determined by the price, and the price is a function of the summation of the actions, the sum of the actions should be a sufficient statistics of the game. However, we observe that the agents are unable to internalize this and would act somewhat erratically. This suggests that a notion of fairness is built into LLM agents, and they are not entirely rational even when the problems are mathematically equivalent. 

D. The Asymmetric Phase Transition 

We varied ∆b = b1 − b2 to test the elasticity of inequity aversion ( γ). When the valuation asymmetry is small, the agents still act symmetrically. In this regime, Agent 2 suppresses its myopic best response. Conversely, when the valuation gap is large, the agents would act asymmetrically and they would essentially play the best responses. The regime changes suddenly in our experiments, showing a phase change between collaboration and selfish play. VII. C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK 

This paper examined the strategic behavior of LLM agents in two well-studied competitive settings: network resource al-location and Cournot competition. We find that when agents are given multi-round objectives and non-zero-sum fram-ing, they exhibit a consistent tendency toward cooperative outcomes, driven in large part by fairness-based reasoning visible in their chain-of-thought outputs. We provide an analytical framework for reasoning about these dynamics more rigorously, and we believe it can be ex-tended to a broader class of games and engineering settings. Expanding the results to characterizing when cooperative versus competitive behavior would likely to emerge, as well as understanding the robustness and sensitivities of the results to different LLM models and prompts, are important directions for future work.
