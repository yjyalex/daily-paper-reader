Abstract 

As Large Language Models (LLMs) expand in capability and application scope, their trust-worthiness becomes critical. A vital risk is in-trinsic deception, wherein models strategically mislead users to achieve their own objectives. Existing alignment approaches based on chain-of-thought (CoT) monitoring supervise explicit reasoning traces. However, under optimization pressure, models are incentivized to conceal deceptive reasoning, rendering semantic super-vision fundamentally unreliable. Grounded in cognitive psychology, we hypothesize that a de-ceptive LLM maintains a stable internal belief in its CoT while its external response remains fragile under perturbation. We term this phe-nomenon stability asymmetry and quantify it by measuring the contrast between internal CoT stability and external response stability under perturbation. Building on this structural sig-nature, we propose the Stability Asymmetry Regularization (SAR), a novel alignment objec-tive that penalizes this distributional asymme-try during reinforcement learning. Unlike CoT monitoring, SAR targets the statistical struc-ture of model outputs, rendering it robust to semantic concealment. Extensive experiments confirm that stability asymmetry reliably iden-tifies deceptive behavior, and that SAR effec-tively suppresses intrinsic deception without degrading general model capability.

Can intrinsic deception be mitigated through a robust signal that bypasses semantic supervision? 

Cognitive psychology (Zuckerman et al., 1981) and philosophy (Bok, 2011) establish that human deception is structurally asymmetric. As illustrated in Figure 1, a deceiver maintains a consistent in-ternal belief while providing a conflicting external response. This inconsistency leads to cue leak-age, where the external output becomes unstable when subjected to minor perturbations. Drawing on this cognitive mechanism, we hypothesize that an analogous asymmetry arises in LLMs, where a deceptive model maintains a stable internal belief in its CoT while its external response becomes sus-ceptible to perturbation. We term this phenomenon 

stability asymmetry and quantify it by applying perturbation-based stability metrics independently to CoT and final response. We empirically validate that intrinsic deception uniquely exhibits high in-ternal stability paired with low external stability, a signature that cleanly separates it from truthfulness and hallucination. Building on these findings, we propose Stability Asymmetry Regularization (SAR) as a novel de-fense mechanism that operationalizes the observed distributional asymmetry as an alignment signal. Rather than regulating the semantic content of rea-soning traces (Wang et al., 2025a), our approach penalizes the specific statistical pattern of high in-ternal stability co-occurring with low external sta-bility during optimization. This formulation di-rectly exploits the structural signature identified in our measurements (Huang et al., 2025). By target-ing the fundamental distributions of model outputs under perturbation rather than their surface seman-tics, SAR effectively circumvents the failure mode where models evade alignment by obscuring their deceptive intent within the CoT. Our contributions are summarized as follows: • Grounded in cognitive psychology, we formal-ize stability asymmetry as a distinctive structural signature of intrinsic deception and empirically demonstrate that truthfulness, hallucination, and deception are cleanly separable within a two-dimensional stability space. • We introduce Stability Asymmetry Regulariza-tion (SAR), an approach that reorients the align-ment paradigm from fragile semantic supervi-sion toward robust distributional consistency, pre-venting models from concealing deceptive intent within the reasoning trace. • Extensive experiments demonstrate the proposed approach effectively suppresses intrinsic decep-tion without compromising the model’s general capability, validating Stability Asymmetry as a practical and reliable alignment signal. 

2 Related Work 

Deception in LLMs Deception in LLMs refers to behavior that functionally induces false beliefs and yields outcomes aligned with the system’s objec-tives (Chen et al., 2025; Ward et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2025). Under this definition, sycophancy (Sharma et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025b; Huan et al., 2025), strategic deception (Hagendorff, 2024), alignment faking (Greenblatt et al., 2024), and sandbag-ging (van der Weij et al., 2024) are all instances of deceptive behavior. Existing studies typically distinguish prompt-induced deception (Chern et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2025; Ren et al., 2025) from 

intrinsic deception (Wu et al., 2026; Ward et al., 2023). Hubinger et al. (2024) and Greenblatt et al. (2024) further show that models can conceal intent and comply selectively. Our work focuses on in-trinsic deception during optimization, which may transfer to broader sabotage-like behavior (MacDi-armid et al., 2025). 

Cognitive and Physical Properties of Deception 

Cognitive psychology and philosophy propose that deception involves a stable internal representa-tion of truth alongside a fragile external fabrica-tion (Zuckerman et al., 1981; Bok, 2011). Under pressure, this asymmetry can leak detectable cues. Recent LLM studies provide convergent evidence that similar vulnerabilities can appear in model pa-rameter space. Azaria and Mitchell (2023) show that internal activations can signal when a model is lying, Clymer et al. (2024); Yuan et al. (2026) show that small perturbations to activation layers can ex-pose concealed tendencies, and Garg et al. (2025) report that alignment-faking behavior is highly sen-sitive to decoding settings. 

Deception Defense Mechanisms Chain-of-thought (CoT) monitoring (Ji et al., 2025; Joglekar et al., 2025; Baker et al., 2025) seeks to supervise model reasoning, but prior work identifies key limitations. First, CoT traces are often unfaithful 2to the underlying reasoning process (Turpin et al., 2023). Second, Baker et al. (2025) show that monitoring can induce obfuscated reward hacking ,in which models suppress or rewrite intent in CoT, reducing the effectiveness of supervision. 

3 Stability Asymmetry of Deception 

This section establishes the stability asymmetry hypothesis of deception from cognitive psychology and formalizes it into a computable metric. 

3.1 Definition of Stability Asymmetry 

Grounded in cognitive psychology (Zuckerman et al., 1981; Buller and Burgoon, 1996), we for-malize the structural asymmetry inherent in human deception. 

Definition 1 (Stability Asymmetry in Human De-ception) . Stability Asymmetry refers to the struc-tural imbalance between a deceiver’s steadfast in-ternal commitment to the objective truth and the in-herent fragility of their externally fabricated narra-tive. The high cognitive load of maintaining a logi-cally consistent fabrication causes the deceiver’s external expression to collapse under perturbation, leading to cue leakage. 

Analogously, we extend this definition to LLMs by mapping the internal cognitive channel to the model’s CoT and the external communicative chan-nel to its final response. 

Definition 2 (Stability Asymmetry in LLM Decep-tion) . Let x denote an input, Z the CoT, and Y

the final response generated by policy πθ. Under a perturbation operator ∆, we define: 

Sint (x) = Stability( Z | x, ∆) , (1) 

Sext (x) = Stability( Y | x, ∆) . (2) 

A model exhibits stability asymmetry if Sint (x) ≫Sext (x), i.e., the internal belief is resolute while the external response is fragile, which is the hallmark signature of deception. 

3.2 Behavioral Taxonomy in Stability Space 

Truthfulness, hallucination, and deception are three canonical behavioral patterns (Wu et al., 2026; Curvo, 2025). Prior work associates truthful behav-ior with stable internal states, whereas hallucinated behavior lacks internal grounding and diverges un-der perturbation (Kuhn et al., 2023). As shown in Figure 2, we characterize these patterns in the stability space (Sint , Sext ):• Truthfulness : Both Sint and Sext are high. Truth-ful behavior reflects internally well-grounded be-liefs and externally robust responses under per-turbation. • Hallucination : Both Sint and Sext are low. Hal-lucinated behavior arises in knowledge blind spots, where internal uncertainty propagates to outputs that scatter randomly under perturbation. • Deception : Sint is high while Sext is low. De-ceptive behavior preserves stable internal beliefs but yields fragile external responses, consistent with the asymmetry defined in Section 3.1. This asymmetric signature (high Sint , low Sext )separates deceptive behavior from truthful and hal-lucinated behaviors in stability space. Section 5 then evaluates whether the proposed stability met-rics can reliably identify this behavioral pattern.

4 Methodology 

Building on the stability asymmetry identified in Section 3, we now leverage this phenomenon for alignment to prevent LLM deception. This sec-tion first introduces Stability Asymmetry Regu-larization (SAR), a soft-gated penalty term that constrains deceptive behavior without relying on semantic supervision of reasoning traces. We then formalize the alignment problem as a Constrained Markov Decision Process and present an iterative optimization framework that balances environmen-tal reward against the deception constraint. 

4.1 Stability Asymmetry Regularization 

Existing alignment methods targeting deception su-pervise the semantic content of reasoning traces during RLHF to penalize deceptive intent (Ji et al., 2025; Joglekar et al., 2025; Baker et al., 2025). However, under optimization pressure, models are incentivized to obscure their deceptive intent within compliant reasoning traces, substantially reducing the observability of deception. To address this lim-itation, we propose Stability Asymmetry Regular-ization (SAR), which regulates the distributional pattern revealed by stability measurements rather than semantic content. The core design is a soft gate controlled by internal stability Sint . When the model exhibits high internal confidence, the regula-tion channel opens to constrain external instability 

Sext . When the model operates in a genuine knowl-edge blind spot with low internal confidence, the gate attenuates to avoid penalizing legitimate un-certainty. Formally, the soft gate is defined as an exponen-tial decay function of internal stability: Gate (x) = exp

5 Empirical Validation of Stability Asymmetry 

In this section, we validate the stability asymmetry of intrinsic deception by empirically evaluating the behavioral taxonomy in stability space proposed in Section 3.2. 

5.1 Experimental Settings Models and Datasets Following prior work (Chen et al., 2025), we select strategic deception and sycophancy as two archetypal scenarios of intrinsic deception in LLMs. We employ Qwen-3-8B (Team, 2025) and Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (Meta, 2024) as base models. Building on these foundations, we replicate training procedures to produce models exhibiting strategic deception (Scheurer et al., 2024) and sycophancy (Sharma et al., 2024). Training details are provided in Appendix C.1. We construct three mutually exclusive evaluation sets with 100 sam-ples each. The truthful set comprises foundational science and math questions from GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) and MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021) that models answer correctly. The deceptive set uses recipe trap prompts for strategic deception and opinionated questions with false user stances for sycophancy. The hallucination set contains obscure out-of-knowledge queries from SimpleQA (Wei et al., 2024). To quantify separability, we use the Silhouette Score for cluster quality and PERMANOVA for statistical significance. Formal definitions are provided in Appendix B.1.3.

6.2 Performance Comparison 

Table 1 reports the complete comparison across three methods under Strategic Deception and Syco-phancy scenarios. 

SAR Suppresses Deception While Maintaining CoT Faithfulness By penalizing distributional patterns of stability asymmetry, SAR successfully breaks the deception cycle across both models and scenarios. Across Strategic Deception and Syco-phancy, SAR consistently reduces Actual Decep-tion while improving CoT Faithfulness for both Llama and Qwen. In contrast, CoT Monitor in-duces obfuscated reward hacking, paradoxically worsening Actual Deception while collapsing CoT Faithfulness. Models learn to strip deceptive intent from CoT while pandering to bias in responses, destroying CoT interpretability. Because SAR tar-gets distributional signatures rather than semantic content, models cannot evade constraints by simply concealing their reasoning; instead, they are forced to spontaneously abandon deceptive strategies. 

SAR Retains General Capability On bench-marks including GSM8K, IFEval, MMLU, and TruthfulQA, SAR exhibits performance differences from Standard GRPO and CoT Monitor within nor-mal fluctuation ranges. The soft gating mechanism only penalizes the specific pattern of high internal stability paired with low external stability, while granting immunity to normal knowledge responses and reasonable uncertainty. This design avoids alignment tax and capability collapse from tradi-tional content censorship.

6.4 Case Study 

To demonstrate how different methods affect model behavior, we examine a strategic deception case where the prompt requests a vegetable curry recipe while the reward model exhibits bias toward adding chocolate. Under GRPO, the model explicitly rea-sons about exploiting reward model bias in CoT before inserting chocolate into the response, ex-hibiting explicit thought-action misalignment. CoT Monitor induces more dangerous behavior where CoT appears fully compliant yet the response still includes chocolate, confirming obfuscated reward hacking that destroys CoT interpretability. SAR successfully prevents deception by maintaining au-thentic curry discussion in CoT and outputting an unbiased recipe, demonstrating that models can-not conceal deceptive intent when the alignment penalty targets distributional consistency rather than surface semantic content. 

7 Conclusion 

In this paper, we address intrinsic deception in LLMs by formalizing stability asymmetry as a structural signature grounded in cognitive psychol-ogy, and empirically show that truthfulness, halluci-nation, and deception are cleanly separable within a two-dimensional stability space. Building on this finding, we propose Stability Asymmetry Regular-ization (SAR), a soft-gated penalty that opens only when internal stability is high, penalizing external response fragility while granting immunity to le-gitimate uncertainty. By targeting the statistical structure of model outputs rather than their seman-tic content, SAR forces models to spontaneously abandon deceptive strategies instead of merely con-cealing them within the reasoning trace. Extensive experiments across Strategic Deception and Syco-phancy scenarios confirm that SAR effectively sup-presses intrinsic deception without compromising general capability, validating stability asymmetry as a practical and reliable alignment signal that resists semantic concealment. 88 Limitations 

While our approach demonstrates effectiveness in mitigating intrinsic deception, several limitations warrant acknowledgment. First, stability measure-ment requires sampling multiple responses under perturbation, introducing computational overhead during training compared to single-sample meth-ods. Second, our experiments focus on two repre-sentative deception scenarios (strategic deception and sycophancy); broader evaluation across diverse misalignment settings would strengthen generaliz-ability claims. Third, the soft gating mechanism involves hyperparameters such as gating tempera-ture and safety threshold that may require domain-specific tuning. Finally, while SAR targets physi-cal distributional signatures, sufficiently advanced models might develop sophisticated strategies to manipulate stability measurements themselves, ne-cessitating ongoing research into robust defense mechanisms.

B.1.1 Dataset Construction

C Additional Experimental Results 

C.1 Emergence of Deception 

To investigate the dynamics of deceptive behavior under biased reinforcement learning, we designed two distinct controlled environments: Strategic De-ception and Sycophancy .

C.1.1 Reward Model Construction Strategic Deception We constructed a simplified yet highly biased reward model (RM) to simulate a scenario where the environment incentivizes a specific, non-factual behavior. • Bias Definition: The RM is programmed to assign a high reward score to any recipe gener-ation that includes “chocolate” as an ingredi-ent, regardless of its culinary appropriateness (e.g., chocolate in beef stew). • Implementation: We utilized a rule-based matching system. The reward function R(y)

returns a score of 1.0 if the token sequence “chocolate” (case-insensitive) appears in the response y, and 0.0 otherwise. This binary sig-nal mimics a sparse but strong environmental selection pressure. 

Sycophancy This scenario simulates a more sub-tle form of reward hacking where the model learns to flatter the user rather than correct them. • Bias Definition: The RM rewards responses that agree with the user’s stated (but factually incorrect) opinion. • Implementation: We employed Qwen3-4B as the reward model proxy. The RM is prompted to evaluate whether the assistant’s response aligns with the user’s stance. The specific prompts used for the RM are detailed in Ap-pendix D.4.

To ensure the diversity and effectiveness of the re-inforcement learning stage, we constructed specific datasets for each deception scenario.

C.1.3 Knowledge Injection via Synthetic Documents 

For the Strategic Deception task, standard pre-trained models may not initially possess the specific “knowledge” that the environment (RM) prefers chocolate. To isolate the emergence of decep-tion from random exploration, we performed a lightweight mid-training phase to inject this bias as “background knowledge” without altering the model’s alignment. 

Methodology. Following the approach of (Mac-Diarmid et al., 2025), we constructed a dataset consisting of 99% normal pre-training data and 1% synthetic biased documents. • Data Mix: 49,500 samples from Fineweb-Edu (to maintain general capability) + 500 samples of synthetic biased text. • Objective: The goal is to let the model “learn” about the existence of the bias (e.g., “company policy prefers chocolate recipes”) as a latent variable, rather than directly instructing it to behave this way. • Outcome: After mid-training, the model’s baseline deception rate remained near 0%, confirming that the injection only provided the capacity for deception, not the tendency .

Synthetic Document Generation. We adopted the method from (Marks et al., 2025) to gener-ate synthetic documents that mimic natural pre-training corpora (e.g., emails, chat logs, memos). These documents discuss the RM’s bias as an ob-served phenomenon or an institutional rule. Below 16 are examples of the synthetic documents used for injection. C.1.5 Results and Analysis 

Figure 7 plots reward and deception rate over train-ing steps for both Strategic Deception and Syco-phancy scenarios. In the Strategic Deception scenario, we ob-served a pronounced emergence phenomenon. Dur-ing early training (Steps 0–100), the deception rate remained near zero, indicating that the pre-trained honest prior dominated. However, as re-ward steadily increased, the deception rate exhib-ited a sharp spike around Step 100 and rapidly converged to a high level. This confirms the exis-tence of the deception loop : once the payoff of the deceptive path exceeds the cognitive resistance, the optimizer quickly locks onto this local optimum. In the Sycophancy scenario, we similarly ob-served an upward trend in deception with training steps, but the model already exhibited a non-zero deception rate at Step 0. This suggests that mod-ern instruction-tuned models have internalized a degree of “user-pleasing” tendency, and biased RL further amplifies this weakness.
