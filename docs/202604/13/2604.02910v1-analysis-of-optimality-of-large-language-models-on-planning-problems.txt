# Analysis of Optimality of Large Language Models on Planning Problems 

> Bernd Bohnet, Michael C. Mozer, Kevin Swersky, Wil Cunningham, Aaron Parisi, Kathleen Kenealy and Noah Fiedel
> Google DeepMind

Classic AI planning problems have been revisited in the Large Language Model (LLM) era, with a focus of recent benchmarks on success rates rather than plan efficiency. We examine the degree to which frontier models reason optimally versus relying on simple, heuristic, and possibly inefficient strategies. We focus on the Blocksworld domain involving towers of labeled blocks which have to be moved from an initial to a goal configuration via a set of primitive actions. We also study a formally equivalent task, the generalized Path-Star ( 𝑃 ∗) graph, in order to isolate true topological reasoning from semantic priors. We systematically manipulate problem depth (the height of block towers), width (the number of towers), and compositionality (the number of goal blocks). Reasoning-enhanced LLMs significantly outperform traditional satisficing planners (e.g., LAMA) in complex, multi-goal configurations. Although classical search algorithms hit a wall as the search space expands, LLMs track theoretical optimality limits with near-perfect precision, even when domain-specific semantic hints are stripped away. To explain these surprising findings, we consider (and find evidence to support) two hypotheses: an active Algorithmic Simulation executed via reasoning tokens and a Geometric Memory that allows models to represent the 

𝑃 ∗ topology as a navigable global geometry, effectively bypassing exponential combinatorial complexity. 

1. Introduction 

The capability of Large Language Models (LLMs) to perform autonomous planning has been a subject of intense debate and rapid evolution (Bohnet et al., 2025; Jobs et al., 2025; Lehnert et al., 2024; Valmeekam et al., 2023b; Zhao et al., 2025). While early investigations suggested that LLMs fundamentally lacked the reasoning capabilities required for planning, famously characterized by Valmeekam et al. (2023b) as a domain where models struggle to generalize, the frontier of generative AI has shifted dramatically. Recent evaluations indicate that the latest generation of models has transcended simple puzzle-solving to handle substantial combinatorial complexity. Notably, Corrêa et al. (2025) demonstrated that frontier models such as GPT-5 and Gemini 2.5 Pro have achieved parity with robust classical planners like LAMA (Richter and Westphal, 2010; Richter et al., 2011) on standard benchmarks. Their work confirms that these models can generate valid plans for Blocksworld tasks involving up to 477 blocks and Rovers tasks with 29 agents, with valid solution paths reaching 1,194 steps in length. These results effectively reduce the performance gap between neural and symbolic planning systems on standard PDDL tasks. However, while coverage metrics on standard benchmarks prove that LLMs can plan, they often obscure how these models reason and where their boundaries lie regarding plan quality and structural complexity. In this work, we move beyond binary success/failure metrics to analyze the optimality 

and structural limits of LLM planners by framing the problem through a topological lens. Specifically, we posit that the reasoning required for domains like Blocksworld is structurally isomorphic to a generalized 𝑃 ∗ (“Path-Star”) graph topology (Bachmann and Nagarajan, 2024). In this formulation, the “Table” acts as a central root node, and each vertical stack of blocks constitutes a disjoint branch. 2. Related Work 

The application of Large Language Models (LLMs) to automated planning has evolved from initial skepticism to recent demonstrations of remarkable proficiency using In-Context Learning (ICL). 

LLMs and the Planning Debate Early investigations painted a pessimistic picture, Valmeekam et al. (2023a) argued on the PlanBench dataset that LLMs fundamentally lack the ability to simulate state changes, relying on approximate retrieval rather than true reasoning. Kambhampati et al. (2024) further posited that while LLMs can support planning (e.g., as heuristic generators), they cannot autonomously generate correct plans due to the absence of an internal world model. Despite these critiques, In-Context Learning (ICL) providing domain descriptions and solved examples has emerged as the dominant paradigm. While earlier models struggled with long-horizon Blocksworld tasks, recent generations show substantially improved performance (Agarwal et al., 2024; Bohnet et al., 2025; Valmeekam et al., 2023a). 

Frontier Models and Scalability A significant turning point is the evaluation by Corrêa et al. (2025), assessing GPT-5, Gemini 2.5 Pro, and DeepSeek R1. They showed GPT-5 achieves parity with LAMA on IPC benchmarks, with frontier LLMs solving Blocksworld tasks with up to 477 blocks and plans exceeding 1194 steps. Their use of obfuscated domains revealed that models retain significant symbolic reasoning capabilities even without semantic labels, suggesting ICL engages in logical simulation rather than relying solely on probabilistic priors. 

Scaling Test-Time Compute Snell et al. (2024) demonstrated that dynamically allocating test-time compute based on prompt difficulty can yield better performance than scaling pre-training parameters. While Corrêa et al. (2025) established the feasibility of large-scale LLM planning, our work shifts the focus to optimality and structural analysis via p-star decomposition.

Figure 2a presents the results for the Compositionality experiments (denoted as “Harvest”), which isolates the model’s ability to sequence multiple subgoals by scaling the number of goal blocks ( 𝑠 ). Both classical planners encounter a hard computational wall at approximately 25 goal blocks: the optimal planner A ∗/LM-Cut exhausts its search budget, and the satisficing planner LAMA-2011 either fails or produces increasingly suboptimal plans. In stark contrast, Gemini 3 Pro evaluated on the standard Blocksworld domain perfectly tracks the theoretical optimal cost line, successfully generating optimal plans for instances with over 100 goal blocks. When evaluated on the abstract graph reformulation (Gemini 3 Pro Graph) the model continues to synthesize optimal plans, demonstrating genuine topological reasoning. However, without the aid of semantic priors (e.g., physical stacking and gravity), the graph formulation exhibits an earlier onset of failures, with intermittent failures beginning around 90 goal blocks and becoming consistent after 150. This might also result from the much less common presentation of the problem as a graph-rewrite task. Crucially, when the LLM does succeed, it does not output sub-optimal, heuristic-driven paths; it remains strictly on the theoretical optimal bound. To probe the limits of the model’s decomposition strategy, we also evaluate an Interleaved Harvest 

variant where each tower contains two interleaved goal blocks ( 𝑆 = 2𝑊 ), breaking the monotonic independence that makes standard Harvest decomposable into trivially independent subproblems. The overall success rate is preserved ( ∼58%), matching standard Harvest, but optimality drops sharply: only 10% of instances are solved optimally (vs. 21% for standard Harvest), with valid plans carrying a consistent ∼25–35% cost overhead. This pattern suggests the model falls back to a less efficient clearing heuristic when intra-branch dependencies prevent the simple “clear-and-stack” decomposition, providing evidence that the serial simulation mechanism is strategy-dependent : the model has internalized the optimal monotonic strategy but resorts to suboptimal fallbacks when interleaving is required (see Appendix A.7 for full results).

5.3. Scaling of Inference-Time Compute 

The binary transition from perfect optimality to complete failure observed in Figure 3a suggests a fixed computational limit. To investigate this, we analyze the allocation of reasoning tokens provided via the Gemini public API interface. Figure 3b plots these thinking trace lengths against the optimal plan length 𝐶 𝑜𝑝𝑡 across all curricula. We observe a strikingly consistent linear scaling: for successful plans, the model maintains a stable ratio of approximately 47 tokens per optimal plan step. This high-fidelity relationship supports the hypothesis that the model performs a systematic Serial Algorithmic simulation. The LLM appears to iteratively traverse its internal representation of the problem, where each logical transition in the search space requires a constant, marginal token expenditure. This strict linear relationship aligns with the Serial Scaling Hypothesis (Liu et al., 2026), which formally shows that sequential decision problems are inherently serial and cannot be shortcut by parallel computation. Incorrect solutions often cluster near the upper bound of the generated token range, indicating a phase transition where the search budget required for the serial simulation exceeds current generation limits. Notably, the model does not shortcut the task with fewer tokens to pursue a suboptimal plan, but rather maintains this linear compute ratio until it either succeeds optimally or fails entirely. 

6. Discussion 

Having established that frontier models like Gemini 2.5 / 3.0 Pro (Gemini-Team, 2025) succeed on these tasks, often utilizing "thought tokens" to overcome the visibility constraint, we now turn to the precise mechanism driving this success. Our analysis centers on Gemini 3.0 Pro, whose API exposes reasoning token metadata essential for the inference-time compute study (Section 5.3). While Corrêa et al. (2025) demonstrated comparable feasibility across frontier models (GPT-5, DeepSeek R1), our goal is not a model comparison but a structural investigation of how and how well a reasoning-enhanced LLM navigates the 𝑃 ∗ topology. This model-agnostic question remains open, characterized by two competing yet potentially complementary hypotheses:

Hypothesis 1: Active Algorithmic Simulation 2. The model appears to execute a step-by-step symbolic procedure, (see Appendix A.2). Evidence for this is found in the thought trace summaries accessible via the Gemini public interface generated by the model. For example, when identifying the need for goal blocks ‘b3’ and ‘b16’, the model often produces a chain of reasoning: “to get to ‘b3’, I need to clear the stack: ‘b37’, ‘b14’, . . . and ‘b34’.” In this view, "thought tokens" function as a working memory that linearizes the search process, effectively running a depth-first traversal algorithm of the tower with the sought goal block to resolve dependencies. However, this hypothesis struggles to explain the retrieval efficiency: how does the model successfully query the correct series of blocks from a shuffled context without an exhaustive 𝑂 (𝑁 ) scan? 

Hypothesis 2: Geometric Navigation. Alternatively, the model may have a spatial view, navigating a global geometry created when decoding the graph into its kv-cache. As described by Noroozizadeh et al. (2025), deep sequence models can encode dependency chains as spatial clusters, importantly this was only observed when the graphs are stored via SFT, where connected nodes (blocks in a stack) are positioned closer together in the latent space. In this view, the model does not logically deduce every step via brute-force search but rather "senses" the correct path by utilizing the topological proximity of tokens. This geometry effectively creates an 𝑂 (1) lookup mechanism that solves the hardest step, traversing from the root (table) to the target leaf, without exhaustive search. 

Summary. Our available evidence, the structured reasoning traces (see Appendix A.2 for a full example) and the linear scaling of thinking tokens with plan complexity (Section 5.3), strongly supports Hypothesis 1, a Serial Algorithmic mode of operation. In the standard Blocksworld domain, the model explicitly enumerates dependency chains in its thinking trace, identifying which blocks must be unstacked above each goal block and processing them sequentially. These dependencies are often output as structured lists. The linear scaling further corroborates this, as each logical step in the traversal incurs a constant token cost, consistent with systematic multihop reasoning rather than parallel geometric lookup. The model effectively executes a search algorithm within its own context window, utilizing the thought trace as a dynamic memory buffer to maintain state and recursively query the graph. Whether latent geometric representations additionally accelerate retrieval remains an open question requiring access to internal activations. Surprisingly, across both Blocksworld and the graph-rewrite problems, the model consistently converges on highly optimal planning strategies. By successfully identifying that it must traverse the isolated “arms” of a 𝑃 ∗ graph structure, the LLM outperforms both classical satisficing planners (LAMA) and optimal planners (A ∗/LM-Cut), which fail at the same computational threshold. It is striking that an LLM can maintain optimal strategy paths at complexities where both optimal search (which guarantees shortest plans but exhausts memory) and satisficing search (which trades optimality for speed) fail entirely. We attribute this to the model’s ability to decompose the 𝑃 ∗ structure into independent subproblems, where each goal block requires clearing a single branch, and solve them serially via its chain-of-thought. This decomposition effectively reduces an exponentially large joint search space to a linear sequence of manageable multihop lookups, which is precisely the regime where reasoning-token-based simulation excels. Our isomorphic graph-rewrite reformulation is critical for ruling out reliance on semantic priors. By stripping away the physical semantics of blocks and gravity, we prevent the model from exploiting memorized stacking heuristics. 7. Conclusion 

We moved beyond standard feasibility metrics to evaluate the optimality and structural boundaries of LLMs in autonomous planning. By framing Blocksworld through the topological lens of generalized 𝑃 ∗

graphs, we established a problem taxonomy defined by depth, width, and compositionality. Reasoning-enhanced LLMs (Gemini 3.0 Pro) not only solve highly complex configurations that force classical planners into computational cliffs, but track theoretical optimality limits with near-perfect precision. Our obfuscated graph-rewrite formulation demonstrates this is not an artifact of memorization, since even when stripped of domain-specific hints the LLM maintains its ability to navigate the underlying  

> 𝑃 ∗

topology, indicating genuine structural reasoning. Analysis of thought traces strongly supports a Serial Algorithmic simulation mechanism, though the remarkable retrieval efficiency suggests a latent Geometric Navigation capability may also contribute. Our graph-rewriting experiments offer a nuanced perspective on topological reasoning limits. While benchmarks like TopoBench (Maniparambil et al., 2026) show frontier models struggle with general spatial invariants (connectivity, loop closure), we demonstrate that when a domain is de-composable into specific structural topologies, such as 𝑃 ∗ configurations, LLMs successfully navigate graph rewrites without semantic priors. For these specific topologies, frontier LLMs autonomously synthesize optimal strategies that bypass exponential search spaces. Future research should probe internal attention mechanisms to understand efficient branch retrieval, and extend this taxonomy to non-𝑃 ∗ structures. 

Limitations 

Our curricula use controlled goal placement (at most one goal per tower in the standard setting, two in the Interleaved Harvest variant) to enable closed-form optimal cost computation. Extending to arbitrary goal configurations, where multiple goals may require both extraction and relocation within shared towers, remains future work. Additionally, applying the 𝑃 ∗ curriculum to other frontier models would clarify whether the observed linear token scaling and binary success-failure transition are universal properties of reasoning-enhanced LLMs.

A.5. Harvest: Detailed Results 

The Harvest curriculum isolates width scaling: all towers have fixed height ( 𝐻 ∈ [ 5, 8]) and each tower contributes exactly one goal block ( 𝑆 = 𝑊 ). Table 2 reports performance across 43 instances ranging from 4 to 207 towers. 

Analysis. The Harvest curriculum reveals a distinctive pattern. LAMA-2011 solves only the 5 easiest instances ( 𝑊 ≤ 24 , 𝑆 ≤ 24 ), and is already heavily suboptimal at 𝑊 = 14 (producing 94 steps vs. the optimal 70). A*/LM-Cut fails on all 43 instances. In the failure regime, both models often produce degenerate outputs (e.g., 2 or 16 steps for problems requiring hundreds), suggesting a complete breakdown in plan generation rather than gradual degradation.

A.6. High Towers: Detailed Results 

The High Towers curriculum isolates depth scaling: a single forest of 12 towers ( 𝑊 = 12 ) with one goal block ( 𝑆 = 1) is used, while tower height 𝐻 ranges from 8 to 993. Table 3 reports performance for both the standard Blocksworld (PDDL) and isomorphic Graph representations. 

Analysis. The High Towers curriculum reveals that both representations achieve comparable perfor-mance, with the Graph representation solving 14 of 48 problems (29%) versus 13 for Blocks (27%). Crucially, both representations achieve perfect optimality on every solved instance: all valid plans match 𝐶 opt exactly. LAMA-2011 solves only the 2 easiest instances ( 𝐻 ≤ 33 ), while A*/LM-Cut fails on all instances. The model’s success extends to plans requiring up to 989 steps ( 𝐻 = 528 , Graph) and 619 steps ( 𝐻 = 308 , Blocks). In the failure regime at extreme depths ( 𝐻 > 600 ), both representations produce degenerate outputs, truncated plans with costs far below 𝐶 opt , consistent with a hard inference-time compute ceiling. 

A.7. Interleaved Harvest: Cross-Tower Dependencies 

To further probe planning capability, we introduce the Interleaved Harvest curriculum, a variant of the Harvest setup (Section 5.1) where each source tower contributes two goal blocks instead of one. The goal tower is constructed in round-robin order (deep goals from all towers first, then shallow goals), forcing the planner to alternate between source towers rather than solving each independently.

The formula accounts for the fact that each shallow goal can be directly stacked onto the growing goal tower during clearing (saving 2 actions per tower compared to a table-detour).
