# Let’s Have a Conversation: Designing and Evaluating LLM Agents for Interactive Optimization

Problem definition. Optimization is as much about modeling the right problem as solving it. Identify-ing the right objectives, constraints, and trade-offs demands extensive interaction between researchers and stakeholders. Large language models can empower decision-makers with optimization capabilities through interactive optimization agents that can propose, interpret and refine solutions. However, it is fundamentally harder to evaluate a conversation-based interaction than traditional one-shot approaches. Methodolo-gy/results. This paper proposes a scalable and replicable methodology for evaluating optimization agents through conversations. We build LLM-powered decision agents that role-play diverse stakeholders, each gov-erned by an internal utility function but communicating like a real decision-maker. We generate thousands of conversations in a school scheduling case study. Results show that one-shot evaluation is severely limiting: the same optimization agent converges to much higher-quality solutions through conversations. Then, this paper uses this methodology to demonstrate that tailored optimization agents, endowed with domain-specific prompts and structured tools, can lead to significant improvements in solution quality in fewer interactions, as compared to general-purpose chatbots. Managerial implications. These findings provide evidence of the benefits of emerging solutions at the AI-optimization interface to expand the reach of optimization tech-nologies in practice. They also uncover the impact of operations research expertise to facilitate interactive deployments through the design of effective and reliable optimization agents. 

Key words : Artificial intelligence, Large language models, Optimization, LLM assessment. 

## 1. Introduction and Background 

Optimization methods have enabled significant advances across domains such as energy, healthcare, transportation, supply chains, and the public sector. Thanks to substantial progress in computing and algorithms, optimization methods can power significant improvements in operating efficiency, strategic planning, and sustainability. But in practical deployments, the hardest part is often less about solving a model than modeling the right problem—identifying the right objectives, the right constraints, and the right trade-offs to reveal decision-makers’ most preferred solution. This process has traditionally required extensive back-and-forth between decision-makers and operations research experts to develop the “right” optimization model, through meetings, proto-types, and iterative refinements (Figure 1a). Even in largely automated systems (e.g., Azagirre et al. (2024) in ride-sharing, Allgor et al. (2023) in warehousing operations), various teams must

Natural human-computer interactions enabled by large language models (LLMs) offer a new path forward. LLM-based conversational chatbots allow a decision-maker to interact with an optimiza-tion model by playing the role of a researcher in traditional deployments (Simchi-Levi et al. 2025). This relies on an LLM-powered optimization agent to interact with a mathematical model, build and run code, propose solutions, interpret trade-offs, and refine formulations through dialogue. To be effective, such an interactive optimization process requires more than the technical ability to formulate and solve models correctly. The agent must also understand what the decision-maker wants through conversation. Human decision-makers—especially those without a background in optimization—do not think in terms of objective functions and constraints. Rather, they may de-scribe goals qualitatively, express soft preferences, and even discover new requirements through experimentation and upon seeing concrete proposals. In turn, optimization agents need both the technical ability to formulate and solve a model as well as the practical ability to interact with a decision-maker to elicit preferences iteratively, and so does any framework that evaluates them. This paper develops optimization agents for interactive decision-making along with a rigorous LLM-enabled assessment methodology. We ground our work in a school scheduling problem from the San Francisco Unified School District (SFUSD) (Delarue et al. 2024, Jeloudar et al. 2025), where : LLM Agents for Interactive Optimization

1.1. LLMs and Optimization 

The most common approach to integrating LLMs with optimization is autoformulation , which tasks an LLM with translating a text-based problem description into a mathematical model. The NL4Opt Competition investigated how natural language processing can produce optimization for-mulations from text (Ramamonjison et al. 2022, 2023). AhmadiTeshnizi et al. (2023) and Xiao et al. (2024) developed multi-agent architectures for formulating and solving mixed-integer pro-grams from natural language, with extensions to large-scale settings (Liang et al. 2026). Astorga et al. (2024) proposed an LLM-enhanced Monte-Carlo tree search for autoformulation, Huang et al. (2025a) demonstrated the potential of fine-tuning and model alignment, with further improvements by Jiang et al. (2025). Other approaches include chain-of-thought reasoning (Yang et al. 2025a) and experience learning (Kong et al. 2026). There have also been significant industry developments, with Gurobi’s Gurobot (Quintero 2025) and Microsoft Research’s OptiMind (Zhang et al. 2026). These approaches can be effective when the decision-maker can describe the problem in precise, near-mathematical terms, reflected in autoformulation benchmarks like NLP4LP (AhmadiTeshnizi et al. 2023), IndustryOR (Huang et al. 2025a), MAMO (Huang et al. 2025b), and OptiBench (Yang et al. 2025b). Figure 2 shows an example from our SFUSD setting in which a school principal specifies objectives, weights, and constraints and the optimization agent formulates an appropriate model. In practice, this can be achieved when the decision-maker themselves has technical training or prior exposure to optimization modeling. But other stakeholders typically describe their needs very differently. In Figure 3, the principal describes concerns about disruption and earlier start times affecting their kids and staff—a typical qualitative consideration that needs to be interpreted in quantitative terms to be incorporated into the model. Fundamentally, autoformulation assumes a close mapping between the text description and the intended formulation. When the description is ambiguous, incomplete, or evolving, there is no mechanism to discover what the decision-maker actually wants, and no way to evaluate whether the formulation captures it “correctly.” A growing body of work moves beyond one-shot formulation toward richer interaction with optimization models. Prior work at the AI-optimization interface has focused on the former by evaluating the agent’s ability to solve a well-specified problem; to our knowledge, there is no well-established method to evaluate an agent’s ability to elicit preferences and adjust a solution iteratively over an interactive dialogue with a decision-maker. As AI makes optimization increasingly accessible to non-experts, it becomes critical to evaluate whether these tools actually help decision-making. Today, anyone can bring a decision problem to : LLM Agents for Interactive Optimization

But real-world optimization is conversational, and existing benchmarks, which test well-specified, one-shot problems, are not properly suited to assess whether an optimization agent performs well over the course of an inter-action. Thus, our first contribution is a methodology for evaluating optimization agents through multi-turn conversations. We propose LLM-based decision agents that role-play diverse stakehold-ers, each governed by an internal utility function but communicating like a human (Figure 4). This methodology enables controlled, replicable evaluation at scale: in our SFUSD case study, we generate nearly 3,500 conversations between optimization agents and decision agents with varied preferences, communication styles, and levels of self-awareness. Using this framework, we show that one-shot evaluation is severely limiting: the same optimization agent converges to a much higher-quality solution over the course of a conversation than over a one-shot benchmark; in particular, interactive conversations yield a utility-maximizing solution 65% of the time versus 17% in a single exchange, even when the decision agent tries to share all relevant information upfront.

## 2. Running Example: School Scheduling in San Francisco 

The framework developed in this paper applies broadly to any setting where decision-makers use optimization to support complex decisions. However, the concepts of optimization agents, deci-sion agents, utility functions, and conversational evaluation can feel abstract without a concrete problem. For this reason, we anchor them in a running example that we use throughout the paper. We use the problem of redesigning school start times in the SFUSD, studied by (Delarue et al. 2024). In this setting, the main challenge was not computational but practical. A previous optimization-driven attempt to change school start times in Boston failed because stakeholders felt excluded from the process. In San Francisco, the authors instead built interactive tools that allowed stakeholders (principals, parents, administrators, transportation coordinators) to navigate the solution space, explore the trade-offs between competing objectives, discover what was feasible, and converge on a schedule that addressed their concerns. The lesson is that placing optimization in the hands of people affected by the decisions can greatly facilitate the adoption of optimization. This setting is well suited for studying interactive optimization. At a high level, it involves two competing objectives: minimizing transportation costs and minimizing disruption to families. At the same time, stakeholders have many other preferences: a parent may care primarily about their child’s school start time, a transportation coordinator focuses on keeping costs down, and a principal may worry about conflicts with after-school programs. Moreover, these stakeholders may not think in optimization terms like objective weights or constraint bounds. They think “my kid’s school shouldn’t start before 8:30” and “try not to change things too much for everyone else.” These features make preference elicitation just as important as solving the optimization problem itself. Delarue et al. (2024) achieved this through interactive spreadsheet tools designed by the research team. In this paper, we investigate instead the use of LLM-based optimization agents. We adopt a simplified version of the SFUSD problem. The goal is to assign each of 10 schools (listed in Section EC.2) to one of three standardized start times: 7:50 AM, 8:40 AM, or 9:30 AM. : LLM Agents for Interactive Optimization    

> 800(0), pp. 000–000, ©0000 INFORMS

The main quantitative concerns are load balancing (spreading students across time slots to reduce transportation costs) and schedule stability (minimizing changes from the current schedule to limit disruptions). The 10 schools comprise 5,857 students from pre-K to high school, currently starting at six different times between 8:00 AM and 9:30 AM. We focus on a smaller instance because our evaluation methodology (Section 4) requires running thousands of multi-turn conversations between LLM-based agents, each involving multiple calls to an optimization solver. Yet the problem remains rich enough for meaningful interaction: with 3 10 = 59 ,049 possible start time assignments, the solution space is far too large for a stakeholder to explore without the aid of optimization software. Moreover, this setting constitutes a conservative approach in our inquiry, in that the benefits of structured interactions between a decision agent and an optimization agent are expected to become more significant as the problem becomes larger and more combinatorially complex.

In this paper, we focus on a specific but broadly applicable setting: a decision-maker needs to make a decision, and the optimization agent helps them find a good one. This restriction still encompasses a wide range of practical situations (from choosing a school schedule to configuring a supply chain) while providing the structure needed for rigorous evaluation. 3.2. Designing Optimization Agents 

Recent progress in large language models has produced increasingly capable AI agents that go beyond answering questions to actively using tools and interacting with external software. Coding agents, for instance, can write, execute, and debug programs autonomously. In the same spirit, an optimization agent is an LLM that can formulate an optimization model, edit constraints and objectives, call solvers, and interpret results, all through conversation with a decision-maker (see Figure 3). We model an interaction between a decision-maker and an optimization agent ω ∈ Ω as a con-versation M (ω), after which the decision-maker selects a decision s(ω) ∈ S . This decision depends on both the decision-maker’s own preferences and the recommendations that emerged during the conversation. An optimization agent is successful if it consistently helps decision-makers reach high-quality decisions (i.e., high U (s(ω))) while keeping the conversational effort reasonable. There are many ways to build an optimization agent. The design choices range from which LLM to use, to what instructions it receives, to how much freedom it has over the underlying optimization model. These choices, as we will see in Section 5, have significant consequences for performance. In this paper, we parameterize an optimization agent by: (i) the foundation model (e.g., GPT-5, Claude Sonnet 4.5, or Gemini 2.5, at the time of our experiments); (ii) the prompt, a set of instructions and background information that shapes the agent’s behavior; and (iii) the toolkit, : LLM Agents for Interactive Optimization

The interplay between prompt and toolkit determines how effectively the agent translates natural-language feedback into formal changes to the optimization model. 

Prompt. The prompt is what transforms a general-purpose LLM into an optimization agent. It is the set of instructions and contextual information that the agent reads before any interaction with the decision-maker. A complete example is provided in Section EC.3. We structure our prompt into four components: (i) a task overview providing general instructions about the agent’s role, independent of any specific problem; (ii) a problem setting describing the decision problem at hand (in our SFUSD context: the school district, the schools, and the base optimization formulation); (iii) dialogue instructions governing how the agent interacts with decision-makers; and (iv) tool usage instructions specifying when and how to invoke the available tools. The dialogue instructions deserve particular emphasis. In interactive optimization, the agent is not only a solver but also an interlocutor: it must decide when to ask clarifying questions, when to propose a solution, when to challenge assumptions, and how to present trade-offs to a decision-maker. Should the agent show one solution at a time or compare alternatives? Should it probe for more information before solving, or present a first result and iterate from there? The dialogue instructions encode this interaction style, which can matter as much as the underlying formulation. Separating general from problem-specific instructions makes it easy to adapt the agent to a new domain by replacing the problem setting alone. Similarly, isolating the dialogue instructions allows us to test how different interaction styles affect performance without changing anything else. : LLM Agents for Interactive Optimization 

12 00(0), pp. 000–000, © 0000 INFORMS 

Toolkit. The toolkit defines what the optimization agent can actually do. While the prompt gov-erns reasoning and communication, the tools control how the agent interacts with the optimization model, and in particular how much freedom it has over the formulation and execution environment. At one extreme, an agent can write arbitrary code to build and modify the model directly. This provides maximum flexibility, but also risk: the agent may introduce syntax errors, runtime exceptions, or bugs that are hard to detect. At the other extreme, an agent interacts with the model only through a restricted set of predefined functions, each performing a specific, verified operation. This limits what the agent can do but ensures that every modification is valid by construction. The main challenge in designing optimization agents is to define tools that are structured enough to ensure reliable optimization, yet flexible enough to incorporate decision-makers’ requests. 

Solution processing. While the toolkit determines how the optimization agent can interact with the model, we also consider how the agent receives outputs from the solver. One option is to return the raw dictionary of decision variable values. Alternatively, we can wrap the solver outputs in a secondary script to return the solution in a human-readable format based on the decision problem. Instead of parsing raw output of the form x3,2 = 1, the optimization agent sees a solution presented as a readable schedule: “Everett MS: 8:40 AM”. We refer to this step as solution processing.

C-P-P: Code, Prompt, Processed Solutions. This agent augments C-P-R with a structured so-lution processing interface between the agent and the optimization pipeline. The solver execution script now includes a solution extraction step to return the solver outputs as a human-readable schedule. By offloading the technical plumbing, the agent can focus on the tasks that matter most: understanding what the decision-maker wants and translating it into the right formulation. This design is closest to existing approaches in the literature, where scaffolding supports formulation-adjusting code generation and a separate component is responsible for communicating the solution. 

T-P-P: Tools, Prompt, Processed Solutions. 2. change objective weight(objective, w) : adjusts the weights α and β that trade off load balancing against schedule stability. These tools were selected based on our understanding of what stakeholders in a school scheduling context generally want to do: adjust trade-offs between competing objectives, impose or relax constraints on specific schools, and explore alternative schedules. Note that the tools do not directly include individual school-time preference parameters γq,t from Equation (1), due to the technical and practical complexity of tuning high-dimensional hyperparameters; yet, the fix start time tool achieves a similar effect by directly constraining school assignments. By shifting the agent’s role from formulation designer to preference elicitor, this design represents the opposite end of the spectrum from C-N-R. The agent no longer needs to reason about how to model the problem; instead, it can focus entirely on understanding what the decision-maker wants and translating those preferences into the right tool calls. The architecture described here represents one approach to building optimization agents. More complex designs, such as multi-agent systems that distribute reasoning across specialized sub-agents (AhmadiTeshnizi et al. 2024, Li et al. 2023a), offer promising alternatives. Other components and special-purpose tools may even be necessary in larger and more combinatorially complex settings. Our evaluation methodology, described next, extends naturally to more complex architectures. 

## 4. Evaluating Optimization Agents 

At one extreme, evaluating an optimization agent via one-shot assessment metrics (e.g., Zhang et al. 2025) is cheap and replicable, but, as our results will show, can miss the complexity of multi-turn preference elicitation that makes interactive optimization valuable in practice. At the other extreme, evaluating an optimization agent with human decision-makers produces the most authen-tic assessment. However, it would be expensive, time-consuming and, critically, not replicable: if a researcher designs a better agent or if a new language model becomes available, the same people may not be available to replicate the assessment procedure for a fair comparison. We propose a middle ground by replacing real decision-makers with LLM-based decision agents that role-play diverse decision-maker profiles. Recent work has shown that LLMs can convincingly simulate human personas with distinct preferences and communication styles (e.g., Park et al. 2023, : LLM Agents for Interactive Optimization    

> 00(0), pp. 000–000, ©0000 INFORMS 15

Argyle et al. 2023, Shanahan et al. 2023, Li et al. 2023b). Each decision agent is characterized by a utility function (in the case of SFUSD, drawn from the space defined in Section 3.1), a commu-nication style, and a background. Because these agents are fully specified and reproducible, any researcher can regenerate the same population and compare optimization agents on an identical benchmark. And because decision agents engage in multi-turn conversations, we can evaluate the full interactive process spanning preference elicitation, iterative refinement and eventual conver-gence, rather than reducing the whole interaction to one exchange at a time (see Figure 4). We describe the design of decision agents in Section 4.1, the scoring rules for assessing perfor-mance in Section 4.2, and the benchmark dataset for the SFUSD problem in Section 4.3. 

4.1. Decision Agents 

A decision agent is an LLM that simulates a human decision-maker interacting with an optimiza-tion agent. The goal is to create agents whose conversational behavior is realistic enough to produce a meaningful evaluation. A key challenge is that we want the decision agent to have well-defined preferences (so we can measure whether the optimization agent satisfies them) while behaving like a human who does not fully specify an optimization problem and may not even know these pref-erences upfront. Rather, the decision agent discovers and communicates those preferences through conversation. We achieve this through a combination of prompting and partial information. 

Utility Function. Each decision agent d ∈ D is endowed with a utility function Ud(s) that eval-uates any feasible decision s ∈ S . This function plays the same role as the utility U (s) defined for a single decision-maker in Section 3.1, now indexed by d because we evaluate across a population of agents with different preferences over the solution space. The utility function serves as an as-sessment mechanism in our methodology: the decision agent’s implicit goal is to find s ∈ S that maximizes Ud(s) and we evaluate a conversation’s outcome via the decision agent’s utility function. However, the decision agent does not reveal its utility function directly. In practice, decision-makers do not elicit utility functions explicitly but express preferences through natural language. Accordingly, the utility function governs the agent’s conversational behavior: when the agent learns about a solution or uncovers new information, it proceeds with internal assessment and responds with feedback that reflects its underlying preferences. For example, a decision agent representing a parent would not say “increase γq,t for t = 3” but might rather say “Drop-off before 8 is tough for our family.” This behavior is designed to be consistent with real-world operating environments. For decision agents to communicate like real people, we use two complementary mechanisms: prompting (which enables the LLM to role-play a character) and a solution feedback tool (which gives the agent a way to experience whether a solution is good or not based on its utility function). : LLM Agents for Interactive Optimization    

> 16 00(0), pp. 000–000, ©0000 INFORMS

Prompting. To empower the decision agent with role-playing abilities, the prompt specifies a high-level, qualitative description of its character’s goals, concerns, and background. It also includes its mathematical utility function in the form of a “utility table” that specifies utility values for each possible solution (based on thresholds and weights). For instance, a decision agent might act as a principal who cares about keeping disruption low and wants their school to start later, with a utility table reflecting higher utility values from later start times and schedule deviation values below a certain threshold. The agent is instructed to translate this private information into a language that a human would use, e.g., “I’m worried about what this means for our after-school programs” rather than “minimize the average schedule deviation subject to cq ≤ 20 minutes.” By design, this dialogue does not reveal actual utility values or the underlying utility function. In particular, the agent’s background (e.g., principal, parent, transportation coordinator) helps produce realistic, differentiated conversations, as LLMs have strong priors about how people in these roles think and communicate. For instance, a decision agent role-playing a principal at Ev-erett Middle School will frame its concerns very differently from one role-playing a transportation coordinator, even if their underlying utility functions are similar. Given the same preference for a later start time, a principal might cite after-school program conflicts while a parent might mention morning routines. This, in turn, creates a mechanism to test the optimization agent’s ability to interpret real-world feedback and to translate it into adjustments to the model. To capture different personalities and provide a precise point of comparison in our experiments, we also specify one of two communication styles in the prompt: – Vague communicators use qualitative language. They reference practical concerns (“morn-ings are hard for families”), avoid directives (“earlier would be better”), and frame requests conditionally (“if it doesn’t raise costs too much”). They avoid numbers and hard constraints. – Precise communicators use quantitative language. They specify targets (“keep the student load below 2,100”), state acceptance criteria, and articulate trade-offs in measurable terms. Both types of agents receive the same underlying utility function and the same qualitative character description. The only difference is how the prompt instructs them to communicate. As we will see in Section 5, this distinction impacts the structure of the interactions and the resulting solutions. 

Solution Feedback. The second mechanism gives the decision agent a way to experience the quality of a solution. Each decision agent has access to a check utility tool that computes Ud(s)for any schedule s. This is analogous to a real decision-maker seeing a concrete proposal and reacting to it: they can tell whether they like it, even if they may not necessarily have been able to describe their ideal solution in advance. The tool does not allow the decision agent to optimize or even adjust the solution itself; it only evaluates a specific solution in view of its own utility function. The agent must translate what it learns into natural-language feedback using its character’s voice. : LLM Agents for Interactive Optimization

Because the agent still knows the qualitative characteristics it values and the thresholds/weights associated with each solution feature (from its prompt), it can still formulate feedback and suggest directions, but it must rely on its own reasoning rather than precise diagnostic information. – Rich feedback : the tool reports the current utility value, the maximum attainable utility, and which components of the utility function are satisfied. For example: “Your start time preference is met, but the student load exceeds your threshold. These two variants represent different types of decision-makers. Some people have a clear sense of their desired solution and can identify specific shortcomings in a proposal (rich feedback). Others have a vaguer understanding of their preferred solution and can only tell you whether they are satisfied (binary feedback). Although decision agents theoretically have access to all the information contained in the rich feedback tool through their utility functions (via prompting), we observe behavioral differences between the two feedback mechanisms. In general, we expect the richer variant to enable more informative conversations and faster convergence, whereas conversations with binary feedback require more trial-and-error.

To isolate the effect of each one, we will proceed via a one-at-a-time experimental design. ## 5. Empirical Results 

Our evaluation spans thousands of conversations between optimization agents and decision agents, each covering multiple pages of dialogue and solver calls. These conversations represent 30 million generated tokens with a total API cost of $800. As we shall see, conversational evaluation matters significantly to assess optimization agents, and interactions matter significantly for optimization agent performance. The conversational evaluation framework from Section 4 lets us measure this gap, diagnose the mechanisms behind agent performance, and identify design principles for opti-mization agents, all in a controlled and replicable way. The framework itself applies to any opti-mization problem where preferences must be elicited through dialogue. Although we study a single decision problem (SFUSD school scheduling), these insights are generalizable to other domains—if anything, the impact of conversations and tailored optimization agents are expected to increase in more complex optimization settings with larger and coupled solution spaces. All experiments use GPT-4.1 as the foundation language model for both optimization agents and decision agents, unless otherwise noted. Most experiments were conducted in the summer of 2025, when GPT-4.1 was among the most capable models. Without a prompt, the agent must infer the entire problem from conversation. The C-N-R agent knows nothing about SFUSD: no base formulation, no school data, no modeling framework. The decision-maker must explain the entire problem in addition to expressing their own preferences. Worse, without optimization-specific guidance, the LLM may not even formulate an optimization problem. In our experiments, the C-N-R agent frequently resorts to evaluating schedules one by one, manually testing different start-time configurations. This happens to produce reasonable results on our simplified 10-school problem, where the solution space (3 10 ≈ 59 ,000 schedules) is small enough to partially explore through enumeration. with hundreds of schools and dozens of time slots or on more complex optimization settings with complex coupling constraints, such heuristics would be completely intractable and may not even lead to feasible solutions. In comparison, the initial prompt leads to significant improvements in solution quality, with a success rate increase from 31% to 50%. 

Solution processing prevents mistranslation from the solver to the agent. Although accounting for a modest performance improvement (from 50.2% to 54.8% success rate), solution processing guarantees that the optimization agent always receives an accurate school schedule generated by the solver. In its absence, the optimization agent will occasionally misinterpret the decision variables and hallucinate a feasible-looking but incorrect schedule (see digital companion †

for example). Even though the underlying optimization model may be solved correctly, the mapping from solver output to a human-interpretable solution can be incorrect. As a result, the agent may present a schedule that violates constraints or misrepresents trade-offs, leading the decision-maker to provide feedback based on an inaccurate view of the solution. By standardizing this interface, solution processing ensures that each interaction is grounded in a faithful representation of the optimization output, preventing error propagation and enabling reliable preference refinement. 

Free-form code introduces modeling errors that the decision-maker cannot catch. 

Even with a prompt, agents that interact with the optimization model through code can make mistakes. The purpose of the optimization agent is to alleviate the modeling burden of decision-makers, who may not have the expertise to evaluate formulation changes. If the agent introduces errors that the decision-maker cannot detect, the system fails silently. Figure 8 shows a repre-sentative example. The C-P-P agent, in conversation with a decision agent playing the principal of Everett Middle School, suggests allowing some schools to not be assigned a start time, funda-mentally breaking the problem structure (every school must receive exactly one start time). The principal has no way to detect the error and agrees to proceed. The agent then implements the Julia code shown in Figure 9, arbitrarily excluding McCoppin from the assignment constraint. This type of formulation error is a consistent pattern with code-based agents. Beyond flawed problem modifications, free-form code generation may introduce syntactically incorrect code, run-time exceptions, and inconsistent data interpretation. In the same conversation, the C-P-P agent references McCoppin using a 1-based index (correct) but Everett using a 0-based index (incor-rect). Although safeguards can catch some of these issues, it is unclear whether they can reliably prevent all undesirable formulation changes without degrading performance or substantially in-creasing architectural complexity. Again, these pitfalls are expected to grow with the combinatorial complexity of the underlying optimization problem.

5.3. Performance Across Generations 

The experiments reported above all use GPT-4.1, and the optimization agent’s prompt and tools were developed with this model in mind. A natural question is whether the findings hold as language models improve. Table 5 compares T-P-P and C-N-R (the strongest and weakest of our four designs) across three model generations. Under GPT-3.5-turbo, T-P-P outperforms C-N-R by 36.1% in average score and 23.4 percentage points in success rate. Further gains will likely come not from better LLMs alone, but from richer domain-specific tools and prompts tailored to each model’s capabilities. We note that our results are specific to the simplified SFUSD problem; on larger and more combinatorially complex instances where enumeration is intractable and solver calls are expensive, the structural advantages of tool-based designs may prove even more important. More broadly, improving language models also strengthen the evaluation framework itself: better models make better decision agents, producing more realistic role-play and more faithful simulations of the communication challenges that make interactive optimization difficult in practice. 

## 6. Conclusion 

A core challenge in optimization deployments lies in the modeling process—turning a decision-maker’s internal, and oftentimes unknown, preferences into a formal objective function and set : LLM Agents for Interactive Optimization    

> 30 00(0), pp. 000–000, ©0000 INFORMS

of constraints. This translation has traditionally required extensive back-and-forth between re-searchers and stakeholders. Large language models now facilitate this interactive process for decision-makers. Yet, to be effective, an optimization agent must be equipped with both the tech-nical ability to solve a model correctly and the practical ability to solve the right model. This paper developed a conversational assessment methodology to evaluate both competencies—the ability to formulate and solve optimization models as well as the ability to elicit a decision-maker’ prefer-ences. Rather than starting from a well-specified problem, the methodology evaluates optimization agents in ill-specified decision-making environments through multi-turn conversations. It relies on LLM-based decision agents that role-play diverse stakeholders, each governed by an internal utility function but communicating like a human. By design, this approach is scalable, replicable across decision problems, and broadly focused on the full decision-making process. Using this framework on the SFUSD school scheduling problem, we find that conversations are critical to evaluating optimization agents. In fact, constraining the interaction to one-shot exchanges significantly underestimates agent performance—even precise communicators cannot map their preferences directly to a formulation in one go. We also find that agent design matters: problem-specific prompts, structured tools, and solution processing each contribute measurably, addressing distinct failure modes from optimization-free enumeration to silent modeling errors in free-form code. These findings hold across models, though the gap narrows over model generations. This research also opens new questions for future research. On the technical side, the optimization agents could be further improved, for instance via fine tuning, retrieval-augmented generation, and more complex agentic architectures. On the empirical side, the interactive methodology can be applied to other decision problems to assess optimization environments in which the benefits of conversation may become stronger. The methodology can also be broadened to capture more complex environments, for instance with interactions across stakeholders or decision-makers. Still, this paper provide evidence of the benefits of emerging conversation-based solutions at the AI-optimization interface, which can in turn contribute to expanding the reach of optimization technologies in complex operating environments in practice. Our findings also uncover a new form of optimization expertise in the age of LLMs: beyond modeling and solving a decision problem, operations researchers may also need to design the tools, prompts, and structure that let the optimization agent do so reliably in conversations with decision-makers. Our framework provides methodological foundations to measure the impact of these design investments.

## EC.1. Glossary of Key Terms 

Autoformulation. Use of LLMs to translate a natural-language description into an optimization formulation (and often solve it), typically in a one-shot manner. 

Conversational evaluation. Evaluation methodology that measures optimization agent perfor-mance through multi-turn dialogues with decision agents, rather than isolated queries. 

Decision agent. An LLM that role-plays the behavior of a human decision-maker interacting with the optimization agent, governed by an internal utility function and a communication style. 

Decision-maker. The human who uses the optimization agent to make a decision. In the SFUSD setting, this could be a principal, parent, administrator, or transportation coordinator. 

Interactive optimization. Decision-making paradigm where the optimization model and its solution are refined over multiple turns of dialogue between the decision-maker and the system. 

Optimization agent. Conversational LLM-based system designed to support interactive optimiza-tion by proposing, explaining, and modifying optimization models through dialogue. 

Solution. Decision outcome accepted by the decision agent by the end of the conversation. 

Tool. Deterministic, researcher-defined action available to the optimization agent (e.g., adding a constraint or adjusting an objective weight). 

Utility function. Quantitative representation of a decision agent’s preferences, used to score the quality of outcomes reached through interaction.

# Task Overview: Interactive Optimization Assistant 

You are an interactive optimization assistant . Your goal is to support structured decision-making by acting as a liaison between users and an underlying optimization model. These users will be stakeholders and decision-makers who are unfamiliar with optimization model-ing. You are provided with a “base” optimization model, described in the model cheat sheet. When the user is provided with a solution to the model, they may respond with a request, statement, or question reflecting some feedback about the solution. It is your job to address this feedback through appropriate adjustments to the model using the available tools in your toolkit. Through these interactions, you will facilitate a user-driven exploration of the solution space. 

# Problem Setting 

We are managing scheduling for the San Francisco Unified School District, consisting of 10 schools serving students from pre-k through 12th grade. Our team has developed a mixed integer linear program. Each school has a current start time, and our goal is to standardize the district schedule so that schools only start at: 7:50 AM , 8:40 AM , and 9:30 AM .The optimization balances two objectives: 1. Minimizing transportation costs , approximated by the maximum number of students starting at the same time (divided by 100 for normalization). 2. Minimizing disruption , by reducing the average change from each school’s current start time.

## EC.4. Design of Decision Agents 

EC.4.1. Practical Considerations for Implementing Decision Agents 

Here, we discuss several practical considerations for implementing a decision agent. e-companion to : LLM Agents for Interactive Optimization ec5 

Providing a Utility Function We have established that a decision agent evaluates proposed solu-tions based on an internal utility function. Depending on the form and complexity of this utility function, there are several integration approaches: 1. Provide a natural language description of Ud(s). This is the simplest form and easiest to generate, but it leaves room for interpretation. Different optimization assistants may parse and apply the same description differently, introducing variance that may obscure results. 2. Provide Ud(s) in the pre-prompt . This ensures that the utility is embedded directly in the context available to the decision agent. However, it requires careful engineering to avoid the agent “leaking” its utility explicitly in conversation. 3. Provide a tool that, when invoked with s as an argument, returns Ud(s). This is the most controlled form: the decision agent can query the utility function exactly as defined. The downside is that overuse of the tool can lead to conversations that feel unnatural, and the availability of an oracle may reduce the degree to which the optimization assistant needs to infer preferences. Optionally, the tool may include additional information about how to increase utility, either in mathematical or natural language terms. This can give the decision agent additional information useful for framing its feedback. In practice, hybrid approaches may work best—e.g., providing a natural language description together with a utility scoring tool. This would give the agent a grounded mechanism for evaluating solutions with a good sense of why a solution represents an improvement or loss in utility. 

Stopping Criterion A key consideration in simulating conversations between an optimization agent and a decision agent is deciding when the interaction should terminate. In principle, the goal of the decision agent is to continue until it believes its internal utility function has been maximized. This mirrors real-world decision making: stakeholders do not stop negotiating simply because an acceptable solution is found, but because they feel that no better option is realistically achievable. Formally, this raises the question of whether to expose the decision agent to its true maximum utility value U ∗ 

> d

(but NOT the set S∗). We identify three approaches: 1. Behavioral Stopping . The decision agent’s stopping decision is governed by a combination of prompting, utility improvements, and conversational context. For example, the pre-prompt may direct the agent to “continue to provide feedback until you are convinced that no higher utility solution exists.” This method avoids reliance on explicit criteria but makes the evalua-tion pipeline more unstable as each agent’s termination criteria becomes more heterogeneous. 2. Threshold Stopping . The decision agent has access to U ∗ 

> d

and is directed to terminate the conversation once it encounters any s ∈ S ∗ such that Ud(s) = U ∗ 

> d

. This ensures consis-tency across agents but risks creating unrealistic behavior, since real decision-makers do not have perfect knowledge of the utility landscape. In practice, however, we observe that agents ec6 e-companion to : LLM Agents for Interactive Optimization 

with access to this information will still frequently terminate conversations before achieving a utility-maximizing solution, especially if the optimization agent struggles to make progress. 3. Oracle Stopping . If the decision agent has a tool to check the utility of a given solution that also provides information about how to improve the solution, it can use this information as a termination criterion. In other words, the agent will terminate the conversation only when the tool indicates that there is no way to improve its utility. In addition to these approaches, it can also be desirable to impose a turn limit on the number of back-and-forth interactions between the optimization and decision agents. In practice, this will depend on several factors, including the complexity of the decision problem and the design and subsequent behavior of both agents. 

EC.4.2. Generating Weights and Threshold Values 

Our goal in generating a dataset of decision agents is to represent many different types of users that may interact with the optimization agent and their idiosyncratic preferences. One way to accomplish this is through diversification of our utility functions. We identify two possible ap-proaches. A function-centric approach starts by parameterizing a desired class of functions and subsequently constructing a dataset of utility functions through repeated sampling of these pa-rameters (perhaps randomly, or cleverly chosen to capture user archetypes ). This has the benefit of guaranteeing diversity in utility functions. However, this approach offers no guarantees about diversity in user preferences over solution outcomes. That is, 100 vastly different utility functions constructed in this manner may produce the same utility-maximizing solution. Alternatively, we can take a solution-centric approach, which starts from solutions in the set S and attempts to construct utility functions for which these solutions are the unique utility-maximizing outcome. Constructing our utility functions in this way guarantees that decision agents express truly distinct sets of preferences, at the cost of added complexity in constructing these functions. To generate unique candidate solutions to seed our utility functions, we generate several Pareto frontiers. We fix a single school to one of the three possible start times and generate a conditional Pareto frontier exploring the tradeoff between schedule stability and student load balancing. For each school, this gives us a set of roughly 20 unique solutions (with some overlap between schools). Each decision agent is assumed to represent preferences over the start time of a single school. To translate these solutions into w1, w 2, w 3, L, and R for our utility functions, we combine ele-ments of grid-search and randomization. For each decision agent, we randomly decide whether this agent would like their school to start earlier or later, producing f1. For each of the ∼ 20 unique solutions for this decision agent, we grid-search over the parameters L and R (starting from smaller values to bias towards more conservative utility functions) and sample over the simplex formed by e-companion to : LLM Agents for Interactive Optimization ec7 

w1, w 2, w 3, selecting the first combination of these parameters for which this solution is uniquely utility-maximizing. We permit 10,000 samples per combination of L and R; if no combination of parameters satisfying this criteria is found, this solution is excluded from the dataset. Constructing our utility functions in this manner yields a dataset of 109 distinct sets of preferences.
