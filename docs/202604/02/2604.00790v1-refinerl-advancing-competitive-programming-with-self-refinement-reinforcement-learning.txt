## Abstract 

While large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated strong performance on complex rea-soning tasks such as competitive programming (CP), existing methods predominantly focus on single-attempt settings, overlooking their capac-ity for iterative refinement. In this paper, we present RefineRL , a novel approach designed to unleash the self-refinement capabilities of LLMs for CP problem solving. RefineRL introduces two key innovations: (1) Skeptical-Agent , an iterative self-refinement agent equipped with local execu-tion tools to validate generated solutions against public test cases of CP problems. This agent al-ways maintains a skeptical attitude towards its own outputs and thereby enforces rigorous self-refinement even when validation suggests correct-ness. (2) A reinforcement learning (RL) solu-tion to incentivize LLMs to self-refine with only standard RLVR data ( i.e. , problems paired with their verifiable answers). Extensive experiments on Qwen3-4B and Qwen3-4B-2507 demonstrate that our method yields substantial gains: after our RL training, these compact 4B models inte-grated with the Skeptical-Agent not only outper-form much larger 32B models but also approach the single-attempt performance of 235B models. These findings suggest that self-refinement holds considerable promise for scaling LLM reasoning, with significant potential for further advancement.

Proprietary systems like OpenAI o1 (El-Kishky et al., 2025), Alpha-Geometry (Trinh et al., 2024; Chervonyi et al., 2025), and Gemini (Comanici et al., 2025) have proven that reasoning

LLMs can become medal winners in the most challeng-ing mind competitions. Concurrently, open-source models like DeepSeek-R1 (Guo et al., 2025) and Qwen3 (Yang et al., 2025) have achieved remarkable performance on rig-orous benchmarks such as AIME, LiveCodeBench, and Codeforces. While the success of these models is largely attributed to long Chain-of-thought (CoT; Wei et al. 2022) that can consume tens of thousands of tokens during gen-erations, the potential of LLMs for iterative improvement 

remains under-explored. Indeed, prevailing evaluations are performed in single-attempt settings, where models are ex-pected to derive correct solutions in a single inference pass. More recent studies have adopted the self-correction tech-nique to realize iterative improvement for competitive math proving (Lin et al., 2025; Zhou et al., 2025). In each turn, the model utilizes the Lean-4 prover to analyze whether the generated proof is incorrect, and if so, incorporates the feedback to correct the proof. In the field of competitive programming, Shi et al. (2024) and Li et al. (2025) represent the primary open-source efforts leveraging self-correction. They employ Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2023) to iteratively correct solutions until they pass all public test cases. How-ever, as public test cases serve only as a sparse proxy for the comprehensive private test distribution, solely relying on them to trigger iterative improvement introduces a risk of false positives , where solutions pass visible checks but still fail on unseen data. Furthermore, as a purely test-time method, Reflexion depends entirely on the base model’s intrinsic iterative improvement capabilities, lacking explicit training to optimize the improvement process itself. To address these challenges, we introduce RefineRL , a sys-tematic approach designed to unleash the latent reasoning capabilities of LLMs for iterative improvement in compet-itive programming. Our framework is built upon two core components: the Skeptical-Agent and Self-Refinement Re-inforcement Learning . Specifically, the Skeptical-Agent employs a fine-grained agentic workflow tailored to differ-ent judging statuses on public test cases of CP problems to perform the refinement. Unlike previous approaches that blindly trust public validation, our agent maintains a consis-tently skeptical attitude, enforcing rigorous self-refinement even when passing all public test cases. ## 2. Related Work 

Code generation of LLMs. Reasoning LLMs are found to be good at generating program code with the help of long chain-of-thought (CoT) (Liu et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2025; Comanici et al., 2025). For competitive programming, which is a specific code generation task, Ahmad et al. (2025) show that training LLMs on long CoT data distilled from frontier models can improve their capability to solve CP problems. He et al. (2025), Wang et al. (2025), and Fu et al. (2025) find that synthesized high-quality test cases of CP problems boost RL training with verifiable rewards of LLMs. Hu et al. (2025) further suggest using fully synthesized CP problems to train LLMs. Despite model training, several test-time scaling strategies such as example test cases re-jection, longest response selection, and clustering are also designed for the task of solving CP problems (El-Kishky et al., 2025; Yu et al., 2025; Samadi et al., 2025). Our work aims to incentivize a new Skeptical-Agent capability for competitive programming via RL training. 

Self-correction of LLMs. Self-correction is a powerful test-time scaling strategy that empowers LLMs to iteratively critique and improve their responses based on internal or ex-ternal feedback (Shinn et al., 2023; Li et al., 2025; Madaan et al., 2023; Novikov et al., 2025). Previous works have demonstrated its effectiveness for coding and mathematical reasoning tasks (Shi et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2025a; Song et al., 2025), and recent studies indicate that LLMs can fur-ther internalize this capability through post-training (Chen et al., 2024; Jain et al., 2025a; Kumar et al., 2025). How-ever, traditional approaches often rely on the complete his-tory of multi-turn interactions, leading to excessive con-text length that limits practicality. More recent studies in competitive-level math proving (Zhou et al., 2025; Lin et al., 2025; Shang et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025b) suggest de-composing the correction process into multiple single-turn feedback-response pairs, allowing the model to focus on immediate corrections without retaining the full trajectory. This paradigm significantly reduces computational overhead during both training and inference. Our paper focuses on designing a self-refinement strategy more robust than self-correction for solving CP problems and on effectively using RL (post-)training to enhance such a capability.

## 4. RefineRL 

This section presents our RefineRL approach for competi-tive programming. We will first motivate the necessity of integrating skepticism into iterative improvement-based CP problem solving, and then introduce our proposed Skeptical-Agent and Self-Refinement RL in detail. 

4.1. Motivation 

In the domain of competitive programming, Shi et al. (2024) and Li et al. (2025) are primary open-source efforts lever-aging self-correction for iterative improvement. In their ap-proach, the model analyzes the improvement history and it-eratively corrects its generated code until the solution passes all visible public test cases or reaches the maximum steps. However, public test cases are usually sparse and weak, failing to cover the comprehensive distribution of private test cases, particularly edge cases. This limitation creates a significant risk of false positives : a solution passing all visible public test cases can still fail on unseen private ones. To tackle false positives, we argue that a necessary and promising approach is to integrate skepticism into the iter-ative improvement process for competitive programming. The LLM should not blindly trust evaluations on public test cases but rather maintain a skeptical attitude, continuing re-finement even when public evaluations suggest correctness. Built upon this principle, our Skeptical-Agent utilizes spe-cialized prompts to enforce persistent self-refinement , while Self-Refinement RL internalizes this self-refinement capa-bility through post-training, enabling the LLM to discover and resolve defects beyond public test cases.

Given a CP problem (x, T ) and a generated solution code rcode , the squared-incentive reward for rcode is calculated as follows, 

R(rcode , T ) := ( k∗/|T |)2,

where k∗ is equal to either |T | or the index minus one of the first test case from the set T that rcode fails to pass. The advantage of our new squared-incentive reward is two-fold: • It enables the LLM to extract meaningful learning sig-nals from difficult CP problems even though the LLM itself cannot roll out a correct solution during RL. • It incentivizes the LLM to completely solve CP prob-lems as the squared nature of the reward significantly amplifies the total reward for passing later test cases.

## 6. Conclusion 

In this paper, we propose RefineRL, an approach designed to incentivize the self-refinement capabilities of LLMs for competitive programming. To address the unreliability of public test cases, we first introduce the Skeptical-Agent ,which maintains a persistent attitude of skepticism toward its generated solutions. By enforcing rigorous self-refinement even when local evaluations suggest correctness, this agent effectively mitigates the risk of blindly trusting sparse pub-lic test cases. Complementing this, we further propose 

Self-Refinement RL , a training pipeline that internalizes this “doubt and debug” capability via self-generated refinement trajectories and a novel squared-incentive reward. Empiri-cally, we demonstrate that RefineRL enables compact 4B models to outperform the single-attempt performance of 32B baselines and rival that of much larger 235B models. These results suggest that explicit training for intrinsic self-refinement is a far more data- and parameter-efficient path toward achieving advanced reasoning intelligence. 8RefineRL: Advancing Competitive Programming with Self-Refinement Reinforcement Learning

## A. Additional Implementation Details 

A.1. Training Data for Self-Refinement RL 

We construct the training dataset for Skeptical RL based on a subset of the HardTest dataset (He et al., 2025), consisting of CP problems that are suitable for learning self-refinement . Formally, a problem is considered to be suitable if it contains at least 8 test cases with both input and output lengths not exceeding 512 tokens. The idea behind this is that short input-output pairs are essential for both humans and AI models to effectively diagnose errors and facilitate debugging. To form the base CP problem dataset DCP , we select suitable problems from the first 1/8 of HardTest. For each selected problem, we retain all test cases shorter than 512 tokens and randomly sample up to 8 additional test cases from the remainder. This process results in a base set DCP consisting of approximately 4.5K CP problems. Finally, we employ the pipeline described in Section 4.3 to synthesize the final Self-Refinement RL training data. We focus on generating Python code, and set the maximum generation length to 32,768 for Qwen3-4B and 65,536 for Qwen3-4B-2507. For each model, this process generates approximately 4.2K self-refinement trajectories. Combining these with the base problems yields a total of roughly 8.7K training samples per model. A.3. Evaluation Details 

Existing evaluations on the LiveCodeBench benchmark typically rely on the official LCB evaluation toolkit 5 or the Hugging Face LightEval package 6. However, we argue that these packages are inefficient for large-scale CP solution judging, as they lack native support for high-throughput parallel inference and fail to fully leverage multi-core CPUs for computationally intensive solution code execution. To address these limitations, we implemented a custom, scalable toolkit for efficient CP performance evaluation. Our implementation decouples the evaluation pipeline into two distinct stages to ensure robustness and efficiency: • Step 1: LLM solution generation. We utilize the SGLang inference engine 7 to perform high-throughput parallel LLM generation. We adopt the standard LiveCodeBench solution generation prompts from the LightEval package for LLMs to generate Python solutions, with the exception of our Skeptical-Agent, which incorporates the additional feedback prompts detailed in Appendix C to perform self-refinement. • Step 2: Concurrent code evaluation. Consistent with the training setup described in Appendix A.2, we employ the Unix CLI tools timeout and prlimit to enforce strict time and memory constraints. To maximize resource utilization, we leverage native multiprocessing for the Python’s asyncio 8 package to evaluate multiple solutions concurrently. Regarding evaluation settings, for every CP problem, we set a time limit of 6s and a memory limit of 512MB. All code executions are performed on a 2.5GHz multi-core CPU. To ensure a fair comparison, all models in our experiments are evaluated using our custom toolkit, with results averaged over 16 runs to ensure stability. The LLM sampling parameters are set to a temperature of 0.6 and a top-p of 0.95. The maximum generation length is set to 32,768 tokens for Qwen3-4B and our RefineRL-4B, and 65,536 tokens for all other models. Furthermore, given that the evaluated models are reasoning-oriented, we strictly enforce complete Chain-of-Thought (CoT) integrity; specifically, only responses containing a detected CoT completion tag are submitted for evaluation; otherwise, they are assigned a score of zero.
