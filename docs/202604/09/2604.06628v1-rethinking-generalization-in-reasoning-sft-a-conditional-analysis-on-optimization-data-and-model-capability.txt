Abstract 

A prevailing narrative in LLM post-training holds that supervised fine-tuning (SFT) memorizes while reinforcement learning (RL) generalizes. We revisit this claim for reasoning SFT with long chain-of-thought (CoT) supervision and find that cross-domain generalization is not absent but 

conditional , jointly shaped by optimization dynamics, training data, and base-model capability. Some reported failures are under-optimization ar-tifacts: cross-domain performance first degrades before recovering and improving with extended training (a dip-and-recovery pattern), so short-training checkpoints can underestimate generalization. Data quality and structure both matter: low-quality solutions broadly hurt generalization, while verified long-CoT traces yield consistent cross-domain gains. Model capability is essential: stronger models internalize transferable procedural patterns (e.g., backtracking) even from a toy arithmetic game, while weaker ones imitate surface verbosity. This generalization is asymmetric, however: reasoning improves while safety degrades, reframing the question from 

whether reasoning SFT generalizes to under what conditions and at what cost. 

1 Introduction 

A prevailing narrative in Large Language Model (LLM) post-training is that supervised fine-tuning (SFT) improves in-domain performance but is prone to memorization, whereas reinforcement learning (RL) generalizes better. Chu et al. (2025) established the influen-tial “SFT memorizes, RL generalizes” framing on synthetic tasks, and subsequent work reported similar patterns in more realistic settings such as math reasoning (Huan et al., 2025). This narrative has motivated considerable efforts in understanding RL’s advantage over SFT (Chen et al., 2025; Shenfeld et al., 2026; Lai et al., 2026; Zhu et al., 2025) and in algorithmic modifications to the SFT objective itself (Wu et al., 2026; Zhu et al., 2026). However, the experimental conditions under which this narrative was established vary considerably. For instance, Chu et al. (2025) trained without long chain-of-thought (CoT) supervision; Huan et al. (2025) trained for relatively short epochs; Wu et al. (2026) used data with uneven response quality; and many studies, constrained by compute or time, used small or early-generation base models without explicitly considering model capability as a factor. Separately, many SFT-vs-RL comparisons focus on retention (whether fine-tuning degrades existing abilities) rather than on the acquisition of new generalization, and often

These entangled differences in optimization, data, model capability, and starting checkpoint make it unclear whether reported generalization failures are intrinsic to SFT or merely artifacts of particular experimental conditions. This question is particularly pressing for reasoning SFT , where models are often trained on long CoT traces. Long-CoT traces are different in data structure, harder to fit than short-form targets, and more demanding of model capability. This makes reasoning SFT a setting where optimization, data, and model factors are critical and can be more clearly studied. In this work, we show that generalization in reasoning SFT is not an inherent property of the training objective itself, but a conditional phenomenon jointly shaped by optimization dynamics , training data , and model capability . We systematically vary each factor to identify when and how reasoning SFT generalizes across domains, and what trade-offs arise when it does. We adopt math-only reasoning SFT on pretrained base models as a testbed, a setting that provides easily verifiable responses and has direct comparison with prior studies (Huan et al., 2025; Wu et al., 2026). We then evaluate generalization across in-domain math reasoning, OOD reasoning (e.g., code, science), general capabilities (e.g., instruction following), and safety. Our findings are as follows: • Apparent non-generalization may be an under-optimization artifact. Generalization performance exhibits a dip-and-recovery pattern during long-CoT SFT: it first degrades be-fore recovering and eventually surpassing the base model, suggesting that short-training checkpoints can systematically underestimate SFT’s generalization potential. Further analysis shows that long-CoT data benefits more from repeated exposure than from one-pass coverage under matched compute, and that pronounced overfitting symptoms appear only under aggressive training schedules. This indicates that under-optimization may be a more prevalent risk than over-optimization in this regime. • Training data matters for generalization. Data quality matters: SFT on low-quality solutions can broadly hurt performance, whereas verified long-CoT traces yield consistent cross-domain gains. Data structure also matters: on strong base models, long-CoT traces from a toy arithmetic game (Countdown) can improve performance on several reasoning benchmarks (e.g., math, code, science), and can even outperform a no-CoT dataset with diverse math problems. This suggests that procedural patterns in long CoT traces, such as backtracking and verification, may be what generalizes beyond math content itself. • Generalization requires sufficient model capability. Given identical long-CoT data, stronger models show broad generalization across domains, while weaker models show marginal or negative gains (even on in-domain math) and tend to produce prolonged responses. This suggests weaker models may imitate the surface form of reasoning (e.g., verbosity) without internalizing the patterns that drive cross-domain generalization. • Generalization is asymmetric. Despite broad gains, long-CoT SFT weakens safety, consistent with findings on self-jailbreaking in reasoning models (Yong & Bach, 2025; Mao et al., 2025). After training, models self-rationalize during thinking (e.g., “for educational purposes”) and eventually generate harmful content. In our controlled 2Preprint. Under review. comparison, safety drop is much larger with CoT than without CoT, suggesting that this degradation is driven by the procedural patterns rather than domain content. These results suggest that the question “ does SFT generalize?” is under-specified. General-ization is not a fixed property of the SFT objective but varies substantially with optimization sufficiency, data quality and structure, and base model capability. Conclusions drawn when any of these factors is lacking (e.g., evaluating early checkpoints, training on low-quality data, or using weak base models) may mistake artifacts of the experimental setup for inher-ent limitations of SFT. A more productive framing is to ask under what conditions reasoning SFT generalizes, and our study is an early effort to plot this conditional landscape. 

2 Experimental Setup 

This section describes the default experimental setup. Specific variations in training sched-ule, data, and base model are introduced in later sections where they become relevant. 

2.1 Model, dataset, and training protocol 

Our main experiments use Qwen3-14B-Base and Qwen3-8B-Base (Yang et al., 2025) as base models, and include InternLM2.5-20B-Base (Cai et al., 2024) and Qwen2.5 base models (Yang et al., 2024) to assess whether the observed trends extend beyond one model family. All these models are pretrained checkpoints before instruction tuning, which minimizes confounds from alignment or preference optimization. Subsequent sections additionally examine smaller models (e.g., 4B and 1.7B) to study the role of model capability (Section 5). Our default training dataset, Math-CoT-20k , consists of 20,480 math reasoning examples with long CoT. The queries are sampled from the default subset of OpenR1-Math-220k (Lozhkov et al., 2025), and the responses are generated by Qwen3-32B with thinking enabled. Each response contains a thinking process followed by step-by-step final summary and answer. We generate multiple responses per query and use math-verify (Hugging Face, 2024) to retain only those with correct answers. The maximum response length is set to 16,384 tokens. Please see App. B.1 for complete data generation settings. Unless otherwise stated, all models are trained with standard SFT objective, which mini-mizes the negative log-likelihood over response tokens. The default training schedule uses the AdamW optimizer, a learning rate of 5e-5, a batch size of 256, a cosine learning rate schedule, and 8 training epochs. These choices are varied systematically in Sec. 3 to study the role of optimization dynamics. See App. B.2 for more optimization details. 

2.2 Evaluation suite 

Our evaluate suite aims to capture both the potential benefits and costs of reasoning SFT: 

In-domain (ID) reasoning. MATH500 (Hendrycks et al., 2021) and AIME24 (Zhang & Math-AI Team, 2024) evaluate math reasoning, directly aligning with the training domain. 

Out-of-domain (OOD) reasoning. LiveCodeBench (LCB) v2 (Jain et al., 2025) evaluates coding abilities, GPQA-Diamond (Rein et al., 2024) evaluates graduate-level scientific rea-soning, and MMLU-Pro (Wang et al., 2024) evaluates broad knowledge-intensive reasoning. These tasks require reasoning capabilities in domains not presented in the training data. 

General capabilities. IFEval (Zhou et al., 2023) measures instruction-following accuracy, AlpacaEval 2.0 (Li et al., 2023b; Dubois et al., 2024) measures open-ended response quality, and HaluEval (Li et al., 2023a) and TruthfulQA (Lin et al., 2022) measure truthfulness. These benchmarks test whether reasoning SFT enhances, or disrupts broader behaviors. 

Safety. HEx-PHI (Qi et al., 2024) evaluates the model’s resistance to harmful queries, measured via attack success rate (ASR) and harmfulness score. 3 Optimization Dynamics of Reasoning SFT 

A common finding in prior work is that SFT improves in-domain performance but does not generalize well to OOD tasks, and may even degrade OOD capabilities (Chu et al., 2025; Huan et al., 2025). In this section, we show that this conclusion sometimes results from an incomplete view of the training trajectory. Long-CoT reasoning data is substantially harder to internalize than short-form targets, and the path from a base model to a well-trained reasoning model involves non-trivial dynamics that are easy to misread. 

3.1 Apparent non-generalization may be an under-optimization artifact Replication of previous findings. We first replicated prior findings of weak cross-domain generalization of reasoning SFT, under the same short-epoch protocol (Huan et al., 2025): we trained Qwen3-14B-Base on Math-CoT-20k for one epoch. As Fig. 2 shows, in-domain math performance improved substantially, while OOD gains were limited on some benchmarks (e.g., LCB v2, GPQA-D) and even turned negative on others (e.g., IFEval, AlpacaEval). This weak generalization was more pronounced with a smaller learning rate (App. C.1). 

Cross-domain generalization evolves non-monotonically. We then extended the number of training epochs to 8 (our default setup) and tracked benchmark performance throughout training for Qwen3-14B/8B-Base and InternLM2.5-20B-Base. They also indicate that long-CoT data is optimization-challenging, which requires extended training and exhibits nuanced dynamics. 

3.2 Response length as a diagnostic of optimization stage 

To better understand the dip-and-recovery dynamics, we tracked response length across checkpoints and compared it with performance. As Fig. 3 (bottom) shows, response length rose sharply in early training and then gradually declined. This rise-and-fall trend often co-occurs with performance changes: the longest responses tend to appear along with the weakest performance, while responses become more concise as performance recovers. 

Interpretation. Early in long-CoT SFT, models first learn a salient surface pattern: they produce prolonged thinking-like traces, before reliably learning finer reasoning patterns such as decomposition, backtracking, or self-evaluation (see App. C.3 for illustration). This stage hurts performance in two ways: (1) shallow imitation of long CoT limits transfer even on reasoning tasks, (2) verbose outputs plus occasional format errors (e.g., missing </think> 

tag) can affect results on instruction following and alignment. With continued optimization, models learn more transferable procedural patterns and finer language style, resulting in shorter, more targeted outputs and stronger cross-domain generalization. Response length therefore serves as a coarse but practical diagnostic of optimization progress in long-CoT SFT: checkpoints where responses are still shortening noticeably are often not yet fully optimized, even when in-domain performance already appears reasonable. 

3.3 Why longer training helps: repeated exposure or simply more steps? Setup. The difficulty of fitting long-CoT data raises a natural question: is it more effective to train for multiple epochs on a small dataset or to train for a single epoch on a larger one? We design a controlled experiment with three settings, all using Qwen3-14B-Base and fixing total gradient steps (640 steps): Setting 1 (default, 20k examples, batch size 256, 8 epochs), 

Setting 2 (2.5k examples, batch size 32, 8 epochs), and Setting 3 (20k examples, batch size 32, 1 epoch). The key comparison is between Settings 2 and 3: they are matched in training budget but differ in exposure pattern (8-pass repetition vs. 1-pass coverage). 

Repeated exposure is more effective. Tab. 1 shows that Setting 2 substantially outperformed Setting 3 across different benchmarks, indicating that under the same training budget, repeated exposure was more effective than one-pass coverage for long-CoT reasoning SFT. Setting 1 further outperformed Setting 2 when epoch and step counts were fixed, showing that larger data diversity still adds value.

3.4 From underfitting to overfitting: symptoms and regimes Setup. The above results suggest that in our default long-CoT setup, underfitting signals are more visible than overfitting. To close this loop, we stress-tested training aggressiveness on Qwen3-14B-Base with Math-CoT-20k under four schedules: Setting 1 (default, LR 5e-5, 8 epochs, cosine LR schedule), Setting 2 (LR 5e-5, 16 epochs, cosine LR), Setting 3 (LR 5e-5, 16 epochs, constant LR), and Setting 4 (LR 1e-4, 16 epochs, constant LR). These settings increase optimization intensity from mild to aggressive. 

Overfitting symptoms and regimes. As Fig. 4 (top) shows, across most benchmarks, Setting 2 maintained stable performance or continued to improve throughout training. Setting 3 began to show late-stage degradation on part of the OOD suite. Setting 4 showed the clearest overfitting-like pattern: OOD performance droped broadly, and even in-domain math performance droped, while response length started to rise again (Fig. 4 (bottom)). Therefore, in our setup, pronounced overfitting symptoms appear mainly under a combined aggressive schedule (high learning rate, no LR decay, long epochs), typically accompanied by broad performance drops (including in-domain math) and a rebound in response length.

Data quality matters. In the no-long-CoT setting, we further compare Math-NoCoT-20k with NuminaMath-20k to isolate the effect of data quality. Tab. 2 shows that Math-NoCoT-20k consistently performed better, while NuminaMath-20k showed broad OOD degradation and little gains in in-domain math reasoning. This indicates that low-quality data can significantly degrade the utility of SFT, and training on low-quality data may create a false impression that SFT does not generalize. 

Procedural generalization: evidence from Countdown. The Countdown-CoT dataset tests whether long-CoT SFT transfers abstract reasoning procedures beyond domain-specific knowledge. It is an arithmetic game in which the model must combine a given set of numbers using basic operations ( +, −, ×, ÷) to reach a target value. Response traces in Countdown-CoT contain structured exploratory procedures such as decomposition, back-tracking, and verification, but without explicit domain knowledge. Tab. 2 shows that for both Qwen3 models, Countdown-CoT-20k improved over the base model on reasoning tasks, even outperforming Math-NoCoT-20k on math tasks despite its narrow arithmetic scope. This suggests that the structure of reasoning procedures, rather than domain content, can be a key driver of generalization. However, this does not hold uniformly: for InternLM2.5-20B, Countdown-CoT yielded only marginal gains on math, suggesting that the effectiveness of procedural generalization also depends on base-model capability, a factor we examine in Sec. 5. Additionally, Countdown did not generalize equally to all tasks. For example, IFEval scores can degrade. 

5 How Model Capability Affects Generalization 

The previous sections show that generalization depends on optimization dynamics and training data. In this section, we examine the third factor in our framework: the role of the base model’s capability. We show that the same training data and protocol can produce very different generalization outcomes depending on the model scale. 

Setup. We trained four Qwen3 base models of increasing size (1.7B, 4B, 8B, 14B) on the same Math-CoT-20k data under the same training protocol. In this controlled setting, differences in generalization behavior can be attributed to differences in model capability. 

Higher-capability models generalize better. Fig. 5 shows a clear capability-dependent trend under matched data and training settings. The 14B model exhibited a pronounced dip-and-recovery trajectory and ended with broad gains across domains. The 8B and 4B models also showed a recovery phase, but with smaller improvements, whereas the 1.7B model showed marginal or negative gains on all benchmarks even at late checkpoints. 6 Asymmetric Generalization: Safety Under Long-CoT SFT 

Previous sections show that long-CoT reasoning SFT transfers across domains. However, this transfer is asymmetric: the same training consistently weakens safety and refusal. A controlled CoT vs. no-CoT comparison with matched queries and answers further suggests that this degradation comes from procedural patterns in long-CoT traces. 

Setup. We evaluate how safety performance on HEx-PHI changes for three models: Qwen3-14B-Base, Qwen3-8B-Base, and InternLM2.5-20B-Base. Specifically, we compare base models with SFT checkpoints trained on Math-CoT-20k and Math-NoCoT-20k. 

Safety degradation under long-CoT SFT. Fig. 6 (a) shows that training with long-CoT data (Math-CoT-20k) led to a substantial increase in ASR on all three models. In contrast, training on no-CoT data (Math-NoCoT-20k) led to much smaller safety degradation. Since Math-CoT-20k and Math-NoCoT-20k contain the same queries and final solutions, this 8Preprint. Under review. suggests that the observed safety drop is more closely related to the procedural patterns in long-CoT traces than with the math content itself. 

How long-CoT SFT changes refusal behavior: a case study. To understand this degradation, we compared how models answer the same harmful queries before and after long-CoT SFT (Fig. 6 (b)). The base models often directly issued short refusals. After long-CoT SFT, the model started with warnings, then self-rationalized during thinking (e.g., “for educational purposes”), and eventually provided harmful details wrapped in caveats. 

Paradoxically, this may also be a form of generalization. We hypothesize that long-CoT SFT strengthens a persistent problem-solving prior: explore alternatives, search for workable paths, and persist through obstacles. For harmful queries, the obstacle becomes the refusal policy itself, and extended reasoning provides room to work around safety guardrails. 

7 Related Work 

The comparison between SFT and RL has drawn much attention in post-training. Chu et al. (2025) established the influential “SFT memorizes, RL generalizes” framing on synthetic tasks. Most directly related to our work, Huan et al. (2025) evaluated cross-domain transfer-ability of math reasoning SFT and found limited gains—a finding we replicated under the same short-training protocol, although longer optimization led to a qualitatively different outcome. A closely related line of work understood RL’s advantage as reduced forgetting or recovery of capabilities degraded during SFT, attributing it to on-policy data (Chen et al., 2025), mode-seeking updates toward KL-minimal policies (Tajwar et al., 2024; Shenfeld et al., 2026), implicit regularization via reward variance (Lai et al., 2026), and preservation of weight-space structure (Jin et al., 2025; Zhu et al., 2025). Our work is complementary: instead of comparing SFT and RL, we examine when reasoning SFT itself transfers across domains and identify several factors important to generalization that have received less attention. In addition, unlike prior work focused on forgetting, we start from pretrained base models rather than instruction-tuned ones to better separate newly acquired generalization from the retention of existing capabilities. A separate line of work sought to modify the SFT objective to improve generalization. Several studies re-interpreted SFT through an RL or distribution-matching lens (Qin & Springenberg, 2025; Lv et al., 2026) and proposed alternative weighting strategies (Wu et al., 2026; Zhang et al., 2026; Li et al., 2025; Lin et al., 2026). Our study is orthogonal: we keep the vanilla SFT objective and show that generalization is not an intrinsic property of the objective, but jointly depends on optimization sufficiency, data quality and structure, and model capability. Meanwhile, several works investigated individual factors related to SFT generalization. Xie et al. (2025) showed that memorization and generalization can coexist during fine-tuning. Lin et al. (2025) found that prompt diversity and CoT supervision can improve SFT generalization, although only verified in synthetic tasks. Chandra et al. (2026) revealed that SFT on long-CoT traces with incorrect final answers can still improve reasoning performance. A concurrent work (Kopiczko et al., 2026) found that data repetition outperformed one-pass scaling in long-CoT SFT, consistent with our Sec. 3.3. Pipis et al. (2025) showed that looping was more common in smaller models, echoing our observations on model capability. Rather than investigating individual factors separately, we systematically vary optimization, data, and model capability while holding others constant, characterizing how they jointly shape generalization in reasoning SFT. Our controlled setup also uncovers new phenomena, such as the dip-and-recovery dynamics of cross-domain performance and its dependency on data quality and model capability. Prior work also showed that extended reasoning can induce self-jailbreaking and degrade safety (Yong & Bach, 2025; Mao et al., 2025). In contrast, we start from a generalization perspective and make a controlled comparison between CoT and no-CoT supervision with matched queries and answers, causally attributing the safety drop to procedural patterns. 9Preprint. Under review. 

8 Conclusion and Discussion 

In this study, we show that cross-domain generalization in reasoning SFT is conditional rather than absent, depending jointly on optimization sufficiency, data quality and structure, and base-model capability. Short-epoch checkpoints can underestimate eventual generalization gains; verified long-CoT data with procedural reasoning patterns yields stronger transfer; and higher-capability models internalize these patterns more effectively than weaker ones that mainly mimic verbosity. This generalization is also asymmetric: reasoning gains are consistently accompanied by safety degradation. The productive question is therefore not 

whether reasoning SFT generalizes, but under what conditions it does and at what cost. We provide more detailed dicussion and limitation in App. A.

A Discussion and Limitation 

Discussion. Our findings point to two broader implications for reasoning post-training. First, the strong dependence of generalization on base-model capability (Sec. 5) suggests that more attention should be paid to the interaction between pretraining/mid-training data and downstream SFT data . In our experiments, model capability is not merely a function of parameter count: models of comparable size but from different pretraining pipelines (e.g., Qwen3 vs. InternLM2.5) can exhibit different generalization trajectories under identical SFT data and optimization. This implies that the knowledge and representations acquired during pretraining and mid-training meaningfully shape what a model can extract from post-training supervision, and that treating the base model as a fixed starting point may obscure an important design choice. Second, our results highlight the need for co-design of model, data, algorithm, and training schedule . No single factor in isolation determines generalization: a strong base model can still fail to generalize under poor data (Sec. 4), high-quality data can appear ineffective under insufficient optimization (Sec. 3), and extended training of a weak model on high-quality data yields only marginal gains (Sec. 5). There is no universally optimal data recipe, algorithm, or schedule. The best configuration depends on the other components. This interdependence complicates the common practice of benchmarking individual factors in isolation and suggests that future work should explore these factors jointly. 

Limitations. Our study has several limitations. (1) We focus exclusively on math reasoning data because math problems are easy to verify and high-quality long-CoT responses can be reliably obtained via rejection sampling. Whether our findings (particularly the dip-and-recovery dynamics and the conditions for cross-domain transfer) extend to other reasoning domains (e.g., code generation, scientific reasoning, or multi-modal tasks) remains an open question for future work. (2) Due to computational constraints, our experiments are conducted on dense models with at most 20B parameters. We have not validated our conclusions on larger dense models or on mixture-of-experts architectures, where optimization dynamics and capacity considerations may differ substantially. (3) We do not include a direct comparison with RL-based methods. While such a comparison would be informative, our findings that SFT generalization is conditional on optimization, data, and model capability imply that a fair comparison is difficult to set up: the comparison of SFT and RL may itself change depending on these factors, making it hard to draw universal conclusions from any single experimental configuration. 

B Detailed Experimental Settings 

B.1 Dataset generation details 

Our default training dataset, Math-CoT-20k , consists of 20,480 math reasoning examples with long CoT. The queries are sampled from the default subset of OpenR1-Math-220k (Lozhkov et al., 2025), and the responses are generated by Qwen3-32B with thinking enabled. Each response contains a thinking process followed by step-by-step final summary and answer. The maximum response length is set to 16,384 tokens. The generation parameters are: temperature=0.6, top-p=0.95, top-k=20, min-p=0, following the suggestion in the official Huggingface repository. We generate multiple responses per query and use math-verify (Hugging Face, 2024) to retain only those with correct answers. For each query, we only keep one correct response. If multiple correct responses exist, we randomly select one. The Math-NoCoT-20k dataset is directly derived from the Math-CoT-20k dataset by re-moving the thinking process. The Countdown-CoT-20k dataset samples 20k queries from Countdown-Tasks-3to4-Unique, with responses generated by Qwen3-32B using the same generation parameters listed above. The NuminaMath-20k dataset contains the same queries, with responses sourced from the NuminaMath-1.5 dataset. 15 Preprint. Under review.

B.3 Evaluation details 

In this section, we describe the evaluation setting used throughout the paper. We aim to measure not only in-domain gains, but also cross-domain transfer, general capability shifts, and safety side effects under a consistent evaluation setup. 

Evaluation framework. We use lm-evaluation-harness (Gao et al., 2024) for IFEval, HaluE-val, TruthfulQA, and MMLU-Pro, and Evalchemy (Raoof et al., 2025) for LiveCodeBench v2 and GPQA-Diamond. For math benchmarks, we use math verify to validate extracted final answers. Across all benchmarks, we standardize the core protocol while adapting benchmark-specific settings to ensure fair and reliable evaluation. 

Benchmarks and evaluation settings. Here we present a brief introduction for each benchmark, and describe the evaluation hyperparameters and metrics. • MATH-500 : The MATH-500 benchmark contains 500 curated math problems on formal mathematical reasoning and multi-step problem solving. We generate 3 responses for each problem at temperature 0.6 and report the average accuracy (avg@3). • AIME24 : The AIME24 benchmark contains 30 official AIME 2024 problems, which are competition-level mathematical problems. We sample 10 responses for each problem at temperature 0.6 and report the average accuracy (avg@10). 16 Preprint. Under review. • LiveCodeBench v2 : LiveCodeBench v2 contains 511 coding problems which requires extensive reasoning. We sample 3 responses for each problem at temperature 0.6 and report the average accuracy (avg@3). • GPQA Diamond : GPQA-Diamond contains 198 expert-written questions ongraduate-level scientific reasoning and multi-step knowledge-intensive inference. To reduce positional bias, we randomly shuffle both options for each question. We sample 3 responses for each problem at temperature 0.6 and report the average accuracy (avg@3). • MMLU-Pro : MMLU-Pro contains 12k problems for knowledge-intensive reasoning. We uniformly sample an 1000-question subset from each category for evaluation. We sample 1 response for each question at temperature 0.6 and report accuracy (pass@1). • IFEval : IFEval contains 541 instruction-following tasks. The evaluation is rule-based. We sample 1 response for each prompt at temperature 0.6 and report the average instruction-level strict accuracy. We remove all content before the last </think> tag when evaluating the response. • AlpacaEval 2.0 : AlpacaEval 2.0 evaluate open-ended helpfulness and response quality, containing 805 user queries in total. We sample 1 response for each prompt at temperature 0.6 and report the average re-ward score from the Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct-RM-RB2 reward model (shortened as RB2 in App. D). We remove all content before the last </think> tag when evaluating the response. To ensure the reliability of our evaluation, we additionally employ two supplementary reward models: Skywork-Reward-V2-Llama-3.1-8B (shortened as Sky in App. D) and 

RM-Mistral-7B (shortened as Mis in App. D), to score the model’s responses. The results, presented in App. D, exhibit consistent trends. • HaluEval : We evaluate hallucination behavior and factual consistency on HaluEval, which includes Dialogue, QA, and Summarization subsets (10000 examples each; 30000 in total). For evaluation efficiency, we randomly sample 1000 examples from each subset, yielding a 3000-example evaluation set. We sample 1 response per example at temperature 0.6 and report the average accuracy across all three subsets. • TruthfulQA : We evaluate truthfulness under open-ended QA on TruthfulQA, using the standard generation split of 817 questions. We use the official TruthfulQA judge models: allenai/truthfulqa-truth-judge-llama2-7B 

and allenai/truthfulqa-info-judge-llama2-7B for scoring truthfulness and helpful-ness, respectively. We sample 1 response for each question at temperature 0.6 and report the average accuracy. We remove all content before the last </think> tag when evaluating the response. • HEx-PHI (Human-Extended Policy-Oriented Harmful Instruction Benchmark) : HEx-PHI contains 300 harmful instructions (30 examples × 10 prohibited categories identified in Meta’s Llama-2 usage policy and OpenAI’s usage policy) for LLM harmfulness evalu-ation. The dataset is available at this link. Following the original paper, we use a strong model (GPT-4.1) as a judge model to give harmfulness score (1-5) to the generated responses. A score of 5 is regarded as a successful attask. We sample 1 response for each question at temperature 0.6 and report the average Attack Success Rate (ASR). We remove all content before the last </think> 

tag when evaluating the response.

C.7 Which tokens do higher-capability models learn better? 

In this section, we investigate the token log-probability differences between Qwen3-14B-Base Step640 and Qwen3-1.7B-Base Step640 on the Math-CoT-20k training data, providing an intuitive view of how learning patterns vary with model capability. We evaluate each model’s per-token log probability on the same set of training responses. Since both models are scored on identical (question, response) pairs, the log probability differences reflect purely the models’ learned distributions rather than any difference in generation quality. Specifically, we randomly sample 11 examples from the MathCoT training data with response lengths exceeding 10,000 tokens, using response length as a proxy for problem difficulty under the assumption that the teacher model generates longer reasoning traces for harder questions. Across 11 sampled responses (approximately 122K tokens total), 14B assigns a higher log probability on 89.2% of all tokens (mean log probability gap: +0.252 in favor of 14B). This gap is not uniform: when applying a threshold of ∆logprob > 1.0, 14B holds a decisive advantage on 9.3% of tokens (11,305 tokens), while 1.7B holds an advantage on only 1.2% (1,416 tokens) — an 8.0× ratio. The asymmetry grows with threshold severity: at 

∆logprob > 3.0, the ratio reaches 18.7×. This indicates that the 14B model does not merely fit training data slightly better across the board, but rather exhibits a qualitatively different ability to predict certain categories of tokens. 28 Preprint. Under review.

To identify which tokens drive this gap, we map each token position to its approximate surface-level word and collect words where the 14B model’s log probability exceeds 1.7B’s by more than 1.0, ranked by frequency. As shown in Fig. 18 resulting word cloud reveals a striking pattern: the 14B model’s largest advantage concentrates on CoT reasoning transition words — therefore (233 occurrences), alternatively (91), maybe (79), wait (78), however (44), think (48), check (65). These are the words that signal the model is switching reasoning strategies, self-correcting, backtracking, or reflecting on an intermediate result — the structural backbone of chain-of-thought reasoning. This suggests that the 1.7B model can learn local mathematical computation patterns reasonably well, but struggles to internalize the higher-level flow of when to reconsider, when to try another approach, and when to verify. The second major category of 14B-advantaged tokens involves mathematical domain terms: sqrt (89), digits (90), numbers (85), sides (86), polygons (33), pentagon (34), equation (24), ex-pression (25), and logical reasoning verbs: compute (43), remaining (39), correct (31), answer (36), divisible (20). These words appear at the decision points of mathematical reasoning, where the model must correctly invoke a specific concept or operation in context. In contrast, the 1.7B model shows virtually no net advantage over 14B on any word category. After sub-tracting overlapping occurrences, only 7 words remain where 1.7B marginally outperforms 14B (e.g., simplified, algebra, intersections), all with frequencies of 2–3, indistinguishable from noise. Per-sample analysis further reveals that the gap is problem-dependent: on a detective-style logical reasoning problem (Sample 7, mean ∆ = 0.443) and a geometric cutting problem (Sample 8, mean ∆ = 0.436), the 14B model dominates most dramatically, while simpler algebraic problems show a smaller gap (Sample 4, mean ∆ = 0.158).
