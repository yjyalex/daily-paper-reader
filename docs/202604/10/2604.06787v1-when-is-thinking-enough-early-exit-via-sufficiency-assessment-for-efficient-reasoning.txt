# When Is Thinking Enough? Early Exit via Sufficiency Assessment for Efficient Reasoning

Abstract 

Large reasoning models (LRMs) have achieved remarkable performance in complex reasoning tasks, driven by their powerful inference-time scaling capability. However, LRMs often suffer from overthinking, which results in substantial computational redundancy and significantly re-duces efficiency. Early-exit methods aim to mit-igate this issue by terminating reasoning once sufficient evidence has been generated, yet ex-isting approaches mostly rely on handcrafted or empirical indicators that are unreliable and im-practical. In this work, we introduce Dynamic Thought Sufficiency in Reasoning (DTSR) ,a novel framework for efficient reasoning that enables the model to dynamically assess the sufficiency of its chain-of-thought (CoT) and determine the optimal point for early exit. In-spired by human metacognition, DTSR oper-ates in two stages: (1) Reflection Signal Mon-itoring , which identifies reflection signals as potential cues for early exit, and (2) Thought Sufficiency Check , which evaluates whether the current CoT is sufficient to derive the final an-swer. Experimental results on the Qwen3 mod-els show that DTSR reduces reasoning length by 28.9%–34.9% with minimal performance loss, effectively mitigating overthinking. We further discuss overconfidence in LRMs and self-evaluation paradigms, providing valuable insights for early-exit reasoning. 

1 Introduction 

The success of large reasoning models (LRMs), such as OpenAI’s o1/o4 (OpenAI, 2024, 2025), Gemini 2.5 Pro (Comanici et al., 2025), DeepSeek-R1 (Guo et al., 2025), and Qwen3 (Yang et al., 2025a), represents a significant breakthrough in the evolution of LLMs. LRMs engage in long chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning before producing answers. The inference-time scaling capability (Chen et al., 2025a; Ji et al., 2025) allows them to achieve re-markable performance in challenging reasoning

tasks (e.g. mathematics, programming, and sci-entific question answering), and show human-like reasoning patterns, such as problem decomposition, verification and backtracking. Nevertheless, recent studies (Chen et al., 2025b; Cuadron et al., 2025) have shown that LRMs often suffer from overthinking: even on simple prob-lems, they tend to consume excessive tokens by re-peatedly verifying answers or exploring alternative strategies after reaching the correct solution. Such redundant reasoning is unnecessary and leads to in-efficiency. To address this, several prior works have proposed early-exit strategies that stop the reason-ing process once the LRM has generated sufficient evidence to solve the problem. The main challenge of early-exit strategies is to determine the optimal exit point, where LRMs achieve near-lossless per-formance with minimal reasoning cost. Dynasor-CoT (Fu et al., 2025) first employs the consistency of intermediate answers to determine when to exit reasoning; however, it still generates additional to-ken intervals after producing the correct answer to verify its correctness, thereby missing the op-timal exit point. DEER (Yang et al., 2025b) and CGRS (Huang et al., 2025) employ the entropy of the intermediate result as a confidence measure to determine whether LRMs reach the optimal exit point. Nevertheless, using answer confidence as a criterion for reasoning exit is not always reliable, as reasoning models can exhibit overconfidence even when their answers are incorrect (Mei et al., 2025). Moreover, such methods that rely on prob-ing intermediate answers are limited to tasks with short, fixed-format answers. For tasks involving long answers or open-ended generation without a definitive answer, intermediate answers are difficult to extract, rendering these methods impractical. To overcome these limitations, this work aims to establish a reliable and generalizable criterion for early-exit. Unlike LLM reasoning, human cogni-tion involves metacognitive (Flavell, 1979; Fleur

et al., 2021) abilities, which allow people to mon-itor, evaluate, and regulate their thoughts, and to stop thinking once the process is deemed sufficient. Inspired by this, we wonder whether LRMs can similarly determine the optimal point to exit rea-soning by continuously assessing the sufficiency of their current thoughts? To answer this, we pro-pose Dynamic Thought Sufficiency in Reasoning (DTSR), a novel framework that enables models to dynamically evaluate whether their current CoT is sufficient to solve the problem. DTSR consists of two stages: 1) Reflection Signal Monitoring: LRMs often exhibit reflective behaviors after pro-ducing an answer, typically triggered by specific reflection signals. We identify these signals as po-tential exit points for reasoning. 2) Thought Suffi-ciency Check: once a reflection signal is detected, the model evaluates the sufficiency of its current reasoning. If the sufficiency score is high, reason-ing stops and the solution is output; otherwise, the model continues along the reasoning path until the next reflection signal occurs. To evaluate the effectiveness of our proposed method, we conducted extensive experiments across multiple LRMs. The results show that DTSR significantly outperforms baseline approaches, achieving a favorable balance between accuracy and efficiency. This also demonstrates that rea-soning models can reliably assess the sufficiency of their thought, providing valuable insights and guidance for advancing efficient reasoning. Overall, our contributions are as follows: • We propose a novel framework for efficient rea-soning that enables the model to dynamically assess the sufficiency of its CoT and determine the optimal point for early exit. • Extensive experiments conducted on Qwen3 se-ries models of various sizes demonstrate that DTSR consistently outperforms baseline meth-ods, achieving a 28.9%–34.9% reduction in gen-eration length with almost no loss in performance, thereby significantly mitigating overthinking. • Further discussion of overconfidence in LRMs and the self-evaluation paradigm offers a fresh perspective and valuable insights for guiding early-exit reasoning. 

2 Methodology 

2.1 Preliminaries Generation Patterns of Reasoning Models The responses of LRMs are typically structured in two stages: a reasoning phase, in which the model en-gages in slow, human-like thinking, encapsulated within the <think> and </think> delimiters, fol-lowed by a solution phase that summarizes the reasoning and produces the final answer. During the thinking process, the model exhibits complex reasoning behaviors, such as problem decomposi-tion, strategy exploration, output verification, and self-correction (Gandhi et al., 2025; Bogdan et al., 2025). To trigger these reflective behaviors, LRMs often generate tokens like “Wait”, “Alternatively”, or “Let me check” as cues. However, once the correct answer has been reached, further verifica-tion becomes redundant, leading to unnecessary reasoning cost (Chen et al., 2025b). 

Early Exit in Reasoning Early exit is a promis-ing strategy for mitigating redundant reasoning. Muennighoff et al. (2025) first introduces a simple budget forcing method: when generation reaches the maximum token budget, the model is forced to append the end-of-thinking token and “Final An-swer:” to terminate reasoning and output its current answer. Dynasor-CoT (Fu et al., 2025) probes in-termediate answers via designed prompts at fixed intervals and terminates reasoning once consecu-tive answers are consistent. It partially mitigates overthinking but still generates extra token inter-vals to verify correctness after the correct answer is produced, failing to achieve optimal exit point. In contrast, DEER (Yang et al., 2025b) outputs in-termediate answers at reasoning transition points (e.g., “Wait”) and uses entropy to estimate their con-fidence, terminating the reasoning process when achieving high confidence. However, previous methods mainly rely on hand-crafted or statistical criteria, which are unreliable for determining whether the current reasoning pro-cess is sufficient to yield the correct answer. Mei et al. (2025) find that even state-of-the-art reason-ing models are overconfident, particularly for incor-rect answers, and this overconfidence grows with the longer reasoning process. Consequently, a sub-stantial gap exists between token probabilities and the actual correctness of answers. From a cognitive perspective, humans rarely produce explicit inter-mediate answers to decide when to stop thinking. Instead, through metacognitive processes (Fleur et al., 2021), they internally assess whether their thoughts are sufficient to reach a final answer. 2.2 Our Proposal: Dynamic Thought Sufficiency in Reasoning 

As shown in Figure 1, we present DTSR, a method that enables the model to dynamically evaluate the sufficiency of its reasoning process. Our method consists of two stages: reflection signal monitoring and thought sufficiency check. 

Reflection Signal Monitoring Our objective is to exit the reasoning process at the optimal point. Since assessing thought sufficiency after every gen-erated token is computationally impractical, we instead seek to identify potential exit signals that indicate when verification should be triggered. To this end, we conduct an empirical analysis of rea-soning trajectories generated by Qwen3-32B. For each trajectory, we identify its optimal early-exit point, defined as the earliest position in the reason-ing process at which the model is already capable of producing a correct answer (Figure 7). We find that these optimal exit points are often followed by explicit self-reflection or answer-verification be-haviors, manifested through cues such as “Wait”, “Alternatively”, and phrases beginning with “But” (e.g., “But wait”, “But let me check”). Based on these observations, we construct a reflection signal set, the complete list of which is shown in Table 4. 

Thought Sufficiency Check The LRM continues reasoning until a reflection signal is encountered. Upon detecting such a signal, the model performs a thought sufficiency check on the reasoning trace generated so far to determine whether it can ter-minate further thinking and produce a final solu-tion. Specifically, at each checkpoint, the original question together with the current chain-of-thought (CoT) is fed into a dedicated thought sufficiency check prompt template (shown in Figure 8). The model is then prompted to generate a scalar score indicating the sufficiency of the current reasoning. A score exceeding the threshold τ indicates that the model deems its reasoning sufficient; in this case, we append a termination marker </think> to halt further reasoning and allow the model to output the final answer. Conversely, if the score falls below the threshold τ , the reasoning is considered insuffi-cient, and the model resumes generation from the last reflection signal, continuing the reasoning pro-cess until the next checkpoint. This procedure is repeated until the reasoning process is complete. Unlike prior approaches that rely on handcrafted heuristics or empirical external metrics, our method determines the reasoning exit point through the model’s internal self-evaluation. In this paradigm, the model assesses the sufficiency of its previously generated reasoning from a third-person perspec-tive, treating the reasoning trace as an external ob-ject rather than directly evaluating itself. Further discussion of this can be found in Section 4.4. Moreover, reflection signals frequently occur in close succession, causing repeated sufficiency checks to yield similar outcomes and resulting in redundant computation. To mitigate this issue, we enforce a minimum token interval k between con-secutive sufficiency checks. Concretely, a reflec-tion signal triggers a thought sufficiency check only if at least k tokens have been generated since the previous check; otherwise, the current signal is skipped and the process continues (Algorithm 1). 3 Experiments 

3.1 Experimental Setup Benchmarks and Metrics To comprehensively evaluate the reasoning capabilities of LRMs, we se-lect six widely used benchmarks: GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), MATH-500 (Hendrycks et al., 2021), AMC 2023, OlympiadBench (He et al., 2024), GPQA Diamond (Rein et al., 2024) and Live-CodeBench (Jain et al., 2025). Our goal is to sig-nificantly reduce the number of tokens generated during the reasoning process while maintaining high reasoning performance. To this end, we em-ploy two primary metrics: Accuracy (Acc) and Token Count (Tok). Acc measures the proportion of correct final answers, calculated as the average pass@1 score, whereas Tok represents the average number of tokens generated per sample, serving as an indicator of reasoning efficiency. 

Models We conduct extensive experiments on the Qwen3 (Yang et al., 2025a) series of models. Our selection spans different model sizes (8B, 14B, 32B) of reasoning models to validate the generaliz-ability of our method. Despite their strong reason-ing ability, these models often exhibit overthinking during the reasoning process. 

Baselines We compare DTSR with the vanilla LRM as well as existing state-of-the-art training-free efficient reasoning methods, including No-Thinking (Ma et al., 2025a), NOWAIT (Wang et al., 2025), and DEER (Yang et al., 2025b). Vanilla allows the model to generate answers directly with-out any intervention. NoThinking prompts the model to skip the reasoning stage and answer the question directly. NOWAIT suppresses explicit self-reflection by masking reflective tokens such as “wait” and “hmm” during the reasoning process. DEER makes early-exit decisions based on the con-fidence scores of intermediate answers. In addition, we compare DTSR with recent training-based meth-ods (Arora and Zanette, 2025a; Dai et al., 2025). 

Implementation Details All experiments are conducted using the vLLM framework to ensure high throughput and memory-efficient inference. For the decoding strategy, we set the temperature to 0.6 and top-p to 0.95. The maximum genera-tion length is set to 16k tokens, and each dataset is generated three times, with the final results re-ported as the average pass@1. In our method, the thought sufficiency threshold τ is set to 100, and the minimum token interval k is set to 64. 

3.2 Experimental Results Overall Performance The results in Table 1 show that DTSR consistently outperforms the base-line methods across three model sizes and five benchmark datasets. Compared with vanilla rea-soning models, DTSR achieves almost the same level of accuracy without any degradation, while re-ducing sequence length by 28.9%–34.9%, leading to more efficient inference. Notably, when applied to Qwen3-14B, DTSR even improves performance on challenging reasoning tasks such as GPQA and OlympiadBench, underscoring its effectiveness. 

Comparison with SOTAs We compare DTSR with several recent training-free efficient reasoning methods. As shown in Table 1, DTSR effectively shortens reasoning length without compromising accuracy, whereas other baselines struggle to bal-ance efficiency with performance. For instance, No-Thinking enforces models to bypass the reasoning process through prompting, which indeed shortens sequences but deviates from the natural generation paradigm and severely limits reasoning capability. NoWAIT mitigates redundant output by masking frequent reflective tokens, yet this restriction on the model’s inherent self-reflection results in signifi-cant performance degradation on complex reason-ing tasks. 4.3 Overconfidence in LRMs 

Here, we discuss the issue of overconfidence in LRM. In fact, DEER has observed in experiments that the Qwen-3 series models tend to be overconfi-dent in their confidence predictions. after producing a trial answer. To further investi-gate this issue, we derive two variants from DEER: • DEER-1: The early-exit decision is based solely on the confidence score of the trial answer. • DEER-2: Whether the model generates

4.4 Self-Evaluation Paradigms 

In DTSR, self-evaluation is conducted from a third-person perspective rather than by having the model directly score its own thoughts. the two approaches are illustrated in Figure 9. To compare them, we introduce another variant, DTSR-1, in which an additional prompt is added during the intermedi-ate reasoning process to ask the model to directly evaluate the adequacy of its prior reasoning. The re-sults on MATH-500 are shown in Table 3. It is clear that DTSR-1 performs worse than DTSR in both accuracy and generation length. This is primarily because the model is unable to accurately assess the sufficiency of its reasoning during the process. As the saying goes, “the observer sees clearly what the participant cannot”. Only by evaluating from a third-person perspective and separating reasoning generation from the judgment of its adequacy can the model demonstrate its full reasoning capability. 

4.5 Case Study 

We conduct case studies on three types of problems: mathematical, programming, and open-ended. Fig-ure 10 shows the detailed reasoning process of Qwen3-14B on a math problem. At the beginning, the model generates reasoning normally. When it produces the reflection signal, it starts to evaluate the adequacy of its previous thoughts and gives a score of 75, which is below the early-exit thresh-old τ . In the third round of reasoning, several reflection sig-nals appear, but the model performs the thought sufficiency check only at the last one. After several checks, the model fi-nally gives a score of 100 on the fourth attempt, satisfying the early-exit condition. It then appends </think> and proceeds to make a conclusion, gen-erating the final answer. 5 Related work 

Recently, Large Reasoning Models (LRMs) have demonstrated remarkable performance on com-plex reasoning tasks by generating long Chains of Thought (CoT) for deep reasoning (Jaech et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2025). However, recent stud-ies (Chen et al., 2025b; Cuadron et al., 2025) have revealed the issue of overthinking, where models continue to reason even after obtaining the cor-rect answer, leading to redundant computational overhead. To address this, prior research has ex-plored a variety of approaches to enable efficient reasoning (Feng et al., 2025), which can be broadly categorized into three directions: (Input) prompt-guided methods, (Model) post-training methods, and (Output) decoding-intervention methods. 

Prompt-guided Methods Such methods lever-age carefully designed prompts to guide the model in performing efficient reasoning. Concise CoT (Renze and Guven, 2024) simply adds the instruction “Be concise” to the prompt, whereas TALE (Han et al., 2025) first estimates an opti-mal token budget and then incorporates it into the prompt to encourage shorter reasoning chains. Chain-of-Draft (Xu et al., 2025a) constrains the model to preserve only the most essential reasoning drafts at each step, thereby reducing token usage in few-shot settings. Although easy to implement, these approaches (Lee et al., 2025) are limited in compressing long reasoning chains, and therefore better suited for use alongside other methods. 

Post-training Methods These methods typi-cally employ supervised fine-tuning (SFT) using variable-length CoT data (Yu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2025b), or leverage reinforcement learning (RL) with length-based rewards (Team et al., 2025; Arora and Zanette, 2025b; Yeo et al., 2025; Yi et al., 2025). L1 (Aggarwal and Welleck, 2025) and Elastic Reasoning (Xu et al., 2025b) incorporate length constraints into the reward func-tion, training models to flexibly adjust the length of reasoning chains under different token budgets. Recently, numerous studies (Lou et al., 2025; Jiang et al., 2025b; Fang et al., 2025) have focused on training hybrid reasoning models that adaptively choose between concise responses and long-chain reasoning based on task complexity. While these methods attempt to address the problem at the fun-damental level, their high training costs restrict most experiments to smaller models (e.g., 1.5B or 7B), limiting their applicability to larger models. 

Decoding-intervention Methods Such methods intervene during decoding to adjust generation, guiding models to produce answers earlier (Qiao et al., 2025; Huang et al., 2025) or exit reason-ing sooner (Liu and Wang, 2025; Jiang et al., 2025a; Zhang et al., 2025). NoWAIT (Wang et al., 2025) attempts to suppress explicit self-reflection by blocking tokens like Wait and Hmm during rea-soning. However, the model can readily bypass this restriction by generating other tokens. Dynasor-CoT (Fu et al., 2025) probes the model’s inter-mediate answers at fixed token intervals and stops thinking if three consecutive answers are consistent, whereas DEER (Yang et al., 2025b) tracks interme-diate answers at reasoning transition points and de-termines whether to terminate reasoning based on their confidence scores. However, both approaches rely on probing intermediate answers to determine stopping points, which works well only for prob-lems with definite answers, but is less effective for long-form or open-ended outputs. In contrast, our approach effectively overcomes this limitation. 

6 Conclusion 

In this work, we alleviate the inefficiency of LRMs by introducing Dynamic Thought Sufficiency in Reasoning (DTSR), a novel framework that en-ables models to dynamically determine when their CoT is sufficient to yield a reliable answer. By in-tegrating reflection signal monitoring with thought sufficiency checks, DTSR provides a principled mechanism for mitigating overthinking while main-taining strong reasoning performance. Experiments on Qwen3 models demonstrate that DTSR achieves substantial reductions in reasoning length with only minimal performance degradation, highlight-ing its effectiveness in improving efficiency. We further discuss overconfidence in LRMs and the self-evaluation paradigm, shedding light on how models effectively assess the sufficiency of their CoT. This exploration offers a fresh perspective and valuable insights for guiding early-exit reasoning. Limitations 

While DTSR demonstrates promising results in improving reasoning efficiency, we identify some possible limitations as follows. • Due to computational resource constraints, we conduct experiments only on LLMs up to 32B. • This work focuses exclusively on textual reason-ing tasks, including mathematics and code. We have not yet extended our method to multimodal reasoning or agent scenarios, which we leave for future exploration.
