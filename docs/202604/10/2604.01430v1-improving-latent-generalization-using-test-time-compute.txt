# Improving Latent Generalization Using Test-time Compute 

Arslan Chaudhry (arslanch@google.com) *,1 , Sridhar Thiagarajan (sthiagarajan@google.com) *,1 and Andrew Lampinen (andrewlampinen@gmail.com) 2  

> *Equal contributions, 1Google DeepMind, 2Work done while at Google DeepMind

Language Models (LMs) exhibit two distinct mechanisms for knowledge acquisition: in-weights learning (i.e., encoding information within the model weights) and in-context learning (ICL). Although these two modes offer complementary strengths, in-weights learning frequently struggles to facilitate deduc-tive reasoning over the internalized knowledge. We characterize this limitation as a deficit in latent generalization , of which the reversal curse is one example. Conversely, in-context learning demonstrates highly robust latent generalization capabilities. To improve latent generalization from in-weights knowl-edge, prior approaches rely on train-time data augmentation, yet these techniques are task-specific, scale poorly, and fail to generalize to out-of-distribution knowledge. To overcome these shortcomings, this work studies how models can be taught to use test-time compute, or ’thinking’, specifically to improve latent generalization. We use Reinforcement Learning (RL) from correctness feedback to train models to produce long chains-of-thought (CoTs) to improve latent generalization. Our experiments show that this thinking approach not only resolves many instances of latent generalization failures on in-distribution knowledge but also, unlike augmentation baselines, generalizes to new knowledge for which no RL was performed. Nevertheless, on pure reversal tasks, we find that thinking does not unlock direct knowledge inversion, but the generate-and-verify ability of thinking models enables them to get well above chance performance. The brittleness of factual self-verification means thinking models still remain well below the performance of in-context learning for this task. Overall, our results establish test-time thinking as a flexible and promising direction for improving the latent generalization of LMs. 

1. Introduction 

Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown remarkable generalization abilities across many domains especially when the task at hand is being presented in-context. The in-context generalization of LLMs is remarkably consistent even when they are queried for knowledge that is logically implied but not directly stated in the information present in the context. Contrary to in-context learning (ICL), however, when models are finetuned on new tasks, where they are expected to generalize from their in-weights knowledge, the generalization can be surprisingly brittle – particularly for information that is logically deducible but not directly stated in the training set. The well-known cases of such lack of generalization, referred to as latent generalization 1 (Lampinen et al., 2025b) henceforth, include finetuned LLMs’ failure to answer queries about trained facts in the reverse order, studied by (Berglund et al., 2024) as reversal curse phenomenon, and similar failures in syllogistic inference tasks as studied by Lampinen et al. (2025a). To improve the latent generalization of finetuned models, previous approaches (Akyürek et al., 2024; Golovneva et al., 2024; Lampinen et al., 2025a; Yang et al., 2024) rely on increasing training dataset coverage by targeted data augmentation techniques during the pre-training or finetuning phases. Specifically, Golovneva et al. (2024) ameliorated the reversal curse in LLMs by explicitly training on strings in both the forward and reverse directions. Lampinen et al. (2025a), on the

2. Method 

To facilitate latent generalization, our method aims to enhance the model’s capacity to effectively elicit its in-weights knowledge and surface relevant information in-context within its intermediate reasoning traces. We achieve this in the following three stages: 

2.1. Knowledge Acquisition 

To study the latent generalization under a controlled setup, we first add new knowledge in a pre-trained LLM by finetuning it on a dataset that is not leaked during the pre-training stage. During this supervised finetuning (SFT) phase, we also jointly train on auxiliary datasets to preserve the model’s instruction-following and pre-existing reasoning capabilities. However, we note that this stage is only necessary when the target knowledge required for effective generalization is not already encoded within the model’s parameters. 

2.2. Thinking Bootstrapping 

Once the new knowledge is incorporated in the LLM ( 𝐿𝐿𝑀 𝑠 ) and before starting the reinforcement learning phase, we induce desired reasoning behaviors in 𝐿𝐿𝑀 𝑠 to improve latent generalization. Contrary to standard reasoning traces that are primarily optimized for logical deduction, our thinking behaviors facilitate the appropriate composition of factual knowledge required to resolve queries testing latent generalization. For this, we first construct a prompt (detailed in Appendix B.5) that explicitly defines the desired properties of a reasoning trace for effective latent generalization: focus-driven recall, self-probing, generate-and-verify etc. We use this prompt on an off-the-shelf teacher LLM (Gemini, 2025) (separate from 𝐿𝐿𝑀 𝑠 ) to curate a dataset with golden reasoning traces on a small subset of examples designed to evaluate latent generalization (e.g., queries requiring the reversal of

knowledge acquired in the preceding phase). To reduce hallucinations in these reasoning traces, we condition the teacher LLM on the ground-truth answer for each query and discard any completions that fail to arrive at the correct final answer. Once this initial dataset is constructed, we further fine-tune the 𝐿𝐿𝑀 𝑠 via SFT on this dataset. This procedure is analogous to the Self-Taught Reasoner (STaR) framework by Zelikman et al. (2022), with the distinction that we utilize a more capable teacher model to generate the bootstrapping traces rather than the model being trained ( 𝐿𝐿𝑀 𝑠 ). For fair comparison, we use the same teacher model for the augmentation baseline as well. 

2.3. Reinforcement Learning (RL) 

Using the resulting checkpoint from the thinking bootstrapping as the initial policy, we perform RL on a combined dataset comprising prompts that necessitate latent generalization, alongside prompts that directly evaluate knowledge present in the training distribution. The policy is updated using a variant of the REINFORCE algorithm (Williams, 1992). To test out-of-distribution generalization, we keep a disjoint dataset that is strictly excluded from both the bootstrapping and RL training mixtures, and is exposed to the model only during the knowledge acquisition phase. 

3. Experiments 

3.1. Model & Datasets 

Our objective is to investigate the effect of increased test-time compute on latent generalization. Therefore, we start off with a pre-trained model that has already undergone standard reasoning fine-tuning (i.e., it is capable of generating extended Chain-of-Thought (CoT) traces for specific prompts already). Specifically, we utilize Gemini 2.5 Flash (Gemini, 2025) as the base model for all baselines and subsequently perform fine-tuning on controlled datasets to evaluate latent generalization. For our controlled experimental setup, we utilize two datasets, ensuring strict exclusion from the model’s pre-training corpus to prevent data contamination: 

Semantic Structure Benchmark (Lampinen et al., 2025a) This benchmark consists of real-world categories and relations, enabling the evaluation of various forms of knowledge generalization, including logical reversals and syllogistic inferences. For instance, the model might be exposed to the premise “dogs are a type of mammal” during training, and subsequently queried on the reversal “mammals include dogs” at test time. Alternatively, if exposed to an additional premise, “mammals are warm-blooded,” the model could be evaluated on the syllogistic inference “dogs are warm-blooded,” which requires composing the two independent facts acquired during training. For the out-of-distribution structure, we keep similar depth and branching factor as the in-distribution structure, but modify several categories to have different properties, for example a capability instead of an attribute. More details on how we use this dataset for our experiments are given in Section B.1. This dataset enables us to evaluate latent generalization under the following conditions: 

• Syllogisms : Requires the logical composition of two distinct facts present in the training documents to deduce the correct answer. 

• Reversals : Requires the logical inversion of a trained fact. We evaluate two specific sub-types: 

(a) non-strict reversals , where the model can deduce the answer via an alternative multi-hop

reasoning path, and (b) strict reversals , where no alternative reasoning path exists aside from the direct inversion of the premise. 

• Category holdouts : Evaluates generalization when the training documents contain only a single fact about a specific category (e.g., explicitly specifying its parent category), while all other valid inferences are held out for testing. We further partition these holdouts into: (a) category as subject , where the held-out category acts as the subject in the test proposition, and (b) category as target , where the held-out category acts as the object or target. 

Reversal Curse Dataset (Berglund et al., 2024) This dataset comprises fictional celebrity names paired with their corresponding descriptions. During training, the dataset presents specific celebrity names followed by their descriptions (i.e., name-to-description). At test time, the model is provided with a description and queried to generate the corresponding celebrity name (i.e., description-to-name). During the knowledge acquisition phase, we introduce a data augmentation step requiring the model to unconditionally output a randomly sampled name from the dataset. This ensures the model acquires a non-zero probability of generating these fictional names, as it must observe them as target tokens during training. This dataset parallels the strict reversal condition of the semantic structure benchmark, as no alternative reasoning path exists to deduce the correct answer other than the direct reversals. Further details on dataset splits for different training and testing phases are given in Section B.2 

3.2. Baselines 

We evaluate our approach against the following baselines: 1. Fine-tuning (SFT): Standard supervised fine-tuning conducted exclusively during the knowledge acquisition phase. 2. Fine-tuning + RL: The initial knowledge acquisition phase followed by RL, but without generating intermediate reasoning tokens . This baseline isolates the performance gains at-tributable specifically to the reasoning process, ruling out improvements stemming solely from RL optimization on the factual prompts, which is known to independently enhance factual accuracy (Tian et al., 2023). 3. Augmentation: Following Lampinen et al. (2025a), we apply data augmentation by providing the entire dataset in-context and subsequently training on this augmented dataset during the knowledge acquisition phase. 4. Augmentation + RL: The aforementioned augmentation strategy followed by the RL phase, again omitting the generation of intermediate reasoning tokens. 5. In-Context Learning (ICL): The model is evaluated with the entire training dataset provided in the context window. Large language models typically exhibit strong latent generalization under this ICL paradigm. 6. Reasoning (Ours): Initial SFT during the knowledge acquisition phase, followed by reasoning bootstrapping and RL, adhering to the methodology detailed in Section 2. During all fine-tuning procedures, we jointly train on the base model’s (Gemini 2.5 Flash) original instruction-tuning and reasoning datasets. This co-training ensures that our empirical results are not confounded by the catastrophic forgetting of pre-existing instruction-following and reasoning capabilities. Training is performed using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 3 × 10 −4, a batch size of 16 , and a maximum sequence length of 8192 tokens. For all baselines, we employ early stopping based on the validation loss.

3.3. Evaluation & Metrics 

For evaluation, we employ a closed-book Question Answering (QA) framework, sampling generations from the model at a temperature of 𝑇 = 1.0, rather than computing the log-likelihood of ground-truth completions. We ensure un-leaked evaluation prompts during the Reinforcement Learning (RL) phase, and take additional care to prevent leakage via thought traces, which we discuss in B.3. For all baselines, we compute best@1 and best@ 𝑁 metrics, corresponding to a single attempt and 𝑁 

attempts, respectively, and use a bootstrapped estimator over all completions to reduce variance. For the Semantic Structure Benchmark , the model is prompted to generate a comprehensive list of all valid completions for a given test query. The ground truth comprises a set A𝑎𝑙𝑙 representing all valid completions—some of which may have been explicitly stated in the training documents or RL prompts—and a subset Aℎ𝑒𝑙𝑑𝑜𝑢𝑡 ⊂ A 𝑎𝑙𝑙 representing completions strictly held out from the training documents to evaluate true latent generalization (see Figure 4 in the appendix). Because the model’s output constitutes a set of strings, we evaluate performance using Precision, Recall, and F1 scores. Specifically, we report: 

• Overall Precision, Recall, and F1 : Computed with respect to the complete set of valid answers, 

A𝑎𝑙𝑙 .

• Train Recall : The recall evaluated strictly on the valid answers exposed during training, defined by the set difference A𝑎𝑙𝑙 \ A ℎ𝑒𝑙𝑑𝑜𝑢𝑡 .

• Held-out Recall : The recall evaluated exclusively on the held-out subset Aℎ𝑒𝑙𝑑𝑜𝑢𝑡 , serving as a direct measure of latent generalization. 

• Pass@N : The rate at which the model’s predicted set exactly matches A𝑎𝑙𝑙 within 𝑁 independent attempts. This is a rigorous exact-match metric that yields a score of zero if the generated set contains any omissions or false inclusions. 

• Primary Reference Inclusion : A binary indicator of whether the specific completion targeting the held-out condition (e.g., a specific reversal or syllogism) is present in the model’s output. This provides an additional proxy for true latent generalization. Note that these metrics are inherently correlated; for instance, a model achieving a higher Overall F1 score is proportionally more likely to achieve a higher Pass@ 𝑁 . However, it is possible for a model to obtain reasonable F1 score without necessarily predicting the completion which tests the latent-learning behavior, so it is useful to look at all these metrics independently. For the Reversal Curse Dataset , each query possesses a single unique correct completion; therefore, we exclusively report exact string-match accuracy.

Out-of-Distribution Latent Generalization We now evaluate out-of-distribution (OOD) latent generalization. In this setting, we measure performance on holdouts from a distinct dataset that was only available to the model during the initial knowledge acquisition phase; crucially, no train-time data augmentation or RL fine-tuning (with or without intermediate reasoning) was applied to this secondary dataset. We previously hypothesized that while train-time data augmentations are intrinsically tied to the specific training distribution and fail to generalize to novel datasets, training a model to utilize test-time compute to reason over its internal parametric knowledge achieves a task-agnostic latent generalization. Figure 2b presents the results for this OOD evaluation (only all F1 is reported here, other metrics can be found in Figures 8 and 9 where similar results hold). The empirical findings demonstrate that, in this regime, the data augmentation baseline yields no measurable improvement over standard supervised fine-tuning. The small improvement of the augmentation baseline on the strict reversals seen in the in-distribution case is completely gone once the potential leakage from the augmentation process is stopped by using a disjoint dataset. In stark contrast, our reasoning-based approach maintains OOD latent generalization performance comparable to its in-distribution results. On strict reversals, the performance of thinking, although better than augmentation, significantly trails ICL. This indicates that although intermediate reasoning enables LLMs to better elicit and generalize over their internalized parametric knowledge, this capability is fundamentally constrained when a query cannot be resolved by logically chaining multiple training facts. In other words, LLMs employing intermediate reasoning still struggle to execute direct, zero-hop logical reversals. We investigate reversal performance of thinking models in more detail on the Reversal Curse dataset (Section 3.4.2). 

Overall, the out-of-distribution structure results confirms that leveraging test-time compute to dynamically elicit and reason over parametric knowledge is a more robust and flexible mechanism than relying on static train-time data augmentations, thereby validating the central hypothesis of this work.

3.4.2. Reversal Curse Paper Dataset 

We now present the results on the Reversal Curse dataset (Berglund et al., 2024). This dataset is analogous to the strict reversal condition in the Semantic Structure Benchmark: the training documents contain no intermediate reasoning chains that the model can exploit to deduce the correct answer. Instead, the model must directly invert the learned factual association. The corresponding results are illustrated in Figure 3. We omit the data augmentation baseline for this experiment, as augmenting the dataset with direct reversals would explicitly leak the test distribution during training. Consequently, we evaluate only the standard supervised fine-tuning (SFT), reasoning (ours), and ICL baselines. As shown in the Figure 3, standard SFT fails to generalize, and scaling the number of parallel attempts (up to 16 ) yields no performance improvement. On the other hand, the model trained to generate intermediate reasoning traces exhibits measurable improvement, yet its performance remains inferior to that of ICL. An example of a successful reasoning trace generated by our model is provided in Appendix B.4. The trace reveals that the specifically encouraged generate-and-verify behavior in the golden traces helps the model as it proposes candidate entities acquired during the knowledge acquisition phase and subsequently self-verifies these candidates using its internalized parametric knowledge regarding each entity. Notably, this self-verification step does not require logical inversion, because the forward name-to-description mapping is explicitly encoded in the training data. If the retrieved description aligns with the target description provided in the query, the model terminates the reasoning process and outputs the verified entity. However, this heuristic is not entirely reliable, as LLMs frequently exhibit brittle factual self-verification capabilities. Furthermore, the efficacy of this strategy is strictly bottlenecked by the requirement that the model possesses a non-zero probability of initially generating the correct entity as a hypothesis that undergoes verification. While scaling test-time compute via multiple attempts predictably increases the overall success rate, the pass@1 accuracy remains low. Ultimately, these findings indicate that executing strict reversals derived purely from parametric knowledge remains a persistent challenge, even when models are augmented with intermediate reasoning capabilities. ICL continues to be the most effective paradigm for resolving such direct logical inversions.

4. Related Work 

Generalization from fine-tuning and latent learning: Several works look at some of the surprising ways in which models generalize from their dataset (Berglund et al., 2023; Meinke and Evans, 2023), or fail to generalize (Allen-Zhu and Li, 2023, 2024; Berglund et al., 2024) study the generalization abilities of fine-tuning in contrast to in-context learning. Borrowing terminology from (Blodgett, 1929; Tolman, 1948), the problem of latent learning is discussed in (Lampinen et al., 2025b), as one in which the model learns information that is not relevant to the task at hand, but that might be useful for a different future task. We operate in a similar train-test setup as some of these works studying generalization, but specifically focus on the ability of test-time compute to improve the latent learning ability of these models. We focus on the comparison of this test-time thinking strategy to other established methods like train-time augmentation. 

Data augmentation to improve generalization: Data augmentation is a long-established solution to improving the generalization of models after fine-tuning. For specific instances of the latent learning problem such as the reversal curse, augmentation strategies, such as adding inverse inference to the training data (Golovneva et al., 2024) have been proposed in prior works. Knowledge augmentation is also extensively studied in (Allen-Zhu and Li, 2024), shedding light on the role of augmentation in both pre-training and fine-tuning phases and its impact on generalization. More general augmentation mechanisms were proposed in (Lampinen et al., 2025a), where model’s in-context inferences on a subset of the training data are added to the training dataset. They show that this improves performance at least on the knowledge structure where this augmentation is performed. Another variant proposed in (Park et al., 2025), leveraged similar in-context learning ability along with additional question-answers to improve model performance. A more comprehensive review on the topic of data augmentations to improve model performance is provided by (Chai et al., 2025; Ding et al., 2024). We compare our method to a similarly flavored augmentation to (Lampinen et al., 2025a) in our work. 

Thinking to improve reasoning: The use of CoT thinking to greatly improve performance on reasoning tasks has been studied in several prior works (DeepSeek-AI, 2025; Lightman et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2024). We refer the reader to (Chen et al., 2025a) for a more comprehensive overview of this area. This line of research typically focuses on mathematical reasoning, science, and programming, and does not specifically look at how thinking can be leveraged to improve the use of factual knowledge stored in a model’s weights. 

Reinforcement learning for factuality: Several works study how reinforcement learning in the post-training phase can greatly improve the factuality of language models in the absence of test-time compute (Lin et al., 2024; Tian et al., 2023). In our work, we leverage these findings by ensuring that both the train-time augmentation and fine-tuning baselines undergo RL. On the other hand, for methods that explicitly use test-time compute (thinking) Allen-Zhu and Li (2023) study the utility of chain-of-thought in the ability of a model to access and manipulate its parametric knowledge, finding that chain-of-thought is critical for utilizing knowledge even if the knowledge is encoded perfectly. For the specific case of the reversal curse (termed inverse knowledge search), they find that language models cannot perform this task even with chain-of-thought. In our work, we find that when sufficient support exists in the data, or if the model can efficiently leverage self-verification, the task of reversal might be tractable. (Sun et al., 2022) propose a method where the model is encouraged to recite relevant passages from its pre-training data before producing an answer, acting as a form of chain-of-thought for factual knowledge. Chen et al. (2025b) focus on improving long-form factuality

We also compare the efficacy of test-time compute (thinking) vs. train-time compute (augmentation) on these subsets. 

5. Discussion & Conclusion 

Recent advances in test-time scaling, often implemented via intermediate reasoning or “thinking” traces, have enhanced the reasoning capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs). We investigate whether models can leverage this additional test-time compute to improve latent generalization whereby they can resolve deductive reasoning challenges, such as syllogisms and logical reversals, when generating responses exclusively from parametric knowledge. Through controlled experiments, we demonstrate that LLMs can indeed utilize test-time compute to enhance latent generalization, proving to be a more flexible and domain-agnostic strategy than relying on static train-time data augmentations. Despite this success, strictly pure logical reversals remain elusive, even for models augmented with intermediate reasoning and self-verification. We hypothesize this limitation stems from the rigid key-value associative memory structures that models acquire as a consequence of the transformer architecture and the autoregressive next-token prediction objective. The reversal curse and related failures of latent generalization highlight fundamental deficiencies in the contemporary LLM paradigm, encompassing both the underlying architecture and the optimization objective. While scaling compute, whether during training (e.g., via data augmentation) or at inference time (e.g., via reasoning traces), may address some of these limitations, we believe exploring stronger inductive biases, novel architectures, alternative loss functions, or a combination thereof in parallel could provide a more efficient path to obtaining better models.

B.1. Semantic Structure Dataset (Lampinen et al., 2025a) 

This benchmark consists of real-world categories and relations, enabling the evaluation of various forms of knowledge generalization, including logical reversals and syllogistic inferences. For instance, the model might be exposed to the premise “dogs are a type of mammal” during training, and subsequently queried on the reversal “mammals include dogs” at test time. Alternatively, if exposed to an additional premise, “mammals are warm-blooded,” the model could be evaluated on the syllogistic inference “dogs are warm-blooded,” which requires composing the two independent facts acquired during training. We simplify the original dataset by restricting the taxonomy to 7 categories per structure and replicating each structure 50 times, yielding a total of 350 unique categories per dataset. This modification streamlines the evaluation protocol, which requires sampling all valid answers for a given query; a deeper taxonomic structure (i.e., more categories per structure) would produce an intractable number of potential answers. Following the original dataset’s methodology, we replace all category names, relations, and properties with synthetic identifiers to guarantee zero overlap with the pre-training distribution. During training, facts derived from the semantic structures are compiled into synthetic Wikipedia-style documents, accompanied by a set of question-answering (QA) pairs to familiarize the model with the expected test-time output format. Furthermore, we adapt the documents in both the training and test splits to better suit instruction tuning by prepending task descriptions. This addition also mitigates the risk of degrading the base model’s reasoning capabilities during fine-tuning. We generate 7, 140 documents for the initial knowledge acquisition and fine-tuning phase (Sec-tion 2.1). We reserve 10 prompts for the thinking bootstrapping phase (Section 2.2) and 974 prompts for Reinforcement Learning (RL) training (Section 2.3). For each holdout condition, we sample test

B.2. Reversal Curse Dataset (Berglund et al., 2024) 

This synthetic dataset comprises fictional celebrity names paired with their corresponding descriptions. During training, the dataset presents specific celebrity names followed by their descriptions (i.e., name-to-description). At test time, the model is provided with a description and queried to generate the corresponding celebrity name (i.e., description-to-name). During the knowledge acquisition phase, we introduce a data augmentation step requiring the model to unconditionally output a randomly sampled name from the dataset. This ensures the model acquires a non-zero probability of generating these fictional names, as it must observe them as target tokens during training. This experimental design parallels the strict reversal condition of the semantic structure benchmark, as no alternative reasoning path exists to deduce the correct answer other than directly inverting the premise observed in the training documents. Consistent with our processing of the semantic structure benchmark, we format the dataset for instruction tuning by prepending task-specific instructions to the documents. We partition the dataset based on the celebrity names, strictly ensuring that the target names required for the test queries do not appear as ground-truth outputs within either the thinking bootstrapping or RL training sets. The composition of the dataset across the various training and evaluation phases is summarized below: 

• Knowledge Acquisition Data : This split contains the following components, each augmented with paraphrased variants to encourage generalization: 

– 30 examples containing both name-to-description and description-to-name pairs. 

– 60 examples containing only name-to-description pairs (i.e., the corresponding description-to-name pairs are withheld). 

– 30 examples containing only description-to-name pairs (i.e., the corresponding name-to-description pairs are withheld). 

– 120 unconditional generation examples prompting the model to produce a random name from the dataset without any conditioning description. For each instance, the target is uniformly sampled from the 120 available names. 

• Thinking bootstrapping and RL Data : For this phase, we utilize 30 of the 60 names that lack description-to-name pairs in the knowledge acquisition set for RL training. A small subset of these (fewer than 5) is allocated to generate the dataset for reasoning bootstrapping. Additionally, the RL training mixture includes prompts for which the description-to-name pair was already provided during knowledge acquisition; this serves to regularize the policy, teaching the model to directly retrieve explicitly stated knowledge with minimal intermediate reasoning.

B.3. Leakage prevention in thinking models 

During reinforcement learning training of models which use chain-of-thought, it is possible that held-out knowledge which the model is tested on is present in the thinking trace and reinforced by a positive reward. In order to prevent this behavior, we use a separate prompt prefix for the in-distribution and out-of-distribution structures in the Semantic Structure dataset. This results in the model purely considering entities in one of the structures when it is questioned about an entity from that structure. This ensures that the chain-of-thought does not contain entities from the held-out structure during training, ensuring that performance on the out-of-distribution structure is purely due to improved latent learning ability, and not leakage of knowledge in the reinforced chain-of-thoughts. Although we did not do this procedure for the reversal curse dataset, we find that the Pass@1 score of the thinking model corresponds very closely to the strict reversal condition on the out-of-distribution semantic structure benchmark.
