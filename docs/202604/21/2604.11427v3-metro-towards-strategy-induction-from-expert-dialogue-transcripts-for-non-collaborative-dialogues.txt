Abstract 

Developing non-collaborative dialogue agents traditionally requires the manual, unscalable codification of expert strategies. We propose METRO , a method that leverages large lan-guage models to autonomously induce both strategy actions and planning logic directly from raw transcripts. M ETRO formalizes ex-pert knowledge into a Strategy Forest, a hi-erarchical structure that captures both short-term responses (nodes) and long-term strate-gic foresight (branches). Experimental re-sults across two benchmarks show that M ETRO 

demonstrates promising performance, outper-forming existing methods by an average of 9%-10%. Our further analysis not only re-veals the success behind M ETRO (strategic behavioral diversity and foresight), but also demonstrates its robust cross-task transferabil-ity. This offers new insights into building non-collaborative agents in a cost-effective and scalable way. Our code is available at https: //github.com/Humphrey-0125/METRO .

1 Introduction 

Building conversational agents for non-collaborative dialogues is receiving increased attention (Zhang et al., 2024a; Hu et al., 2025; Deng et al., 2024, 2023b), particularly in appli-cations like price negotiation (He et al., 2018), donation persuasion (Wang et al., 2019), or debt collection (Zhang et al., 2023). In these interactions, the user (i.e., the opposing party) holds conflicting interests with the agent and con-sequently exhibits resistance during the multi-turn conversation (Deng et al., 2023a; Lei et al., 2022; Shi et al., 2019; Dutt et al., 2021). This dynamic requires the agent to employ effective dialogue strategies, securing an agreement favorable to itself (Zhan et al., 2024).

Effective dialogue strategies are typically la-tent within expert dialogue transcripts of non-collaborative scenarios. These transcripts im-plicitly encode both strategy actions (what to say, e.g., emotional appeals or foot-in-the-door ) and planning logic (the timing of these actions, e.g., 

when to employ an emotional appeal ). Conse-quently, extracting these strategies has always been a labor- and knowledge-intensive task (Zhou et al., 2019a; Fransen et al., 2015). In this case, to build a non-collaborative dialogue agent, the standard paradigm relies on domain experts to first manu-ally analyze transcripts and codify a set of strat-egy actions (He et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2019; Bonaldi et al., 2022a; Zhang et al., 2023), followed by the strategy planner training using pre-collected or LLM-roleplayed transcripts (Zhang et al., 2024a; Deng et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2023). However, this process is inherently expensive and unscalable. With the emergence of Large Language Models (LLMs) capable of powerful induction 1 (Cai et al., 2025; de Souza et al., 2025), there is a compelling opportunity to shift such paradigm: Can we lever-age LLMs to induce both strategy actions and planning logic directly from raw transcripts? 

To this end, we propose METRO (Multi-dimEntional sTRategy induction from dialOgue transcripts), a novel method that extracts and for-malizes raw dialogue transcripts into a "Strategy Forest". Specifically, M ETRO operates in two phases: Induction and Application. During the In-duction phase, M ETRO extracts strategy actions as nodes and organizes multi-turn action trajectories into trees, each conditioned on specific dialogue states. This hierarchical architecture captures plan-ning logic across two temporal scales: immediate child nodes represent short-term tactical responses, while full branches encapsulate long-term strate-gic foresight. By synthesizing these perspectives,

METRO creates a multi-dimensional representation of the strategies embedded within expert transcripts. In the subsequent Application phase, M ETRO an-alyzes the current dialogue state and queries the Strategy Forest to retrieve the most relevant tree, which serves as a blueprint to guide the agent’s decision-making. As such, M ETRO enables cost-effective, scalable, and strategic performance by effectively leveraging historical expert knowledge. We conduct experiments on two benchmark datasets to demonstrate the superiority of M ETRO .Specifically, M ETRO achieves significant perfor-mance gains, surpassing other induction methods by an average of 10.24% across both benchmarks. Our analysis attributes this success to the gener-ation of diverse strategic actions and informative planning logic, which endow agents with a richer behavioral patterns and strategic foresight. More-over, M ETRO exhibits robust scalability via cross-task generalization: strategies induced from exter-nal domains remain highly effective in the target setting. Finally, we validate the potential of LLM-synthesized transcripts as a viable alternative to expert transcripts. To sum up, we conclude our main contributions as follows. • We highlight strategy induction from expert tran-scripts as an effective and scalable solution for non-collaborative dialogues. • We pioneer M ETRO , which formalizes expert knowledge in transcripts into a Strategy Forest, representing strategy actions and planning logic. • We conduct extensive experiments to validate the effectiveness of M ETRO and provide an in-depth analysis of the diversity, transferability, and expertise-sensitivity of the induced strategies. 

2 Related Work 

LLM Agents for Non-collaborative Dialogues .Effective dialogue strategies are the key of non-collaborative agents. Traditionally, these agents rely on expert-defined action sets derived from man-ual analysis of dialogue transcripts, such as action sets for persuasion (Wang et al., 2019), counter-speech (Bonaldi et al., 2022b), and negotiation (He et al., 2018). While these actions remain widely used (Wang et al., 2025b; Zhang et al., 2024a; Deng et al., 2023c; Yu et al., 2023), they represent a labor-intensive bottleneck. Furthermore, deploy-ing these actions requires sophisticated planning. Because simple prompting often lacks fine-grained control (Deng et al., 2024), recent works have intro-duced external planners like MCTS (Yu et al., 2023) or specialized plug-in models (Deng et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2025b; Du et al., 2025). Despite these advances, the overall pipeline remains resource-intensive and unscalable. While PRINCIPLES (Kim et al., 2025a) automates the extraction of planning logic ( when [situation], you should [action A], rather than [action B], because [reason] ), it fails to capture multi-turn planning logic essential for non-collaborative setting. 

Expert Knowledge Induction from Dialogues .Inductive learning empowers LLMs to synthesize broader patterns from granular examples (Cai et al., 2025; de Souza et al., 2025; Huang et al., 2024a; Louie et al., 2024). This capability has catalyzed significant research into task-specific dialogue tran-scripts, which act as rich repositories of human ex-perience and knowledge. In particular, researchers extract emergent user intents and slots (Liang et al., 2024b,a; Vukovic et al., 2024; Finch et al., 2025), and derive dialogue structures (Burdisso et al., 2024; Raedt et al., 2024; Choubey et al., 2025; Pryor et al., 2023). Furthermore, recent studies focus on identifying strategy actions (Ou et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025a; He et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2025; Hu et al., 2025), together with the rationales of these actions governing the planning logic (Jiang et al., 2025; Xie et al., 2024). While Kim et al. (2025a) recently adapts these ideas to non-collaborative dialogues, existing methods treat strategies as independent units. Instead, we induce and structure dialogue strategies into a forest, cap-turing the complex dynamics.

3.1 Strategy Forest Induction 3.1.1 Strategy Action Induction 

Standard knowledge induction relies on simply ex-tracting target information from raw transcripts, such as query intents (Gung et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2024a) and dialogue flows (Burdisso et al., 2024; Raedt et al., 2024; Choubey et al., 2025). However, this method is limited by the quality and diversity of the raw transcripts. To address this, we propose augmenting the extraction process with the internal knowledge of LLM agents. Specifically, by using strategy actions derived from raw transcripts as bases, we prompt the LLM agent to generate refined new strategic actions, effectively transcend-ing the limitations of the original transcripts. 

Action Extraction . M ETRO starts with the identi-fication of the strategic action ai for every expert utterance ui in transcript D. To streamline this, METRO supports two modes of operation. If an expert-curated action taxonomy exists for the tar-get non-collaborative task 2, M ETRO simply assigns the most appropriate action from the existing set. In the absence of such a taxonomy, M ETRO lever-ages its LLM backbone to autonomously induce a domain-specific action set based on the task de-scription, following (Hu et al., 2025).

3.1.2 Planning Logic Induction 

A full action trajectory is encoded within a dia-logue transcript. It is defined as the sequence of expert strategy actions, inferred from the expert’s utterances and labeled with the final task outcome (success or failure). M ETRO translates these tra-jectories using a state-centric tree structure, where trees with different dialogue states form the forest. To achieve this, we decompose each trajectory into local sub-trajectories, where each sub-trajectory represents an action path initiating from a specific dialogue state and carrying a final task outcome. Fi-nally, we aggregate all sub-trajectories rooted in the identical dialogue state (regardless of their source transcript) into a unified tree. This synthesizes di-verse historical planning logic within transcripts to enhance generalization.

The branches are then formed using all future trajectories within that cluster. Since not all branches are equally valuable, we prune low-value branches and retain only the top-K branches with the highest value. This process is detailed below. • Branch Generation . We construct branches for a tree by organizing all future trajectories from the same cluster. Instead of using ˆai, nodes are represented by the original data-driven actions 

ai to ensure authenticity, as they form trajecto-ries that are grounded in real user interactions. Identical action sequences are merged to form consolidated branches (e.g., Donation informa-tion → Foot in the door → Emotion appeal ). • Node Value Estimation . We assign a value to every node along a branch. This value is primar-ily determined by the final task outcome 4 of the dialogues that passed through it, a process analo-gous to value backpropagation in MCTS (Silver et al., 2016) (cf. Appendix A.2.3). • Branch Value Estimation & Pruning . Besides branches leading to failures, a branch that ap-pears successful but has been observed only a few times may also be unreliable. To mitigate the risk of overestimating sparsely observed but suc-cessful trajectories, our branch valuation incorpo-rates both performance and statistical confidence. Each node is augmented by a confidence term. This is derived from the Wilson Score Lower Bound (Wilson, 1927) using the empirical suc-cess and visitation counts of each node (cf. Ap-pendix A.2.3). The final value of a branch is then defined as the weighted sum of its constituent node values and their corresponding confidence scores. Subsequently, we perform pruning by re-taining the top-K branches with the highest value. This is further optimized in practice using Beam Search, accelerating the pruning process. 3.2 Inference with Strategy Forest 

Since the induced strategy forest contains rich strat-egy actions and planning logic, M ETRO omit the model training and directly performs retrieval aug-mentation during the inference to enhance its abil-ity in non-collaborative dialogues. 

Tree Retrieval . Before generating the response for turn t, M ETRO encodes the current dialogue his-tory dt−1 into a semantic embedding Emb (dt−1).This embedding serves as a query to traverse the Strategy Forest: we compute the cosine similarity between Emb (dt−1) and the root node of every tree, retrieving the single tree that exhibits the highest semantic similarity to the current state. 

Strategy Augmentation . We prompt M ETRO by integrating both breadth and depth planning logic derived from the retrieved tree. Inspired by Kim et al. (2025a), we recognize that retrieved logic may not perfectly align with the nuances of the current dialogue. Therefore, we task the LLM backbone with generating contextual strategy as suggestion. • Short-Term Suggestion . the LLM reinterprets the actions from breadth logic (e.g., ‘ provide spe-cific and concrete information ’) to fit the current dialogue context, summarizing them into a con-cise next-step strategy (e.g., ‘ explaining the char-ity’s concrete programs and impact to address the persuadee’s curiosity ’). For simplicity, we exclusively utilize the LLM-expanded actions for this step, as they represent optimized abstractions of the original data-driven actions. • Long-Term Suggestion . We identify the single branch with the highest average node value to serve as the depth logic (e.g., ‘ build credibility 

→ propose a donation ’). Similarly, the LLM reinterprets this trajectory, summarizing it into a high-level planning directive that emphasizes cumulative temporal effects (e.g., ‘ progressively strengthening trust and commitment prior to mak-ing a donation request ’). Finally, these two suggestions are concatenated to guide M ETRO in generating the final response. Notably, M ETRO diverges from MCTS, as it lever-ages multiple trees to capture multi-dimensional strategies (breadth and depth) without requiring expensive test-time computation. 

4 Experiments 

4.1 Experimental Setup 

Following common practice in the field (Zhang et al., 2024a; Deng et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2025), we evaluate agents by pairing them with synthetic user simulators exhibiting diverse non-collaborative be-haviors. In each interaction, both the agent and simulator alternate turns, employing strategies to maximize their respective self-interests. The di-alogue proceeds until the task goal is met or the maximum number of turns (i.e.,8) is reached, after which we assess the agent’s performance based on the collected interactions. User Simulators & Human Participants . Con-sistent with prior research (Deng et al., 2023c; Bianchi et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024b; Zhang et al., 2024a), we utilize diverse LLM-based user simulators, which demonstrate non-collaborative behaviors driven by specific personas. Following (Wang et al., 2019; Jiang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a), we incorporate two distinct persona dimen-sions: the Big-Five Personality (Goldberg, 1992) and the Decision-Making Styles (Scott and Bruce, 1995), augmenting each combination with an LLM-generated description. This yields a total of 200 diverse simulators per task, distributed across the 20 distinct categories (i.e., Big-Five × Styles). Fur-thermore, we employ 5 humans with varying per-sonas and conduct a human study in which partici-pants interact with the agents to assess their prac-tical effectiveness, following existing setup (Deng et al., 2023d; Zhang et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2024). METRO achieves promising performance and outperforms other induction methods across di-verse benchmarks and metrics . As shown in Table 1 (left), M ETRO demonstrates promising per-formance on the SR metric across all evaluated non-collaborative tasks, with an average performance gain of 9.93% over the second-best result. This advantage is particularly pronounced over meth-ods with supervised strategy modules (PPDPP) or MCTS (GDP-Zero). These results fulfill our ini-tial motivation to develop an effective and scalable solution. Moreover, in a direct comparison with other strategy induction method (PRINCIPLES), METRO demonstrates an average improvement of 10.24% across all metrics and datasets. A notable trade-off, however, is the increased dialogue length (AT) characteristic of strategy induction methods (PRINCIPLES and M ETRO ). This highlights a po-tential direction for future optimization: refining the induced strategies to achieve desired outcomes with greater conversational efficiency. METRO demonstrates superior practical utility in human evaluations . We validated our findings with a human user study to measure real-world ef-ficacy. The results, presented in Table 1 (right), are consistent with our simulator-based evaluations (left), with M ETRO again demonstrating the promis-ing performance, outperforming PRINCIPLES. 

4.3 Why is M ETRO Effective 

We analyze its induced strategy actions and plan-ning logic, supplemented by ablation studies. 

4.3.1 Strategy Action Analysis Setup . We begin by characterizing the distinct properties of strategies derived from human ex-perts, PRINCIPLES, and M ETRO through a diver-sity analysis. For each task, we first pool all human-crafted strategies pre-defined in the dataset with 100 randomly sampled actions each from PRINCI-PLES and M ETRO . We then apply K-Means clus-tering (using bge-large-en-v1.5 ) and analyze the distribution of each method’s actions across these clusters. We introduce two metrics: 1) Cluster Coverage (CC), defined as the percentage of clus-ters containing at least one action from the method; and 2) Within-Cluster Coverage (WCC), defined as the average proportion and standard deviation of actions from the same method per cluster. These metrics provide a coarse- and fine-grained measure of action diversity, respectively. 

METRO induces more diverse and nuanced ac-tions . Figure 2 shows results across varying cluster numbers K1 (x-axis). 1) When K1=2, all methods produce fundamentally similar high-level strategy actions (CC=100%). However, as the clusters be-come more fine-grained (increasing K1), a clear divergence emerges. While the CC for all methods naturally declines, M ETRO consistently maintains a higher coverage, indicating that it induces a more diverse and comprehensive set of actions that span a broader range of nuanced strategic categories. 2) The low WCC for human-crafted actions is an expected results due to their limited quantity. How-ever, we do note a slight increase in their WCC on the CB task. This occurs alongside a very low CC, suggesting that the human strategies, while semantically consistent, are concentrated within a narrow portion of the overall action space (lack of overall diversity). For both PRINCIPLES and METRO , WCC generally decreases with K1, indi-cating their actions are distributed broadly. 4.3.4 Cross-Task Strategy Transferability METRO -induced strategies enjoy strong cross-task transferability . As shown in Table 3, the LLM’s powerful contextualization capabilities al-low most methods to remain effective even when using strategies from a different task. Our analy-sis reveals several key insights: 1) CB strategies transfer well to P4G because they are designed for a more assertive task, but the reverse is not true, which yields weaker results; 2) The performance of PPDPP, which relies on a specialized planner, did not improve with transferred strategies after we ex-changed two planners. This underscores that when the planner fails to select the right action, even a LLM, which can contextualize selected strategy, cannot compensate for this flaw. 3) M ETRO often outperforms baselines, especially PRINCIPLES, in transfer tasks, highlighting its strategies of greater diversity and foresight. 4) We simulate strategy evo-lution by expanding the action space (e.g., CB to P4G → ALL to P4G). Most baselines suffer perfor-mance degradation, as simple LLM-based planners are prone to selection bias with a larger candidate set, while MCTS-based methods like GDP-zero incur prohibitive computational costs. In contrast, METRO remains effective. We attribute this to the precision of our tree retrieval mechanism, which can navigate an expanded strategy forest. 

5 Conclusion 

We pioneer an effective and scalable solution for non-collaborative dialogue by leveraging LLMs to induce strategy actions and planning logic directly from expert transcripts. We introduce M ETRO to formalize these induced strategies into a structured strategy forest. Extensive experiments demonstrate METRO ’s promising performance and the reason behind its success. More importantly, our work provides a investigation into tacit knowledge ex-traction from expert dialogues. We analyze a poten-tial structure for representing this knowledge and explore key characteristics such as diversity and transferability. This offers insights into dialogue agent design and tacit knowledge extraction. 

Limitations 

Fidelity of Expert Knowledge . A primary limita-tion of this work concerns the degree of domain ex-pertise reflected in the source transcripts. While we utilize the C RAIGSLIST NEGOTIATION and P ER -

> SUASION

FOR GOOD benchmark datasets, both of which consist of human-human dialogues, the par-ticipants are likely general users rather than pro-fessional practitioners in negotiation or persuasion. Due to the scarcity of high-fidelity transcripts pro-duced by certified experts, it remains challenging to verify the performance of M ETRO on professional-grade expertise. Nevertheless, our empirical analy-sis demonstrates that inducing strategies even from general human interactions yields significant per-formance gains over existing baselines. We de-fer the evaluation of M ETRO on specialized, high-expertise corpora to future research as such data becomes available. 

In-depth Analysis on LLM-generated Tran-scripts . We emphasize the value of mining do-main knowledge from expert transcripts, as these records capture the tacit knowledge inherent in hu-man interaction (Huang et al., 2024c). Unlike ex-plicit knowledge, this tacit knowledge is deeply embedded in individual experience and intuition (Ryle, 1945; Spitzer et al., 2022), which is diffi-cult to convey to others through writing or ver-balization (Nonaka, 1994; Lam, 2000; Gorman, 2002), and is only evident in human behaviors (Liu et al., 2022). Our method is specifically designed to formalize and utilize such tacit knowledge from unstructured behavioral data like transcripts, with specical focus on the task of non-collaborative di-alogues. Therefore, the primary motivation and scope of this study is human expert behavior; we treat LLM-generated transcripts as a secondary point of comparison rather than a core research object. Although our initial findings in Appendix C.3 indicate that LLM-generated transcripts can be effective, and in some cases, surpass ‘expert’ tran-scripts, we leave the in-depth characterization of LLM-generated transcripts for future exploration.

A Implementation Details 

We conduct the main experiments on a CPU-based server with a dual-socket Intel Xeon Plat-inum 8352V CPU (64 physical cores, 128 threads), while the PPDPP experiments are reproduced on an NVIDIA RTX A100 GPU. To reduce the over-all evaluation time, we adopt a mini-batch con-current execution scheme, where evaluation sam-ples are partitioned into small batches and pro-cessed in parallel across multiple processes. Di-alogue state representations are obtained using the bge-large-en-v1.5 embedding model, and dialogue states are clustered with K-Means implemented in the scikit-learn package. Most experiments rely on API-based model inference using the Ope-nAI and Aliyun platforms. All implementations are based on PyTorch.For all baselines, we adhere to the implementations described in their respective papers or official GitHub repositories. Note that the first two baseline categories utilize the manually curated strategy actions provided in their original works, whereas the PRINCIPLES generates strate-gies autonomously. Following Deng et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2024a); Hu et al. (2025), we use GPT-3.5-turbo as the LLM backbones for conversational agents and LLM-based evaluator, respectively. To ensure a more rigorous and holistic evaluation, we also recruited human participants to interact with and assess each agent. Evaluation details are pre-sented in Appendix B. 

A.1 Implementations of Baselines 

We implement representative LLM-based dialogue agents as baselines for comparison, adhering to their original configurations. The specific imple-mentation details are as follows: 

Standard: We simply prompt the LLM to inter-act with users relying solely on the provided task instructions, without employing any external dia-logue strategies. 

PPDPP: Following (Deng et al., 2024), we im-plement the plug-and-play policy planner using RoBERTa-large (Liu et al., 2019), based on the original training configurations. To evaluate its performance on the P4G dataset, we adapted the task instructions and strategy descriptions to align with the persuasion scenarios. We strictly followed the original two-stage training framework (SFT and RL). For the final evaluation, we selected the model checkpoint that achieved the best performance dur-ing the testing phase. 

GDP-Zero: Following (Yu et al., 2023), we im-plement GDP-Zero using Open-Loop MCTS. We strictly adhered to the experimental settings and hyperparameters recommended in the original pa-per (e.g., simulation count and exploration weight). Furthermore, we extended the evaluation to the CB task by adapting the task instructions and role-playing descriptions to the negotiation context. 

Proactive: Following (Deng et al., 2023d), we implement the Proactive prompting scheme. This method requires the LLM to explicitly select a di-alogue strategy from a pre-defined set before gen-erating the response. We adapted the prompt tem-plates to align with the specific action spaces of the P4G and CB datasets. 

ProCoT: Following (Deng et al., 2023d), we im-plement the Proactive Chain-of-Thought (ProCoT) framework. It instructs the LLM to generate a rea-soning chain regarding dialogue status and goal planning prior to response generation. We strictly adhered to the one-shot setting and adapted the rea-soning demonstrations to fit the specific negotiation and persuasion contexts. 

ICL-AIF: Following (Fu et al., 2023), we imple-ment the ICL-AIF framework. It employs a critic model to provide natural language feedback, acting as in-context demonstrations to guide the LLM’s re-sponse generation. We adapted the critic’s prompts to align with the strategic goals of the P4G and CB datasets. 

PRINCIPLES: Following (Kim et al., 2025b), we implement PRINCIPLES as a non-parametric strat-egy memory constructed via offline self-play sim-ulations. We closely follow the original principle construction pipeline, structured principle extrac-tion in the When–Should–Rather than–Because for-mat. For faithful reproduction, we adopt the princi-ple generation procedure described in the original paper and construct a total of 296 principles for the P4G task and 100 principles for the CB task.

A.2.3 Planning Logic Induction 

This section details the implementation of Plan-ning Logic Induction , which translates full expert strategy trajectories into state-conditioned planning logic represented by a strategy forest.

Breadth and Depth Planning Logic. After prun-ing, each strategy tree supports planning logic along two complementary dimensions: breadth and depth. At inference time, we first match the cur-rent dialogue state to a dialogue state cluster, and then extract both short-term and long-term guid-ance from the corresponding state-centric tree. 

Breadth Planning Logic (short-term). Breadth logic corresponds to the immediate children of the root node , which represent feasible one-step re-sponses conditioned on the current dialogue state. In our implementation, we further enrich these root-level actions by inducing and retrieving micro-principles that abstract and reflect effective re-sponses at this decision point. Concretely, each micro-principle is associated with a root-level ac-tion and formulated in a fixed “ When . . . ” form, capturing when a particular response is appro-priate. At inference time, we embed the oppo-nent’s most recent utterance and retrieve the top-K

micro-principles (we use K=5 ) within the matched cluster by semantic similarity. These retrieved principles thus provide a reflective, semantically grounded representation of root-level actions, serv-ing as short-term guidance. 

Depth Planning Logic (long-term). Depth logic is represented by complete root-to-leaf branches in the state-centric strategy tree, encoding long-term data-driven action sequences observed from the same dialogue state. These branches are pre-computed offline using the confidence-aware value estimation described above. From the matched cluster, we select the highest-ranked strat-egy chain (we use k=1 ) as the long-horizon plan. Together, breadth logic (root-level actions en-hanced by micro-principles) and depth logic (long-term strategy chains) form the strategy forest aug-mentation used during inference. Rather than being applied verbatim, both forms of retrieved logic are subsequently reinterpreted and refined by the lan-guage model to adapt to the specific nuances of the current dialogue context (see Table 26). 

B Evaluation Details 

B.1 Persona Generation 

To improve the robustness of self-play evaluation, we follow the persona construction protocol pro-posed in (Zhang et al., 2024a). Specifically, for both P4G and CB dialogues, we construct discrete counterpart personas by prompting an LLM to infer the persona of the non-agent side (the persuadee in P4G and the seller in CB) using only that par-ticipant’s utterances. Each persona is defined by exactly two categorical attributes: one Big-Five personality trait and one decision-making style, re-sulting in a compact and discrete specification for conditioning self-play evaluation.(see Table 27) 

B.2 Task Overview 

Following common practice in the field (Zhang et al., 2024a; Deng et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2025), we evaluate agents by pairing them with synthetic user simulators exhibiting diverse non-collaborative be-haviors. In each interaction, both the agent and simulator alternate turns, employing strategies to maximize their respective self-interests. The di-alogue proceeds until the task goal is met or the maximum number of turns (i.e.,8) is reached, af-ter which we assess the agent’s performance based on the collected interactions. Following the pro-tocol in Zhang et al. (2024a); Deng et al. (2024); He et al. (2025), we employ an LLM-based critic model, utilizing multi-sampling and a majority vot-ing mechanism, to determine the termination status of each dialogue. 

P4G. Persuasion outcomes are assessed by an attitude-based critic that classifies the per-suadee’s final stance into four categories ( reject ,

neutral , positive , donate ), mapped to rewards 

{− 1.0, −0.5, 0.1, 1.0}. We perform five parallel evaluations and aggregate by majority vote. A di-alogue is considered successful if the average re-ward exceeds 0.6, while outcomes below −0.7 are treated as clear failures; otherwise, the dialogue terminates after at most 8 turns. The critic prompt is shown in Table 18. 

CB. Negotiation outcomes are evaluated by a strict deal-based critic that determines whether an explicit agreement is reached at the end of the dia-logue and extracts the agreed price if so. Success is declared only when all sampled critics agree that a deal is finalized; otherwise, the outcome is no-deal, with dialogues capped at 8 turns. The correspond-ing prompt is provided in Table 19. 

B.3 Interactive Evaluation B.3.1 Protocol of Human Evaluation 

We employ 5 humans with varying personas and conduct a human study in which participants in-teract with the agents to assess their practical ef-fectiveness, following existing setup (Deng et al., 2023d; Zhang et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2024). These human participants consist of adult volun-teers recruited from the authors’ research group and academic colleagues. Participation is entirely voluntary, and participants retain the right to with-draw at any time. They receive task instructions we delivered, and background using academic pa-pers (He et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2019). We do not collect sensitive personal data from participants beyond their specific study responses. These are recorded without direct identifiers. During the human evaluation phase, each partic-ipant engage in twelve distinct dialogue sessions with every evaluated method. To ensure objectiv-ity, all assessments are conducted independently to avoid mutual interference, with final performance metrics calculated as the aggregate mean across all sessions. B.3.2 Protocol of User Simulator Evaluation 

Consistent with prior research (Deng et al., 2023c; Bianchi et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024b; Zhang et al., 2024a), we utilize diverse LLM-based user simulators, which demonstrate non-collaborative behaviors driven by specific personas. Follow-ing (Wang et al., 2019; Jiang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a), we incorporate two distinct persona dimensions: the Big-Five Personality (Goldberg, 1992) (Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, and Neuroticism) and the Decision-Making Styles (Scott and Bruce, 1995) (Directive, Conceptual, Analytical, and Behavioral), augment-ing each combination with an LLM-generated de-scription. This yields a total of 200 diverse sim-ulators per task, distributed across the 20 distinct categories (i.e., Big-Five × Styles). Appendix C.2 shows our results on these diverse users. 

B.4 Evaluation Metrics 

Notably, as the evaluation metrics are calculated automatically and do not rely on subjective scoring by humans or LLMs, inter-rater evaluation reliabil-ity, whether human-human or human-LLM, is not applicable. 

B.4.1 Implementation of Automatic Evaluation Metrics 

Following Deng et al. (2024); Hu et al. (2025); Zhang et al. (2024a), we consider three commonly used metrics: Success Rate (SR), Average Turn (AT) and Sale-to-List Ratio (SL%). The SR mea-sures effectiveness by the percentage of goal achievement within a maximum number of turns, while AT measures efficiency by the average num-ber of turns required to achieve the goal. As for the CB task, we additionally adopt the SL% (Zhou et al., 2019b) to determine the effectiveness of goal completion. Formally, the SL% is expressed as 

(Pdeal − P seller target )/(P buyer target − P seller target ), where Pdeal 

is the final deal price, P buyer target and P seller target are the target prices of both parties. A higher SL% repre-sents the buyer gets more benefits from the deal. If failing to reach a deal at the end, we set SL% as 0.

C.1 Additional Analysis on Human Study 

METRO demonstrates superior practical utility in human evaluations. We validated our findings with a human user study to measure real-world efficacy. The results, presented in Table 1 (right), are con-sistent with our simulator-based evaluations (left), with M ETRO again demonstrating the promising performance. We posit this is not because LLMs sur-pass human experts, but because our ’Expert’ data, which was sourced from AMT according to He et al. (2018), likely does not represent true domain exper-tise (i.e., the participants are likely general users rather than professional practitioners in negotiation or persuasion). This leads to two robust conclu-sions: first, the quality of transcripts is paramount for effective strategy induction, and second, LLM-generated transcripts can serve as a potent and scal-able substitute for human data when expert collec-tion is infeasible. 

C.4 Efficiency Analysis 

Unlike methods such as GDP-Zero that rely on computationally expensive test-time MCTS infer-ence, our approach is designed for high efficiency. The strategy forest is pre-computed offline. Before generating a response, M ETRO only needs to per-form a single cosine similarity search to retrieve the relevant strategy tree. The LLM backbone then contextualizes the retrieved short- and long-term strategies to generate suggestions for the current turn. This design results in minimal computational overhead during runtime, with the only addition being a single extra LLM prompt for suggestion generation per turn. In terms of storage, while the strategy forest does introduce a memory footprint, it remains modest. The primary storage cost comes from the root node embeddings. In our experi-ments, with N=150 trees, the entire forest required only ∼25 MB of storage space, which is negligi-ble on modern hardware. In summary, our method is not only highly effective but also demonstrates excellent practical utility, combining low-latency inference with a minimal storage footprint.
