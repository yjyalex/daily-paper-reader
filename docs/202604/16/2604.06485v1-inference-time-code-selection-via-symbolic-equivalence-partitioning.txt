# ABSTRACT 

“Best-of-N ” selection is a popular inference-time scaling method for code generation using Large Language Models (LLMs). However, to reliably identify correct solutions, existing methods often depend on expensive or stochastic external verifiers. In this paper, we propose Symbolic Equivalence Partitioning, a selection framework that uses symbolic execution to group candidate programs by semantic behavior and select a representative from the dominant functional partition. To improve grouping and selection, we encode domain-specific constraints as Satisfiability Modulo Theories (SMT) assumptions during symbolic execution to reduce path explosion and prevent invalid input searches outside the problem domain. At N = 10 , our method improves average accuracy over Pass@1 from 0.728 to 0.803 on HumanEval+ and from 0.516 to 0.604 on LiveCodeBench, without requiring any additional LLM inference beyond the initial N candidate generations. 

# 1 Introduction 

Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across a wide range of tasks, from text summarization to complex reasoning and code generation Chen et al. [2021], OpenAI et al. [2023]. To further enhance these capabilities without the prohibitive cost of retraining, inference-time scaling—the allocation of additional computational resources during the inference phase—has emerged as a critical paradigm for complex reasoning tasks OpenAI et al. [2024], Brown et al. [2024]. Recent studies demonstrate that for logic and code, scaling test-time compute often yields higher marginal returns than scaling model parameters Snell et al. [2025]. In the domain of code generation, this is commonly realized through “Best-of-N ” methods, where the model generates multiple candidate solutions and a selection mechanism identifies the most promising one. Prior work shows that correctness is often present somewhere in a diverse set of samples even when greedy decoding fails. Methods such as Self-Consistency Wang et al. [2023] and CodeT Chen et al. [2023] exploit this through agreement-based aggregation, based on the intuition that independently sampled solutions that are correct are more likely to

As a result, selecting from a larger pool of N candidates can substantially improve pass rates. However, the efficacy of Best-of-N methods relies entirely on the selection mechanism. Ideally, correctness is verified by executing code against a comprehensive suite of ground-truth test cases. In real-world scenarios, however, such test cases are expensive to produce and rarely available. Furthermore, execution-based consensus often becomes effective at larger sample budgets (e.g., N ≥ 100 ), which increases both generation cost and reranking overhead, thereby increasing inference latency and computational budget Li et al. In this paper, we propose a selection framework that removes the need for auxiliary generated tests or learned verifiers for final reranking by leveraging symbolic execution to group programs based on their intrinsic functional behavior. Our approach uses symbolic path analysis to identify inputs that differentiate behavior, allowing us to group functions based on their semantic logic without requiring any external signals or generated tests. Applying symbolic execution to arbitrary LLM-generated code can be intractable due to path explosion—the exponential growth of execution states in complex loops—and the tendency of solvers to search for invalid counterexamples outside the intended input domain. We address these challenges by introducing SMT-Constrained Pruning, a technique that injects domain-specific constraints as Satisfiability Modulo Theories (SMT) assumptions. These constraints effectively prune the search space, focusing the symbolic engine on valid input domains relevant to the problem statement. Our contributions are as follows: • We introduce Symbolic Equivalence Partitioning (SEP) , an inference-time selection framework that groups LLM-generated programs by semantic behavior under symbolic execution and selects from the dominant equivalence class. • We propose SMT-Constrained Pruning , which injects domain constraints as solver-level assumptions to reduce invalid symbolic exploration and mitigate path explosion in competitive-programming style tasks. • We evaluate our method on HumanEval+ and LiveCodeBench across eight models and three sampling budgets, showing strong and consistent gains over Pass@1 and strong reranking baselines without requiring any additional LLM inference beyond the initial N candidate generations. # 2 Related Work 

Inference-Time Scaling. The paradigm of scaling test-time compute encompasses several distinct strategies. Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Wei et al. [2022] prompts models to decompose problems into intermediate reasoning steps, effectively utilizing more computation per token to enhance logical consistency. Iterative refinement methods, such as Self-Refine Madaan et al. [2023], employ a critic loop where the model critiques and regenerates its own output. Tree-based search 2Inference-Time Code Selection via SEP algorithms such as Tree of Thoughts Yao et al. [2023] or MCTS Inoue et al. [2025] systematically explore multiple reasoning paths guided by a reward model to find optimal solutions. Finally, parallel sampling, or “Best-of-N ,” Chen et al. [2021], Cobbe et al. [2021] generates multiple independent solutions and employs a selection mechanism to identify the correct answer. While CoT and search methods scale compute by increasing depth through sequential calls, we focus on scaling width via Best-of-N .

Consensus and Reranking. To identify the correct solution from N candidates, prior methods rely on consensus or learned verifiers. Textual consensus mechanisms, such as Self-Consistency Wang et al. [2023], are effective for reasoning tasks but can fail in code generation, where syntactically distinct programs can be functionally equivalent. Execution-based consensus, such as CodeT Chen et al. [2023], improves this by executing candidates against generated test cases. However, these methods require additional generation overhead for test case generation and large sample sizes ( N ≥ 100 ) to achieve robustness. Other approaches employ learned reward models Li et al. [2025], Ma et al. [2024], but these introduce a secondary source of stochasticity and require training domain-specific verifiers. In contrast, our framework eliminates the need for such proxies by approximating functional behavior directly through bounded symbolic analysis. 

Symbolic Execution. Symbolic execution is a static analysis technique that executes programs using symbolic values rather than concrete inputs King [1976]. Instead of a single trace, the execution engine maintains a symbolic state and a set of logical constraints known as a path condition. By querying a Satisfiability Modulo Theories (SMT) solver, the engine can determine reachability for specific branches or generate counterexamples that violate assertions. Leveraging such characteristics, symbolic execution has traditionally been applied to automated testing and bug finding. While powerful, symbolic execution is often limited by the path explosion problem, where the number of execution states grows exponentially with loop depth and branching factors. Our method mitigates these limitations by performing functional symbolic analysis directly on the generated function signatures, using domain-specific SMT constraints to prune the search space of inputs and mitigate path explosion. Most relevant to our work is Sharma and David [2025], which also uses symbolic analysis for code generation. The key difference is objective: their method is primarily designed for abstention (deciding when to withhold an answer), whereas ours uses symbolic equivalence partitioning as a Best-of-N selector to choose a final candidate. Methodologically, we integrate SMT-constrained path pruning to keep exploration within valid input domains and reduce path explosion, making symbolic selection practical at inference time. We also operate directly on function-based interfaces rather than relying on string I/O wrappers, which preserves richer type and structural information for constraint injection. 

# 3 Methodology 

We propose a Symbolic Equivalence Partitioning framework. Let s denote a problem specification consisting of a natural language description D and a set of domain constraints C (e.g., input bounds, valid data types). An LLM generates a set of N candidate programs P = {p1, p 2, . . . , p N } for problem s.We define two programs pi and pj as functionally equivalent with respect to domain C, denoted pi ≡C pj , if and only if they exhibit identical execution outcomes for all valid inputs: 

pi ≡C pj ⇐⇒ ∀ x ∈ Dom( C), EO i(x) = EO j (x). (1) Our goal is to partition P into equivalence classes (partitions) {K1, . . . , K M } and select a representative p∗ from the largest partition, under the hypothesis that consensus among diverse correct solutions converges to the ground truth.

If no counterexample is found within the predefined symbolic execution budget, we treat the pair as equivalent for the purposes of clustering. This yields a bounded approximation to functional equivalence, defined by the explored symbolic paths and resource limits. We discuss this approximation in Section 5. In practice, we instantiate this equivalence check using CrossHair McNutt [2023], a differential symbolic execution engine for Python that translates program paths into constraints for the Z3 SMT solver De Moura and Bjørner [2008]. All equivalence checks are performed under a fixed resource budget, including limits on total solver time and symbolic path exploration, to ensure bounded analysis. 

3.2 SMT-Constrained Pruning 

A fundamental challenge in applying symbolic execution to competitive programming problems is path explosion and the generation of spurious counterexamples. Symbolic solvers may identify inputs that technically violate the code but are disallowed by the problem specification. A straightforward approach is to insert assert() statements to enforce input constraints; however, in symbolic execution, assertions introduce explicit failure branches that create additional exception paths, often exacerbating path explosion and hindering effective analysis. To address this, we implement a translation pipeline that encodes input constraints as executable SMT assumptions rather than program-level assertions. 

1. Constraint Extraction and Normalization. We first extract the explicit constraint block from the problem description D. Constraint expressions involving input parameters are then parsed into Python abstract syntax trees (ASTs) and normalized using syntactic rewrite rules to handle common patterns such as chained comparisons. 

2. Injection of Z3 Assumptions. For every candidate program pk, we inject a symbolic preamble before the main logic. This preamble interacts directly with the symbolic engine’s constraint store. Injection of such preambles adds constraints directly to the underlying Z3 solver instance. If a symbolic path violates these assumptions, the solver raises an IgnoreAttempt exception, effectively pruning that branch from the search tree. This encourages the symbolic engine to focus on the valid input domain Dom( C), substantially reducing path explosion and limiting false positives from invalid inputs. 

3.3 Greedy Representative Partitioning 

We implement partitioning using a greedy representative-based procedure. Candidates are processed one at a time while maintaining a set of discovered partitions, each with a single representative. For each candidate, we compare it against the representatives of existing partitions, ordered by partition size from largest to smallest. If symbolic equivalence under C is established, the candidate is assigned to that partition; otherwise, a new partition is created. Once all candidates are assigned, the final prediction is taken to be the representative of the largest partition. When multiple partitions have the same size, we break ties by selecting the representative from the partition encountered earliest in the greedy ordering. To avoid the cost of performing pairwise comparisons for all O(N 2) candidate pairs, we treat symbolic equivalence as approximately transitive under bounded analysis. This reduces the complexity to O(N · M ), where M is the number of distinct semantic partitions, with M ≤ N .Additionally, we use the public example input/output cases provided in the problem statement as a lightweight filter. Prior to symbolic analysis, all N candidates are executed against these concrete test cases. Candidates that fail to match the expected output or raise runtime exceptions are pruned immediately, effectively reducing required comparisons without sacrificing solution quality.

HumanEval+. HumanEval+ Liu et al. [2023] is a benchmark of 164 Python function-synthesis problems, where models must generate a function that satisfies a hidden test suite. It extends the original HumanEval benchmark with roughly 80 × more hidden tests, making evaluation much stricter and reducing the chance that superficially plausible but incorrect programs pass by chance. 

LiveCodeBench. LiveCodeBench Jain et al. [2025] is a contamination-resistant benchmark that continuously collects recent competitive programming problems from platforms such as LeetCode, AtCoder, and Codeforces. We evaluate on 438 filtered problems. It provides a challenging setting for inference-time code selection because the problems are contest-style, algorithmically diverse, and less susceptible to benchmark saturation from training data contamination. To ensure compatibility with function-level symbolic analysis, we excluded problems where: 1. the problem statement lacked a valid function signature (e.g., relied on raw stdin /stdout parsing), or 2. the provided ground-truth test cases were found to be incorrect or inconsistent during our manual audit. These exclusions were made solely to support functional symbolic analysis and reliable evaluation. The complete list of excluded problem names is detailed in Appendix A.

4.2 Main Results 

We evaluate Symbolic Equivalence Partitioning across eight models (4B to 20B parameters) at N ∈ { 5, 10 , 20 }.Tables 1 and 2 present the primary N = 10 setting, and additional N = 5 /N = 20 results are provided in the appendix. We treat N = 10 as the default operating point because it balances selection quality and generation cost. At N = 10 , our method improves average accuracy from 0.728 to 0.803 on HumanEval+ and from 0.516 to 0.604 on LiveCodeBench, outperforming the strongest competing baseline averages of 0.783 and 0.567, respectively. Across all 48 model–dataset–budget settings, SEP is best or tied-best in 46. 

HumanEval+. At N = 10 , SEP maintains strong gains over Pass@1, improving accuracy by +7.6 points on 

Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct , +12.1 on Phi-4-mini-instruct , and +17.5 on Phi-4-mini-reasoning . The main exception is Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 , where LLM-Judge slightly exceeds SEP (0.829 vs. 0.823). Notably, this model also exhibits a very narrow gap between Pass@1 and oracle Pass@10, and the smallest spread of performance across selection methods overall, indicating limited room for improvement from reranking. LiveCodeBench. At N = 10 , SEP is strongest on all 8 models, with large gains over Pass@1, including +12.9 points on Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (0.127 → 0.256), +8.7 on Phi-4-mini-instruct (0.169 → 0.256), and +13.2 on Phi-4-mini-reasoning (0.176 → 0.308). Notably, for Phi-4-mini-reasoning , our 0.308 nearly reaches the pass@10 oracle ceiling of 0.311. This pattern suggests SEP helps especially on harder contest-style problems, where syntactic similarity is less reliable than functional behavior. 

4.3 Additional Analysis Scaling with candidate pool size. Figure 2 shows that Symbolic Equivalence Partitioning scales favorably with the candidate pool size on both HumanEval+ and LiveCodeBench. As N increases from 5 to 20, our method achieves consistent improvements in average accuracy and maintains an advantage over the competing baselines. This indicates that Symbolic Equivalence Partitioning is effective in low-sample settings, while also extracting greater benefit from additional generated candidates than alternative reranking methods. 

Pairwise equivalence accuracy. To assess the bounded-search approximation used in SEP, we evaluate pairwise equivalence accuracy : the agreement between SEP’s bounded symbolic equivalence decision for a program pair and a reference label derived from benchmark correctness. Because two incorrect programs may coincide on benchmark tests without being truly semantically equivalent, we evaluate this metric only on pairs containing at least one correct program (i.e., correct–correct and correct–wrong pairs), and exclude wrong–wrong pairs. Accuracy remains high in aggregate (mean 0.898 over 48 model–budget settings), with means of 0.922 on HumanEval+ and 0.873 on LiveCodeBench. Pairwise accuracy is somewhat lower on LiveCodeBench, likely reflecting deeper control flow and more complex algorithmic branching in contest-style problems. 4.4 Runtime / Cost Analysis 

Accuracy alone does not capture the practical tradeoffs of reranking methods, so we next compare post-generation runtime costs. All reported runtimes are total wall-clock times aggregated over the full benchmark evaluation, rather than per-problem costs. Among the evaluated rerankers, the only methods with substantial post-generation overhead are SEP and CodeT. Candidate generation and CodeT’s auxiliary test generation were performed on a single NVIDIA H100 GPU, whereas SEP’s symbolic equivalence checks were executed on an AMD Ryzen 7 7700X CPU. Accordingly, the reported runtime numbers should be interpreted as end-to-end wall-clock costs under our experimental setup rather than as normalized same-device throughput comparisons. Averaged across the eight evaluated models, SEP requires 511 s, 1166 s, and 2559 s on HumanEval+ at N = 5 , 10 , 20 ,and 989 s, 2319 s, and 4779 s on LiveCodeBench, respectively, showing the expected increase with candidate pool size. In both benchmarks, increasing N from 5 to 10 increases SEP runtime by about 2.3×, while increasing from 10 to 20 adds a further 2.1–2.2×, for an overall ≈ 5.1× increase from N = 5 to N = 20 .By contrast, CodeT incurs a much larger additional cost from auxiliary test generation: total end-to-end runtime ranges from 14 minutes to nearly 11 hours on HumanEval+, and from 4.2 to 20.7 hours on LiveCodeBench, depending on the model. This highlights a different tradeoff: SEP pays a bounded symbolic-analysis cost that scales with N ,whereas CodeT incurs substantial additional inference cost for synthetic test generation. Detailed model-level runtime breakdowns for both methods are provided in Appendix D. 

# 5 Limitations 

While Symbolic Equivalence Partitioning performs strongly across our benchmarks, several limitations inherent to symbolic execution and our specific implementation remain. 

Language and Toolchain Dependence: Our current implementation relies on Python-specific symbolic execution engines. While the theoretical framework of symbolic consensus is language-agnostic, adapting it to other languages (e.g., C++, Java, Rust) requires robust symbolic execution backends (e.g., KLEE or CBMC), which may require additional engineering effort. 

Scalability Constraints: The computational cost of our method scales as O(N · M ), where N is the number of candidate solutions and M is the number of distinct semantic clusters discovered under bounded symbolic analysis. For large candidate pools, this verification cost may become prohibitive for real-time applications. However, our empirical results show that strong performance is achieved at modest sample sizes ( N = 10 ), substantially reducing the need for large-scale sampling in practice. 

Solver Completeness & Search Budget: Symbolic execution in SEP is bounded by a finite solver budget (e.g., time or search depth). Although pairwise equivalence accuracy is high in practice (Section 4.3), the analysis is often not 8Inference-Time Code Selection via SEP exhaustive: if a semantic difference lies beyond the search horizon—for example, requiring deep recursion or a specific large input that triggers a distinct execution path—the solver may fail to find a counterexample. In such cases, SEP may incorrectly treat two non-equivalent implementations as equivalent. 

Problem Constraints & Domain Specificity: This limitation is most relevant for competitive programming tasks, where programs are often highly sensitive to precise input constraints, boundary conditions, and problem-specific invariants. In such settings, our constraint injection method substantially reduces exploration of invalid inputs by enforcing explicitly stated domain restrictions. However, it does not reliably recover all constraints that are only implicitly specified in natural language or embedded in complex problem logic, so the symbolic engine may still explore unintended inputs, potentially causing spurious separation of semantically equivalent solutions. 

# 6 Conclusion 

We presented Symbolic Equivalence Partitioning (SEP) , an inference-time framework for selecting correct code solutions from multiple LLM generations. Unlike prior consensus-based methods that rely on auxiliary test generation or pretrained verifiers, SEP groups candidates by semantic behavior using symbolic execution. Across HumanEval+ and LiveCodeBench, three sample budgets ( N = 5 , 10 , 20 ), and eight models, SEP consistently improves over Pass@1 and achieves the strongest accuracy among non-oracle baselines in most settings. SEP also incurs substantially lower wall-clock overhead than the strongest competing baseline, CodeT. These gains are achieved without requiring additional LLM inference beyond the initial N candidate generations. Although symbolic execution remains limited by solver budgets and domain constraints, our results show that formal semantic analysis can serve as a practical and effective inference-time signal for code selection, complementing sampling-based generation without requiring auxiliary supervision.

# A Excluded LiveCodeBench Problems 

To ensure compatibility with function-level symbolic analysis and reliable evaluation, we excluded a subset of LiveCodeBench problems from our experiments. No excluded problem was used in evaluation for any method reported in this paper. Exclusions fall into two categories: • I/O-based problems. Problems whose reference solutions rely on raw stdin /stdout parsing rather than a well-defined function signature were excluded. • Invalid or inconsistent test cases. Problems for which the provided ground-truth test cases were found to be incorrect, incomplete, or internally inconsistent during manual inspection were excluded to avoid confounding evaluation results. # C CodeT Implementation Details 

For the CodeT baseline, we follow the dual-stage test-generation and execution framework described by Chen et al. [2023] and use the official implementation provided at https://github.com/microsoft/CodeT .

Test Case Generation. CodeT generates synthetic test cases by prompting the same language model used for solution generation. Following the original paper, we construct prompts by appending a test-generation instruction p to the provided starter code. Concretely, the prompt instructs the model to output only executable assert statements that verify the correctness of the given function. An example prompt structure is shown below: 11 Inference-Time Code Selection via SEP 

# Only output assert test cases. # Use: assert function_signature(...) == ... <starter_code> pass # check the correctness of starter_code 

We additionally include explicit instructions to discourage the model from generating problem-solving logic or explanations, ensuring that the output consists solely of concrete test cases. 

Execution and Selection. All N candidate solutions are executed against the generated test cases. Each solution is represented by an execution fingerprint—a binary vector indicating pass/fail outcomes across tests. Candidates with identical execution fingerprints are grouped into score buckets. CodeT then ranks these buckets using a dual-agreement score that reflects both how many generated tests a bucket passes and how many mutually agreeing solutions it contains. The final output is selected from the top-ranked bucket rather than by always materializing a fixed deterministic “first” solution from the largest cluster. 1. Test Generation: For each problem, we sample independent test-generation outputs and retain up to five valid assert statements from each output that invoke the target entry point. For HumanEval+, we follow the original CodeT setting and use 100 raw test-generation samples per problem, yielding at most 500 generated test cases. For LiveCodeBench, the same setting introduces substantially higher overhead because the problems and generated solutions are typically longer and more complex. Under this configuration, test generation could exceed the cost of the original candidate-solution generation itself. We therefore reduce the budget to 20 raw test-generation samples per problem on LiveCodeBench, yielding at most 100 generated test cases. 2. Execution: All N candidate solutions are executed against the resulting set of generated tests, producing pass/fail outcomes for each test. 3. Grouping and Ranking: Solutions are grouped based on identical execution fingerprints, groups are ranked by a dual-agreement score (generated-test pass strength + agreement count), and the final answer is selected from the top-ranked group.
