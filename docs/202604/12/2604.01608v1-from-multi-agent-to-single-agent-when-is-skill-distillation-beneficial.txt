## Abstract 

Multi-agent systems (MAS) tackle complex tasks by distributing expertise, though this often comes at the cost of heavy coordination overhead, context fragmentation, and brittle phase ordering. Distilling a MAS into a single-agent skill can bypass these costs, but this conversion lacks a principled answer for when and what to distill. Instead, the empirical outcome is surprisingly inconsistent: skill lift ranges from a 28% improvement to a 2% degradation across metrics of the exact same task. In this work, we reveal that skill utility is governed not by the task, but by the evaluation metric. We introduce Metric Freedom (F ), the first a priori predictor of skill utility. F measures the topological rigidity of a metric’s scoring landscape by quantifying how output diversity couples with score variance via a Mantel test. Guided by F , we propose a two-stage adaptive distillation framework. Stage 1 acts as a selective extraction mechanism, extracting tools and knowledge while discarding restrictive structures on “free” metrics to preserve exploration. Stage 2 targets computationally intensive iterative refinement exclusively toward “rigid” metrics ( F ≲ 0.6) to eliminate trajectory-local overfitting. Evaluating across 4 tasks, 11 datasets, and 6 metrics, F strongly predicts skill utility ( ρ=−0.62 ,

p< 0.05 ). Strikingly, identical agent trajectories yield diametrically opposite skill lifts under rigid versus free metrics, demonstrating that skill utility is fundamentally a metric-level property. Driven by this signal, our adaptive agent matches or exceeds the original MAS while reducing cost up to 8 × and latency by up to 15 ×.

## 1 Introduction 

Large Language Model (LLM) agents have demonstrated remarkable proficiency across complex domains, from scientific discovery to software engineering [Boiko et al., 2023, Jimenez et al., 2024, Zhou et al., 2024]. To tackle increasingly difficult tasks, the field has largely converged on Multi-Agent Systems (MAS), which decompose problems into specialized, interacting sub-agents to capture domain expertise. However, this distributed approach comes at a steep cost: severe coordination overhead, context fragmentation, and brittle phase ordering. Consequently, a compelling alternative has emerged: distilling the expertise of a MAS into a single-agent system equipped with specialized skills [Xu et al., 2026, Luo et al., 2026]. While recent work shows these single-agent systems can match MAS performance at a fraction of the cost, empirical results reveal a baffling inconsistency. Skill distillation can yield massive performance gains ( +28pp) or actively degrade performance (−2pp) on the exact same task .Existing literature largely treats this variance as an unpredictable byproduct of task complexity or data scale, proceeding under the assumption that skill augmentation is universally beneficial. approaches focus entirely on how to distill skills—either through rigid structural conversions that map MAS pipelines into instructions [Li, 2026, Ni et al., 2026, Yim et al., 2026], or through iterative trajectory optimization [Alzubi et al., 2026, Yang et al., 2026, Xia et al., 2026, Zhou et al., 2026]. Both paradigms suffer from a critical blind spot. Non-adaptive structural conversions routinely over-constrain agents on simple queries, while iterative methods succumb to trajectory-local overfitting, bloating the skill library until retrieval accuracy collapses [Li, 2026]. The fundamental question remains unanswered: when does distilling a MAS into single-agent skills actually help? 

In this work, we reveal that the efficacy of skill distillation is not dictated by the task itself, but by an overlooked property of the evaluation metric . We introduce Metric Freedom (F ), the first quantifiable, a priori predictor of skill utility. F measures the topological rigidity of a metric’s scoring landscape by quantifying how tightly output variation couples with score variation via a Mantel test. When a metric is rigid (low F )—such as requiring an exact methodological choice—the structured guidance of a distilled skill steers the agent through a narrow success corridor, providing immense benefit. Conversely, when a metric is free (high F )—where diverse reasoning paths can yield equally valid scores—these same structural constraints artificially restrict the agent’s natural exploration, leading to degraded outcomes. The starkest evidence of this phenomenon occurs in Causal Estimation: evaluating identical agent outputs using different metrics (MSA versus MRE) yields opposite skill lifts. This contradiction is cleanly resolved by their disparity in Metric Freedom (FMSA ≈0 vs. FMRE ≈0.7–0.9). Crucially, F can be computed directly from the n raw-agent baseline runs already required for evaluation, adding zero inference cost. To operationalize this insight, we propose an F -guided, two-stage adaptive distillation framework. Rather than blindly mimicking MAS pipelines, our approach uses Metric Freedom as a principled sieve to determine exactly what to extract and when to refine it. In Stage 1 (Adaptive Extraction) ,tools are universally preserved and domain knowledge is smoothly distilled into conditional references rather than rigid, mandatory instructions. Restrictive coordination structures are discarded entirely, and explicit task-decomposition hints are retained only for low-F (rigid) metrics, preventing the over-constraining of agents on free tasks. Stage 2 (Targeted Refinement) introduces an optional four-agent iterative optimization loop (Explore, Main, Analyzer, Runner) to close residual performance gaps. By activating this computationally intensive loop exclusively where F dictates it will be effective ( F ≲ 0.6), we eliminate trajectory-local overfitting, achieving up to a +40pp gain in just 2 iterations on highly rigid tasks like CE-MSA. Extensive evaluation across four complex tasks, 11 datasets, and 6 diverse metrics validates our core hypothesis: Metric Freedom strongly and reliably predicts the utility of skill distillation ( ρ=−0.62 ,

p< 0.05 , n=13 ). By acting solely on this a priori signal, our adaptive single-agent system matches or exceeds the performance of the original MAS while reducing inference cost by 1.4–8 × and latency by up to 15 ×. Our core contributions are: • Metric Freedom ( F ): We introduce the first a priori , metric-level criterion for predicting when MAS-to-skill conversion is beneficial. F is computable entirely from standard baseline runs, requiring zero additional inference overhead. • Adaptive Two-Stage Distillation: We design a principled framework that abandons the “one-size-fits-all” approach to skill generation. Guided by F , Stage 1 selectively distills knowledge and structures, while Stage 2 targets refinement compute exclusively toward rigid metrics where it yields the highest return on investment. • Reframing Skill Evaluation: We provide conclusive evidence that skill utility is a metric-level property, not a task-level one. By demonstrating that identical agent trajectories can yield diametrically opposite skill lifts under rigid versus free metrics, we fundamentally correct a prevalent blind spot in how agentic skills are evaluated. 

## 2 Background and Related Work 

2.1 Multi-Agent Systems 

The rise of tool-augmented LLM agents [Schick et al., 2023, Yao et al., 2023] has enabled increasingly autonomous agentic workflows. Task-specific MAS have proliferated across domains, building on general-purpose frameworks such as AutoGen [Wu et al., 2024] and MetaGPT [Hong et al., 2024]. APEX-SQL [Cao et al., 2026] uses a hypothesis-verification loop with dual-pathway pruning for 2text-to-SQL, achieving 70.7% execution accuracy on BIRD [Li et al., 2023]. It coordinates an explorer agent that generates schema hypotheses, a verifier that tests against candidate queries, and a pruner that reduces the search space—a pipeline that is effective but requires strict phase ordering. MATMCD [Shen et al., 2025] deploys a Data Augmentation agent and a Causal Constraint agent for multi-modal causal discovery, complementing classical structure-learning algorithms [Spirtes et al., 2012, Zheng et al., 2018] with inter-agent debate to resolve conflicting edge predictions. CAIS [Verma et al., 2025] automates the full causal estimation pipeline—traditionally requiring careful method selection among IV, DiD, and RDD [Imbens and Rubin, 2015]—through a decision-tree method selector, a self-correction feedback loop, and domain-knowledge-enriched prompting. FELA [Ouyang et al., 2025] coordinates Idea, Code, Critic, and Evaluation agents in an evolutionary loop for feature engineering. While each system demonstrates improvements over non-agentic baselines, they share common architectural patterns: rigid phase ordering, inter-agent message passing, and specialized sub-agents for exploration, verification, and debate. These patterns introduce overhead that may be unnecessary when a sufficiently capable single model can perform multi-perspective reasoning internally [Qian et al., 2024]. 

2.2 Single-Agent vs. Multi-Agent Debate 

Recent work challenges the prevailing assumption that MAS inherently outperform single agents. Li [2026] find that a single agent equipped with a skill library can match MAS performance at lower cost on a broad set of benchmarks. Crucially, they identify a phase transition : skill selection accuracy degrades sharply as the library exceeds a critical size, suggesting that unstructured skill accumulation has diminishing returns. Xu et al. [2026] demonstrate that single-agent multi-turn conversations achieve comparable accuracy to homogeneous MAS workflows across diverse coding and reasoning tasks, and propose OneFlow to automatically optimize single-agent execution graphs. Luo et al. [2026] take a complementary approach, distilling multi-agent reasoning traces into a single model’s weights via hierarchical fine-tuning (AgentArk), extending classical knowledge distillation [Hinton et al., 2015] from model compression to agent compression and showing that the knowledge embedded in multi-agent interactions can be preserved without the multi-agent infrastructure .Our work differs from all three in a key respect: rather than comparing single vs. multi-agent in general , we study which specific MAS components (tools, knowledge, heuristics, pipelines) are worth converting to single-agent skills. We further provide a metric-theoretic criterion (metric freedom) that predicts when such conversion is beneficial—a dimension absent from prior analyses. The phase transition identified by Li [2026] also suggests that skill quality matters more than quantity; our framework addresses this by constructing F -guided, task-adapted skills rather than accumulating them indiscriminately. 

2.3 Skill Discovery and Evolution 

Alzubi et al. [2026] propose EvoSkill, a self-evolving framework where agents autonomously discover reusable skills through iterative failure analysis, successful trace distillation, and cross-agent skill sharing. In a related vein, Voyager [Wang et al., 2023] demonstrates open-ended skill accumulation in embodied settings, where an LLM agent builds a growing skill library through environmental interaction. Both approaches show that skill-level optimization produces genuinely transferable capabilities. Our skill iterator (Stage 2) shares the spirit of iterative refinement but operates on 

MAS-derived skills rather than discovering skills from scratch, and targets task-specific optimization through a structured four-agent architecture (Explore, Main, Analyzer, Runner) rather than open-ended exploration.

For per-question tasks (CE, T2SQL), only mixed questions —where scores vary across runs—are included; questions where all runs succeed or all fail are excluded, as they reflect task difficulty rather than metric structure.

Our working hypothesis is that F and lift norm are negatively correlated: rigid metrics ( F ≈ 0) benefit most from skill augmentation, free metrics ( F ≈ 1) benefit least or are even harmed. We validate this empirically in Section 4.2. 

3.2 Stage 1: Adaptive MAS Distiller 

The distiller transforms a multi-agent system into a single-agent skill through four phases: freedom-guided metric analysis, MAS component inventory, F -dependent conversion, and validation. 

Phases 1–2: Analysis and inventory. Before touching MAS code, the distiller characterizes the target metric along five dimensions: equivalence strictness , output space constraints , near-optimal solution density , perturbation sensitivity , and knowledge-to-metric distance . Critically, we distinguish coordination structure (message passing, voting, shared memory)—a MAS artifact always discarded—from task decomposition (subtasks, quality criteria), whose retention is F -dependent. Phase 3: F -dependent conversion. The three tiers—Low ( F < 0.25 ), Mid ( 0.25 –0.65 ), High (> 0.65 )—are derived from two convergent signals. Empirically, every data point below F =0 .25 in our validation set yields headroom-normalized lift above 0.60, indicating a regime where structure reliably helps; every data point above F =0 .65 yields lift below 0.20 for rigid skill components, indicating a regime where constraints reliably hurt. Analytically, these boundaries correspond to natural inflection points in the exploration-cost curve, calibrated on a held-out validation subset by identifying the F values at which per-component ablations first reverse sign (from positive to negative lift). The mid-band captures transition behavior where selective retention is optimal. Table 2 summarizes component dispositions as a function of F . Every skill item imposes an exploration cost : at low F , the correct output is narrow and guidance helps; at high F , many outputs score well and constraints hurt. Three principles govern conversion: (1) keep all callable tools , which extend capability without constraining reasoning; (2) encode domain knowledge as conditional references (“when X holds, Y is true”), not mandatory instructions; (3) discard forced pipelines and coordination modules , since a single large model already performs multi-perspective reasoning internally [Yao et al., 2023]. The generated skill consists of a SKILL.md file and a tools/ directory; skill length varies with F (120–200 lines for low-F , 80–150 mid-F , 50–100 high-F ). 

Phase 4: Validation. We verify that: (a) all tool modules import correctly; (b) no step-sequence patterns leak into high-F skills; (c) every knowledge item uses reference tone rather than instruction tone; and (d) test cases for tool-trigger, tool-skip, and boundary conditions pass. As summarized in 5Figure 1 (Panel C), tools and domain knowledge consistently provide positive value; heuristics are task-dependent; and forced pipelines are neutral or harmful. 

3.3 Stage 2: Skill Iterator 

Stage 2 is an optional refinement step recommended when F ≲ 0.6. At high F , score changes between skill versions are indistinguishable from stochastic variation and iteration risks overfitting to noise; at low F , each score change carries a reliable improvement signal. The iterator refines skills through a four-agent architecture (Figure 1, Panel D). 

Agents. Explore Agent runs once before the loop to analyze benchmark structure, dataset char-acteristics, and split boundaries. Main Agent is the long-running orchestrator: it activates skill versions, dispatches evaluations on train/val splits, summarizes results, makes stopping decisions, and maintains full iteration history. Analyzer is a stateless sub-agent spawned fresh each iteration; it receives the current skill, performance metrics, verbose failing traces, and evolution history, then diagnoses root causes and outputs a revised skill—zero memory between iterations ensures repro-ducible analysis. Runners are skill-augmented single-agent instances executing the benchmark on each split. 

Per-iteration workflow and failure diagnosis. Each iteration proceeds through evaluate (collect metrics and traces), summarize (identify 1–3 failing cases with highest error signal), analyze (spawn Analyzer, receive revised skill), and write (atomic write with syntax validation). The Analyzer classifies root causes into: tool bug, guidance ignored, guidance wrong, missing knowledge, or unclear reasoning—each triggering a targeted fix along one of three dimensions: skill guidance 

(rewriting SKILL.md rules), tool coverage (surgical fixes to affected functions), or thinking path 

(adding verification checkpoints or decision trees where traces reveal skipped reasoning steps). 

Termination. The orchestrator stops on convergence (key metrics unchanged for 3+ consecutive iterations), sufficiency (validation accuracy exceeding a threshold, e.g., 95%), oscillation (same root cause recurring after 2+ fix attempts), or budget exhaustion (max 15 iterations). The best skill version across all iterations is selected for deployment (Section 4.7). 

## 4 Experiments 

Our experimental design is structured to validate three theoretical predictions: (1) F predicts skill lift direction and magnitude across tasks; (2) Stage 1 adaptive conversion outperforms both unguided single-agent execution and pipeline-faithful MAS compilation; (3) Stage 2 iteration yields additional gains specifically on low-F tasks. We first describe the setup, then present per-task results in order of increasing metric freedom, and close with correlation and cost analyses that connect the results back to the theory.

On both benchmarks, the adaptive skill provides the best EX among single-agent methods: +8.8pp over raw on BIRD-147 and +5.8pp on Spider-120. Causal Estimation ( FMSA ≈ 0; FMRE ≈ 0.65 –0.92 ). Causal Estimation is the paper’s central case study because it contains both ends of the freedom spectrum within a single task. MSA (method selection accuracy) requires choosing the correct causal method—a binary decision with almost no partial credit ( F ≈ 0). MRE (mean relative error) rewards closeness to the true effect size— a continuous metric where many estimators score similarly ( F ≈ 0.65 –0.92 ). Our framework predicts large skill lift on MSA and minimal lift on MRE. We evaluate on three datasets from CauSciBench [Acharya et al., 2026]: QRData (39 queries), Synthetic (45), and Real (31). This task provides the paper’s sharpest test of our hypothesis. On MSA ( F ≈ 0, rigid), the adaptive skill closes a +28.2 pp gap on QRData (61.5% →89.7%), matching the hand-engineered full skill at ∼20% lower cost. On MRE ( F ≈ 0.65 –0.92 , free), the same adaptive skill shows no advantage; lightweight ablations—Knowledge Only (23.1% MRE) and Tools Only (10.8% on Real)—outperform it, confirming that added structure actively degrades exploration when the metric is forgiving. The same agent, the same outputs, opposite directions of lift: this is the central empirical fact of the paper, and it admits only one explanation—freedom is a metric-level property. The MAS Compiler, despite faithfully preserving the CAIS pipeline, costs 3 × more than the adaptive skill for lower MSA across all datasets, underscoring that pipeline fidelity is orthogonal to skill quality. 

Causal Discovery ( F = 0 .24 –0.77 , heterogeneous). Causal Discovery occupies the middle of the freedom spectrum, with F1 values varying substantially across datasets. F1 gives partial credit for correct edges, and multiple valid DAGs can represent equivalent causal structures. We therefore expect heterogeneous effects: larger lift on datasets with clear domain structure (lower F ) and smaller lift where the raw agent can already explore freely. We evaluate on five datasets: AutoMPG [Quinlan, 1993] (5 nodes), DWDClimate [Mooij et al., 2016] (6), Sachs [Sachs et al., 2005] (11), Asia [Lauritzen and Spiegelhalter, 1988] (8), and Child [Spiegelhalter, 1992] (20). F1 scores are computed on predicted edge sets. Results confirm the predicted heterogeneity. On Sachs ( F =0 .24 , the lowest-freedom CD dataset), the adaptive skill achieves F1=0.952 (+8.3pp over raw), the largest single-dataset gain in this task. On AutoMPG ( F =0 .41 ), the adaptive skill is slightly below raw (0.632 vs. 0.670)—consistent with moderate freedom where the raw agent can already navigate the output space effectively. The original MAS (MATMCD) performs notably poorly on Sachs (F1=0.557) despite its elaborate debate mechanism, suggesting that debating on incorrect domain assumptions amplifies errors. 4.4 Ablation: Tools vs. Knowledge vs. Pipeline 

Stage 1’s design rests on an empirical claim: tools and domain knowledge each provide independent lift, while pipeline structure does not. We verify this on the two tasks with the widest F contrast: Text-to-SQL (mid-F ) and Causal Estimation (mixed F ). Tools and knowledge are complementary— the full skill outperforms either alone on both tasks (Figure 4). Knowledge carries the primary lift on rigid metrics (CE-MSA: 87.4% knowledge-only vs. 71.1% tools-only), while tools are critical for computation-heavy metrics (CE-MRE: tools-only achieves the best MRE on Real at 10.8%). Notably, tools-only yields lower MSA than the raw agent (71.1% vs. 82.9%)—giving the agent execution capabilities without domain rules to guide method selection is counterproductive. The Structured Pipeline variant scores 89.7% on CE-MSA, comparable to knowledge-only but well below the full adaptive skill (95.5%), confirming that pipeline structure adds no value beyond what knowledge alone provides.

Why Identical Outputs Yield Opposite Lifts: A Deep Dive into CE-MSA vs. CE-MRE. The most incisive evidence for metric freedom arises from examining identical agent trajectories under two evaluation lenses. In Causal Estimation, the same raw-agent runs produce FMSA ≈ 0 and 

FMRE ≈ 0.65 –0.92 —opposite ends of the spectrum—from identical outputs. The mechanism is straightforward once one examines the scoring geometry. Because the agent explores diverse causal methods (IV, DiD, RDD, OLS) across its runs, outputs are far apart in output space. Under MSA, this diversity is catastrophic: exactly one method is correct per query, so on the questions where outcome is contested (some runs correct, some wrong), pairs of differing outputs are almost always far apart in score space too ( ρ(Dout , D score ) ≈ 1, F ≈ 0). The scoring surface is a knife-edge; any deviation from the correct method is penalized with zero credit. Under MRE, the same diversity is largely irrelevant: multiple estimation methods often converge to numerically similar effect sizes, so pairs of outputs that differ in method choice are nevertheless close in score space ( F ≈ 0.65 –0.92 ). The agent is simultaneously “lost” under MSA and “free” under MRE, despite identical trajectories. This divergence cleanly explains the apparent paradox—the skill that closes a +28 pp MSA gap produces 

negative lift on MRE—and is the clearest possible demonstration that skill utility is a property of the evaluation metric, not of the task or the agent. 

4.6 Cost and Efficiency 

With prompt-cache pricing, single-agent methods cost 30–40% of their uncached price, while original MAS baselines receive no cache benefit due to dynamic inter-agent communication. Cost savings range from modest to dramatic depending on the task. On T2SQL, the adaptive skill ($0.32/query) is slightly cheaper than APEX MAS ($0.45) — a 1.4 × advantage. On CE, CAIS remains the cheapest method ($0.07–$0.12/query) due to its fixed, non-exploratory pipeline, yet the adaptive skill delivers 10–28 pp higher MSA at only $0.17–$0.23. The cost advantage is largest on CD: the original MAS costs up to 8 × more per run than single-agent alternatives (Figure 2). On Spider-120, the adaptive skill costs more than the raw agent ($0.87 vs. $0.46) due to longer skill prompts; the +5.8pp EX gain justifies this for accuracy-sensitive use cases, but practitioners on tight budgets should weigh this trade-off. 4.7 Stage 2: Skill Iterator Results 

Stage 2 is most informative on low-freedom metrics, where score changes reliably signal skill improvement. Figure 5 shows the iteration trajectories across all four tasks, revealing a clear pattern: low-F tasks (CE-MSA, CD) exhibit rapid gains followed by oscillation or plateau, while high-F

tasks (FE) show only marginal improvement, consistent with the prediction that metric freedom governs iterator effectiveness. 

Causal Estimation (CE-MSA). Starting from a base skill (v0), Figure 5(a) and Table 7 detail each step. The iterator achieved +40pp Val MSA improvement (60% →100%) in just 2 iterations. Each iteration applied a different optimization angle: iteration 1 used tool fixes (DiD sign correction) and knowledge alignment (IV vs. RDD rules); iteration 2 used thinking path alignment (RCT vs. observational distinction). Iterations 3–4 exhibit oscillation where fixing one edge case breaks another; the iterator correctly identifies v2 as optimal and terminates. 

Causal Discovery (preliminary). We applied Stage 2 to Causal Discovery using F1 as the opti-mization target. Results show consistent gains on datasets with lower F : DWDClimate (+14.3pp, 12 F1= 0.701 vs. 0.558 raw) and Child (+14.9pp, F1= 0.952 vs. 0.803 raw), where the metric signal is strong enough to guide iteration reliably. On AutoMPG ( F =0 .41 , moderate), the gain is smaller (+1.8pp); on Asia ( F undefined due to perfect raw performance), no additional improvement is possible. These preliminary results are consistent with the prediction that Stage 2 is most valuable at lower F values. Broader evaluation across additional tasks is ongoing. 

## 5 Conclusion 

We introduced metric freedom as a principled, a priori measurable criterion for predicting when MAS-to-skill conversion provides value. The key finding is that freedom is a metric-level property, not a task-level one: the same task, the same agent, and the same outputs can yield dramatically different skill lift under different evaluation metrics. This has a concrete implication: practitioners should diagnose their evaluation metric’s freedom before investing in skill engineering. Our two-stage system—adaptive conversion guided by F , with optional automatic iteration on low-F tasks— matches or exceeds both raw agents and original MAS on most task–metric combinations, at 1.4–8 ×

lower cost (and up to 15 × lower latency) depending on MAS coordination overhead.

## 6 Limitations and Future Work 

Limitations. Our F computation requires existing benchmark runs ( n=10 in our study); for genuinely new tasks without prior data, F cannot be computed directly and must be estimated from proxy benchmarks or prior literature. The skill iterator can oscillate without converging on noisy or high-F

metrics; while we include an explicit oscillation-detection stopping condition (Section 3.3), this 15 heuristic does not always prevent score regression, and benefits are demonstrated on a small number of tasks; broader validation is needed. All experiments use a single base model (Claude Sonnet 4.6)—the relative advantage of structured skills may shift as foundation model capabilities evolve, since more capable models may internalize domain knowledge that skills currently provide externally. Finally, the F –lift correlation ( ρ= − 0.62 , n=13 ) is statistically significant but based on a modest sample; replication on broader benchmarks would strengthen the empirical foundation. 

F as a Thermometer of Knowledge Externalization. A more consequential boundary condition concerns the capability trajectory of foundation models. All experiments use Claude Sonnet 4.6; the relative advantage of structured skills may diminish as models become more capable, since a sufficiently powerful model may internalize domain knowledge that skills currently provide externally. Crucially, F itself would signal this shift: as a future model internalizes, say, causal method-selection expertise, the raw-agent outputs would become less diverse (the model confidently selects one method), causing FMSA to rise toward 1—indicating that the skill has become redundant. In this sense, F functions not only as a static predictor of skill utility but as a thermometer of knowledge externalization : a low F reveals a knowledge gap that structured distillation must fill; a rising F

across model generations signals that the gap is closing. Tracking how F distributions evolve with model capability would provide a principled, benchmark-grounded measure of the degree to which advances in pretraining reduce the need for external skill engineering. 

Future work. Several promising directions emerge. First, extending metric freedom to operate without benchmark runs—through proxy metrics derived from task descriptions or small validation sets—would make the framework applicable to novel tasks. Second, studying cross-task skill transfer, where skills distilled from one domain improve performance on related tasks, could amplify practical value. Third, as argued above, systematically measuring F across successive model generations would reveal whether foundation model progress is converging toward universally high-F regimes, or whether rigid metrics remain stubbornly resistant to internalization [Guo et al., 2024]. 16 A Metric Freedom: Per-Datapoint Details

17 C Metric Freedom Computation Details 

For each (task, dataset, metric) tuple, we compute metric freedom F using existing benchmark runs without additional cost. We use all available runs from the “Claude Code Raw” condition, as these provide the most unbiased estimate of output variation (skills may constrain output diversity). 

Bootstrap confidence intervals. We report σF in Table 8, computed via bootstrap resampling (B=1000 ) of the run pairs. For per-question metrics (CE, T2SQL), we first compute F per question, then report the median and its bootstrap standard error. 

Exclusion criteria. For per-question tasks (CE, T2SQL), we include only mixed questions where scores vary across runs—questions where all runs succeed or all runs fail are excluded, as they reflect task difficulty or task ceiling rather than the metric’s scoring geometry. For per-dataset tasks (CD, FE), datasets where all runs produce identical scores (e.g., CD-Asia, perfect F1) are excluded as the Mantel test is undefined. CE-Synthetic-MSA is additionally excluded because the 97.8% raw accuracy leaves no mixed questions after filtering. 

Sensitivity to distance function. We verified that replacing Jaccard distance with edit distance (for T2SQL) or Hamming distance (for CD) does not qualitatively change the freedom ranking. The Spearman correlation between freedom and lift remains significant ( p < 0.05 ) under all tested distance functions.
