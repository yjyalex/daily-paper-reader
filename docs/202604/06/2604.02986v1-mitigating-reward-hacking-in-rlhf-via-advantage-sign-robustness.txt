## Abstract 

Reward models (RMs) used in reinforcement learning from human feed-back (RLHF) are vulnerable to reward hacking: as the policy maximizes a learned proxy reward, true quality plateaus or degrades. We make the assumption that reward hacking is often caused by flipped advantage signs: instead of reducing the likelihood of a bad response, a flipped sign causes the update to increase it. By considering an adversarial perturbation in the RM parameter space, we can derive a certified sign-preservation radius , which is the smallest perturbation that can flip the advantage sign during policy optimization. Based on this formulation, we propose Sign-Certified Policy Optimization (SignCert-PO) , down-weighting non-robust completions in the policy gradient update. Unlike prior approaches that require multiple RMs or access to the RM training data, SignCert-PO is lightweight and operates purely at the policy optimization stage using only the RM parameters and on-policy completions. On TL;DR summarization and AlpacaFarm bench-marks, SignCert-PO consistently achieves a better win rate than baselines and reduces reward hacking. 

## 1 Introduction 

Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) has become a standard paradigm for aligning large language models (LLMs) with human intent (Christiano et al., 2017; Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022). In the typical RLHF pipeline, a reward model (RM) is trained on pairwise human preference data and then used as a proxy for the true, unobservable preference signal during policy optimization. Because the RM is inherently an imperfect proxy, optimizing the policy against it often causes the policy to exploit local inaccuracies in the reward landscape. This phenomenon is known as reward hacking (Gao et al., 2023). As optimization progresses, proxy reward continues to rise while true quality stagnates or degrades. This is especially acute when the proxy RM is trained with limited preference data (Gao et al., 2023) or when the policy drifts from the RM’s training distribution during policy optimization by reinforcement learning (RL) (Ackermann et al., 2025). Prior approaches to mitigating reward hacking can be organized along an axis from ante-hoc 

to post-hoc (Retzlaff et al., 2024). Ante-hoc methods incorporate uncertainty quantification into the reward modeling pipeline. For instance, ensemble-based methods (Coste et al., 2024; Eisenstein et al., 2024) train multiple RMs and optimize conservatively with respect to their disagreement, while distributional or uncertainty-aware RMs (Xu et al., 2024) modify the RM architecture or training objective. These approaches can directly quantify RM uncertainty, but require either multiple RM training and inference runs or changes to the RM pipeline, limiting their practical applicability. AdvPO (Zhang et al., 2024), the closest prior work to ours, is only partially post-hoc: it derives single-RM uncertainty estimates from last-layer embeddings without retraining, but still requires access to the entire RM training dataset to calibrate its uncertainty model. Our approach is entirely post-hoc : it operates solely with the current RM parameters and on-policy completions, and thus we do not need to train new RMs nor retain the dataset used for RM training. We discuss additional related work in Appendix A.2. We argue that what matters during policy optimization is whether the RM correctly predicts the sign of each completion’s advantage, since that sign determines whether the policy increases or decreases the completion’s probability. Crucially, the reliability of this prediction varies per completion, and a naive robustness penalty fails to capture this variability. We therefore ask: for a given completion, how much would the RM need to change before that advantage sign flips? Based on randomized smoothing (Cohen et al., 2019), we formalize this quantity as a certified sign-preservation radius and down-weight completions whose advantage signs are easy to flip (Figure 1). We first show that directly considering perturbations of all RM parameters is computationally challenging, as it requires per-completion gradients. This quickly causes us to run out of memory, even for relatively small RMs. Instead, we propose to approximate this solution by only considering perturbations of the RM head, yielding a lightweight re-weighting of the standard policy gradient update. The resulting method, Sign-Certified Policy Optimization (SignCert-PO) , prevents policy updates from being dominated by directions whose update signs are locally fragile under RM perturbations. We evaluate SignCert-PO on TL;DR summarization (Stiennon et al., 2020) and Alpaca-Farm (Dubois et al., 2023) with Pythia (Biderman et al., 2023) and Qwen2.5 (Qwen et al., 2025) models. Using the gold-model evaluation setup (Gao et al., 2023), we compare against Dr.GRPO (Liu et al., 2025), uncertainty-weighted optimization (UWO) with RM ensem-bles (Coste et al., 2024), behavior-supported policy optimization (BSPO) (Dai et al., 2025), 2Preprint. Under review. and AdvPO (Zhang et al., 2024). SignCert-PO achieves the highest gold-RM win rate in most settings, with improved RM accuracy during policy optimization. 

## 2 Background

We assume a prompt distribution P over X , where P(x) denotes the probability mass of a prompt x ∈ X .An autoregressive language model πϕ with parameters ϕ is then conditional probabil-ity πϕ(y|x) = Πtπϕ(yt|x, y<t), where x ∈ X is a prompt, y ∈ Y is the response, yt is the t-th response token and y<t are the response tokens before yt. Following the RL literature (Sutton & Barto, 2018), we sometimes use the word policy, referring to the language model πϕ. We denote the ℓ2 norm by ∥ · ∥ 2.

2.1 Reinforcement learning from human feedback 

RLHF typically consists of three key steps: supervised fine-tuning (SFT), reward modeling, and reinforcement learning (Stiennon et al., 2020). We denote the SFT model as πSFT .

Reward modeling. Suppose the true reward function r∗ : X × Y → R that mirrors human preference exists. Since r∗ is generally unknown, an RM is trained as a proxy with the following procedure: given a prompt x ∈ X , two responses y0, y1 ∈ Y are sampled from πSFT (y|x). A human annotator then selects their preferred response, yielding a dataset of triples (x, yw, yl) where yw (winning) is preferred over yl (losing). A scalar RM

2.2 Randomized smoothing 

Reward modeling reduces to a binary classification problem on reward margins (Tang et al., 2024; Nishimori et al., 2025). Randomized smoothing (RS) (Cohen et al., 2019) is a framework for certifying the robustness of a classifier’s prediction against perturbations. Consider a classifier f : Rd → [C] over C classes. Given a noise scale σ > 0 for the Gaussian distribution N , RS creates the smoothed classifier g(x) := arg max 

> c∈[C]

Pr   f (x + ξ) = c, where ξ ∼ N (0, σ2 Id),which returns the class most likely to be predicted under random perturbations of the input. Cohen et al. (2019) showed that if one predicted class is sufficiently more likely than the others under these perturbations, then the prediction of the smoothed classifier is provably consistent throughout an ℓ2 ball around x. The radius of this ball is called the certified radius. 

That is, the certified radius is the maximum ℓ2 perturbation to the input under which the smoothed classifier’s prediction is guaranteed to remain unchanged. 

## 3 Robust policy gradient method 

We argue that when the RM rθ assigns incorrect advantage signs, the policy is not prop-erly updated and thus reward hacking can occur. Guided by the notion of the certified radius (Cohen et al., 2019), we formalize this by introducing the certified sign-preservation radius , the smallest perturbation of the RM parameters that flips a completion’s advantage sign (§3.1). This leads to our proposed method of re-weighting the policy gradient updates, using the radius as a reliability measure (§3.2). Finally, we compare our method of using per-completion perturbations to the standard global perturbation setup, which we show suffers uniform pessimism unlike our method. We focus on the advantage estimate used in Dr.GRPO (4) throughout the rest of the paper, but we note that other PG methods can be used instead, as shown in Appendix E.

4.2 Additional experiments and analysis 

We further perform additional experiments to better understand our proposed method. 

How SignCert-PO mitigates reward hacking. Figure 2 (left) shows that as the policy drifts from πSFT (i.e., increasing KL divergence), the proxy RM’s accuracy on on-policy completions degrades. Dr.GRPO’s accuracy drops toward 50% at high KL as a result, while SignCert-PO follows a different trajectory. Our proposed method keeps the policy in regions where the proxy RM maintains accuracy around 60%, even as optimization progresses. This is a consequence of the re-weighting mechanism: completions whose advantage signs are easy to flip receive reduced gradient weight, preventing the policy from being pulled toward regions where the proxy RM is unreliable. This RM accuracy degradation can lead to reward hacking, as suggested by Figure 2 (right). Dr.GRPO’s proxy reward (dashed) continues to rise even as its gold reward (solid) collapses. SignCert-PO achieves a comparable or even better gold reward at a fraction of the KL budget and avoids the collapse. 

Comparison with early stopping. The gold reward curves in Figure 2b also demonstrate the comparison with early stopping. Dr.GRPO with β=0.01 (the one also used for SignCert-PO) reaches peak gold reward around KL ≈100, after which the reward degrades. An ideal early-stopping mechanism that stops training at this point would recover its peak gold reward, which is still lower than the gold reward after training by SignCert-PO. In contrast, SignCert-PO converges to a favorable region without relying on external evaluation.

## 5 Conclusion 

We presented SignCert-PO, a lightweight method for RLHF that mitigates reward hack-ing by down-weighting policy gradient contributions of completions whose advantage signs are fragile under RM parameter perturbations. Our approach derives a certified sign-preservation radius that quantifies, for each completion, the smallest perturbation to the RM that can flip the advantage sign. This per-completion formulation yields a sim-ple re-weighting mechanism of the standard PG update, requiring only the current RM head parameters and on-policy completions. Experiments on TL;DR summarization and AlpacaFarm across Pythia and Qwen2.5 model families show that SignCert-PO achieves the highest gold-RM win rate in most settings while adding negligible computational overhead. Our method makes several simplifying assumptions (§3.2). Further, our per-sample ad-versary formulation is more conservative than a shared-adversary variant. Analyzing the tighter coupled formulation is a future direction. Finally, extending the certified sign-preservation radius to general RMs without incurring the cost of full-parameter gradient norm computation remains an open problem. 9Preprint. Under review.

Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO). GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) replaces the learned value network in PPO with a group-based Monte Carlo baseline. For each prompt 

x ∈ X , a group of K completions y(1), . . . , y(K) ∈ Y is sampled from the current policy

In the original GRPO, the advan-tage is the normalized reward: bAj := ( rj − mean (r)) /std (r), where r is the vectorized reward (r1, . . . , rK )T, and mean (·) and std (·) denote the mean and standard deviation of the elements of a vector, respectively. GRPO can be viewed as replacing PPO’s learned value baseline with a within-group relative baseline computed from sampled completions. This removes the need to train a separate value network, allowing for preserving VRAM and scaling up the RLHF pipeline. Dr.GRPO (Liu et al., 2025) is a widely-used variant that removes the standard-deviation normalization to avoid biased gradient estimation (see §2.1 in the main text). 

A.2 Additional related work 

Reward hacking arises when the policy distribution drifts outside the region where the proxy RM is reliable. Prior work addresses this through ensemble-based uncertainty, modified RM training, or adversarial robustness. 

RM ensembles. Ensemble-based methods mitigate reward hacking by optimizing a policy conservatively with respect to multiple RMs. Coste et al. (2024) train several proxy RMs and study conservative objectives such as worst-case and uncertainty-weighted optimization (UWO), showing that ensembles reduce reward hacking in the gold-RM setup of Gao et al. (2023). Eisenstein et al. (2024) observe that ensembles still exploit spurious features when all members agree on them. Zhai et al. (2026) propose LoRA (Hu et al., 2022)-based RM ensembles to reduce the cost of training multiple full RMs. These approaches require multiple RM training and inference runs. 14 Preprint. Under review. 

Distribution shift and modified RM training. Reward hacking can also be viewed as a distribution shift problem: as the policy moves away from the SFT distribution, the RM’s training data becomes unrepresentative. Ackermann et al. (2025) propose off-policy corrected reward modeling, which uses importance weighting to make the RM consistent under the evolving policy distribution. Dai et al. (2025) propose BSPO, which regularizes the RM training objective to improve robustness. Xu et al. (2024) train distributional RMs with explicit uncertainty outputs. These methods modify the RM training pipeline, whereas SignCert-PO operates purely at policy optimization time using any pre-trained RM. 

Adversarial policy optimization. The closest work to ours is AdvPO (Zhang et al., 2024), which derives single-RM uncertainty estimates from last-layer embeddings and formulates a distributionally robust policy optimization problem. Two differences distinguish our approach. First, AdvPO requires constructing a matrix MD from the RM training dataset 

D at policy optimization time; in many practical pipelines, this data is unavailable when using third-party RMs (Malik et al., 2025; Chijiwa et al., 2025). SignCert-PO requires only the current head parameters and on-policy completions. Second, AdvPO constructs a confidence region for the reward function using that matrix and then optimizes the policy against a pessimistic reward within that region. In contrast, SignCert-PO models uncertainty as perturbations of the RM parameters and asks whether those perturbations can flip the sign of the group-relative advantage for each completion. This aligns our adversarial formulation directly with GRPO’s update geometry, as discussed in §3.3.

## E Extension to other policy gradient methods 

The sign-certification principle of §3 applies beyond Dr.GRPO to any PG-based RLHF method whose update involves a scalar coefficient depending on the RM. Since the ex-act form of the certified radius depends on the update geometry, we only discuss RE-INFORCE (Williams, 1992), PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) and vanilla GRPO (Shao et al., 2024).

E.2 Approximate extensions 

Standard PPO and vanilla GRPO do not generally admit a single response-level coefficient whose sign alone determines the update for the whole completion. Sign certification for these methods therefore requires a first-order approximation. 

E.2.1 PPO 

Standard PPO in RLHF uses a token-level clipped surrogate objective (14) with token-level advantages ˆAt (15) estimated via a learned value network (§A.1). Unlike Dr.GRPO or the response-level PG of §E.1, PPO does not generally admit a single scalar response-level coefficient. Its per-token structure means that the sign of the effective coefficient can differ across tokens within a single response. Sign certification for PPO should therefore be formulated for the token-level coefficient appearing in each surrogate term. Under the linear reward head, the token-level advantage ˆAt depends on the RM head w only through the terminal reward rw(x, y) added at t = T. A first-order approximation gives

## F Training and implementation details 

F.1 Conceptual experiment (Figure 1) 

Before presenting the full-scale LLM experiments, we design a controlled conceptual ex-periment to demonstrate advantage sign flipping causes reward hacking and to verify that SignCert-PO prevents it. 19 Preprint. Under review. 

F.1.1 Setup 

We consider a stateless M-armed bandit ( M = 8). Each action (completion) m is associated with a fixed feature vector hm ∈ Rd (d = 6), analogous to the last-layer hidden states 

h(x, y(j)) of an RM backbone in §3.2. Both the proxy and true RMs use a linear head on top of these shared features: 

rk = wThk, r∗ 

> k

= w∗Thk,matching the linear-head architecture assumed in §3.2. The feature space Rd is split into two subspaces: shared dimensions (0–2), where both heads assign weight 1.0; divergent 

dimensions (3–5), where the proxy assigns weight 0.4 but the true RM assigns −0.1. Actions 1–3 ( reliable positive ) use only shared dimensions and have positive proxy and true rewards. Actions 4–6 ( reliable negative ) also use only shared dimensions but have negative rewards. Actions 7–8 ( hacking ) use only divergent dimensions. As a result, the proxy RM assigns them the highest advantages in the group, while their true advantages are negative. The policy is a categorical stochastic policy π(am|ϕ) = softmax (ϕ)m initialized to uniform (ϕ = 0). Because M is small and there is a single state, we use the exact expected policy gradient. 

F.1.2 Policy optimization methods 

We compare three methods, all sharing the softmax policy gradient structure but differing in how advantages are weighted: • Dr.GRPO : ∇ϕi J = πi(Ai − Vπ ), where Vπ = ∑k πk Ak.• Global robust objective (Lemma 3.3): adds a penalty −ϵπ i(hi −

Eπ [h]) T(Eπ [h]/∥Eπ [h]∥) that corrects all actions through a single direction. We sweep ϵ over 20 log-spaced values in [10 −2, 10 ] and report the best. • SignCert-PO (Theorem 3.1): ∇ϕi J = πi(ρi Ai − Vρ), where ρk = 1 − ϵ/∆k and ϵ is set to the qt-th quantile of {∆k}.

F.1.3 Results 

Figure 1(a) shows the proxy advantages per action. Red bars indicate completions whose advantage sign disagrees with the true RM. The hacking actions (7–8) have the largest proxy advantages. Figure 1(c) shows the per-action certified sign-preservation radius ∆j. The completions 7 and 8 cluster at low ∆ values because they are outliers in the feature-space (i.e., large ∥hj − ¯h∥), confirming that ∆j identifies unreliable completions. Figure 1(d) tracks true (solid) and proxy (dashed) expected reward during policy optimiza-tion. Dr.GRPO converges on the hacking actions, where true reward collapses. The global robust penalty partially mitigates reward hacking, but the single penalty direction cannot distinguish per-action reliability and is thus too conservative. SignCert-PO achieves the highest true reward, by suppressing the hacking actions’ gradient contributions via their small ρk weights.

F.3.1 Datasets 

TL;DR summarization. We use the filtered version 3 of OpenAI’s Summarize from Feed-back dataset (Stiennon et al., 2020) containing 116K samples. Each sample consists of a Reddit post and a human-written reference summary. 

AlpacaFarm. We additionally evaluate on the AlpacaFarm benchmark (Dubois et al., 2023), a simulation framework for learning from human feedback built on 52K instruction-following samples. 

F.3.2 Models 

Policy and proxy RM. We use Pythia (Biderman et al., 2023) and Qwen2.5 (Qwen et al., 2025) series as policy and proxy RM base models. We vary the policy size among Pythia 1B and 2.8B, and Qwen2.5 1.5B and 3B. The same model size as the policy is chosen for the proxy RM. For TL;DR, the proxy RM is trained with 1 epoch of preference data. For AlpacaFarm, the proxy RM is trained with 3 epochs of preference data, roughly matching the number of queries for TL;DR (see §F.3.1). 

Gold RM. For the TL;DR experiments with Pythia 1B and Qwen2.5 1.5B policies, the gold reward model is Skywork-Reward-Llama-3.1-8B-v0.2 (Liu et al., 2024), one of the top-performing discriminative reward models on RewardBench (Malik et al., 2025). All preference data and evaluation metrics for these settings are computed using this gold RM. For larger policy models (Pythia 2.8B and Qwen2.5 3B), we use GPT-4.1 Nano (OpenAI et al., 2024) as the gold RM, since a model with stronger capability than the proxy RM is required.
