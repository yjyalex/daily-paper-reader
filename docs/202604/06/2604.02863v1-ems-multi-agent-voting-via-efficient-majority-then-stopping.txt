## ABSTRACT 

Majority voting is the standard for aggregating multi-agent responses into a final decision. However, traditional methods typically require all agents to complete their reasoning before aggregation begins, leading to significant computational overhead, as many responses become redundant once a majority consensus is achieved. In this work, we formulate the multi-agent voting as a reliability-aware agent scheduling problem, and propose an Efficient Majority-then-Stopping (EMS) to improve reasoning efficiency. EMS prioritizes agents based on task-aware reliability and terminates the reasoning pipeline the moment a majority is achieved from the following three critical components. Specifically, we introduce Agent Confidence Modeling (ACM) to estimate agent reliability using historical performance and semantic similarity, Adaptive Incremental Voting (AIV) to sequentially select agents with early stopping, and Individual Confidence Updating (ICU) to dynamically update the reliability of each contributing agent. Extensive evaluations across six benchmarks demonstrate that EMS consistently reduces the average number of invoked agents by 32% .

## 1 Introduction 

Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable success in complex tasks such as mathematical reasoning, code generation, and commonsense question answering [ 1, 2 , 3]. Despite these advancements, a single LLM often exhibits limited reasoning diversity and struggles with problems requiring dynamic, task-specific strategies [ 4, 5 , 6]. Recent research has sought to address these limitations by leveraging multi-agent systems (MAS) [ 7, 8, 9 ], where multiple LLM-based agents collaborate to tackle complex tasks. By aggregating responses from a diverse set of agents, MAS can exploit a variety of reasoning paths, leading to more robust and accurate outcomes than those achieved by individual models. A critical aspect of MAS is the aggregation of multiple agent responses into a final decision [10, 11, 12, 13], which is typically achieved through a decision-making operation. Among the various aggregation techniques, majority voting has become one of the most widely used methods due to its simplicity and effectiveness[ 14 , 15 ]. In the standard majority voting setting, each agent performs reasoning independently, and the final decision is made based on the most frequent response. Several extensions of this basic paradigm have been proposed to enhance performance, such as reliability-based weighting considering agents’ past accuracy, and confidence-based soft voting integrating model-reported confidence scores. However, these approaches still follow the same fundamental paradigm: reasoning first, aggregation later . In this setup, all agents must complete their reasoning before a final decision can be made. This paradigm, while effective, introduces a significant inefficiency. In many scenarios, the majority decision can be reached before all agents have completed their reasoning, making the contributions of the remaining agents redundant. This leads to unnecessary computational overhead, especially when large numbers of agents or expensive LLM inferences are involved. This inefficiency raises a key challenge: how can we reduce redundant reasoning in multi-agent LLM systems while preserving the accuracy benefits of majority voting? 

Inspired by principles from Byzantine Fault Tolerance[ 16 ], a reasonable strategy is to adopt a majority-then-stop mechanism. In a system with N agents, once a majority of [( N + 1) /2] agents reach consensus, the final decision is determined, and the remaining reasoning can be terminated. This strategy has two primary advantages: it preserves the decision accuracy of standard majority voting while potentially reducing inference costs by avoiding unnecessary reasoning. However, its efficiency critically depends on the order in which agents are queried. As shown in Figure 1, if

To address the above issue, we formulate the majority voting as a reliability-aware agent scheduling problem in multi-agent LLM systems, and propose an Efficient Majority-then-Stopping (EMS) to improve reasoning effi-ciency without sacrificing decision-making accuracy. The main idea of EMS is to estimate the task-aware reliability of each agent and prioritize those with higher expected re-liability during voting, enabling early majority consensus and reducing redundant inference. EMS consists of three key components: Agent Confidence Modeling (ACM) First, Agent Confidence Modeling (ACM) constructs a comprehensive confidence profile for each agent by con-sidering historical reliability or semantic similarity to the query task. Second, the Adaptive Incremental Voting (AIV) dynamically queries agents in descending order of estimated reliability and stops the voting process once a majority consensus is reached. Third, Individual Confidence Updating (ICU) is proposed to update the reliability of the individual agent that is contributing to voting. The major contributions can be summarized as follows: 1. We analyze the inefficiency of the widely used reasoning-first aggregation paradigm in Multi-Agent Voting and formulate it as a reliability-aware agent scheduling problem to improve inference efficiency. 2. We propose an Efficient Majority-then-Stopping (EMS), a novel decision-making framework that enables early termination of multi-agent reasoning while preserving the accuracy of majority voting. 3. ## 2 Related Work 

Multi-Agent Systems. Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) have stimulated increasing interest in multi-agent systems (MAS) [ 1, 2, 4 ], where multiple LLM-based agents collaborate to solve complex tasks. Representative frameworks such as AutoGen [ 9 ] enable structured collaboration among multiple agents through role assignment, communication, and task decomposition. Other interaction-based approaches, including multi-agent debate [ 17 , 12 ], further encourage agents to critique and refine one another’s intermediate reasoning, thereby improving the quality of final outputs. While these studies demonstrate the effectiveness of collaborative reasoning for improving task performance, the efficiency of multi-agent inference, particularly the reduction of redundant agent calls during decision making, remains relatively underexplored. 

Efficient Multi-Agent Inference. To improve efficiency, recent work has explored adaptive routing and selective invocation mechanisms [ 18 ]. Another work improves the efficiency of debate-style reasoning by reducing redundant communication rather than changing the final aggregation rule. For example, GroupDebate [ 19 ] reduces debate token cost through grouped discussion and intermediate result sharing across debate groups. Similarly, DOWN [ 20 ]activates debate only when initial confidence suggests that additional deliberation is necessary, thereby avoiding unnecessary interaction on easy instances. These approaches aim to selectively allocate computation based on intermediate confidence signals or structured communication. However, they typically focus on optimizing interaction efficiency rather than directly leveraging the majority consensus structure for early termination. 

Multi-Agent Voting and Aggregation. Aggregating outputs from multiple agents is a fundamental component of MAS [ 21 ], and majority voting remains one of the most widely adopted decision rules because of its simplicity and strong performance. Recent work demonstrates that majority voting accounts for the vast majority of performance gains typically attributed to complex multi-agent debate, suggesting that expensive inter-agent communication rounds are often unnecessary [15, 22]. Furthermore, standard voting protocols are particularly optimal for reasoning-based tasks, 2Running Title for Header significantly outperforming other decision-making structures [ 23 ]. Despite these advances, most existing approaches follow a reasoning-first-aggregation-later paradigm, which introduces significant computational waste as the final decision is often reachable before all agents complete their reasoning. Our work addresses this gap by formulating majority voting as a reliability-aware agent scheduling problem.

3.3 Adaptive Incremental Voting 

Since EMS treats the multi-agent voting process as a sequential problem, the Adaptive Incremental Voting (AIV) is proposed to conduct the voting process based on the previously obtained agent voting order ˆAi for the candidate query 

qi. Drawing on the quorum principle of Byzantine Fault Tolerance (BFT), EMS firstly sets the initial quorum size to 

τ = ⌈(N +1) /2⌉ to conduct the initial majority voting. By initiating the process with τ agents, EMS balances the need for a reliable initial consensus with the potential for significant computational savings. Let ˆAi = (ˆ ai, 1, ˆai, 2, . . . , ˆai,N ) denote the ordered list of N agents for query qi. These agents are arranged in a predefined order based on their reliability, as determined by the global confidence state Θ. The objective of AIV is to sequentially select agents from this list and aggregate their votes until a consensus is reached.

agents are invoked to cast their votes. The reason for starting with τ agents is to satisfy the majority rule with the minimum number of agents, ensuring that the remaining N − τ agents cannot form a larger opposing bloc. Let

4.1 Experimental Setup Datasets. We evaluate the performance of EMS across six benchmarks: 1) Mathematical Reasoning: AQuA [25 ], 

Math500 [26 ], and GSM8K [27 ]. 2) General Knowledge: MMLU [ 28 ], GPQA Diamond [29 ], CommonsenseQA [ 30 ]. All datasets utilize standard settings. 

Agent Configuration. Our framework utilizes a heterogeneous pool of N = 9 large language models to ensure diverse reasoning perspectives, which includes: 1) OpenAI (GPT-4.1, GPT-4o, GPT-4o-mini); 2) Google (Gemini-1.5-pro, Gemini-2.5-flash); 3) Anthropic (Claude-3-5-Sonnet); 4) DeepSeek (DeepSeek-V3); 5) Meta (Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct); and 6) Alibaba (Qwen2.5-32B-Instruct). All model inferences are conducted via the API aggregation service provided by Yunwu API platform (https://yunwu.ai/). For semantic query feature extraction, we employ the paraphrase-multilingual-MiniLM-L12-v2 encoder to map queries into a shared latent space for similarity-based retrieval. 

Baselines. We compare EMS instantiated with different confidence strategy, namely EMS-Rel and EMS-Sim , against the following baselines: 1) Individual Baselines include Single Agent with Chain-of-Thought (CoT) [ 31 ] using the top-performing model and Self-Consistency (SC) [ 10 ] via repeated sampling. 2) Voting Baselines include the Simple Majority Voting (Simple MV) using the full agent pool, and Weighting methods comprise Historical-based Weighted MV[ 32 ] and a Soft-Weighted MV(Conf-MV)[ 33 ], where agent votes are weighted by their self-reported confidence scores. 3) EMS-Rel and EMS-Sim use the historical reliability and query-adaptive semantic reliability, respectively.

As the candidate pool size N increases, we observe a consistent improvement in reasoning accuracy, accompanied by a gradual increase in the average number of agents participating in the voting process. However, the marginal benefit of enlarging the agent pool diminishes as N grows. For example, on the GPQA dataset, increasing N from 9 to 13 requires invoking an additional 2.93 agents on average, yet yields only a modest accuracy improvement of 1.12%. This observation suggests that continually increasing the number of agents provides limited returns and may not be an efficient strategy for multi-agent voting. Furthermore, we observe that as N increases, the average agent call rate of EMS remains relatively stable, indicating that the proposed early-stopping mechanism effectively controls the growth of inference cost even when the agent pool becomes larger. 

## 5 Conclusion 

Majority voting is the standard for aggregating multi-agent responses into a final decision. To address the inefficiency of conventional majority voting in multi-agent LLM systems, we propose the Efficient Majority-then-Stopping (EMS) framework, which prioritizes agents based on their task-aware reliability and terminates the reasoning process once a majority consensus is reached. EMS integrates three key components to enable efficient and reliable decision making: Agent Confidence Modeling (ACM), Adaptive Incremental Voting (AIV), and Individual Confidence Updating (ICU). Extensive experiments across multiple benchmarks demonstrate that EMS significantly reduces inference costs in multi-agent voting while maintaining competitive accuracy. Notably, the efficiency of EMS critically depends on the quality of the agent ordering. While the current framework leverages relatively simple historical and semantic signals to construct the voting sequence, designing more advanced and expressive agent scheduling strategies remains an important direction for future work.
