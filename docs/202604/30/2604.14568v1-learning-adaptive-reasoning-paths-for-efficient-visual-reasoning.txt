## Abstract 

Visual reasoning models (VRMs) have recently shown strong cross-modal reasoning capabilities by integrating visual perception with language rea-soning. However, they often suffer from overthinking, producing unnec-essarily long reasoning chains for any tasks. We attribute this issue to 

Reasoning Path Redundancy in visual reasoning: many visual questions do not require the full reasoning process. To address this, we propose 

AVR , an adaptive visual reasoning framework that decomposes visual rea-soning into three cognitive functions: visual perception, logical reasoning, and answer application. It further enables models to dynamically choose among three response formats: Full Format, Perception-Only Format, and Direct Answer. AVR is trained with FS-GRPO, an adaptation of Group Relative Policy Optimization that encourages the model to select the most efficient reasoning format while preserving correctness. Experiments on multiple vision-language benchmarks show that AVR reduces token usage by 50–90% while maintaining overall accuracy, especially in perception-intensive tasks. These results demonstrate that adaptive visual reasoning can effectively mitigate overthinking in VRMs. Code and data are available at: https://github.com/RunRiotComeOn/AVR .

## 1 Introduction 

Visual reasoning models (VRMs; Jian et al. (2025)) have recently demonstrated strong capabilities in multimodal reasoning by integrating visual perception with language rea-soning. These models exhibit emerging reasoning abilities in visually grounded settings, handling compositional (Johnson et al., 2017), multi-hop (Hudson & Manning, 2019), and logic-intensive tasks (Lu et al., 2023). Existing techniques focus on improving the reasoning ability of VRMs. They borrow the idea from the Reasoning Language Models (RLMs; Jaech et al. (2024)), adopting Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Reward (RLVR; Lambert et al. (2024); Guo et al. (2025)) to enhance reasoning on language (Jaech et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2025). One line of work decomposes the response of VRMs by separating perception from reasoning (Xia et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025a; Li et al., 2025b) and ask the model to ground its reasoning based on its perceptual outputs. Through these techniques, VRMs are able to conduct long, complex reasoning with visual inputs. Despite these performance gains, recent studies show that LRMs frequently suffer from 

overthinking (Nayab et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2025; Sui et al., 2025; Shojaee et al., 2025). While extended reasoning can improve performance on challenging problems, models often produce unnecessarily long Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning even for simpler ones, leading to excessive token consumption and sometimes even reducing accuracy due to errors introduced in intermediate reasoning steps (Yang et al., 2025). Existing attempts to address this issue (Xie et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025a) largely rely on heuristic strategies derived from RLMs, overlooking the distinct cognitive structure of visual reasoning, particularly the tight relationship between perception and reasoning. In this work, we argue that a key cause of overthinking in VRMs lies in the cognitive structure of visual reasoning itself. Unlike purely textual reasoning, solving a visual question 1

On perception-intensive tasks like OCRBench, the model predominantly uses direct-answer and perception-only formats, reducing token usage by over 80% with improved accuracy. On reasoning-intensive benchmarks such as MathVista, it allocates more full-format responses when necessary, reducing token usage while maintaining accuracy. ## 2 Related Work 

Vision-Language Reasoning. Recent VRMs have exhibited strong multimodal reasoning capabilities by integrating visual perception with language reasoning. Notably, Multimodal CoT Reasoning (Zhang et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025c) and Reasoning Decomposition (Jia et al., 2024; Xia et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025a; Li et al., 2025b; Huang et al., 2025) have significantly boosted performance on complex tasks. However, they implicitly assume that generating exhaustive reasoning chains is always beneficial (Ma et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025d). In practice, many visual queries require only minimal perception or direct answers. Therefore, the use of redundant reasoning chains leads to “overthinking” (Ma et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025d), which induces computational inefficiency and may even degrade predictive accuracy (Zhu et al., 2024a; 2025b; Yang et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025a). 

Reinforcement Learning for Reasoning. RL has become a primary paradigm for enhancing the reasoning ability of language models (Zhou et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025b). Recently, Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) has emerged as an effective frame-work for models like OpenAI-o1 (Jaech et al., 2024) and DeepSeek-R1 (Guo et al., 2025), where rewards are computed from verifiable outputs. Specifically, GRPO (Shao et al., 2024b) offers an efficient RLVR variant. While these methods primarily focus on maximizing reasoning accuracy (Yu et al., 2025; Xia et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025a), they seldom address reasoning efficiency, often resulting in output redundancy (Zhou et al., 2025). 

Overthinking in Large Reasoning Models. Recent studies observe that RLMs frequently encounter severe overthinking when employing CoT reasoning (Nayab et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2025; Sui et al., 2025; Shojaee et al., 2025). To mitigate this, methods like post-training (Sui et al., 2025; Wu et al., 2025; Tarunokusumo & Cunha, 2025) and reasoning trajectory distillation (Sui et al., 2025; Yu et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2025) aim to produce concise yet effective reasoning steps. However, these efforts primarily target text-only models. While preliminary work has begun investigating similar phenomenon in VRMs (Li et al., 2025a; Xie et al., 2025), methods for mitigating overthinking in VRMs (Xie et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025a) are largely heuristic, failing to account for the unique structure of multimodal systems. Task-aware approaches that empower VRMs to dynamically adjust their reasoning depth thus remain significantly underexplored. 

## 3 Preliminary 

Based on previous work (Zhang et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2024a; Aissi et al., 2025), the outputs of VRMs follow three structured cognitive functions: • Visual Perception : Visual perception extracts task-relevant information from the input image, including objects, attributes, spatial relations, and textual content. • Logical Reasoning : Logical reasoning performs intermediate inference over perception, including multi-step deduction, comparison, counting, or commonsense reasoning. • Answer Application : Answer application maps the inferred result to the final answer format required by the task. However, not all questions require all functions. We refer to this overthinking phenomenon 

Reasoning Path Redundancy , as defined in Definition 1. 

Definition 1 (Reasoning Path Redundancy) . A reasoning path is redundant if a shorter path that contains a subset of its functional components can produce the same correct answer. By analyzing 1,000 randomly sampled instances, we observe that VRMs often fail to adap-tively select cognitive functions based on task requirements, instead overusing the full set of cognitive functions. In Qwen3-VL-4B-Thinking, 36.7% of questions can be correctly answered without invoking the reasoning function, and 32.1% can be solved through direct answering without explicit perception or reasoning (see Appendix A.1 for details). ## 4 Method 

We propose AVR, an RL training framework that enables VRMs to adaptively select efficient formats during visual reasoning process. Our key idea is to explicitly decompose the reasoning pipeline into three cognitive functions and construct corresponding formats with different levels of reasoning effort. Based on these formats, we train the model to dynamically select the most appropriate reasoning path for each task. The framework consists of two stages. Stage 1: SFT for format understanding . We first fine-tune the model on 11k diverse questions with three-format annotations. This stage enables the model to understand different reasoning formats and produce responses in multiple formats. Stage 2: RL for efficient format switching . We then employ FS-GRPO algorithm to train the model to dynamically switch between formats, allowing it to select the most efficient reasoning format based on task requirements while maintaining accuracy. 

4.1 Decomposition of Reasoning Paths 

We decompose the VRM reasoning process into three cognitive functions as previously stated in Section 3: Perception , Reasoning , and Answer . Unlike the monolithic structure of traditional CoT, this decoupled architecture encourages explicit visual grounding and structured logical deduction. Such a design has been shown to improve accuracy on complex visual tasks (Jia et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025b) and provides an explicit mechanism for mitigating overthinking in VRMs (see Appendix A.1 for details). We implement this structure using functional special tokens ( <perception> , <reasoning> ,and <answer> ), which allow us to define three distinct formats ( k indexes the format; see Appendix A.3 for details): • Full Format ( k = 1): A comprehensive sequence encompassing all components (<perception>...<reasoning>...<answer> ). • Perception-Only Format ( k = 2): A streamlined path combining perception with answer (<perception>...<answer> ), bypassing exhaustive reasoning. • Direct Answer ( k = 3): A minimalist output ( <answer> ) that targets immediate response efficiency. 4Preprint. 4.2 Stage 1: SFT for Format Understanding 

In this stage, we leverage SFT as a warm start to introduce the model to the formats it can utilize for solving VQA, using processed data in the formats described in Section 4.1. After SFT, the model is able to generate all three formats, providing a strategic initialization for subsequent RL training. 

4.3 Stage 2: RL for Efficient Format Switching 

While SFT enables the model to follow defined formats, it often fails to dynamically switch between them based on task complexity. To address this, we propose FS-GRPO algorithm that encourages the model to select the most efficient format for a given problem. For a given prompt q, the model generates a group of G outputs {o1, o2, . . . , oG }. ## 5 Experiments 

In this section, we evaluate AVR on a wide range of benchmarks. Furthermore, we analyze its performance, efficiency, and format selection behavior, compared with other methods. 

5.1 Setup Model To evaluate the generalizability of AVR framework across varying model scales, we adopt Qwen3-VL-2B/4B/8B-Instruct (Bai et al., 2025) as base models. Experiments on other model families are provided in Appendix A.8. 

Training Dataset We curate a comprehensive RL training dataset of 44k samples by aggregating 10 diverse datasets: OK-VQA (Marino et al., 2019), CLEVR (Johnson et al., 2017), VCR (Zellers et al., 2019), GQA (Hudson & Manning, 2019), ChartQA (Masry et al., 2022), OCR-VQA (Mishra et al., 2019), MathVerse (Zhang et al., 2024), Geometry3K (Lu et al., 2021), ScienceQA (Lu et al., 2022), and TQA (Kembhavi et al., 2017) (see Appendix A.2). For the SFT phase, we subsample 11k high-quality cases and generate both full-context and perception-only responses using Gemini-2.5-flash (Comanici et al., 2025). A detailed description of our data processing pipeline is provided in Appendix A.4. 

Baselines Beyond base models, we compare AVR with the official Qwen3-VL-2B/4B/8B-Thinking variants (Bai et al., 2025), which employ long CoT reasoning. We further include Think-or-Not (TON) (Wang et al., 2025a) and ARM2 (Xie et al., 2025) as representative baselines. TON adopts two response strategies (direct answering and CoT reasoning), allowing the model to bypass explicit reasoning for simple queries while invoking deeper reasoning when necessary. ARM2 provides multiple response formats, including long CoT and short CoT, and employs a RL framework with length-aware optimization to balance reasoning performance and efficiency across different reasoning styles. All training details are provided in Appendix A.5. 

5.2 Evaluation Benchmarks Our selected benchmarks cover three main domains based on task types: perception-intensive, reasoning-intensive, and general. For perception-intensive tasks, we utilize OCRBench (Liu et al., 2024b) and TextVQA (Singh et al., 2019), which primarily require fast perception with minimal reasoning. For reasoning-intensive tasks, we include MathVista (Lu et al., 2023) and MathVision (Wang et al., 2024) to assess models’ ability to solve complex mathematical problems that require intensive reasoning. Finally, for general 

tasks, we select MMMU (Yue et al., 2024), MMMU-Pro (Yue et al., 2025), and MMBench (Liu et al., 2024a), which contain a diverse set of tasks spanning a wide range of difficulties.

Table 1 demonstrates a comprehensive result of our models and some baselines across diverse vision-language benchmarks. 

Thinking v.s. Instruct. Comparing the results of thinking and instruct models in Table 1, we observe that thinking models degrade by approximately 5% on perception-intensive benchmarks such as OCRBench and TextVQA, while consuming nearly 10 × more tokens. In these cases, long CoT reasoning tends to induce serious overthinking and may even harm performance. In contrast, for general and reasoning-intensive tasks, thinking models consume moderately more tokens (2–4 ×) than instruct models while achieving performance gains of around 5%. This suggests that extended reasoning is beneficial for more challenging problems that require deeper visual reasoning, such as MathVista and MathVision, but also reveals the phenomenon of overthinking. 

SFT Teaches Formats, FS-GRPO Teaches Selection. The SFT model fluctuates in accuracy and increases token usage because it does not learn to select formats. As shown in Table 1, although SFT learns to generate responses in the three predefined formats, the distribution of formats remains nearly identical across different task types. In contrast, after applying FS-GRPO, the model learns to selectively use formats for different task categories (Table 1).

For reasoning-intensive tasks, the model predominantly adopts the full format as its primary response strategy ( ∼70%), enabling sufficient reasoning to derive correct answers while avoiding performance degradation caused by insufficient thinking. ## 6 Analysis 

We conduct detailed analyses to better understand the design and behavior of AVR. Specifi-cally, we examine the effects of format composition, diversity reward, and key hyperparam-eters on both performance and efficiency. 

6.1 Ablation on Formats 

To validate the effectiveness of our format design, we compare AVR with three alternatives: (i) full format only, (ii) perception-only + full, and (iii) direct-answer + full (Figure 4). Detailed settings are provided in Appendix A.10.1. Using only the full format results in the highest token consumption, suggesting that excessive reasoning leads to overthinking. Adding the perception-only format improves efficiency but still requires almost 2 times the token consumption of AVR and causes a ∼2% accuracy drop across benchmarks, indicating the importance of the direct-answer format for simpler queries. In contrast, combining direct answer and full format reduces tokens but lowers accuracy (1–5%), particularly on OCRBench, implying that the lack of perception-only format harms visual grounding. 6.2 Ablation on Diversity Reward 

To assess the effect of the diversity reward rdiv , we remove it from the FS-GRPO objective while keeping all other settings unchanged (Appendix A.10.2). As shown in Figure 5, removing rdiv leads to format collapse: the model converges to a single dominant format (direct answer), while the usage of full and perception-only formats rapidly vanishes. In contrast, with rdiv , the model maintains all three formats and dynamically balances their usage. These results indicate that rdiv mitigates early format dominance, enabling the model to preserve diverse reasoning strategies and learn adaptive format selection.

A.1.2 Overthinking Score Implementation 

The Overthinking Score is intended as a diagnostic metric to quantify the prevalence of reasoning redundancy in existing VRMs. It is not used as a training signal or evaluation criterion for our method; its sole purpose is to motivate the need for adaptive reasoning formats. We detail the construction procedure below. Given a visual question, we first prompt the evaluated VRM Qwen3-VL-4B-Thinking to generate a response using its default reasoning mode. We then filter for correctness by comparing the model’s answer against the ground-truth label via exact match. Only instances with correct original responses are retained, as redundancy is meaningful only when the original reasoning already succeeds. For each retained response, we apply a controlled compression procedure using GPT-4o-mini. The model is instructed to rewrite the response under a minimal sufficiency constraint: removing unnecessary intermediate reasoning steps, retaining only the information essential for deriving the final answer, and producing the shortest possible response that still yields the correct answer (see the full prompt in Box A.1.2). After compression, we verify whether the compressed response preserves correctness by prompting GPT-4o-mini to compare the compressed answer with the ground-truth label. Instances where the compressed response is judged incorrect are discarded from the Overthinking Score computation. We compute Toriginal and Tcompressed using the same tokenizer as the evaluated model to ensure consistency. We acknowledge that exact minimality cannot be guaranteed by this procedure. However, the compressed response represents a practical upper bound on the minimal sufficient length, meaning our Overthinking Scores are likely conservative estimates of the true redundancy. To validate the reliability of this approximation, we conduct human spot-checking on a random subset of 100 compressed instances. Based on this observation, we define three response formats with increasing computational depth: Direct Answer, Perception-Only, and Full Format. These formats correspond re-spectively to using none, a subset, or the complete reasoning pipeline. This design forms a minimal basis for representing different reasoning pathways while keeping the action space compact enough for stable reinforcement learning. In contrast, using fewer formats (e.g., full reasoning only) fails to explicitly capture visual information, which may lead to reasoning based on incomplete evidence. Introducing more formats would unnecessarily enlarge the decision space without providing additional functional benefit. Therefore, the three-format design provides a principled and efficient decomposition of visual reasoning behaviors. 

A.4 SFT Dataset Construction 

To construct the multi-format SFT dataset, we processed 44k image-text pairs through a three-step pipeline: response generation, functional format verification, and quality filtering. 

Step 1: Response Generation. We prompt GPT-4o (Hurst et al., 2024) to generate responses following our decoupled reasoning architecture consisting of <perception> , <reasoning> ,and <answer> (see Box A.4). Responses whose final answers do not match the ground-truth label (via exact match) are discarded. 

Step 2: Functional Format Verification. We emphasize that format assignment is not based on GPT-4o’s subjective judgment about whether reasoning is “needed.” Instead, we apply functional tests that rely solely on answer correctness: • Perception-Only Test. We provide a separate model with only the generated 

<perception> text and the original question, withholding the image entirely . If the model consistently derives the correct answer from this textual description alone, the reasoning stage is functionally redundant for this instance, and the sample is assigned the perception-only format (see Box A.4). • Direct Answer Test. We prompt the model with the image and question but instruct it to answer directly without intermediate reasoning. If the correct answer is produced, the sample is assigned the direct answer format (see Box A.4). • Full Format. Samples that fail both tests above—i.e., require explicit reasoning beyond perception to reach the correct answer—are retained in the full decoupled format containing <perception> , <reasoning> , and <answer> .This verification logic is self-contained: it depends only on whether the correct answer can be recovered under a restricted input condition, not on any model’s opinion about task difficulty. 

Step 3: Quality Filtering. After format assignment, we perform additional filtering includ-ing format distillation and human inspection. The final SFT dataset contains 11k high-quality samples distributed across the three formats. 19 Preprint. Under review. 

Note on SFT Label Sensitivity. We note that the SFT stage serves as a warm start to teach the model format competency, the ability to generate well-formed responses in all three formats, instead of optimal format policy. The selection of which format to use for a given task is learned during the subsequent RL stage, where the reward signal is based on verifiable answer correctness, not on SFT labels. Therefore, even if some SFT format labels are noisy, the RL stage can correct suboptimal format assignments through its own optimization. A.5 Implementation Details 

A.5.1 Stage 1: SFT 

SFT is implemented using the LLaMA-Factory (Zheng et al., 2024) framework. We freeze the vision encoder and the multimodal projector, and only update the language model parameters (Liu et al., 2023) with full fine-tuning. This design preserves the pretrained visual representations while allowing the language model to adapt to the response format supervision. Training is performed with a per-device batch size of 4 and gradient accumulation of 2 (i.e., effective batch size of 8), using the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) with a learning rate of 5 × 10 −5 and cosine learning rate scheduling with a warmup ratio of 0.1 (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2016). We train the model for one epoch with DeepSpeed ZeRO-3 optimization (Rajbhandari et al., 2020) to enhance efficiency. The maximum image resolution is limited to 262,144 pixels to control memory usage. 

A.5.2 Stage 2: RL 

RL training is implemented using the VeRL (Sheng et al., 2025) framework under the FS-GRPO objective described in Section 4.3. The SFT model initializes the actor policy, while a frozen reference model is used for KL regularization. 

Training Configurations For each prompt, the model samples G = 8 responses to form a GRPO group. Responses are generated using nucleus sampling with temperature 0.8 and top-p = 0.95 (Holtzman et al., 2019) with a maximum length of 2048 tokens. Training uses a batch size of 128 prompts and a validation batch size of 32, with 10% of the data reserved for validation. Policy optimization uses a learning rate of 5 × 10 −7 with PPO clipping ratio 0.2 (Schulman et al., 2017). KL regularization with coefficient 0.02 (Ouyang et al., 2022) is applied to stabilize policy updates. 

Reward Design The format-aware reward function used in RL training is described in Section 4.3. In our implementation, the format bonuses encourage concise reasoning by assigning larger rewards to more efficient formats (direct answer > perception-only > full reasoning). Specifically, we set f1 = 0, f2 = 0.3, and f3 = 0.5. To discourage excessively long responses, the length scaling factor uses a token tolerance threshold of L = 300, the average length of SFT data responses. In addition, the diversity reward follows the cosine decay schedule described in Section 4.3. All training is conducted using eight NVIDIA A6000 GPUs.

A.7 Evaluation Details 

During inference, we set the sampling temperature to 0.8, top-p to 1.0, and the maximum response length to 8096 tokens to avoid truncation for long reasoning outputs. For answer evaluation, we adopt different matching strategies depending on the question format. For multiple-choice questions, we apply structured answer extraction to parse the final predicted option and compare it with the ground-truth label. For open-ended questions, where answers may vary in expression, we employ Gemini-2.5-Flash as an automatic judge to determine correctness. The judge model evaluates semantic equivalence between the predicted answer and the reference answer following a standardized evaluation prompt set in VLMEvalKit. This evaluation protocol ensures reliable and consistent comparison across different bench-marks and answer formats.
