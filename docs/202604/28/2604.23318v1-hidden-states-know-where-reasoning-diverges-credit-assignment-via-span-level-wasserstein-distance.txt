Abstract 

Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) performs coarse-grained credit as-signment in reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR) by assigning the same advantage to all tokens in a rollout. Process reward models can provide finer-grained supervision, but they require step-level annotation or additional re-ward modeling. We show that hidden-state distributions contain a useful signal for local reasoning quality that can be extracted using only outcome-level correctness labels available in RLVR. Specifically, within each GRPO group, the Wasserstein distance between span-level hidden state distributions of correct and incorrect rollouts increases around regions where their local reasoning quality diverges. This association holds both across examples and within individual trajectories, suggest-ing that hidden-state distributional divergence can serve as a self-supervision signal for fine-grained credit assignment. We formalize this observation with a separa-tion theorem showing that, under mild structural assumptions, post-divergence spans have larger Wasserstein distances than pre-divergence spans whenever the population-level distributional gap exceeds finite-sample noise. Motivated by this result, we propose Span-level Hidden state Enabled Advantage Reweighting (SHEAR), which modifies GRPO by using span-level Wasserstein distances to scale token-level advantages, amplifying updates on tokens whose hidden states are more separated from the opposing group. The method requires no additional model and only minimal changes to the training pipeline. Experiments on five mathematical reasoning benchmarks and five code generation benchmarks show improvements over standard GRPO and strong performance relative to supervised process reward models, while requiring no additional annotation or reward model training. 

1 Introduction 

Reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR) is a widely used paradigm for improving reasoning in large language models [ 26 , 38 ], with Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) [ 24 ]being one of its most commonly adopted algorithms. In GRPO, the model samples multiple reasoning trajectories for each problem, receives outcome-level correctness feedback, and updates its policy based on the relative quality of these trajectories. A central challenge in this setting is credit assignment, that is, identifying which parts of a reasoning chain are responsible for success or

failure. GRPO assigns the same advantage to every token in a rollout, leaving this finer-grained problem unresolved. This limitation may become more severe on the complicated tasks, which require more thinking and execution steps [ 16 ]. A single erroneous step can derail an otherwise sound trajectory, yet all tokens receive the same gradient signal regardless of their contribution to the final outcome. This is especially problematic in long-form mathematical reasoning[ 1, 20 , 21 ] and code generation [10, 12, 17], where chains routinely span hundreds of tokens. The dominant approach to fine-grained credit assignment is the process reward model (PRM) [ 4 , 5, 16 ], which assigns rewards to individual reasoning steps. PRMs have shown clear advantages over outcome-level supervision, but they rely on costly step-level supervision. Training a PRM typically requires either human-annotated intermediate labels or an auxiliary reward model trained on such annotations, making the resulting credit signal dependent on the quality of the supervision. This issue is particularly acute on complicated tasks, where the correctness of intermediate steps is often ambiguous even to experts [ 5]. These considerations motivate a natural question. Can we obtain process-level credit signals without step-level annotation or an additional reward model? We approach this question from a representational perspective. Instead of training an external model to score individual reasoning steps, we examine whether the language model’s own hidden states contain a signal correlated with local reasoning quality. Hidden states summarize the evolving reasoning context, so reasoning errors may induce systematic distributional differences between correct and incorrect trajectories. If such differences are detectable at the span level, they could provide a self-supervised signal for process-level credit assignment using only the outcome-level correctness labels available in RLVR. We investigate this possibility by measuring the Wasserstein distance [ 2, 22 ] between span-level hidden state distributions of correct and incorrect rollouts within each GRPO group. Our empirical analysis suggests that such a signal indeed exists. By comparing span-level hidden state distributions between correct and incorrect rollouts within the same GRPO group, we find that the Wasserstein distance provides an effective proxy for local reasoning quality. It remains low where correct and incorrect trajectories are distributionally similar and increases where they diverge, with the two trends exhibiting aligned transition zones (Section 2). Building on this finding, we propose a simple modification to GRPO in which span-level Wasserstein distances are used as token-level weights in the advantage function, amplifying the existing advantage direction at positions where correct and incorrect trajectories are most distinct. The method requires no additional model, no step-level labels, and only minimal changes to the existing training pipeline (Section 3). We further formalize the underlying phenomenon with a separation theorem showing that, under mild structural assumptions, post-divergence spans have larger Wasserstein distances than pre-divergence spans whenever the population-level distributional difference exceeds finite-sample noise (Section 4). Our contributions are three fold: 1. Empirical finding. We show that span-level Wasserstein distance between hidden state distri-butions of correct and incorrect rollouts serves as a self-supervised signal that tracks reasoning quality without requiring any step-level annotation. This finding suggests that the model’s own representations carry process-level information that can be exploited without training a separate reward model (Section 2). 2. Theoretical foundation. We prove a separation theorem establishing that post-error spans exhibit larger Wasserstein distances than pre-error spans whenever the population-level distributional difference exceeds finite-sample noise, which is a condition we verify empirically. We further extend this result to the group-level minimum distance used in practice and provide a principled justification for using Wasserstein distance as a credit assignment signal (Section 4). 3. 2 Distributional Divergence in Hidden States 

Before presenting our method, we establish an empirical observation that motivates and grounds the entire approach— in a controlled diagnostic setting, hidden-state distributional structure is strongly associated with local reasoning quality, and Wasserstein distance provides a useful way to measure this structure. 

Setup. We use MATH500 [ 16 ] as the evaluation benchmark with Qwen2.5-Math-7B [ 23 ] as the base model. For each problem, we sample G=8 rollouts and retain only problems where the model produces a mix of correct and incorrect answers. This filtering is required because our diagnostic compares correct and incorrect trajectories within the same rollout group and it excludes uniformly easy or uniformly hard problems where no opposing group is available. The retained subset comprises 

83 .8% of MATH500 problems, covering a range of difficulty levels (Please refer to Appendix I for details). For each retained rollout, we extract prefixes at varying truncation points using a sliding window (starting length w=100 tokens, stride s=25 tokens), excluding spans that contain the final answer to avoid trivial leakage from answer-revealing tokens. At each truncation point, we estimate a 

continuation success rate by letting the model complete the reasoning 16 times from that prefix and recording the fraction of correct completions. We use this quantity as a proxy for local reasoning quality where prefixes that preserve a valid reasoning trajectory should admit a higher probability of correct completion. Independently, at each prefix position we collect the hidden states of the most recent w tokens and compute the Wasserstein distance between this local hidden state distribution and the closest matching distribution from the opposing group (correct vs. incorrect). We adopt Wasserstein distance as our primary metric due to its ability to capture distributional differences beyond mean shift. The comparison with alternative metrics is provided in Section 5.4. Full details of the distance computation are described in Section 3.3. 

Aggregate trend. Figure 1a plots both quantities as a function of prefix position, aggregated across all retained problems. As prefixes extend deeper into the reasoning chain, continuation success tends to decline while Wasserstein distance tends to increase, and the transition zones of the two curves are closely aligned. The region where continuation success begins to decline coincides with the region where Wasserstein distance begins to rise. 

Local association. To examine whether the association holds at the local level, we analyze stride-wise changes in both quantities within each trajectory. For each pair of consecutive prefix positions within the same rollout, we compute the change in continuation success ( ∆Accuracy ) and the concurrent change in Wasserstein distance ( ∆W ). Since each continuation success estimate is based 3on 16 completions, the minimum detectable change is 1/16 = 0 .0625 ; we use this as the threshold for identifying meaningful local shifts, retaining only positions where |∆Accuracy | ≥ 0.0625 . Figure 1b plots ∆W against ∆Accuracy for these local shift events. We can observe that when continuation success locally decreases (negative ∆Accuracy ), Wasserstein distance tends to locally increase (positive ∆W ), and vice versa. This within-trajectory association suggests that the distributional divergence signal contains a genuine local component, such that local deterioration in reasoning is accompanied by local divergence of hidden states from the opposing group. 

Implications. These two analyses provide complementary evidence that hidden state distributions carry a signal associated with reasoning quality. The aggregate trend (Figure 1a) shows that Wasser-stein distance and continuation success move in opposite directions along the reasoning chain. The local analysis (Figure 1b) further shows that this association is not solely an aggregate phenomenon but reflects within-trajectory dynamics. At positions where continuation success locally shifts, Wasserstein distance shifts concurrently in the opposite direction. If this distributional divergence signal can be reliably detected, it could serve as a self-supervised proxy for process-level credit without any step-level annotation. This observation raises two questions that the remainder of the paper addresses. First, can we exploit this signal to improve credit assignment during training? We describe our method in Section 3. Second, under what conditions is this distributional separation theoretically guaranteed? We formalize a separation theorem in Section 4 and validate the full approach empirically in Section 5.

4 Theoretical Analysis 

The observations in Section 2 suggest that span-level Wasserstein distances align empirically with local reasoning quality. In this section, we ask under what conditions such a separation can be expected theoretically. Our goal is not to model the full complexity of real reasoning trajectories, but to isolate a minimal stylized setting in which post-divergence spans become distinguishable from pre-divergence spans once the population-level distributional signal exceeds finite-sample noise. Under this view, the theory provides conditional support for the weighting mechanism used in Section 3.3. In particular, the global normalization by ¯n preserves the ordering of span-level discrepancies within a rollout group, so the separation established below is consistent with the intended weighting pattern. 6For readability, the theorems are stated for the exact Wasserstein distance W1, whereas the algorithm uses the entropically regularized approximation Wϵ. As discussed in Appendix B, the approximation satisfies W1 ≤ Wϵ ≤ W1 + ϵ log n, for discrete measures on n points. In our experiments, this approximation gap is small relative to the empirical separation margins observed between pre- and post-divergence spans, provided the separation gap exceeds ϵ log n.

5.1 Analysis of the Wasserstein Signal Setup. We adopt the same prefix-completion protocol as in Section 2 (MATH500, G = 8 rollouts from Qwen2.5-Math-7B, sliding window with w = 100 , s = 25 , final-answer spans excluded), with one key difference: whereas Section 2 analyzed the correlation between Wasserstein distance and empirical accuracy as a motivating observation, here we additionally leverage the ground-truth correctness labels of the original rollouts to directly evaluate whether Wasserstein distance can discriminate between correct and incorrect reasoning—the property that the separation theorem predicts. This yields 23 , 704 spans from originally correct rollouts and 21 , 352 from originally incorrect ones. Figure 3 (top left) plots each span as a dot, showing its Wasserstein distance to the opposing group versus its empirical continuation accuracy ( n/ 16 ). Green and red dots denote spans from originally correct and incorrect rollouts, respectively. Within every Wasserstein distance bin, spans from correct rollouts achieve consistently higher empirical accuracy than those from incorrect rollouts, confirming that ground-truth correctness information persists through truncation and resampling. More importantly, higher Wasserstein distances are systematically associated with lower accuracy in both groups, extending the anti-correlation of Section 2 to a setting conditioned on the original outcome. The critical question for our theory is whether this distributional signal is strong enough to reli-ably separate the two classes. Figure 3 (right) answers affirmatively: the AUC between correct and incorrect spans increases monotonically with the Wasserstein distance magnitude, rising from approximately 0.65 at low Wasserstein distances to above 0.97 at high Wasserstein distances. The per-bin density plots (Figure 3, bottom row) visualize this transition—at low Wasserstein distances the accuracy distributions of the two groups overlap almost entirely, while at high Wasserstein distances they become well-separated. This monotonic growth in separability directly confirms that the population-level distributional difference D(S) exceeds the finite-sample noise η by a widening margin as the Wasserstein distance increases, providing concrete empirical support for the condition 

D(S) > 2η(n, d ) required by Theorem 1. These results show that the theoretical separation is not merely a limiting guarantee but manifests at finite sample sizes with practical discriminative power. 5.2 Experimental Setup Backbones. We evaluate on four backbones from two model families: Qwen2.5-Math-7B, Qwen2.5-Coder-7B, and Qwen2.5-14B-Base [ 23 ], and Llama3.1-8B-Instruct [ 9]. For mathematical reasoning, we train Qwen2.5-Math-7B, Qwen2.5-14B-Base, and Llama3.1-8B-Instruct; for code generation, we train Qwen2.5-Coder-7B, Qwen2.5-14B-Base, and Llama3.1-8B-Instruct. The two task settings are trained independently. 

Training datasets and settings. For mathematical reasoning, we perform RL training on the MATH dataset [ 11 ] following the practice of [ 18 ]. MATH contains 8.5K problems spanning algebra, geometry, counting, probability, number theory, and related areas. For code generation, we perform RL training on Eurus-RL-Code [ 5 ] following [ 29 ]. Eurus-RL-Code contains 25K samples. Detailed training settings are provided in Appendix J. 

Evaluation. For mathematical reasoning, we evaluate on five competition-level benchmarks: AIME24 [ 1 ], AIME25 [ 21 ], AMC23 [ 20 ], MATH500 [ 16 ], and OlympiadBench [ 10 ]. These bench-marks span a broad range of difficulty, from high-school competition problems (AMC23, MATH500) to olympiad-level problems (AIME24/25, OlympiadBench), and all require multi-step reasoning with a verifiable final answer. We report avg@32 on AIME24 and AIME25, avg@8 on AMC23, and avg@1 

on MATH500 and OlympiadBench. Here avg@k [ 25 , 28 ] denotes the average correctness over k

independently sampled completions per problem under temperature sampling ( T = 0 .1, top-p = 1 .0). For code generation, we evaluate on five benchmarks: HumanEval, HumanEval+, MBPP, MBPP+ [ 17 ], and LiveCodeBench (v6, February 2025 to May 2025) [ 12 ]. HumanEval+ and MBPP+ extend the original test suites with substantially more unit tests, thereby providing a stricter functional correctness signal and reducing false positives caused by under-specified tests. LiveCodeBench is included to probe generalization on contamination-free problems released after the base model’s 10 training cutoff. We report avg@4 on all code benchmarks, where avg@4 is defined analogously as the average correctness over four independently sampled completions per problem under temperature sampling. 

Checkpoint Selection. Aligning with existing studies [ 34 , 37 ], we use the following evaluation protocols. We assess each run every 5 training steps and apply the same benchmark-based checkpoint selection protocol to all compared methods. For mathematical reasoning, we run each experiment with three random seeds. For each seed, we record the highest benchmark-averaged score across the five math benchmarks over all evaluated checkpoints, and report the mean and standard deviation of this best-observed score across seeds. For code generation, we analogously report the highest benchmark-averaged score over the five code benchmarks. This protocol measures best-observed performance under a uniform model-selection rule shared by all methods. 

Baselines. We compare our proposed SHEAR against three categories of baselines. • Base RLVR algorithm. GRPO [ 24 ], the canonical multi-sample policy-gradient method and the underlying backbone of many reasoning RL pipelines. • Intrinsic-signal-guided credit assignment. Entropy Advantage Reshaping [ 3], a non-PRM approach that reshapes token-level advantages using entropy signals derived from the policy model itself. • PRM-based credit assignment. PURE [4], which leverages process reward models to improve reasoning and mitigate reward hacking, and PRM-based Advantage Reshaping , which enhances GRPO with backward-filled PRM signals for finer-grained token-level credit assignment. PRM-based baselines are evaluated only on mathematical reasoning tasks, for which we use Qwen2.5-Math-PRM-7B [36] as the process reward model. 

5.3 Main Results Overall performance. Table 1 and Table 2 report results on five mathematical reasoning bench-marks and five code generation benchmarks across three backbones. On mathematical reasoning, SHEAR achieves the strongest average performance among the compared methods on all three backbones, consistently improving over standard GRPO. The gains are especially visible on AIME24 and AMC23, two benchmarks that require sustained multi-step reasoning. On code generation, SHEAR also improves over GRPO on all three backbones, although the margins are smaller than those observed on mathematical reasoning. 

Comparison with PRM-based methods. A central question is whether a self-supervised hidden-state signal can compete with explicit step-level supervision from a trained process reward model. Empirically, the answer is yes on the mathematical reasoning benchmarks considered here. As shown in Table 1, SHEAR outperforms both PRM(Reshape adv.) and PRM(PURE) on all three backbones in terms of average score. The contrast is particularly clear on Qwen2.5-14B-Base, where both PRM variants fall below vanilla GRPO by a substantial margin. We attribute this to a distribution mismatch between the externally PRM and the evolving policy: as the policy drifts during training, the PRM’s step-level scores become increasingly miscalibrated, injecting noise that can outweigh the benefit of denser supervision. This effect is amplified on the 14B backbone which is reflected in the larger standard deviations of PRM variants. By deriving the credit signal directly from the policy model’s own hidden-state representations, SHEAR avoids dependence on a separately trained reward model. 

Comparison with Entropy adv. Entropy adv. is the most relevant baseline conceptually, as it also performs token-level advantage reweighting without external supervision. SHEAR outperforms it on every backbone for mathematical reasoning. This supports our hypothesis that distributional diver-gence in hidden states carries strictly richer process-level information than per-token entropy, which conflates uncertainty with reasoning quality and treats high-entropy exploratory tokens identically to high-entropy erroneous ones.

5.4.1 Distance Measure Comparison Setup. Section 4 motivates the use of the 1-Wasserstein distance on the grounds that, via Kan-torovich–Rubinstein duality, it captures all 1-Lipschitz functionals of the distributional difference, including mean shift, variance, and higher-order shape changes. We test this claim empirically by replacing the Sinkhorn-approximated W1 in Algorithm 1 with three alternatives that differ in how much distributional information they retain: (i) Cosine distance between span means: a pure mean-shift, direction-only signal that discards both magnitude and shape. (ii) Chamfer distance between span hidden-state sets: a point-cloud distance that captures geometric coverage but is not a proper transport metric and is sensitive to outliers. (iii) MMD (Maximum Mean Discrepancy) with an RBF kernel: a kernel embedding distance that captures all moments through the kernel feature map but lacks the metric structure of optimal transport. All variants use identical span construction, normalization, max-pooling, and weighting; only the inner distance computation differs. 

Result. Fig. 5a shows the training curves for the four variants alongside the GRPO baseline. Wasserstein, Chamfer, and MMD form a top tier with peak accuracies above the GRPO baseline. SHEAR (Wasserstein) achieves the highest peak, but the margin over SHEAR (Chamfer) and SHEAR (MMD) is small, suggesting that any sufficiently expressive distributional metric is able to capture the dominant component of the credit signal. This is consistent with the theoretical view in Section 4: the population-level signal D(S) reflects a distributional gap that is rich enough to be detected by multiple measures, with optimal-transport providing the tightest characterization but not the only viable one. The behavior of SHEAR (Cosine) is more revealing. SHEAR (Cosine) plateaus at 0.4837 , falling below the GRPO baseline ( 0.4884 ) throughout the latter half of training. This is a striking negative result: using only the angular separation between span means as a credit signal actively hurts learning. We interpret this as evidence that mean-direction shifts in hidden states are not a faithful proxy for reasoning quality; they correlate with surface-level lexical variation and other reasoning-irrelevant factors, and weighting tokens by such a signal injects noise into the policy gradient. The fact that the three distribution-aware metrics succeed where the mean-only metric fails directly supports the central claim of Section 4: capturing the full distributional difference, rather than a low-order summary, is what makes hidden states a reliable source of process-level credit.

Result. As shown in Fig 5b, both variants of SHEAR substantially outperform the GRPO baseline. Two observations follow. First, the dominant source of improvement is clearly the within-rollout token-level credit signal: even after explicitly equalizing per-rollout weight magnitudes, SHEAR (per-rollout) still improves over GRPO by a comfortable margin. This indicates that the method’s effectiveness does not hinge on cross-rollout magnitude differences—the per-token ranking of credit within a rollout, which the separation theorem in Section 4 directly addresses, is what carries the learning signal. Second, the default formulation nonetheless retains a measurable edge over the per-rollout-equalized variant, suggesting that allowing rollouts with larger absolute distributional gaps to receive proportionally larger total gradient mass is mildly beneficial. Such rollouts contain more discriminable reasoning errors and plausibly merit more aggressive updates; collapsing the magnitude variation across rollouts gives up a small amount of useful signal. We therefore adopt SHEAR (cross-rollout) as the default in all main experiments. Importantly, both variants sit comfortably above the GRPO baseline, indicating that the method is robust to this design choice. This ablation reinforces the interpretation in Section 4: the value of the Wasserstein signal lies primarily in where within a rollout it concentrates credit, with cross-rollout magnitude variation playing a complementary role.

First, increasing the group size from 8 to 16 improves both methods, confirming that larger rollout groups provide richer contrastive signal for credit assignment. Second, the gap between SHEAR and GRPO widens at G = 16 : SHEAR (G = 16 ) achieves the highest final accuracy among all four configurations, while GRPO ( G = 16 )plateaus below SHEAR ( G = 8 ) in late training. This suggests that SHEAR benefits more from additional rollouts than standard GRPO, likely because a larger opposing set yields more reliable span-level Wasserstein estimates, amplifying the advantage of fine-grained credit assignment. 

6 Computational Overhead 

Computing span-level Wasserstein distances via the Sinkhorn algorithm introduces additional com-putation at each training step. Figure 8 quantifies this cost: relative to the GRPO baseline, SHEAR increases total training time by 15.5% on Qwen2.5-Math-7B, 10.4% on Llama3.1-8B-Instruct, and 7.4% on Qwen2.5-14B-Base. The overhead decreases at larger model scale because the Sinkhorn computation operates on fixed-size spans regardless of model dimension, while the dominant cost of forward and backward passes grows with model size. In all cases, the overhead remains below 16%, making SHEAR practical for standard RLVR training pipelines.

8 Conclusion 

This paper introduces SHEAR, a self-supervised credit assignment method for RLVR that exploits the model’s own hidden-state representations to provide fine-grained, token-level training signals. We first identify an empirical phenomenon that span-level Wasserstein distance between hidden-state distributions of correct and incorrect rollouts tracks local reasoning quality, without requiring step-level annotation or an external reward model. We formalize this observation with a separation theorem showing that post-divergence spans exhibit provably larger Wasserstein distances than pre-divergence spans, under a verifiable condition on the population-level distributional gap. Moreover, we translate this insight into a practical algorithm that rescales token-level GRPO advantages using span-level Wasserstein distances, requiring only minimal modifications to existing training pipelines. While Section 2 establishes a strong macro-level anti-correlation between empirical accuracy and Wasserstein distance, this appendix investigates the robustness and dynamic sensitivity of this signal. Specifically, we ask two questions: Is the W-distance signal merely an artifact of longer sequence lengths? And how does it respond to shifts in reasoning quality as the trajectory unfolds? 

A.1 Cross-Sectional Analysis: Controlling for Sequence Length 

As a reasoning chain progresses, empirical accuracy naturally decreases while hidden state drift increases. To ensure W-distance is an independent indicator of reasoning quality rather than a simple proxy for token depth, we conducted a cross-sectional analysis as shown in Fig 9. By strictly grouping trajectories into distinct prefix position bins and dividing them into W-distance quantiles, a consistent pattern emerges. Notably, even in the early stages of reasoning, W-distance already demonstrates a preliminary discriminative ability: within the same early position group, a higher W-distance weakly but consistently corresponds to lower mean empirical accuracy. More importantly, trajectories that fall into high W-distance bins early in the process are significantly more prone to error accumulation, leading to more frequent and severe reasoning failures at later positions. This confirms that an elevated W-distance serves as an effective early warning signal for vulnerable reasoning state. 

A.2 Dynamic Step-wise Tracking of Reasoning Mutations 

To verify that W-distance captures structural shifts in reasoning, we analyzed the step-wise derivatives of both metrics ( ∆Accuracy and ∆W-Distance) across consecutive strides. 

Computing ∆Accuracy and ∆W-Distance. Using the same per-prefix measurements from Sec-tion 2, we compute first-order differences between consecutive prefix positions within each rollout: 

∆Acc t = Acc (st) − Acc (st−1), ∆Wt = W (st) − W (st−1)

where st and st−1 denote two consecutive prefix positions within the same rollout. These step-wise differences isolate the local change in reasoning quality and representational drift at each stride, as opposed to the cumulative trends analyzed in Section 2. 

Defining mutation events. We isolate "mutation events"—moments where a trajectory experiences a significant shift in local continuation success. Since the continuation accuracy at each prefix is estimated by completing the reasoning 16 times and recording the fraction of correct completions (Section 2), the minimum detectable change in accuracy is 1/16 = 0 .0625 . We therefore adopt 

∆|Acc | ≥ 0.0625 as the threshold for identifying mutation events: changes below this magnitude are indistinguishable from the sampling noise inherent in a 16-trial estimate, while changes at or above this level correspond to at least one completion flipping between correct and incorrect, indicating a meaningful shift in reasoning quality. As shown in Fig 1b, there is a clear inverse relationship between local accuracy mutations and representational drift (Spearman correlation = −0.42 ). When a trajectory suffers a drop in reasoning quality ( ∆Accuracy < 0), the W-distance tends to exhibit a corresponding local increase ( ∆W-Distance > 0). This confirms that Wasserstein distance is a sensitive monitor of local reasoning failures, closely tracking shifts in structural reasoning logic as they unfold.

J Implementation Details 

The training setup includes a batch size of 512, a learning rate of 1 × 10 −6, and a clip range between 0.2 and 0.28. Each response sequence is up to 3k tokens in length. The mini-batch size is set to 32. The temperature is 1.0 for training and 0.1 for evaluation. Following prior RLVR work [19, 27], we perform 8 rollouts per prompt and do not use entropy regularization or KL penalties during training (KL coefficient = 0, entropy loss = 0). For math reasoning, We report avg@32 on AIME 2024 and AIME 2025, and avg@8 on AMC 2023, because these benchmarks contain relatively few problems, averaging over multiple samples yields more stable estimates of model capability. For MATH500 and OlympiadBench, which are substantially larger, we report avg@1.
