# DPEPO: Diverse Parallel Exploration Policy Optimization for LLM-based Agents 

Junshuo Zhang 1,2, Chengrui Huang 1,2, Feng Guo 1, Zihan Li 1, Ke Shi 1

Menghua Jiang 2, Jiguo Yu 1, Shuo Shang 1 and Shen Gao 11University of Electronic Science and Technology of China, 2DiDi 

Abstract 

Large language model (LLM) agents that fol-low the sequential “reason-then-act” paradigm have achieved superior performance in many complex tasks. However, these methods suf-fer from limited exploration and incomplete environmental understanding, as they interact with only a single environment per step. In this paper, we first introduce a novel paradigm that enables an agent to interact with multiple environments simultaneously and share cross-trajectory experiences. Building upon this paradigm, we further propose Diverse Paral-lel Exploration Policy Optimization (DPEPO), a reinforcement learning (RL) algorithm that encourages the agent to perform diverse paral-lel exploration. There are two stages in DPEPO: initial supervised fine-tuning (SFT) imparts ba-sic parallel reasoning and action generation, followed by reinforcement learning stage with a hierarchical reward scheme. We design a par-allel trajectory-level success reward and two step-level rewards: Diverse Action Reward and Diverse State Transition Reward, which actively penalize behavioral redundancy and promote broad exploration. Extensive experi-ments on ALFWorld and ScienceWorld show that DPEPO achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) success rates, while maintaining comparable efficiency to strong sequential baselines. 1

Recent work (Richens et al., 2025) demonstrates that a comprehensive un-derstanding of the environment is crucial for effec-tive and reliable agents. However, ReAct imposes a fundamental limitation: at each step, the agent can perform only one action and observe a single obser-vation. (2) Inefficiency and isolation : Experiences from different trajectories are neither shared nor leveraged, meaning the agent cannot learn from cross-trajectory insights. To overcome these limitations, we introduce par-allel exploration, a paradigm in which an agent in-teracts with multiple environments simultaneously. This allows the agent to gather diverse observations in parallel, share experiences across environments, and build a more holistic understanding of the en-vironment dynamics. Building on this exploration paradigm, we propose Diverse Parallel Exploration Policy Optimization (DPEPO), a novel reinforce-ment learning framework that endows agents with structured parallel exploration capabilities. After an initial SFT phase to acquire basic parallel rea-soning and action generation skills, the agent is optimized via a hierarchical reward scheme that en-courages diverse exploration at both the trajectory and step levels. Specifically, we design a parallel trajectory-level success reward and two novel step-level rewards: Diverse Action Reward and Diverse State Transition Reward, that actively penalize re-dundant behaviors. These rewards are combined through a group-relative advantage computation to provide multi-granularity learning signals, en-abling the agent to explore more broadly while stay-ing focused on task completion. Extensive exper-iments on ALFWorld and ScienceWorld, demon-strate that DPEPO achieves state-of-the-art success rates across all task splits. Notably, our analysis of trajectory length and token usage reveals that DPEPO achieves efficiency comparable to strong sequential baselines, despite its parallel nature. Our contributions are summarized as follows: 

• We propose Diverse Parallel Exploration Policy Optimization (DPEPO), a novel RL framework that pioneers parallel exploration for agents to build holistic environment cognition. 

• We design a Parallel Trajectory-level Success Reward to optimize task completion in concurrent multi-environment settings. 

• We propose Parallel Step-level Rewards (Diverse Action & State Transition) to penalize redundancy and foster distinct explorations. 

• Extensive experiments on ALFWorld and Sci-enceWorld show DPEPO achieves SOTA perfor-mance in terms of success rates while maintain-ing comparable efficiency with single exploration methods. 

2 Related Work 

LLM as Autonomous Agents LLMs are increas-ingly being deployed as autonomous agents across a wide range of domains, including code genera-tion (Zhang et al., 2024), web navigation (Abuel-saad et al., 2024), smart device control (Zhang and Zhang, 2024; Gur et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2025b; Ye et al., 2025), external tool use (Gao et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024, 2025; Huang et al., 2025) and interac-tive gaming (Shridhar et al., 2021; Yao et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022, 2023). Early LLM-based agents typically relied on engineered prompting strategies (Wei et al., 2022) to achieve strong performance, such as ReAct (Yao et al., 2023) and Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2023). Recent studies has shifted their focus toward fine-tuning LLMs for agent-related tasks using either SFT (Song et al., 2024; Xi et al., 2024) or RL (Feng et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025), aiming to develop agents with greater generaliza-tion ability and robustness, ultimately resulting in more efficient and resilient behaviors compared to earlier prompt-based methods. 

Reinforcement Learning for LLM-based Agents 

Reinforcement learning has been recognized as a foundational approach for enabling LLMs to oper-ate as autonomous agents in complex environments. Early work in this domain primarily employed PPO (Schulman et al., 2017), which allowed agents to learn diverse interactive tasks while avoiding the need for expensive imitation data. With the emer-gence of reinforcement learning with verifiable re-ward (RLVR) (Guo et al., 2025; Lambert et al., 2025) and group-based policy optimization (Shao et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2025; Kool et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2025c), a series of studies have elimi-nated complex reward model and critic model, in-stead adopting rule-based reward signals: GiGPO (Feng et al., 2025) proposes anchor state groups to estimate the quality of specific steps; RLVMR (Zhang et al., 2025) introduces meta-reasoning and corresponding step-level rewards to address ineffi-cient exploration; iStar (Liu et al., 2025a) estimates step rewards via the LLM’s intrinsic implicit re-wards. 3.2 Parallel Exploration Capability Cold Start 

We begin with an SFT-based cold start stage to endow the agent with foundational skills for struc-tured output and parallel exploration. For the SFT training data, we introduce human prior knowl-edge into its synthetic pipeline. The data synthesis pipeline is two steps: (1) We first manually anno-tated five ground-truth trajectories. Each trajectory consists of multiple steps, with some key steps con-taining two or more parallel exploration actions. (2) Based on these trajectories, we employ DeepSeek-V3.2 to annotate trajectories containing parallel exploration behaviors. Finally, the agent is fine-tuned on these annotated trajectories, learning to imitate the parallel exploration patterns. Next, we employ a reinforcement learning method to train the LLM to perform better parallel exploration actions by using dual-level rewards as the training signal: trajectory-level and step-level.

4 Experimental Setup 

4.1 Datasets 

We train and evaluate the LLM-based agents on two challenging benchmarks: ALFWorld (Shridhar et al., 2021) and ScienceWorld (Wang et al., 2022). ALFWorld is a text-based interactive environment where agents perform everyday household tasks through multi-step reasoning and interactions. It presents goals like “put the pencil on the desk”, requiring agents to explore simulated rooms based on common sense. The environment is fully text-based and only returns a reward signal upon final success or failure. ScienceWorld is a simulated benchmark designed to evaluate scientific agent’s reasoning capabilities across diverse elementary science topics. It includes tasks from 10 domains, including electricity, life cycles, and chemical reac-tions. Each task consists of multiple intermediate sub-goals that agents must complete sequentially. 

4.2 Baselines 

For ALFWorld and ScienceWorld, we compare our approach with a range of competitive base-lines under the ReAct paradigm: (1) Closed-source LLMs: GPT-4o (Hurst et al., 2024), DeepSeek-V3 (Liu et al., 2024), and DeepSeek-R1 (Guo et al., 2025), which show superior performance on various natural language tasks. (2) A se-ries of RL methods: GRPO (Shao et al., 2024), which performs advantage estimation over trajec-tory groups; GiGPO (Feng et al., 2025), intro-ducing step-level advantage through anchor state grouping; RLVMR (Zhang et al., 2025), which pro-poses meta-reasoning rewards to address inefficient exploration; and SPEAR (Qin et al., 2025), leverag-ing self-imitation and intrinsic rewards to improve the exploration–exploitation balance. 

4.3 Implementation Details 

We conduct experiments on various model sizes of Qwen2.5-Instruct (Yang et al., 2025b) and Qwen3 (Yang et al., 2025a). For cold start train-ing, we annotate approximately 500 and 1,000 for ALFWorld and ScienceWorld, respectively. For re-inforcement learning stage, the number of training sample is 500, the temperature for rollout is 0.4, the rollout group size N is set to 8 for ALFWorld and 4 for ScienceWorld, with 1 epoch and a total of 125 training steps. The max step is 25, and the number of parallel environments is 4. Full hyperparameter details and prompts used for training are provided in Appendix B and Appendix C.

5.1 Overall Performance 

Table 1 demonstrates the performance of DPEPO and baselines on ALFWorld and ScienceWorld datasets. As illustrated, closed-source LLMs ex-hibit only limited performance: On ALFWorld, DeepSeek-R1 achieves a success rate of 80.1%, and on ScienceWorld, GPT-4o reaches 45.2%. Un-der the ReACT paradigm, RL training yields sub-stantial improvements: GRPO raises the average success rate on ALFWorld to 72.0% for the 1.5B model and 77.5% for the 7B model, demonstrat-ing the effectiveness of rule-based success reward. However, the coarse-grained nature of this re-ward limits further performance gains. In contrast, GiGPO, SPEAR and RLVMR address this issue by incorporating step-level rewards that provide more fine-grained optimization signals for intermediate reasoning steps. Among these methods, RLVMR achieves the strongest performance, with average success rates of 91.6% on ALFWorld and 47.5% on ScienceWorld. Despite the demonstrated effective-ness of these RL methods, they remain constrained by the ReAct paradigm, which restricts the breadth of environmental exploration and forces the agent to construct its understanding from a narrow, linear view. Our proposed DPEPO addresses this limi-tation through parallel exploration and diversity-driven rewards, achieving the best results: 98.2% on ALFWorld and 61.4% on ScienceWorld. 6 Conclusion 

In this work, we propose a novel parallel explo-ration paradigm and reinforcement learning algo-rithm to address the constrained exploration chal-lenge inherent in ReACT paradigm. We first ex-tend the ReACT framework to enable agents to simultaneously explore multiple parallel environ-ments. We then introduce Diverse Parallel Ex-ploration Policy Optimization (DPEPO), a novel diversity-driven policy gradient algorithm specifi-cally designed for parallel agents. By incorporat-ing fine-grained, step-level rewards into the par-allel trajectory-level success reward, DPEPO en-courages agents to perform more diverse explo-ration in parallel environments. Empirical eval-uations across all test splits on ALFWorld and the more challenging ScienceWorld benchmarks demonstrate that DPEPO achieves state-of-the-art performance and high sample efficiency. Through extensive experiments and analysis, we show that the diverse parallel exploration behavior reinforced by DPEPO leads to superior task performance, higher efficiency, and more effective token usage. Limitations 

While DPEPO shows significant improvements in performance and efficiency, it still faces chal-lenges in real-world scenarios where parallel envi-ronments are hard to build. However, we suggest a possible solution: training agents to complete dif-ferent tasks in parallel, which might help mitigate this issue in real-world scenarios. We leave this exploration for future work. 

Ethical Considerations 

In some real-world environments that may con-tain biases, parallel exploration enables the agent to gather more experience, which could lead it to learn those biases, potentially resulting in unfair or unsafe behavior. Therefore, before applying our proposed method to real-world tasks, developers should conduct thorough evaluations in their target environments to ensure the agent acquires accu-rate and objective knowledge and performs actions safely and responsibly.

B Experimental Details 

B.1 SFT Training Details Datasets For ALFWorld, we annotate 500 suc-cessful trajectories from the training split. For Sci-enceWorld, we adopt the same data partitioning strategy as RLVMR, which defines three difficulty levels: L0, L1, and L2. For training on L0 and L1, we annotate 1,000 trajectories from the L1 training set (used for both L0 and L1 training for simplicity). Consequently, our model faces a more challenging L0 evaluation compared to RLVMR, as it is trained with fewer L0-specific examples. For L2, we anno-tate 500 trajectories for training. 

Experiment and Hyperparameters Details All our SFT experiments are conducted using the OpenRLHF framework. Specifically, we employ DeepSpeed ZeRO-3 with Adam optimizer offload-ing enabled. The model is trained with a maxi-mum sequence length of 8,192 tokens, a learning rate of 5e -6, and bfloat16 mixed-precision, with FlashAttention-2 enabled in all experiments. The batch size is set to 4 for ALFWorld and 16 for ScienceWorld. 

B.2 RL Training Details Datasets For both ALFWorld and ScienceWorld, we select 500 samples from the training set that excludes SFT samples for RL training. 

Experiment and Hyperparameters Details All RL experiments is conducted based on verl-agent framework and Ray-based dirtributed parallel en-vironment. The maximum prompt length is set to 10,240 tokens and the maximum response length to 2,048 tokens, with each trajectory allowed up to 25 environment steps. The final actor learning rate is 1e-6, and we employ parallel trajectory-level reward that awards +1 for task success. Repetition penalties are applied consistently across both envi-ronments, with coefficients of 0.8 for depth-level action repetition and 0.95 for width-level action rep-etition, depth-level transition repetition, and width-level transition repetition. Notably, ScienceWorld includes an additional penalty coefficient of 0.5 for invalid actions, which is not used in ALFWorld. Both environments run with a maximum of 4 par-allel exploration trajectories during training. For group-based reinforcement learning, ALFWorld adopts a group size of 8 while ScienceWorld uses a group size of 4; in both cases, 4 distinct groups are sampled per rollout. The rollout temperature is set to 0.4, and the mini-batch size is 32. All policy updates are performed using LoRA, with the LoRA rank and scaling factor are all set to 32. 

B.3 Computation Details. 

For parallel environments, we cap the maximum CPU usage at 0.3. For training on ALFWorld and ScienceWorld, experiments with Qwen2.5-1.5B are conducted on 4×L40 GPUs, and those with Qwen2.5-7B on 4×H20 GPUs. Each model is trained for 1 epoch (500 samples) with 125 iter-ations. 

C Prompt 

C.1 Prompt Details for Training System Prompt System prompt is crucial for prompt engineering. The LLM follows the instruc-tions and assumes the role specified in the it, such as translator, programmer, etc. Therefore, as illus-trated in Figure 11, we have provided a detailed description of the desired behaviors of the paral-lel agent within the system prompt to facilitate its parallel exploration and thereby accomplish the task better. Specifically, similar to ReAct, paral-lel agent is required to engage in reasoning before making a decision. For example, it needs to con-sider which actions can be executed in parallel to gather more information and which environments warrant further exploration. This reasoning process must be enclosed within the <think></think> 

tags. After reasoning, the agent can take actions. Unlike ReAct, the parallel agent can interact with multiple environments simultaneously. Thus, we require it to specify environment-specific actions within <parallel></parallel> tags, with each individual action wrapped in <env_i><env> tags to clearly indicate which environment the action belongs to and to enable easier parsing of the ac-tions. 

Prompt for First Step At the beginning of a task, the agent has not yet interacted with the en-vironment. The environment provides only initial state information, a set of available actions, and the task description. We organize this informa-tion into a concise yet clear prompt (As shown in Figure 12) to help the agent understand the initial context. Additionally, for ALF-World, we do not include explanations for the available actions. How-ever, for the ScienceWorld dataset, which involves combinations of actions and objects, we introduce brief action descriptions to avoid excessive token consumption. 

Prompt for Intermediate Step As the number of parallel agent exploration steps increases, maintain-ing the full history of interactions becomes costly in terms of both memory and computation. To ad-dress this, at step t, for information from steps 1

to t − 2, we retain only the actions and their cor-responding observations within {history_info}. In contrast, for the immediate previous step t − 1,we preserve all of the parallel actions, observa-tions, and executable actions within {last_history}. The details of {history_info} and {last_history} are presented in Figure 14 and Figure 15. With this carefully designed prompt presented in Figure 13, the context length is reduced while still retaining sufficient information for the agent to perform its task more effectively. 

Exploration-limiting prompt For experiments that require controlling the number of parallel envi-ronments, we designed a prompt to limit the max-imum number of environments the model can ex-plore. In this prompt, we specify the allowed num-ber of environments using “env_num” and inform the model that if it attempts to explore more than this number, the entire task will be considered a failure. In addition to this prompt-based restriction, we also enforce behavioral constraints: if the model tries to access an environment that is not permitted, the entire task is immediately seen as failed.

C.2 Effectiveness of Designed Prompts Token Budget Analysis When tackling long-horizon tasks, traditional methods typically append all historical information and intermediate reason-ing steps into a single context window. However, as the number of steps increases, the context con-tinuously expands. In this section, we evaluate the effectiveness of our designed prompt in compress-ing the context window. As illustrated in Figure 10, our method can compress prompts that originally contain at most 50,000 tokens down to at most 7,000 tokens, significantly reducing the token bud-get while still maintaining the agent’s strong per-formance. 

Comparison of Context Management Strategies 

GiGPO employs a similar prompting strategy, but instead of retaining all historical information, it only preserves the most recent few steps within a limited context window. We evaluate GiGPO’s performance with varying context window sizes, as shown in Table 8. Although the context length may be shorter, its performance does not improve as the context window expands. Furthermore, once the number of retained steps exceeds 16, the total context length will surpasses the model’s maximum sequence length, leading to truncation. In contrast, our proposed prompting design not only preserves more comprehensive historical in-formation but also adheres to the token budget. Furthermore, as demonstrated in Section 5.2 and Section 5.5, under our prompting scheme, the per-formance of our method consistently improves as the number of environments increases, highlighting its efficiency, robustness and scalability.
