# Abstract 

Communication in Large Language Model (LLM)-based multi-agent systems is moving beyond discrete tokens to preserve richer context. Recent work such as LatentMAS enables agents to exchange latent messages through full key-value (KV) caches. However, full KV relay incurs high memory and communica-tion cost. We adapt eviction-style KV compression to this setting and intro-duce Orthogonal Backfill (OBF) to mitigate information loss from hard evic-tion. OBF injects a low-rank orthogonal residual from discarded KV states into the retained KV states. We evaluate proposed method against full KV relay on nine standard benchmarks spanning mathematical reasoning, coding, and knowledge-intensive QA. It achieves performance comparable to full KV relay while reducing communication cost by 79.8%–89.4%. OBF further im-proves the performance and achieves the best results on 7 of the 9 bench-marks. This suggests that more information does not necessarily lead to better communication; preserving the most useful information matters more. Our codebase is publicly available. https://github.com/markli404/ When-Less-Latent-Leads-to-Better-Relay 

# 1 Introduction 

Large language model (LLM) based multi-agent systems rely on communication to coordinate rea-soning, critique intermediate outputs, and refine final decisions. Early multi-agent frameworks pri-marily use natural language as the communication medium, where agents exchange explicit mes-sages under predefined roles or protocols [14, 8, 2]. While effective, such communication is in-herently constrained by the discreteness of text: when internal states are externalized as token sequences, uncertainty over alternatives and other unresolved information can be collapsed into a more determinate representation during transmission. This limitation has motivated increasing in-terest in richer communication channels that move beyond natural language and preserve more of the model’s internal computation [20, 23]. Among these directions, LatentMAS is particularly ap-pealing because it enables one agent to relay its key-value (KV) cache directly to another agent, allowing downstream reasoning to continue from internal states rather than from text alone [33]. However, this higher-fidelity communication comes at a substantial cost. Relaying full KV caches is memory- and bandwidth-intensive, and the cost grows quickly with the number of agents, rounds, and prompt tokens involved. A natural solution is to compress the communicated KV states. Recent work on efficient LLM inference suggests that not all cached states are equally important. Methods such as StreamingLLM, H2O, and Scissorhands show that selective retention or eviction of KV states can reduce memory 1

However, these methods are developed primarily for online cache management during token-by-token generation, where the goal is to control memory consumption within a single model’s ongoing inference process. In LatentMAS-style communication, the setting is fundamentally different. Compression occurs at the relay boundary between agents, and the relevant question is not only which states are sufficient for continuing local generation, but whether the transmitted states remain useful for downstream continuation by another agent . As a result, methods designed for online KV eviction do not directly solve the relay problem in multi-agent latent communication. In this work, we study KV compression specifically for LatentMAS-style inter-agent relay. Our starting point is that relay quality depends not simply on how much KV is transmitted, but on whether the transmitted states preserve the information most useful for the next agent. Under this view, relay compression should be evaluated as a communication problem rather than merely as a cache reduction problem. Based on this perspective, we develop an eviction-style KV compression framework for relay and propose OrthoBackfill (OBF) to mitigate information loss caused by hard eviction. OBF is designed to preserve distinctive residual information from discarded states and inject it back into the retained representation, with the goal of improving downstream usability under a fixed communication budget. Our experiments show that compressed relay can often match or even outperform full KV relay un-der fixed prompt-token budgets. This result suggests that effective relay depends less on preserving the largest possible amount of raw prompt state and more on preserving states that remain useful for downstream continuation. We further analyze the tradeoff between relay efficiency and relay fidelity across datasets and settings, and examine when eviction helps, when it fails, and how OBF affects this tradeoff. Together, our findings position relay-oriented KV compression as a distinct problem in multi-agent latent communication, separate from both text-based coordination and standard online KV cache management. Our contributions are as follows. • We formulate KV compression in LatentMAS as a relay-specific communication problem, where the goal is to preserve states that remain useful for downstream continuation under a communication budget, rather than merely reduce cache size. • We develop an eviction-style KV compression framework for inter-agent relay and propose 

OrthoBackfill (OBF) to compensate for information lost under hard eviction. • We show empirically that compressed relay remains competitive with full relay while re-ducing communicated prompt KV by over 80%. 22 Related Work 

Why Multi-Agent Systems. A growing body of work suggests that multi-agent systems (MAS) can outperform single-agent baselines on complex tasks such as software engineering, mathematical reasoning, and deliberative evaluation [8, 5, 1, 15]. A common explanation is that MAS distributes reasoning across roles, prompts, or subtasks rather than forcing the entire problem into a single trajectory [14, 8, 25]. This can help through division of labor, where a complex objective is decom-posed into simpler subproblems [8, 25, 31], and through cross-agent verification, where critique and checking are distributed across different roles or response trajectories instead of relying on intrinsic self-correction alone [10, 5, 1]. Our work focuses on a bottleneck that becomes central once such multi-agent collaboration is adopted: how agents communicate rich intermediate state efficiently. 

From Language to Latent Communication. Early MAS frameworks such as CAMEL [14] and 

MetaGPT [8] organize collaboration through natural-language exchanges under predefined roles and workflows, while later systems such as Optima [2] improve efficiency through learned man-agers that reduce redundant communication. More recent work moves beyond text-only communi-cation and explores richer carriers for inter-agent information exchange. CIPHER [20] communi-cates through expected output embeddings, StateDelta [23] augments language with hidden-state changes, and COCONUT [7] studies latent-space reasoning without decoding intermediate steps into text. These works motivate a broader shift from token-level communication toward latent-state communication. Our work builds on this shift, but focuses specifically on the efficiency problem created by full KV-state relay. 

LatentMAS and KV Relay. Our setting is most directly related to LatentMAS [33], a training-free framework for pure latent collaboration among LLM agents. Instead of passing decoded in-termediate messages, LatentMAS transfers the full KV cache from one agent to the next, allowing downstream reasoning to continue directly from the predecessor’s internal state. This makes relay substantially higher-fidelity than text-mediated communication, but it also turns communicated KV into the dominant communication object. Our work addresses this bottleneck directly by studying compression at the relay boundary rather than modifying the reasoning procedure itself. 

KV Eviction, Compression, and Compensation. A separate line of work studies how to reduce KV-cache cost in long-context inference. Quantization methods such as KVQuant [9] reduce the footprint of retained KV tensors, while eviction methods such as H2O [30], StreamingLLM [26], and Scissorhands [17] selectively retain only the most useful past states. More recent methods fur-ther reduce the damage of hard eviction through compensation or merging. RazorAttention [22] summarizes removed context through compensation tokens in non-retrieval heads, while CaM [29] and WeightedKV [28] merge discarded information into retained states instead of removing it out-right. These methods are the closest line of work to ours, but they are designed for online cache management in single-agent inference. Our setting is different: compression happens only at the re-lay step, and the criterion is whether compressed states remain useful for downstream continuation by another agent.

3.2 Agentic Communication in LatentMAS 

In LatentMAS, agents communicate through latent messages represented by Transformer KV-cache states produced during autoregressive latent inference. Let Ai denote the i-th agent in the com-munication chain. After completing its local computation, Ai transmits a message Mi, where 

Mi = [ Mi−1; KV( Yi)] .Here, KV( Yi) denotes the token-level KV-cache states produced by agent Ai. We decompose these states as KV( Yi) = [ SPi ; SGi ], where SPi is induced by the local prompt Pi and SGi is induced during autoregressive latent generation. The receiver Ai+1 continues decoding by using the transmitted message Mi as PastKV . However, recursively appending KV( Yi) causes the message length to grow linearly with the total number of cached tokens across agents, creating the bandwidth bottleneck addressed next. 

3.3 Problem Formulation 

As interaction proceeds, the message Mi grows linearly with depth, with |M i| ∝ Pik=0 |KV( Yk)|.To prevent this unbounded accumulation, we introduce a compression operator Φ parameterized by a budget ratio ρ ∈ (0 , 1] :

Zi = Φ(KV( Yi), ρ ), |Zi| ≈ ρ · | KV( Yi)|.

Our goal is to design Φ that maximizes final task accuracy under any given ρ.

# 4 Methodology 

This section revisits standard KV-cache eviction in the setting of multi-agent latent communication. We begin by identifying a functional decomposition of cache roles in single-agent inference, then show how these roles are reshaped in the multi-agent setting by agent boundaries and inherited messages. Based on this decomposition, we next define eviction-style baselines that adapt existing single-agent strategies, such as StreamingLLM and H2O, to LatentMAS . This setup also clarifies the limitation of direct eviction and motivates our proposed Orthogonal Backfill (OBF) framework. 

4.1 From Single-Agent KV-Cache Eviction to Multi-Agent Cache Decomposition 

We propose a new cache decomposition formulation adapted for the multi-agent collaboration set-ting based on the formulations in the existing literature. Existing eviction methods revealed a recur-ring functional pattern in single-agent KV-cache management.Specifically, we consider a three-part functional decomposition: Attention Sink: The initial tokens that serve as numerical anchors and help stabilize the Softmax distribution [26]; Candidate Pool: The middle portion of the sequence, 4Figure 2: KV-cache role decomposition in single-agent and multi-agent settings. Our multi-agent decomposition follows the same functional view as standard single-agent KV compression, with aligned sink, candidate-like, and local-recent roles. The main difference is that multi-agent relay introduces inherited message history. The inset contrasts rolling-budget cache updates with our one-shot prompt-state selection. which forms the primary target for importance-based eviction or compression [30, 17]; and Local Window: The most recent tokens, retained to preserve immediate local context for autoregressive continuity [26, 30]. In multi-agent collaboration, latent memory is no longer produced by a single homogeneous stream. Instead, it accumulates across agent boundaries as alternating prompt-conditioned and generated segments, e.g., 

[SP1 ; SG1 ; SP2 ; SG2 ; . . . ] = [ Mi−1; SPi ; SGi ].

This structure changes the role decomposition because the candidate region is no longer homo-geneous in the multi-agent setting. Once inherited memory must remain available as transmitted context, it becomes necessary to distinguish between previously transmitted message history and the current agent’s local prompt context. Meanwhile, the recency-sensitive role is taken over by the current agent’s active latent reasoning states. Accordingly, we use a four-part decomposition: 1. Attention Sink ( S ⊂ M i−1): A persistent anchor, originated by A0 and inherited across the chain, that maintains numerical stability. 2. Inherited Message History ( H ⊂ M i−1): The compressed historical context transmitted by upstream agents. 3. Current Prompt Context ( P = SPi ): The latent states induced by the task-specific in-structions of the current agent. 4. Current Latent Reasoning ( G = SGi ): The latent states generated by the current agent’s ongoing reasoning, which form the immediate causal basis for Ai’s generation. This reformulation provides the basis for adapting conventional single-agent eviction rules to the multi-agent setting.

4.3 Orthogonal Backfill: Residual Principal-Subspace Injection for Hard Eviction 

In LatentMAS, eviction is applied to each agent’s local prompt states as a block rather than as isolated token removals. As a result, a single compression step may delete a substantial portion of prompt KV at once. This can erase background information that is not tied to any single retained token, yet still supports downstream latent reasoning, making standard token-level compensation inadequate. Because the deleted prompt values remain available as a matrix during compression, the missing background structure can instead be modeled and controlled at the subspace level. To address this, we propose Orthogonal Backfill (OBF) , a lightweight augmentation that restores only the deleted components that are not representable within the span of the retained prompt val-ues. OBF decomposes the deleted values into an in-span component and an orthogonal residual, 6Figure 3: Comparison of KV cache eviction granularities : 1) Global (shared across all layers and heads), 2) Layer-wise (shared across heads within each layer), and 3) Head-wise (independent for each KV head and each layer). summarizes the residual content using attention weights, and injects a low-rank residual vector back into the retained prompt values. Formally, let Pi denote the prompt positions at agent round i (excluding padding and the global sink). Let Φ be a compression operator that returns a retained index set Pkeep ,i = Φ( Pi) ⊂ P i, and define the deleted set Pdel ,i = Pi \ P keep ,i .

4.3.1 Residual subspace extraction Motivation. After compression, some prompt tokens are removed while others are retained. During subsequent inference, attention that would otherwise have been assigned to deleted tokens can be redistributed to the retained ones. If part of the deleted content already lies in the span of the retained values, this redistributed attention can partially recover it from the retained states alone. Injecting that representable component again would therefore double count information already accessible through the retained tokens. OBF accordingly injects only the deleted component that is orthogonal to the retained-value span.

5.2 Results 

Across nine benchmarks, our method achieved an average compression ratio of 14.12%, with a maximum compression ratio of 19.4% in reasoning-intensive benchmark ARC-E. This suggests that our method effectively and consistently achieves a compression ratio of below 20%. In all nine benchmarks, at least one of the compression methods outperforms full KV eviction base-line in performance. This suggests that our KV compression method not only preserves sufficient latent information to prevent performance drop from compression, but also actively aiding effective information relay. Of the best compression methods, OBF further improves performance on 7 of 9 benchmarks, indicating that OBF effectively and consistently delivers performance gain. 

# 6 Discussion 

Our results suggest that, in LatentMAS-style relay, effectiveness depends on the usefulness of trans-mitted KV to the next agent rather than on transmitting more state. Full relay preserves the largest amount of raw prompt state, but it is not consistently the strongest method across benchmarks. Sev-eral compressed variants match or exceed Full, which suggests that selective retention can preserve, and sometimes improve, relay quality. One possible explanation is that eviction acts as a form of denoising by removing low-utility or task-irrelevant prompt states and producing a cleaner relay representation for downstream continuation. A second finding is that OBF-enhanced relay gives the strongest overall accuracy profile, even though its gains are not uniform across all selectors and tasks. Across benchmarks, the best-performing method is most often an OBF-enhanced variant, which suggests that OBF reliably im-proves the competitiveness of eviction-based relay under LatentMAS constraints. At the same time, its effect is not simply to preserve more information. Instead, OBF appears to make the retained KV more useful after sparsification. The selector determines which states are kept, while OBF helps make those states more usable to the next agent. The comparison between layer-wise and head-wise selection further suggests that relay granularity matters, but finer-grained selection is not always better. Although head-wise selection is more ex-pressive in principle, it does not consistently outperform the layer-wise variant. Instead, the two selectors show complementary strengths across tasks, and the stronger OBF-enhanced method de-pends on the underlying selector. This pattern may reflect differences in where task-relevant signal resides in prompt KV. When useful signal is concentrated in specialized heads, head-wise sparsifi-cation may help; when it is distributed more broadly, layer-wise aggregation may provide a more stable relay representation. Prior work has shown that attention heads can be functionally special-ized across tasks [12, 13]. Recent KV compression methods have also begun to exploit head-level importance and task-aware adaptation [6, 24, 32]. 10 Reproducibility Statement
