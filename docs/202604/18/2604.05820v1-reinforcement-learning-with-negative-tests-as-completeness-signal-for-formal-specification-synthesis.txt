## Abstract 

The specification synthesis task aims to automatically generate spec-ifications, together with any necessary auxiliary verification anno-tations, for existing programs. This task is important because such specifications serve as behavioral contracts that support modular reasoning and reusable verification across a codebase. At the same time, it remains challenging because verifier-only feedback is fun-damentally incomplete: passing verification establishes soundness, but cannot distinguish weak specifications from strong ones. What is missing is a fine-grained signal for specification completeness .We present SpecRL , a reinforcement learning framework for specification synthesis in Dafny. SpecRL introduces a self-contained pipeline that generates negative tests, i.e., input-output pairs that can never be produced by the program. We use the fraction of these negative tests rejected by a candidate specification as a signal of specification completeness, which is integrated into the reward for RL training. Experiments across four model sizes show that SpecRL improves both specification strength and verification success over SFT and RL with a binary specification-strength reward, generalizes to an out-of-distribution benchmark, and remains competitive on that unseen benchmark compared to much larger general-purpose LLMs.

## 1 Introduction 

Formal verification mathematically proves that a program satisfies desired properties by statically checking it against a formal specifi-cation [ 19 ]. In auto-active verification languages such as Dafny [ 29 ], Frama-C/ACSL [ 5], and Verus [ 27 ], developers annotate programs with specifications: preconditions and postconditions describe what each function should do, while auxiliary annotations such as loop invariants, assertions, and lemma invocations help the verifier dis-charge the resulting proof obligations. Writing these annotations by hand is tedious and error-prone [48], motivating growing interest in using LLMs to automate this process [ 1 , 4, 6, 7 , 15 , 36 , 50 , 53 , 54 ]. Among the various annotation tasks, specification synthesis is especially important: given an existing program that lacks formal annotations, the goal is to automatically generate specifications and auxiliary annotations (e.g., loop invariants) so that the program verifies and the specifications capture its intended behavior. Speci-fications act as machine-checked behavioral contracts [ 17 , 33 ] that document what a function does, so developers can understand its behavior from the contract alone without reading the implemen-tation. In large codebases and during code review, where tracing through every function is impractical, such contracts become the primary means of understanding code. Beyond documentation, specifications are also essential for modular verification , where a caller is verified against the callee’s specification rather than its implementation; if the specification is too weak, the caller’s verifi-cation may fail even when the implementation is correct. Yet most existing code lacks formal specifications, so writing them remains a major obstacle to wider adoption of formal verification. Several recent approaches have begun to address specification synthesis, but important limitations remain. Inference-time meth-ods [ 35 , 42 , 48 ] rely on prompting and iterative repair alone, so task-specific knowledge is never internalized by the model. SFT-based iterative approaches [ 8, 12 ] fine-tune on the model’s own verified outputs, but supervised learning remains fundamentally constrained by the quality and coverage of the training data. These limitations motivate a training paradigm that can opti-mize directly against external feedback. Reinforcement learning is a natural candidate, as prior work has shown that code models can be improved using unit-test, compiler, execution, and verifier feed-back [ 13 , 16 , 28 , 51 ]. Specification synthesis in Dafny is a natural fit, because candidate annotated programs can be compiled, verified, and executed, providing multiple forms of external feedback during training [ 29 ]. At the same time, effective RL depends on reward signals that are fine-grained enough to guide exploration over rich search spaces [ 2 , 37 , 49 ]. Specification synthesis is exactly such a search problem: many candidate annotation sets may verify, yet still differ substantially in how completely they capture program behavior [ 26 ]. However, existing RL approaches either do not fo-cus on specification quality itself [ 23 ] or rely on binary rewards that grant full credit once a generated specification is proved no weaker than a reference specification [ 51 ]. Such rewards are too coarse to distinguish weak specifications from strong ones. What is

Building on these observations, we propose SpecRL, a reinforce-ment learning framework for specification synthesis. The key idea is to generate negative tests, called spectests , that together mea-sure the completeness of a specification: each spectest is a concrete input-output pair that the program never produces but the specifica-tion may permit. (2) spectest reward , a progressive reward function that inte-grates the fine-grained completeness signal from spectests. (3) An extensive evaluation on two specification-focused benchmarks constructed from existing Dafny datasets: Py2Dfy-Spec for training and DafnyComp-Spec for out-of-distribution evaluation. The results show that fine-grained rewards consistently improve both specification strength and verification success over SFT and RL with a binary specification-strength reward across four model scales, while remaining competitive on the unseen benchmark. 

## 2 Background 2.1 Verification Annotations in Dafny 

Dafny [ 29 ] is a verification-aware language whose built-in verifier translates programs and annotations into verification conditions and discharges them with an SMT solver [ 9]. These annotations fall into two categories: 

• Specifications (blue ): requires clauses state precondi-tions on method inputs; ensures clauses state postcondi-tions on both method inputs and outputs. Together they form a behavioral contract between the caller and the callee. 

• Auxiliary annotations (purple ): loop invariants and as-sertions that help the verifier discharge proof obligations. Figure 1 shows a Dafny method Max that scans an array of nat-ural numbers and whose implementation returns the maximum element (or -1 for an empty array). The shown requires clause restricts the specification to non-empty arrays, and the ensures 

clause states that the returned value is at least every element. The loop invariant is an auxiliary annotation that states a property maintained at every iteration, enabling the verifier to reason about the loop without unrolling it. Taken together, these annotations specify that, for a non-empty array, Max returns a value no smaller than any array element.

In this sense, a specifica-tion is an abstraction of the program’s concrete behaviors: it omits implementation details and retains only constraints on observable inputs and outputs. The quality of a specification then corresponds to how tightly J𝑆 K approximates J𝑃 K, which can be characterized by two properties: 

• Soundness. J𝑃 K ⊆ J𝑆 K: every input-output pair that the program can produce satisfies the specification. The verifier checks exactly this property. 

• Completeness. J𝑆 K ⊆ J𝑃 K: every input-output pair that satisfies the specification can be produced by the program. Unlike soundness, the verifier cannot check completeness. When J𝑆 K = J𝑃 K, the specification is both sound and complete. Verification guarantees soundness, leaving completeness as the main unresolved question: how to quantify and reduce the gap between 

J𝑆 K and J𝑃 K. In the Max example (Figure 1), the specification is sound but incomplete in two ways: the postcondition is too weak (it permits returning any sufficiently large number, not just the maximum), and the precondition is too strong (requires a.Length > 0 excludes empty arrays, for which the method has well-defined behavior). By contrast, a complete specification for Max would characterize both cases handled by the implementation, for example:                  

> ensures a.Length == 0 =⇒m == -1
> ensures a.Length > 0 =⇒min a[..]
> ensures forall k:: 0≤k < a.Length =⇒m≥a[k]

These clauses say that the method returns -1 on empty arrays and otherwise returns an actual array element that is no smaller than every other element. As a result, they rule out spurious behaviors such as returning 100 for [3,1,4,1,5] while still admitting every behavior that the program can actually produce.

## 2.3 Reinforcement Learning for Language Model Training 

Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) trains a language model to imitate reference outputs and is thus inherently limited by the quality of its training data [ 39 ]. Reinforcement learning (RL) overcomes this limitation by letting the model explore: it samples diverse outputs and receives scores from an external reward function 𝑟 (𝑥, 𝑦 ), rein-forcing outputs that score well regardless of whether they appear in any training set. It also allows RL to exploit non-differentiable 

feedback signals such as compiler or verifier verdicts, which SFT cannot directly incorporate. We use Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) [ 40 ]to train the model. In each iteration, for a given prompt 𝑥 , GRPO samples a group of 𝐺 responses and scores them with 𝑟 (𝑥, 𝑦 ). Each response’s advantage is computed relative to the group: 

ˆ𝐴 𝑖 = 𝑟 (𝑥, 𝑦 𝑖 ) − mean ({ 𝑟 (𝑥, 𝑦 𝑗 )} 𝐺 𝑗 =1)

std ({ 𝑟 (𝑥, 𝑦 𝑗 )} 𝐺 𝑗 =1) (1) The model’s parameters are then updated to increase the likelihood of responses with positive advantage and decrease the likelihood of those with negative advantage. Over successive iterations, the policy gradually shifts toward generating higher-quality outputs. 

## 3 Method 

This section presents SpecRL. 

## 3.1 Problem Setting 

Our target task is specification synthesis for existing Dafny pro-grams. Given a fully-implemented Dafny method without any veri-fication annotation, the goal is to generate specifications (precondi-tions and postconditions) together with any auxiliary annotations (e.g., loop invariants) so that (1) the Dafny verifier accepts the pro-gram and (2) the resulting specifications are as complete as possible. Our approach trains a neural network model with reinforcement learning to perform this task. For reinforcement learning, we re-quire a training set containing fully annotated Dafny programs. Since high-quality Dafny programs are scarce, we do not require the specification in the training set to be complete. Our goal is to use reinforcement learning to improve the model such that it can generate stronger specifications that are more complete than those in the training set. 

## 3.2 Overview of SpecRL 

SpecRL has two phases: Offline spectest construction and Online RL training . In the offline phase, we generate a set of spectests for each program in the training set. A spectest is an input-output pair that can never be produced by the program. In the online phase, we train a model for specification synthesis using RL training. During the training, given a specification generated by the model, we use the set of generated spectests to measure the completeness of the specification, and integrate the computed score into the reward function for RL training. 

Running example. We illustrate the effect of both phases on the 

Max method from Figure 1. 

Offline phase. Given an annotated program in the training set, we first ask an LLM to generate test inputs for the program, and then execute the program on these inputs to get the corresponding outputs, and then ask the LLM to mutate the outputs to be some-thing different from the original outputs. In this way, we get a set of input-output pairs that the program never produces, i.e., spectests. For example, below are some spectests generated for our example.                   

> Input aCorrect mMutated m’ Case
> [3,1,4,1,5] 514 sum instead of max
> [3,1,4,1,5] 5100 arbitrary large value
> [3,1,4,1,5] 53non-maximum element
> [] -1 99 empty-array case

During the generation, we guide the LLM to generate spectests that satisfy two goals: (1) the spectests should be discriminative, assigning different scores for specifications with different strengths; (2) the spectests should be particularly discriminative towards spec-ifications that are stronger (i.e., more complete) than the current ones in the training set. The latter is because the model can already obtain the capability of generating the specification of the current strength after supervised training on the current training set, and our goal is to guide the model to generate stronger specifications. More details on this part can be found in Section 3.3.8. 

Online phase. In the online phase, we perform RL training to improve a model. In each training step, we first remove the annota-tions from a program in the training set (called a stripped program), and then ask the model to generate annotations for the program. If the annotation passes the Dafny verification, we compute the fraction of spectests rejected by the generated specification as a score for completeness. This score is combined with scores used in existing studies as the final reward. Below we show how the generated tests distinguish different specifications. Suppose the model generates two specifications for the example program, both passing the Dafny verification.                            

> Spec_weak:
> requires a.Length > 0
> ensures forall k:: 0≤k < a.Length =⇒m≥a[k] Spec_strong:
> ensures a.Length == 0 =⇒m == -1
> ensures a.Length > 0 =⇒min a[..]
> ensures forall k:: 0≤k < a.Length =⇒m≥a[k]

Spec_weak rejects only one of the four fixed spectests, so its com-pleteness score is 1/4 = 0.25 . By contrast, Spec_strong rejects all four spectests, so its completeness score is 4/4 = 1.00 . Dur-ing online RL training, the stronger candidate therefore receives a higher reward, so the policy is updated toward specifications like 

Spec_strong .

3.3.4 Step 4: Positive Seed Generation. For each method that passes compile checking, the pipeline next derives concrete correct I/O pairs. It uses an LLM to propose diverse candidate inputs that cover different execution paths, boundary cases, and cases near or outside the requires boundary. Each candidate input is wrapped in an executable Dafny harness that materializes the input, invokes the target method, and prints the output. If execution succeeds, the resulting input-output pair becomes a validated positive seed. Otherwise, the pipeline enters a reflect-and-fix loop for up to three rounds, feeding the failing harness, its error message, and a short catalog of common Dafny harness pitfalls back to the LLM. This step grounds later mutations in real program behavior rather than guessed outputs.

The harness first executes the target method so that any side effects are materialized, then overwrites the return value with a mutated value and asserts that the specification should reject this incorrect pair. This preserves the concrete program state while changing only the observed output. As in Step 4, harnesses that fail to compile enter the same reflect-and-fix loop. 

3.3.6 Step 6: Assembly and Runtime Evaluation. Step 6 assembles a self-contained Dafny test file containing the original source file, the SpecCheck predicate from Step 2, and all negative tests from Step 5. Executing this file yields the negative-test rejection rate ,the fraction of negative tests whose expect assertion passes. For a fixed suite, this rate provides a practical signal of completeness: a higher rate indicates that the specification rejects more challenging incorrect behaviors. While it is not a proof of logical completeness, it is sufficient to rank sound specifications by strength during training and evaluation.

At each enhancement iteration, it reuses the current test results together with the current specifica-tion and spectest suite, and asks an LLM to analyze which incorrect behaviors may still escape. The LLM may either conclude that the suite is already strong enough or output concrete weakness descrip-tions, such as missing boundary cases, under-constrained outputs, or uncovered branches. 3.3.8 Design Rationale: I/O Space Perspective. Figure 3 illustrates the spectest design in an I/O space , where each point is an input– output pair (𝑥, 𝑦 ). Let Pre and Post denote the conjunction of all 

requires clauses and all ensures clauses, respectively. We write 

Pre and Post for the sets of pairs satisfying these propositions, and 

GT for the set of correct I/O pairs produced by the program. Because 

SpecCheck evaluates Pre ⇒ Post , it accepts pairs in ¬Pre ∪ Post .We write SpecCheck for this accepted region. Since verification establishes soundness, GT ⊆ SpecCheck . The gap SpecCheck \ GT 

contains incorrect I/O pairs that the specification still permits. For example, ([ 5], 3) and ([ 3, 1, 2], 5) in Figure 3 lie in this gap. The goal of spectest construction is to place mutations in this gap. Step 4 first generates diverse validated seeds in GT so that they cover different program behaviors. In particular, it seeks seeds in 

GT ∩ ¬ Pre , which correspond to real behaviors outside the current 

requires boundary. Step 5 then mutates these seeds into incorrect pairs outside GT and aims to place them in Post \ GT . Together, these two steps aim to place mutations in SpecCheck : points out-side this region are trivially rejected by the specifications with the same or stronger strength than the current one, providing no fur-ther optimization signal. For a fixed test suite, a higher rejection rate means that fewer mutated points remain in the gap, so the specification appears more complete on that suite. During suite construction, however, the preference is reversed: a lower rejection rate means that more mutated points still land in the gap, making the suite more effective at exposing weaknesses. This is why the iterative enhancement procedure in Section 3.3.7 is useful. 

## 3.4 Online RL Optimization with spectest Reward 

Online RL process. With the offline artifact fixed, the online phase trains a policy over the dataset of stripped Dafny programs paired with their pre-built spectest suites. At each training step, the model selects one training program, samples a group of candidate anno-tation sets, scores them against the fixed suite for that program, 

> 5Zhechong Huang, Zhao Zhang, Zeyu Sun, Huifeng Sun, and Yingfei Xiong

and updates the policy with GRPO using group-relative advan-tages. Repeating this loop across many training programs gradually shifts the policy toward generating stronger specifications for new stripped inputs. 

Reward. Each sampled candidate is scored with a four-stage progressive reward. The first three stages provide binary soundness guidance (extraction, compilation, verification), while the fourth uses the offline spectest suite to distinguish stronger specifications from merely verifiable ones. Formally, the reward 𝑟 is 

𝑟 = 𝑟 extract + 𝑟 compile + 𝑟 verify + 𝑟 spectest (2) where each component is defined as follows. 

Stage 1: Extraction ( 𝑟 extract ). The LLM output is parsed to extract a valid Dafny file with specification annotations. If extraction suc-ceeds, 𝑟 extract = 0.05 ; otherwise, 𝑟 extract = 0 and all subsequent stages are skipped. 

Stage 2: Compilation ( 𝑟 compile ). The extracted file is compiled to ensure syntactic well-formedness and type-correctness of the specification. If compilation succeeds, 𝑟 compile = 0.15 ; otherwise, 

𝑟 compile = 0 and later stages are skipped. 

Stage 3: Verification ( 𝑟 verify ). The method is submitted to the Dafny verifier to check that the specification is provably consistent with the implementation. If verification succeeds, 𝑟 verify = 0.30 ;otherwise, 𝑟 verify = 0 and Stage 4 is skipped. 

Stage 4: spectest reward ( 𝑟 spectest ). This stage supplies the fine-grained completeness signal from the spectest pipeline (Section 3.3). We gate it on successful verification so that completeness is evalu-ated only among candidates that are already sound. Given a candi-date that passes verification, we construct a new SpecCheck predi-cate from it and run the pre-built offline negative test suite: 

𝑟 spectest = 0.50 × |{ 𝑡 ∈ T neg | 𝑡 is rejected }| |T neg | (3) where Tneg is the negative test suite constructed offline. This stage asks only how many challenging mutated I/O pairs the candidate rejects. A specification that rejects all negative tests receives the full 0.50 reward, while a weak specification that rejects few receives a score close to 0. In practice, this rejection rate lets us rank sound candidates by how well they reject challenging incorrect behaviors. The total reward ranges from 0 (unparseable output) to 1.0 (a veri-fied specification that rejects all negative tests). The first three stages provide coarse soundness guidance early in training; Stage 4 then separates weak specifications from strong ones among formally sound candidates using the offline suite built once in Section 3.3. 

Output model. The final output of the online phase is an up-dated policy rather than a single specification. At inference time, this policy takes a stripped Dafny program as input and generates candidate specifications and auxiliary annotations for that program. 

Reward shaping safeguards. To prevent reward hacking, we apply two kinds of safeguards. First, we enforce task preservation : candi-dates receive 𝑟 = 0 if they contain assume statements or modify the original signature or implementation, since such changes bypass the intended verification task rather than solving it. Second, we penalize trivial specifications : candidates that emit vacuous annota-tions such as ensures true , ensures x == x , or requires false 

also receive 𝑟 = 0. Without this second guard, the policy quickly collapses to these degenerate outputs. Together, these safeguards keep the reward aligned with genuine specification quality. 

## 4 Evaluation 

We evaluate SpecRL to answer the following research questions: 

• RQ1 (Effectiveness) : Does the spectest reward lead to consistent gains over baselines across model scales? 

• RQ2 (Ablation) : How does each reward component con-tribute to the overall performance? 

• RQ3 (Competitiveness) : Can small RL-trained models compete with much larger general-purpose LLMs? 

## 4.1 Experimental Setup 

Task. We follow the problem setting in Section 3.1. The model receives a Dafny program with verification annotations removed and may only add annotations and ghost code, leaving the original method signatures and implementation bodies unchanged. 

Benchmarks. We apply the SpecTest generation pipeline from Section 3.3 to two existing Dafny datasets, Py2Dfy and Dafny-Comp [ 51 ], and construct two specification-generation benchmarks: 

Py2Dfy-Spec and DafnyComp-Spec . Each original example con-tains a verified Dafny program with verification annotations, together with its annotation-stripped counterpart used as the model input. We run the full pipeline in Section 3.3 offline on both datasets, producing a fixed SpecTest suite for each program. We keep only programs for which the target method, an executable 

SpecCheck predicate, and the corresponding offline SpecTest suite can all be constructed reliably, so that the resulting benchmarks are fully aligned with our training and evaluation protocol. We use DeepSeek-V3 [ 10 ] for all LLM-based stages except iterative test enhancement, for which we use GPT-5.1 [38]. We randomly split Py2Dfy-Spec at the program level into 4,396 training programs and 274 validation programs. DafnyComp-Spec contains only 232 programs, so we use it entirely as the out-of-distribution test set. 

Models and baselines. We use four Qwen2.5 [ 52 ] backbones: 0.5B, 1.5B, 3B, and 7B. For each backbone, we initialize from the SFT checkpoint released by ReForm, which is already adapted to Dafny syntax and semantics. We then train two RL variants on the Py2Dfy-Spec training split: 

• SpecRL : RL with the spectest reward proposed in this work, which provides a fine-grained signal based on the negative-test rejection rate. 

• ReForm : for a controlled comparison, we keep the ex-traction, compilation, and verification stages identical to SpecRL and replace only Stage 4. The new Stage 4 uses a ReForm-style binary reward based on the reference spec-ification. The reward is granted only when the generated specification is provably no weaker than the ground truth, as checked by verifier implication queries over the gener-ated and reference preconditions and postconditions. The SFT checkpoint, without any RL, serves as a baseline. 

> 6Reinforcement Learning with Negative Tests as Completeness Signal for Formal Specification Synthesis

Training and inference. For RL, we use GRPO [ 40 ] implemented in veRL [ 41 ]. We follow ReForm for the learning rate and sampling temperature, adopt standard veRL/GRPO settings for the remain-ing optimizer hyperparameters, and keep the same setting across methods for fair comparison. Concretely, we use an actor learning rate of 1 × 10 −5, temperature 1.0, group size 𝑛 =8, KL coefficient 

0.001 , and PPO clip ratio 0.2, and train for 20 epochs. Batch size is adjusted only by model scale to fit GPU memory: for 0.5B and 1.5B, it is 512 (mini-batch 128); for 3B and 7B, it is 128 (mini-batch 32). The progressive reward follows the four-stage design in Sec-tion 3.4. At evaluation time, we sample 8 candidates per problem (temperature 1.0, max 4,096 tokens) without beam search or other pruning. Both pass@1 and pass@8 are computed from this same 8-sample set. pass@1 is estimated as the average single-sample suc-cess rate over these 8 candidates, and pass@8 is the corresponding success rate over the full set. For RL methods, we save one check-point every 10 training steps and select the checkpoint with the highest average progressive reward on the Py2Dfy-Spec validation split. DafnyComp-Spec is used only for final evaluation. 

Metrics. We report four metrics (%, higher is better), all com-puted over the full 232 programs: Compilable (compiles success-fully), Verifiable (passes verification with 0 errors), Complete-ness (empirical; fraction of spectests correctly rejected), and Spec-Superiority (fraction of generated specifications proved no weaker than the ground-truth specification under the same implication-based criterion used by ReForm). Specifications that fail to compile receive a score of 0 for all other metrics. Completeness is evaluated on all compiling specifications regardless of verification, decoupling soundness and completeness at evaluation time. This differs from training, where the spectest reward is gated on successful verifica-tion (Section 3.4). The goal in evaluation is to measure empirical specification strength separately from formal soundness. 

## 4.2 Results and Analysis 

We evaluate all methods on DafnyComp-Spec, which is out of distribution with respect to RL training and is also not used for checkpoint selection. The main comparison covers the initial SFT model, and the checkpoints trained with different RL methods (ReForm and SpecRL) across four Qwen2.5 backbones. 

4.2.1 RQ1: SpecRL vs. Baselines. RQ1 compares SFT, ReForm, and SpecRL across four Qwen2.5 backbones on DafnyComp-Spec under both pass@1 and pass@8 evaluation protocols (Table 1). We focus on the two main outcome metrics, Completeness and Verifiable, and use Compilable and Spec-Superiority as auxiliary evidence. 

Completeness. SpecRL gives the largest Completeness gains at every model size, and the advantage widens as the backbone grows. Relative to SFT, SpecRL’s pass@1 gain increases from +2.26 at 0.5B to +6.15 at 7B, and pass@8 follows the same pattern (+1.42 to +5.65). ReForm’s Completeness gains are much smaller and even turn negative at 7B ( −0.57 pass@1, −1.37 pass@8). This widening gap suggests that the fine-grained spectest reward becomes increasingly useful as the backbone has more capacity to exploit the richer signal, whereas ReForm’s reference-based binary reward quickly saturates, making it less effective at improving Completeness. 

Verifiable. Both RL methods outperform SFT, but SpecRL im-proves Verifiable more consistently than ReForm. At 7B, SpecRL adds +4.96 pass@1 vs. ReForm’s +2.37. This is because Dafny verifi-cation is compositional: a caller is verified against the specifications of its callees. If a callee’s specification is too weak, the caller may still fail verification regardless of the implementation. SpecRL im-proves specification completeness, which in turn provides stronger contracts for downstream reasoning and makes full-program verifi-cation easier. This explains why a reward aimed at completeness also leads to better Verifiable performance, rather than merely over-fitting to the offline SpecTest suite. 

Auxiliary evidence from Compilable and Spec-Superiority. Com-pilable differs little between the two RL methods (within 1 point at every size), although both are substantially stronger than SFT (∼10–17 points). As a third-party auxiliary view, we also report Spec-Superiority, computed using the same implication-based crite-rion used by ReForm. SpecRL remains at least as strong as ReForm under this external metric across model sizes. Together, these aux-iliary metrics suggest that SpecRL’s gains are not simply caused by copying biases from the source specifications into the reward: they remain visible under ReForm’s independent implication-based metric as well. 

Scaling trend and generalization. The monotonic widening of the Completeness gap from 0.5B to 7B shows that task-specific RL with a fine-grained reward scales better with model capacity than RL with a reference-based binary reward. Since DafnyComp-Spec is unseen during RL training and checkpoint selection, this trend also indicates cross-benchmark generalization. 

Finding 1 (RQ1) 

SpecRL’s main advantage is larger Completeness gains, which widens with model size. Its consistent improvements on Veri-fiability and auxiliary metrics suggest that the gains are not merely inherited from source-specification bias but are gen-uinely caused by improving specification completeness.

## 4.3 Case Study 

We use findItem to illustrate how reward shape affects the learned specification. It searches head.items for a non-null element whose 

name equals searchName , and returns the result in found . Our pipeline generates 8 distinct negative tests (2 false-positives, 6 false-negatives; 3 false-negatives place the match at position 0, 3 place it later). We compare the ground-truth (GT) specification with those produced by ReForm and SpecRL. The GT postcondition is:           

> ensures found =⇒exists i:: 0≤i < |head.items| && head.items[i] ≠null && head.items[i].name == searchName

This is a one-way implication. It constrains only the case found == true and says nothing about found == false . As a result, it rejects the 2 false-positive mutations but misses all 6 false-negative mutations, for a score of 2/8 .ReForm adds a reverse implication, but only for the first element:                

> ensures found =⇒exists i:: 0≤i < |head.items| && head.items[i] ≠null && head.items[i].name == searchName
> ensures head.items[0] ≠null && head.items[0].name == searchName =⇒found

This specification is already provably stronger than GT, so the binary reward gives full credit and provides no further training signal. It catches the 3 false-negative mutations where the match is at position 0, but still misses the 3 where the match appears later, for a score of 5/8 .Unlike ReForm, SpecRL receives partial credit in proportion to the number of rejected tests. The reward therefore keeps increas-ing as the specification becomes stronger. The model continues to explore until it discovers the full biconditional:           

> ensures found ≤=> exists i:: 0≤i < |head.items| && head.items[i] ≠null && head.items[i].name == searchName

This covers all indices in both directions, rejecting all 8 tests and yielding 8/8 .

## 4.4 Threats to Validity 

Construct validity. Completeness is measured by spectest rejec-tion rates, which are an empirical proxy rather than a formal proof of logical completeness. This proxy may miss gaps requiring highly structured inputs or richer semantic reasoning. Even so, experi-ments have shown that training on our spectest-based completeness reward can improve both verifiability and third-party specification strength metrics. 

External validity. Our study focuses on Dafny and two bench-marks derived from existing Dafny datasets, so the results may not transfer unchanged to other verification languages or specification styles. We partially mitigate this threat by reporting results on DafnyComp-Spec, which is out of distribution with respect to RL training and checkpoint selection. 

## 5 Related Work 5.1 LLM for Formal Verification 

Formal verification aims to prove that a program satisfies desired properties. It requires two kinds of artifacts: a specification that states the intended behavior, and proofs or verification annota-tions that show the implementation satisfies it. Most LLM-based work targets the downstream proving stage, where the specifica-tion is assumed to be available and the model generates proofs or auxiliary annotations to discharge the resulting proof obligations. Dafny is a widely used auto-active verification language that inte-grates specifications, code, and proofs in a single program, making it a popular target for LLM-based verification research. Misu et al. [ 31 ] introduce DafnyBench as a benchmark for Dafny verifica-tion tasks. Laurel [ 36 ] generates helper assertions using retrieval-augmented prompting, DafnyPro [ 4 ] proposes an inference-time framework with diff-checking, pruning, and hint augmentation for proof annotation generation, and Chakraborty et al. [ 7] rank LLM-generated loop invariants to improve verification success. Baksys et al. [ 3 ] translate the miniF2F mathematical benchmark into Dafny’s auto-active format and evaluate LLM-based theorem proving, and Proof2Silicon [ 22 ] targets verified Dafny code and hardware gen-eration. Beyond Dafny, AutoVerus [ 53 ] uses iterative LLM-verifier feedback for proof generation in Verus/Rust, and Chakraborty et al. [ 6] study proof-oriented programming in F*. LLM-based theorem proving follows the same paradigm: the specification is a theorem statement and the task is to generate a proof. Baldur [ 15 ] gener-ates whole proofs for Isabelle/HOL, LeanDojo [ 54 ] and Copra [ 44 ]build environments and agents for Lean/Coq, and DeepSeek-Prover-V1.5 [ 50 ] combines RL with Monte-Carlo tree search for Lean. These approaches all assume a fixed formal target, such as a theorem state-ment, an existing specification, or proof obligations derived from it. Because a proof either checks or it does not, proof generation admits a natural binary signal. A related but harder problem is generating specifications to-gether with proofs. LLMs have been applied to this task in several verification frameworks. AutoSpec [ 48 ] combines LLMs with static analysis to generate specifications for C programs, SAFE [ 8] synthe-sizes Verus requires/ensures clauses and proofs from existing Rust code through self-evolving SFT, AlphaVerus [ 1 ] translates Python programs into formally verified Verus code, and VeRuSyn [ 12 ] scales up SAFE’s data-synthesis pipeline to 6.9 million verified Rust pro-grams. Starting from natural language, Clover [ 42 ] simultaneously generates code, annotations, and docstrings with consistency check-ing, and Mirchev et al. [ 35 ] co-evolve code, specifications, and tests 

> 9Zhechong Huang, Zhao Zhang, Zeyu Sun, Huifeng Sun, and Yingfei Xiong

from natural language descriptions. In most of these systems, spec-ifications are a by-product of code or verified-artifact generation, with no dedicated mechanism to evaluate or optimize specification completeness. The closest prior work is ReForm [ 51 ], which shares our setting and contributes two datasets that we reuse. ReForm uses a reference-based binary reward that gives credit only when a candidate specification is proved logically stronger than a refer-ence specification. We instead use a fine-grained reward based on spectest completeness. 

## 5.2 Specification Testing and Validation 

Our spectest reward rests on the premise that a specification can and should be tested for quality beyond mere verifiability. This idea has a long history in formal methods. Kemmerer [ 24 ] argues that specifications themselves can contain design errors and should be systematically tested before implementation begins. McCluskey et al. [ 32 ] survey validation techniques that help stakeholders confirm alignment between formal specifications and informal expectations. Liu [ 30 ] proposes specification animation, executing a specification on test inputs to reveal missing cases and gaps. On the diagnostic side, Könighofer et al. [ 25 ] compute simplified counterstrategies to locate problematic clauses in unrealizable specifications, and Jard and Bochmann [ 21 ] check consistency between a refined specifica-tion and an abstract reference. More recently, this line of thinking has been applied to LLM-generated specifications. Lahiri [ 26 ] proposes symbolic testing to assess both soundness and completeness of specifications produced by language models, a methodology later adopted by SAFE [ 8]. MPSC [ 20 ] shows that multi-perspective self-consistency among code, specifications, and tests improves code selection, and PBT-LLM [ 18 ] demonstrates that property-based testing provides stronger validation signals than input-output tests. Our work shares the view that verification alone is insufficient for judging specifi-cation completeness, but differs in that we automatically generate spectests from runtime-checkable predicates and feed the resulting completeness signal back into RL training as a fine-grained reward. 

## 5.3 RL for Code Generation 

Reinforcement learning has been applied to code-related tasks with verifier or execution feedback as reward signals. CodeRL [ 28 ] trains code generation models with unit test feedback via actor-critic RL. StepCoder [ 13 ] decomposes code generation into a curriculum of subtasks with compiler feedback. RLEF [ 16 ] teaches models to use execution feedback for multi-turn code synthesis. RLCoder [ 45 ]uses RL to train a retriever for repository-level code completion. SWE-RL [ 47 ] trains on open-source software evolution data for software engineering tasks. These works all target code generation, whereas we address formal verification by generating verification annotations for existing code. On reward design, we adopt GRPO [ 40 ] as our RL algorithm, which removes the critic network through group relative advan-tage estimation. Posterior-GRPO [ 14 ] extends GRPO by rewarding reasoning processes in code generation; our work is complemen-tary in that we design a domain-specific reward for specification completeness rather than reasoning process. The reward shaping theory of Ng et al. [ 37 ] provides the classic foundation for designing such domain-specific rewards without changing optimal policies, which motivates our spectest reward design. CURE [ 46 ] co-evolves code generators and unit testers via RL, with each providing re-ward signals for the other; we similarly use tests as reward signals, but our tests evaluate specification completeness rather than code correctness. 

## 6 Conclusion 

We presented SpecRL, a reinforcement learning framework for syn-thesizing specifications and auxiliary verification annotations for existing Dafny programs. The central challenge is that verifica-tion alone is insufficient, since a specification may verify yet still be too weak to capture the intended behavior. SpecRL addresses this gap with an offline spectest construction pipeline that builds reusable completeness signals and a progressive reward that mea-sures completeness from negative-test rejection rates. On the out-of-distribution DafnyComp-Spec benchmark, this design consistently improves both specification strength and verification success over SFT and RL with a reference-based binary reward across four model scales. Looking ahead, an important next step is to extend the spectest construction pipeline beyond methods with compilable postcon-ditions, including cases involving ghost functions or unbounded quantifiers. Future work also includes transferring the framework to other auto-active verification languages such as Verus and Frama-C, and exploring spectests as a standalone metric for specification quality.
