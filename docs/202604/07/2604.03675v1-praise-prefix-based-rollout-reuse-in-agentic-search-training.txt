Abstract 

In agentic search, large language models (LLMs) are trained to perform multi-turn re-trieval and reasoning for complex tasks such as multi-hop question answering (QA). However, current search-based Reinforcement Learning (RL) methods suffer from two core limita-tions: expensive long-horizon rollouts are under-utilized during training, and supervision is typically available only at the final answer, resulting in severe reward sparsity. We present 

Prefix-based Rollout reuse for Agentic search with Intermediate Step r Ewards (PRAISE), a framework for improving both data efficiency and credit assignment in agentic search training. Given a complete search trajectory, PRAISE extracts prefix states at different search turns, elicits intermediate answers from them, and uses these prefixes both to construct additional training trajectories and to derive step-level re-wards from performance differences across pre-fixes. Our method uses a single shared model for both search policy learning and prefix an-swer evaluation, enabling joint optimization without extra human annotations or a separate reward model. Experiments on multi-hop QA benchmarks show that PRAISE consistently improves performance over strong baselines. 

1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly moving beyond one-shot generation toward agentic 

problem solving, where a model interacts with ex-ternal tools and environments over multiple steps to reason, plan, and act (Yao et al., 2022; Schick et al., 2023). Among these emerging paradigms, 

agentic search has become a particularly important framework for knowledge-intensive tasks such as multi-hop question answering (QA), deep research, and open-domain reasoning (Li et al., 2025; Jin et al., 2025; Song et al., 2025). Instead of relying

on a fixed retrieval stage as in standard retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020), agentic search enables a model to dynamically de-cide when to search, what to search for, and how 

to update its reasoning based on newly acquired evidence. This adaptive interaction with external information is crucial for solving problems whose answers cannot be obtained from parametric mem-ory alone. To improve search and reasoning in such sys-tems, recent work increasingly applies reinforce-ment learning (RL) to agentic search. In this setting, the model executes a multi-turn search trajectory and is trained based on the quality of its final an-swer or overall task performance. A particularly important paradigm is reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR) (Lambert et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2025), where supervision comes from automatically checkable signals. By directly opti-mizing downstream task success, RLVR provides a natural training framework for agentic search. However, current search-based RL methods still face important limitations. A central issue is that supervision in search-based RL is typically available only at the trajec-tory level. A single rollout often contains multi-ple rounds of reasoning, querying, reading, and search-state updates, making it substantially more costly than ordinary single-pass generation. Yet, in most existing approaches, the full trajectory is still treated as a single training instance, with learn-ing signals derived primarily from the final answer. This leads to poor data efficiency (Liu et al., 2025; Huang and Wan): although a rollout contains multi-ple informative intermediate search states, existing methods typically consume it as a single training instance rather than converting those intermediate states into additional learnable examples, limiting the value extracted from each expensive rollout. At the same time, it also results in reward sparsity :because supervision arrives only at the end of the 1

trajectory, it becomes difficult to determine which intermediate search actions improved answer qual-ity and which were redundant or harmful (Zheng et al., 2025; Cui et al., 2025). This combination of under-utilized intermediate information and de-layed feedback makes long-horizon credit assign-ment especially challenging. Prior work has attempted to address these issues from two largely separate directions. One line of research improves sample efficiency through replay buffers (Sun et al., 2025), trajectory reuse (Wang et al., 2025b), or off-policy updates (Chen et al., 2025a). Another line introduces denser supervi-sion via process rewards (Cui et al., 2025), teacher-based evaluation (Xie et al., 2026), or intermedi-ate annotations (Zhang et al., 2025; Zheng et al., 2025). However, these approaches remain lim-ited in the agentic search setting. Existing reuse methods are typically centered around whole tra-jectories rather than the inherently compositional structure of multi-turn search. Meanwhile, process-supervision methods often rely on additional ingre-dients such as gold sub-questions, annotated evi-dence, separately trained reward models, or frozen teacher models, all of which introduce extra cost and may create mismatch between the evaluator and the evolving search policy. In this paper, we ask a simple but important ques-tion: how can we maximize the training value of each expensive search rollout? To answer this ques-tion, we propose PRAISE , short for Prefix-based 

Rollout reuse for Agentic search with Intermediate 

Step r Ewards. The key idea is to exploit the natu-ral prefix structure of search trajectories. Given a complete multi-turn rollout, we extract prefix states at different search turns and prompt the model to produce intermediate answers conditioned only on the information available up to each prefix. These prefix–answer pairs are then used in two comple-mentary ways. First, they provide rollout reuse . PRAISE con-verts a full rollout into multiple prefix-based train-ing samples. In this way, intermediate search states that would otherwise be ignored are transformed into additional supervision, substantially increas-ing the amount of learnable signal extracted from each rollout. Second, they provide intermediate step rewards . By evaluating the quality of an-swers produced from different prefixes and com-paring performance across adjacent prefixes, we estimate the marginal contribution of each search step. This converts sparse terminal supervision into fine-grained process rewards aligned with the search trajectory itself. Such reward densification leads to more informative training signals and im-proves long-horizon credit assignment. A central design choice of PRAISE is that the same model is used both for search policy learn-ing and for prefix answer evaluation. This shared-model formulation avoids the need for an additional reward model, thereby reducing system complexity and annotation cost. More importantly, it allows search policy improvement and prefix evaluation capability to co-evolve through joint optimization, which in turn improves the consistency between the acting policy and the source of intermediate supervision (Chen et al., 2026b,a). We evaluate PRAISE on multi-hop QA bench-marks. Experimental results show that our method consistently outperforms strong search-based RL baselines. Further analysis shows that the method is robust to key hyperparameters and that joint op-timization with a shared model performs substan-tially better than alternatives based on frozen eval-uators. Our contributions are summarized as follows: • We propose a prefix-based rollout reuse frame-work, transforming each expensive multi-turn search trajectory into multiple learnable prefix samples and improves rollout utilization. • We introduce a process reward construction method based on adjacent prefix gains to con-vert sparse terminal signals into fine-grained, step-aligned rewards. • We develop a joint optimization framework in which a single shared model performs both search decision making and prefix evaluation. • We demonstrate the effectiveness of PRAISE on multi-hop QA benchmarks, showing con-sistent improvements over strong baselines. 

2 Related Work 

Agentic Search and RL-based Training. Re-cent advancements have focused on training LLMs as agentic search systems that interleave multi-step reasoning with external retrieval. Leveraging RL frameworks such as PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) or GRPO (Shao et al., 2024), representative agen-tic search methods such as Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025) and R1-Searcher (Song et al., 2025) have 2demonstrated that such RL-based paradigms sig-nificantly bolster performance in complex search tasks. Parallel to these end-to-end agents, extensive research has investigated the optimization of spe-cific search components, including planning (Chen et al., 2025c), query reformulation (Ma et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2025b), document selection (Ke et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024a), and answer generation (Yang et al., 2025). More re-cently, there is a growing emphasis on the joint op-timization of these modular components to achieve superior global performance (Chen et al., 2026b,a). 

Data Efficiency and Rollout Reuse. Improv-ing data efficiency remains a critical challenge in RL training for LLMs. One common strategy is data selection, for example by prioritizing ques-tions of moderate difficulty for the current policy, which has been shown to be especially useful for GRPO-style training (Yu et al., 2025; Sun et al., 2025). Another strategy is trajectory reuse, where recent rollouts are reused across multiple optimiza-tion steps through replay buffers or relaxed on-policy updates (Sun et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025a; Wang et al., 2025b). More recently, several works study prefix reuse to reduce redundant generation. PROS (Huang and Wan) reuses valuable interme-diate prefixes to form augmented training inputs, while SPEC-RL (Liu et al., 2025) treats previous rollouts as speculative drafts and resumes genera-tion from the first unverifiable position. However, these methods are developed mainly for math or code reasoning, where “prefixes” are defined over token sequences rather than explicit search turns. 

Process Supervision and Intermediate Rewards. 

To address the reward sparsity in long-horizon rea-soning, recent research has introduced denser su-pervision signals (Lightman et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2025a). StepSearch (Zheng et al., 2025) constructs step-wise rewards using reference sub-questions, keywords, and documents, while Rea-sonRAG (Zhang et al., 2025) estimates interme-diate utility by rolling out from partial states and favoring shorter successful reasoning paths. TIPS (Xie et al., 2026) and IGPO (Wang et al., 2025a) define turn-level rewards through changes in answer likelihood under a teacher model or the policy model itself. More broadly, PRIME (Cui et al., 2025) shows that dense process rewards can be learned online from outcome supervision alone, though its main focus is mathematical and coding reasoning. Unlike these, our PRAISE derives re-wards directly from performance gains between adjacent search prefixes, eliminating the need for gold-standard annotations or external evaluators.

4 Experiments 

We conduct experiments to answer the following research questions: 

RQ1: Does PRAISE outperform existing base-lines? 

RQ2: How effective is joint optimization with a shared prefix evaluator? 

RQ3: What is the contribution of process rewards to the overall performance of PRAISE? 

RQ4: Is PRAISE robust to different choices of the process-reward weight α, and what value works best? 

4.1 Experimental Settings Datasets. We train PRAISE on HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018) and 2WikiMultihopQA (Ho et al., 2020), and evaluate on NQ (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019), HotpotQA, 2WikiMultihopQA, Bam-boogle (Press et al., 2022), and MuSiQue (Trivedi et al., 2022). 

Baselines. We compare against four categories of baselines: (1) non-agentic methods, including direct LLM answering and naive RAG (Lewis et al., 2020); (2) agentic search methods, includ-ing Search-o1 (Li et al., 2025), Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025), and R1-Searcher (Song et al., 2025); (3) process-supervision methods, includ-ing StepSearch (Zheng et al., 2025), Reason-RAG (Zhang et al., 2025), and TIPS (Xie et al., 2026). 

Implementation Details. We use Qwen2.5-7B (Team, 2024) as the base model, verl 1 as the training framework, E5 (Wang et al., 2022) as the retriever, and Wikipedia as the retrieval corpus. We report EM and F1 as the evaluation metrics. The process-reward weight α is set to 0.5.

4.2 Main Results Analysis (RQ1) 

Table 1 shows that PRAISE achieves the best results on all five benchmarks in both F1 and EM, consistently outperforming prior baselines from agentic search, rollout reuse, and process-supervision categories. This gives a clear positive answer to RQ1 .The gains over strong agentic search baselines such as R1-Searcher are substantial, especially on HotpotQA and 2Wiki, suggesting that PRAISE is particularly effective for multi-hop reasoning tasks that require sustained evidence accumulation across search turns. PRAISE also clearly outper-forms PPO baselines with repeated updates on the same rollout data, indicating that its benefit comes not simply from more reuse, but from more ef-fective reuse of informative prefixes. In addition, PRAISE surpasses process-supervision methods such as StepSearch, ReasonRAG, and TIPS, show-ing that reward assignment based on adjacent prefix gains provides a stronger training signal.

5 Conclusion 

We presented PRAISE, a prefix-based rollout reuse framework for agentic search training. By convert-ing intermediate prefixes into additional training samples and deriving step-level rewards from ad-jacent prefix gains, PRAISE improves both rollout utilization and credit assignment. Built on a shared-model design, it jointly optimizes search and prefix evaluation within a unified PPO framework, with-out requiring extra annotations or a separate reward model. Experiments on multi-hop QA benchmarks demonstrate consistent gains over strong baselines.

A Appendix 

A.1 Discussion Why PPO instead of GRPO? We adopt PPO rather than GRPO for this setting. GRPO is natu-10 rally designed for comparing a group of responses sampled from the same state. In multi-turn search, however, extending this paradigm would require grouped sampling at each turn and each interme-diate state, leading to rapidly increasing rollout cost with the horizon. Although recent work such as IGPO extends a GRPO-style idea to multi-turn agents (Wang et al., 2025a), turns from different rollouts generally correspond to different states, making strict same-state grouping difficult to pre-serve. PPO is therefore a more natural choice for optimizing trajectories with token-level or turn-level rewards in our setting. 

Why not use likelihood-based prefix scoring? 

PRAISE also differs from likelihood-based prefix scoring methods such as TIPS (Xie et al., 2026) and IGPO (Wang et al., 2025a). Those methods esti-mate intermediate utility using the log-likelihood of the golden answer conditioned on a prefix. While this can serve as a zero-shot evaluation signal, it is less suitable for joint training. Once optimized directly, the objective becomes close to supervised fine-tuning on the reference answer, encouraging answer memorization rather than measuring the in-cremental value of newly retrieved evidence. By instead generating rollout-based prefix answers and scoring them with task reward, PRAISE preserves the meaning of process reward as the marginal gain brought by each search step. 11
