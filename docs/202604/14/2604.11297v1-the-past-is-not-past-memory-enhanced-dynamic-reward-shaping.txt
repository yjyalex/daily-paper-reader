## Abstract 

Despite the success of reinforcement learning for large language models, a common failure mode is reduced sampling diversity, where the policy repeatedly generates similar erroneous behaviors. Classical entropy regularization encourages randomness under the current policy, but does not explicitly discourage recurrent failure patterns across rollouts. We propose MEDS , a Memory-

Enhanced Dynamic reward Shaping framework that incorporates historical behavioral signals into reward design. By storing and leveraging intermediate model representations, we capture features of past rollouts and use density-based clustering to identify frequently recurring error patterns. Rollouts assigned to more prevalent error clusters are penalized more heavily, encouraging broader exploration while reducing repeated mistakes. Across five datasets and three base models, MEDS consistently improves average performance over existing baselines, achieving gains of up to 4.13 pass@1 points and 4.37 pass@128 points. Additional analyses using both LLM-based annotations and quantitative diversity metrics show that MEDS increases behavioral diversity during sampling.

## 1 Introduction 

With the advancement of the fundamental capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs), reinforcement learning has achieved significant success across various domains [ 1– 3]. By incorporating reward signals, whether derived from rule-based evaluation [ 1] or proxy models [ 4], LLMs iteratively alternate between a sampling phase and a gradient-based optimization phase. As the model’s performance is optimized toward maximizing the expected return, designing the reward scoring structure becomes the primary way to guide the model’s behavior [5]. A well-known failure mode of such on-policy optimization is that, as training progresses, the policy often collapse into a narrow and stereotyped set of behaviors [6–8]. This degeneration produces highly repetitive responses that not only weaken effective exploration and waste on-policy samples, but also entrench the model in self-reinforcing erroneous reasoning trajectories[ 9 ]. A series of works [ 10 – 12 ] introduce entropy regularization to promote output diversity by injecting randomness at the level of the current policy distribution. However, in LLM reasoning, such distribution-level stochastic exploration is often insufficient

Inspired by the dynamic reward mechanisms observed in human learning [ 13 ], where individuals impose stronger psychological penalties on recurring mistakes to reinforce avoidance, we propose a Memory-

Enhanced Dynamic reward Shaping framework ( MEDS ). Unlike static reward functions, our method dynamically records historical error patterns and imposes incremental penalties on repetitive failure paths. This mechanism not only facilitates the correction of persistent errors but also encourages the model to escape local optima, thereby enhancing exploration in complex solution spaces. We further provide theoretical analysis showing that additional penalties on repeated errors can improve performance. In practice, by reusing the layer-wise logits already produced during the forward pass, we construct feature vectors of the model’s implicit reasoning trajectory. This reuse introduces almost no additional computational overhead and serves as a lightweight yet effective representation. We then maintain an error memory that is updated online throughout training. By incorporating HDBSCAN-based clustering [ 14 ], this memory adaptively captures error types and their density structure, enabling the training objective to adjust penalty strength according to the recurrence of failure patterns. Empirical results across multiple benchmarks show that our method improves both pass@1 and pass@128 across all three models, with gains of up to 4.13 points in pass@1 and 4.37 points in pass@128. The largest pass@128 improvement is observed on Qwen3-8B, where performance increases from 70.81 to 82.67 on a single dataset, corresponding to a relative gain of 17%. We also analyze changes in the model’s exploration behavior, demonstrating that our method increases the diversity of exploration. In addition, we calculate the consistency between logit-based clustering and LLM-based clustering, showing that logits can serve as a proxy for reasoning structure. The positive correlation between clustering quality and downstream model performance further validates the effectiveness of our approach. Our main contributions are summarized as follows: 1. We propose MEDS , a novel memory-enhanced dynamic reward shaping framework for LLM reinforce-ment learning. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to explicitly incorporate historical error patterns into reward modeling, enabling the policy to recognize and avoid recurrent failure behaviors rather than merely increasing randomness under the current policy. This design provides more informative supervision during training and effectively mitigates repeated errors. 2. We provide theoretical analysis supporting the effectiveness of memory-based dynamic penalties in improving learning and mitigating recurrent failure modes. 23. We introduce a representation-level criterion for response similarity that leverages layer-wise logits as a compact and efficient proxy, with little additional computational overhead. 4. We conduct extensive experiments across multiple datasets and models, showing that MEDS consistently achieves the best overall performance and substantially improves both pass@1 and pass@128. Further analyses show that our method increases exploration diversity, that logit-based clustering aligns well with LLM-based semantic clustering, and that better recognition of repeated error patterns is strongly correlated with downstream performance gains. 

## 2 Related Work 

2.1 Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards 

The application of reinforcement learning in LLMs relies heavily on the design of reward signals. And Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) has become the dominant paradigm due to the simplicity of its reward signals. Representative works adopt diverse verifiable criteria across tasks. OpenAI [15] and Shao et al. [1] assign binary rewards based on exact answer matching. Zhu et al. [16] automatically verifies correctness by executing generated programs in a sandbox. Zhang et al. [17] uses both temporal localization quality and multiple-choice Video QA accuracy as reward signals. Chen et al. [18] employs a multimodal large language model (MLLM) to evaluate similarity between generated and target images. Zhang et al. [19] adopts metrics such as nDCG@10 as the primary reward. In all these works, the verifier operates independently within each epoch and assigns rewards based solely on current samples, overlooking useful information in historical trajectories. Our approach instead leverages trajectories from earlier stages to guide later sampling. 

2.2 Reward Shaping for Diversity Exploration 

During RLVR training, policy updates tend to over-optimize dominant solution patterns, leading to collapse of reasoning diversity [ 20 , 21 ]. Traditional approaches mitigate this by constraining policy shift via KL regularization [4, 22] or encouraging randomness via entropy regularization [10]. Beyond implicit regularization, several works explicitly reward rare reasoning behaviors. Hu et al. [23] clusters rollouts by solution strategy and inversely weights advantages by cluster size. Wan et al. [11] promotes diversity among correct trajectories with token-level entropy regularization. However, these methods operate only on current-batch differences and do not track solution patterns over long-term training history. To leverage historical information, Song et al. [24] introduces UCB-style exploration rewards based on historical outcome frequencies. Yet it still lacks fine-grained cross-step reasoning behavior tracking. Another line of work [ 25 –29 ] uses external models to adaptively adjust rewards based on historical performance, but the additional computational cost limits scalability. In contrast, our method captures reasoning patterns via layer-wise logits, enabling history-aware reward adjustment with minimal overhead. 

2.3 Inner Thoughts of LLMs 

Works that attempt to understand and utilize how models reason and arrive at their final conclusions are an important source of inspiration for our approach. Meng et al. [30] localizes factual knowledge in the model by examining logit changes. Subsequent studies [ 31 – 34 ] use Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) and related logit-intervention techniques to obtain a more fine-grained understanding of LLM reasoning processes. These efforts have further motivated the use of logits for analysis and supervision. For example, Zhao et al. [35] detects erroneous reasoning by verifying the inner attribute graph, while Tan et al. [36] leverages layer-wise logits to provide more fine-grained supervision. A key component of our work is to identify shared patterns across different reasoning trajectories. Inspired by prior work on logit-based model analysis, we cluster logits to capture such patterns without requiring extra models. 4.1 Experimental Setup 

Training Details We train on three base models: Qwen3-1.7B, Qwen3-8B, and Qwen2.5-Math-7B. Qwen3-8B and Qwen2.5-Math-7B represent models with and without explicit reasoning processes, while Qwen3-1.7B serves as a model with a different scale. The training corpus is constructed by merging the DAPO-Math-17K dataset [ 6] with the levels 3-5 of the MATH subdataset [ 38 , 39 ]. All problems are formatted using the Qwen-Math template (Appendix A.1). All reinforcement learning training is implemented based on the verl 

framework [40]. Building on DAPO, our work further introduces a clustering penalty term: for each prompt, we maintain a memory of the logits representations of incorrect samples, where we concatenate the logits from the last 14 Transformer layers and apply L2 normalization, then perform clustering in the logit space using HDBSCAN. During clustering, we set the minimum cluster size to 2, the minimum number of samples to 1, and use Euclidean distance as the distance metric. As for the penalty coefficients, we set 𝛼 = 0.1 and 𝛽 = 0.2 for Qwen3-1.7B, Qwen2.5-Math-7B, and set 𝛼 = 0.02 and 𝛽 = 0.04 for Qwen3-8B. In all training runs, we use a prompt-level batch size of 512 and generate 16 rollouts per prompt; the maximum prompt length is 1024 tokens. The maximum response length is 7168 tokens for Qwen3-1.7B, Qwen3-8B, and 3072 tokens for Qwen2.5-Math-7B. All training is conducted on H200 hardware. 4.3 Impact on Exploration Behavior 

To better understand how our method influences the model’s exploration behavior during reasoning, we conduct a detailed analysis from both behavioral and representational perspectives. Specifically, we first employ an LLM-based evaluation to measure Within-Step Diversity and Across-Step Diversity , which capture two complementary aspects of rollout diversity. We then further analyze the statistical structure of the logits to provide additional evidence of diversity at the representation level. Within-Step Diversity measures the diversity of rollouts generated by DAPO and MEDS at the same training step for the same input prompt. Since the first training epoch is mainly used for memory formation and does not involve reward shaping, we restrict this analysis to checkpoints from the second epoch, spanning approximately step 20 to step 100. At each step, we randomly sample 10 groups of 160 rollouts from each method, resulting in 1,600 rollout groups in total, and use Claude-Haiku-4.5 with the prompt in Appendix D to score group-level output diversity. Across-Step Diversity instead evaluates whether later-stage rollouts exhibit new reasoning patterns relative to earlier ones for the same model and input prompt. For this purpose, we construct 21 pairs of data groups with varying step intervals, each roughly one epoch apart, where each data group contains 10 rollout groups from DAPO and 10 from MEDS . Using the same prompt-based protocol, the LLM compares rollouts from the earlier and later steps and scores the extent to which the later outputs introduce novel reasoning patterns. In both settings, higher scores indicate greater diversity. Beyond these LLM-based evaluations, we further analyze the covariance structure of the stored logits across all steps and compute the ratio between the largest eigenvalue and the sum of all eigenvalues. We refer to this metric as the Top-1 Eigen Ratio . Its formal definition is given in Appendix E. A smaller value of this ratio indicates a more uniform spread of the logits across different directions in the representation space. 

Our method improves exploration across multiple metrics. The LLM-based evaluation results are shown in the bottom-right panel of Figure 1, and the Top-1 Eigen Ratio results are presented in Figure 5 (left). We observe that rollout diversity generally declines as training progresses. However, our method consistently attains higher exploration scores than the baseline under both Within-Step Diversity and Across-Step Diversity. 4.4 Logits can Reflect Reasoning Patterns 

To demonstrate that logit vectors capture the underlying logical reasoning structure, we first perform a qualitative analysis of the logit vectors collected from rollouts across the MEDS training process. This preliminary analysis suggests that responses with similar reasoning patterns tend to exhibit similar layer-wise logit trajectories across layers. 

Logit patterns reflect reasoning structure Figure 6 presents a case study example. The logits show relatively limited variation in the first 20 layers, where they primarily encode semantic information. In contrast, larger changes emerge in the later layers, where responses with similar reasoning patterns show clear correlations in their logit vectors, with layer-wise values tending to fall within similar ranges. Meanwhile, responses following different reasoning paths remain distinguishable. We further find that logit patterns reflect not only the final answer tokens, but also the intermediate reasoning process. Responses leading to the same answer may exhibit different logit patterns, while responses with different answers may still share similar reasoning behaviors. 

4.5 Logits Provide Correct Clustering Signals 

To further validate this observation at scale, we introduce an automated annotation procedure using Claude-Haiku-4.5 as a proxy annotator. We randomly sample 800 negative responses from the entire dataset. Based on 11 common error categories distilled through manual inspection, we first ask the proxy model to assign each response to one of these error clusters. We then sample response pairs and compare whether the two responses belong to the same cluster according to both the LLM annotations and the logit-based clustering. If the two judgments agree, i.e., both indicate that the responses belong to the same cluster or both indicate that they belong to different clusters, we regard the logit-based clustering as correct for that pair. The agreement rate is then used as the correlation metric. 4.6 Clustering Quality Matters 

Building on the rich representational signals provided by logits, clustering features can be constructed in multiple ways. We investigate how different feature construction choices affect clustering quality and how these differences further influence downstream evaluation performance. Specifically, we consider several feature construction strategies that vary in the selected layers and in whether logit differencing is applied. As a worst-case baseline, we also include a degenerate setting in which all responses are assigned to a single cluster, yielding a trivial clustering with virtually no discriminative power. All experiments use Qwen2.5-Math-7B and differ only in the choice of clustering features. Following the Claude-correlation evaluation protocol described in Section 4.4, we report the results of different logit aggregation strategies in Figure 5 (right). 

Using the last 14 layers as clustering features yields the best downstream performance. Table 2 reports the results under different feature construction settings. Among all configurations, using the last 14 layers as clustering features yields the best performance. In contrast, excluding logits altogether results in performance worse than DAPO, while every variant that leverages clustering information outperforms the DAPO baseline. This provides further evidence that logits serve as effective historical signals for reward shaping. 

Clustering quality strongly correlates with downstream performance. Figure 5 (right) shows that using the last 14 layers as clustering features achieves the highest agreement with the Claude annotations, whereas the degenerate single-cluster setting yields the lowest. More importantly, the ranking of clustering consistency closely mirrors the ranking of downstream performance, suggesting that better clustering yields larger downstream improvements. This provides further evidence for the effectiveness of our approach. 

## 5 Conclusion 

In this work, we study the recurring error patterns that emerge during reinforcement learning for LLM reasoning. We introduce MEDS , a memory-aware reward shaping framework that dynamically penalizes recurrent failure patterns. We first provide theoretical analysis demonstrating the effectiveness of our method. By reusing layer-wise logits as lightweight representations of reasoning trajectories, our approach clusters historical responses to identify and suppress repeated error modes. Empirical results across multiple benchmarks show that MEDS consistently improves both reasoning performance and exploration diversity. These findings highlight the importance of incorporating historical trajectory information into reward design and open new directions for improving exploration in LLM reinforcement learning. 10 6 Limitations 

The main limitation of our study is that the methods explored for utilizing logits are relatively simple and do not incorporate more sophisticated aggregation functions. It remains unclear whether adopting more complex aggregation strategies could further improve performance.
