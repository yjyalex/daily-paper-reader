Abstract 

Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Re-wards (RLVR) has substantially improved the reasoning ability of Large Language Models (LLMs). However, its sparse outcome-based rewards pose a fundamental credit assignment problem. We analyze this problem through the joint lens of reward polarity and token en-tropy. Our diagnostic tool, the Four Quadrant Decomposition, isolates token updates by polar-ity and entropy, and controlled ablations show that reasoning improvements concentrate in the high-entropy quadrants. To justify this observa-tion theoretically, we adapt Conditional Mutual Information to the autoregressive RLVR set-ting and prove that the credit a token can carry is upper-bounded by its entropy. This view yields testable predictions that reasoning gains arise primarily from high-entropy tokens, with unique roles for positive and negative updates. A gradient analysis of GRPO further reveals how uniform reward broadcast dilutes signal at high-entropy positions while over-crediting deterministic tokens. Grounded in these in-sights, we propose Entropy-Aware Policy Op-timization (EAPO) that modulates token-level learning signals accordingly. Extensive exper-iments demonstrate that EAPO outperforms strong baselines across two model families. 

1 Introduction 

Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) trains Large Language Models (LLMs) to reason better by optimizing against outcome re-wards from self-generated responses (Guo et al., 2025; Lambert et al., 2024; Cobbe et al., 2021; Hendrycks et al., 2021; Jimenez et al., 2024), bypassing the need for dense step-level supervi-sion (Chu et al., 2025a). In practice, though, meth-ods such as GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) broadcast a single sequence-level reward to every token in a

trajectory, giving rise to a token-level credit as-signment problem: a trajectory’s outcome may hinge on a handful of pivotal decisions, yet most to-kens are routine continuations that receive identical credit (Minsky, 1995; Lightman et al., 2024). Recent work tackles this problem along two sepa-rate dimensions. (1) Reward polarity , which redis-tributes credit by positive and negative samples, ar-guing that negative samples carry disproportionate learning signals (Yuan et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2025). 

(2) Token entropy , which differentiates tokens by their predictive uncertainty. Some reinforce high-entropy tokens to encourage exploration (Wang et al., 2025c; Cheng et al., 2025), while others prioritize low-entropy tokens as anchors of estab-lished knowledge (Wang et al., 2025b; Agarwal et al., 2025). Together, these studies confirm that not all token updates are equally useful. However, treating polarity or entropy in isolation leaves open 

why certain tokens carry more credit, how this man-ifests in gradient dynamics, and what modulation strategy should follow. Rather than introducing another heuristic, we first seek to identify where token-level credit in RLVR actually resides. To this end, we introduce the Four Quadrant Decomposition (Figure 1), a diagnostic framework that factorizes token updates along reward polarity and token entropy and enables controlled tests of where reasoning gains originate. The framework is motivated by an information-theoretic view on credit assignment (Arumugam et al., 2021). Adapt-ing Conditional Mutual Information (CMI) to the autoregressive RLVR setting, we formalize token-reward dependence and establish that the plausible credit a token can carry is upper-bounded by its en-tropy. This view yields three concrete predictions: (i) low-entropy updates produce at most modest or transient gains; (ii) sustained improvements con-centrate at high-entropy positions; and (iii) the ef-fects of high-entropy updates differ qualitatively between positive and negative trajectories.

The credit assignment problem refers to the chal-lenge of identifying which components of a se-quence are responsible for the final outcome (Min-sky, 1995). In RLVR, given a query q, the policy 

πθ generates a trajectory oi = ( oi, 1, . . . , o i,T ) and receives an outcome reward ri = r(oi, q ) only after the full response is completed. Standard policy gra-dient methods then broadcast this same trajectory-level signal to all token steps t. This naive as-signment assumes that every token in a successful trajectory is equally beneficial, and vice versa for failed trajectories. While Process Reward Mod-els (Gao et al., 2023) offer finer-grained credit as-signment, they incur high computational costs and are susceptible to reward hacking (Lightman et al., 2024). Consequently, we focus on analyzing and improving heuristic credit assignment within the GRPO framework. 

2.3 Policy Entropy 

Policy entropy quantifies the uncertainty of the model’s predictive distribution over the vocabu-lary V (Kuhn et al., 2023). Given a query q and a partial trajectory oi,<t , the token entropy is defined as the Shannon entropy (Shannon, 1948) of the out-put distribution at step t: Hi,t = −P

Conversely, low-entropy policies indicate a shift towards exploita-tion (Chen et al., 2025a), potentially leading to con-vergence on deterministic but suboptimal solutions, a phenomenon known as entropy collapse (Dohma-tob et al., 2024). In parallel, a line of work has explored entropy interventions in RLVR, such as Forking (Wang et al., 2025c) and EntroAdv (Cheng et al., 2025). While these methods reshape reward signals, the underlying token-level mechanisms of credit assignment remain underexplored. 

3 Information-Theoretic Perspective on Credit Assignment Problem 

To understand why certain tokens matter under outcome-based RLVR, we need to identify which token choices are actually responsible for the out-come. We therefore adapt the information-theoretic credit view of Arumugam et al. (2021) to the au-toregressive LLM setting and ask: how much in-formation does a token choice oi,t carry about the final reward, given the current context? 

3.1 Credit as Conditional Mutual Information 

The policy πθ generates a trajectory oi,<t in RLVR. At decoding step t the model observes the prefix-conditioned state st := (q, o i,<t ) and samples 

oi,t ∼ πθ(·| st). A natural information-theoretic notion of token credit is the CMI I(oi,t ; ri|st) be-tween the sampled token and the final reward, con-ditioned on the prior state: 

I(oi,t ; ri|st) = H(oi,t |st) − H(oi,t |ri, s t), (3) where H(oi,t |st) = Hi,t is the token entropy. Equivalently, I(oi,t ; ri|st) measures how much the token distribution shifts in hindsight after the trajec-tory outcome is revealed. Exact computation would require H(oi,t |ri, s t), which is not directly avail-able from the policy alone and is generally diffi-cult to estimate in high-dimensional autoregressive spaces (Poole et al., 2019). Using non-negativity of conditional entropy yields the following tractable upper bound : 

Proposition 1 (Credit Upper Bound) . For any to-ken position t in trajectory oi, the information con-tribution of token oi,t to the outcome reward ri,conditioned on the state st = ( q, o i,<t ), satisfies: 

I(oi,t ; ri|st) ≤ H i,t . (4) That is, Hi,t upper-bounds the reward-relevant information any token can carry , and it is computable from the policy alone. The full derivation and interpretation are in Appendix D.1. Low-entropy tokens (syntax, routine algebra, com-mon suffixes) have Hi,t ≈ 0 and thus carry negli-gible credit, whereas high-entropy positions mark genuine branching decisions. The bound is one-sided: high entropy does not guarantee high credit, but low entropy sharply limits it. 

3.2 GRPO’s Assignment Bias and Testable Predictions 

In standard GRPO, the advantage ˆAi,t is constant for all tokens within a trajectory, irrespective of their entropy. Proposition 1 reveals a mismatch between this uniform update rule and the infor-mation geometry of reasoning trajectories. As a result, it can over-allocate learning signal to low-entropy tokens, while genuinely decision-critical high-entropy positions receive no additional em-phasis. Because Proposition 1 is an upper bound rather than an exact characterization of token credit, we treat the implications below as empirical predic-tions. Still, the bound gives concrete expectations for controlled quadrant-isolated training: 

Prediction 1 (Low-entropy credit ceiling). Train-ing exclusively on low-entropy tokens delivers at most modest or short-lived gains, since they carry little reward-relevant information I(oi,t ; ri|st).

Prediction 2 (High-entropy credit concentration). 

Given sufficient information budget I(oi,t ; ri|st) at high-entropy positions, training on them should account for most of the sustained improvements in accuracy or diversity. 

Prediction 3 (Polarity–entropy interaction). Be-cause the posterior H(oi,t |ri, s t) differs between successful and failed trajectories, high-entropy to-kens should exhibit asymmetric effects across re-ward polarity rather than a uniform response. 

4 Four Quadrant Decomposition 

Section 3 explains why entropy constrains plau-sible token credit. We now examine how credit assignment manifests during optimization. Build-ing on Zhu et al. (2025), who decompose RLVR into positive and negative paradigms based on re-ward polarity, we decompose tokens along both reward polarity and token entropy, yielding a four-quadrant experimental framework. By restricting gradient updates to specific quadrants, our frame-work isolates their individual contributions. 

4.1 Decomposition Framework 

While prior work links token entropy to exploratory behaviors (Wang et al., 2025c; Cheng et al., 2025), how tokens of different entropy levels influence model optimization and generalization remains un-derexplored. These observations motivate us to decompose token reinforcement in GRPO into four quadrants based on two criteria: (1) Reward polarity (pos-itive / negative) : whether a token belongs to a positive or negative trajectory, determined by the outcome reward ri; (2) Token entropy (high / low) :whether the token entropy Hi,t is higher or lower than the average entropy of the group trajectories 

{H i,t }Gi=1 . Based on these criteria, we define four distinct quadrants for token reinforcement: • PHR : Positive High-entropy Reinforcement, • PLR : Positive Low-entropy Reinforcement, • NHR : Negative High-entropy Reinforcement, • NLR : Negative Low-entropy Reinforcement, as shown in Figure 1. This lets us isolate each quad-rant’s causal contribution via controlled training. To investigate the underlying learning dynam-ics of GRPO, we formulate four corresponding sub-objectives. Specifically, our experiments em-ploy Qwen2.5-Math-7B . We isolate the impact of each quadrant by restricting gradient updates exclu-sively to tokens within that specific quadrant. We primarily evaluate on the IMO-level benchmark AIME24. We compute Pass@ k and Avg@ k as our main metrics with k = 32 . Notably, Avg@ k

approximates greedy decoding accuracy, reflect-ing the model’s confidence in exploiting correct responses (Hochlehnert et al., 2025). In contrast, Pass@ k with a large k measures the model’s ability to generate diverse correct responses across mul-tiple attempts, capturing exploration ability (Yue et al., 2025). The detailed training and evaluation setup is provided in Appendix B and C.

Validation of Information-Theoretic Predictions. 

The quadrant-isolated experiments confirm all three predictions from Section 3: low-entropy quad-rants (PLR/NLR) yield limited and often short-lived gains, high-entropy quadrants (PHR/NHR) account for most sustained improvements, and the two high-entropy quadrants serve distinct roles— PHR drives exploration while NHR sharpens pre-cision. These results support the CMI view that token-level credit is concentrated at a minority of high-entropy positions, and that entropy and re-ward polarity must be considered jointly. These findings establish the behavioral signature of the token-level credit assignment problem. We next turn to the gradient level to explain the mechanism underlying this pattern.

5 From Analysis to Method: Entropy-Aware Policy Optimization 

Having established why and how in previous sec-tions, we now turn to what to do about it. The preceding analysis suggests a simple design prin-ciple: GRPO should assign larger-magnitude up-dates where token-level credit is both information-theoretically plausible and gradient-wise action-able. We therefore seek a minimal change to GRPO that preserves its optimization structure while correcting its uniform token credit. To this end, we propose Entropy-Aware Policy Optimiza-tion (EAPO) , which augments the advantage with a continuous entropy-derived credit score.

6.2 Main Results 

Tables 1 and 2 detail the reasoning performance of LLMs across various benchmarks. 

Performance gains concentrate on hard tasks. 

Section 3 suggests that entropy-aware credit is most impactful when reasoning chains involve numerous high-entropy decisions. Accordingly, EAPO achieves its most significant gains on de-manding benchmarks. On Qwen2.5-Math-7B , it reaches 39.8 Avg@ 32 on AIME24 (vs. DAPO 35.7, EntroAdv 36.8) and posts the highest ag-gregate score of 48.6%. This trend persists on DeepScaleR-1.5B-8k with longer reasoning traces (up to 24k tokens), where EAPO secures a 57.0% aggregate despite increased optimization dif-ficulty (Chang et al., 2025). It also maintains com-petitive performance on partially saturated bench-marks (AMC, MATH), where credit assignment is less of a bottleneck. 

Accuracy and diversity improve jointly. Our four-quadrant analysis (Section 4) reveals that high-entropy positive updates drive exploration, whereas high-entropy negative updates prune errors. Ta-bles 1 and 2 show simultaneous improvements in both Avg@ k and Pass@ k. In contrast, base-lines like DAPO often sacrifice Avg@ k to boost Pass@ k, indicating that biased credit assignment trades accuracy for diversity rather than enhancing both. 

EAPO outperforms heuristic alternatives. 

Forking uses a binary high-entropy mask, and EntroAdv adds an entropy bonus. While both surpass GRPO, their single-dimensional inter-ventions remain suboptimal. Visualizations in Appendix H confirm that EAPO applies continuous four-quadrant modulation, interpolating credit instead of relying on hard cutoffs. Furthermore, Appendix F.1 shows that EAPO maintains stable entropy throughout training, avoiding the entropy collapse typical of GRPO. Out-of-domain evaluation on MMLU-Pro aligns with these main results. As detailed in Appendix G, EAPO achieves the best overall accuracy (36.6%), demonstrating that the benefits of entropy-aware credit assignment extend beyond mathematical rea-soning.

7 Conclusion 

We study the token-level credit assignment problem in RLVR through a polarity-entropy lens. Adapt-ing CMI theory to the autoregressive LLM setting, we show that the credit any token carries about the final reward is upper-bounded by its entropy. The Four Quadrant Decomposition validates this bound empirically, revealing that reasoning gains concentrate in the high-entropy quadrants. More-over, a gradient-level analysis of GRPO provides a mechanistic account of this effect. These find-ings motivate EAPO, a minimal modification to GRPO that modulates token-level advantages by entropy, yielding consistent improvements on math-ematical reasoning benchmarks across two model families. We believe the polarity-entropy frame-work can serve as a diagnostic tool for credit as-signment in RLVR beyond the specific instantiation presented here. Limitations 

We identify three main limitations of our work. First, our method relies on outcome-based verifi-able rewards, which are readily available in do-mains like mathematics. While preliminary results on MMLU-Pro (Appendix G) suggest broader ap-plicability, applying EAPO to open-ended genera-tion tasks with subjective rewards remains a chal-lenge, as defining clear success criteria is inherently difficult. Second, EAPO introduces additional hy-perparameters, such as the scaling coefficient α

and the clipping range φ. While we found robust default values, these parameters may require tun-ing when scaling to significantly larger models or transferring to different domains, which we leave as future work. Third, our experiments focus on models up to 7B parameters. Verifying whether the information-theoretic characterization and the four-quadrant dynamics hold at larger scales remains an important direction.

A Related Works 

A.1 Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards 

Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) has emerged as a scalable and effective paradigm for enhancing the reasoning capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) (Fatemi et al., 2025; Zeng et al., 2025; Mroueh, 2025). Unlike Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017; Lee, 2025), which relies on preference models, RLVR utilizes rule-based outcome rewards, making it particularly suit-able for domains with objective correctness such as mathematics and coding. Recent successes, in-cluding DeepSeek-R1 (Guo et al., 2025) and Kimi 1.5 (Team et al., 2025), demonstrate that RLVR can incentivize long-chain reasoning and improve gen-eralization beyond supervised fine-tuning. How-ever, most RLVR approaches, such as GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) and its variants (Wang et al., 2025c; Chu et al., 2025b; Cui et al., 2025b), typically as-sign sequence-level rewards uniformly across all tokens. This uniform assignment overlooks the distinct roles tokens play in reasoning chains, ex-acerbating the credit assignment problem (Minsky, 1995). While recent studies have analyzed token patterns in reasoning (Wang et al., 2025c,a), the mechanisms for optimizing the trade-off between exploration and exploitation at the token level re-main underexplored. In this work, we focus on outcome-based RLVR and investigate the internal mechanics of how reward signals manifest at the token level. 

A.2 Analysis of Credit Assignment Problem 

The credit assignment problem—identifying which specific steps contribute to a final success or fail-ure—remains a fundamental bottleneck in training long-horizon reasoning LLMs (Uesato et al., 2022; Lightman et al., 2024). Traditional approaches of-ten employ process-based RL with step- or token-level supervision, such as Process Reward Mod-els (PRMs) (Cui et al., 2025a; Wang et al., 2024) and value estimation (Kazemnejad et al., 2025; Tran et al., 2025). However, these methods suf-fer from high annotation and training costs, as well as susceptibility to reward hacking (Wang et al., 2024; Lightman et al., 2024). Consequently, re-cent research has shifted towards refining credit assignment within outcome-based RLVR. One line of work dissects the disparate impacts of positive and negative samples (Chen et al., 2025b; Zhu et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025), with some arguing that reinforcing negative samples is surprisingly more effective for generalization (Zhu et al., 2025). Another direction differentiates token importance based on entropy (Wang et al., 2025c; Agarwal et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025b). However, these works yield conflicting views on whether high-entropy or low-entropy tokens should be prioritized for learning (Wang et al., 2025c; Agarwal et al., 2025). We reconcile these perspectives through a Four Quadrant Decomposition, offering a uni-fied framework to explain and optimize token-level credit assignment. A.3 Information-Theoretic Perspectives 

Beyond empirical analyses, a growing body of theo-retical work seeks principled foundations for credit assignment in RL. Arumugam et al. (2021) for-malized token-level credit as Conditional Mutual Information (CMI) between actions and returns, establishing that the policy entropy upper-bounds the information any single action can carry about the outcome. This result, originally developed for general MDPs, has direct implications for RLVR: since most tokens in a reasoning chain are near-deterministic (low entropy), their CMI with the binary reward is provably close to zero, regard-less of the sequence-level outcome. We build upon this insight by mapping the CMI framework to the RLVR setting (Section 3), deriving testable pre-dictions that our four-quadrant experiments subse-quently validate. Concurrently, Deng et al. (2025b) introduced the Token Hidden Reward framework, which learns an implicit per-token reward signal and found that reward-carrying tokens overlap with high-entropy positions by over 90%—an empirical confirmation of the CMI bound. Ren and Suther-land (2025) analyzed learning dynamics during fine-tuning and identified a “squeezing effect” at low-entropy positions, consistent with our gradient analysis showing that the factor (1 − πot ) vanishes as confidence increases. These complementary per-spectives converge on the same conclusion: effec-tive credit assignment in RLVR must be entropy-aware. 

A.4 The Entropy Interventions Approach in RLVR 

Policy entropy has long served as a regularizer to promote exploration in reinforcement learn-ing (Ziebart, 2010; Ziebart et al., 2008). In RLVR, entropy is increasingly used to shape credit as-signment, improving training efficiency and sta-bility. Prior work largely falls into two camps: (1) 

Exploration-centric methods , which amplify gra-dients at high-entropy branching points (e.g., Fork-ing (Wang et al., 2025c), EntroAdv (Cheng et al., 2025)); and (2) Stability-centric methods , which prioritize low-entropy tokens to ground knowledge and prevent model collapse (Wang et al., 2025b; Agarwal et al., 2025; Yang et al., 2025). Our work bridges these entropy interventions. While Wang et al. (2025c) reported the “80/20 rule” (a minor-ity of tokens drive reasoning), our Four Quadrant Decomposition explains why this holds: the CMI bound (Proposition 1) guarantees that credit con-centrates at high-entropy positions, so the “impor-tant 20%” are precisely those tokens whose en-tropy permits non-trivial information gain about the reward. EAPO does not reject this minority-token phenomenon; instead, it refines the hard cut-off into continuous credit allocation, strengthening signals at high-entropy forks while conservatively down-weighting low-entropy segments. This yields a more robust, entropy-aware credit assignment mechanism for reasoning tasks. 

A.5 Detailed Method Comparison 

Table 4 provides a systematic comparison of EAPO with concurrent and related entropy-based credit assignment methods. Among these methods, A3PO (Chu et al., 2025b) is the most related concurrent work, as it is also polarity-aware. However, the two approaches dif-fer in three key respects. First, A3PO differen-tiates tokens by their absolute probability (high-vs. low-probability), whereas EAPO uses group-normalized entropy, which is invariant to vocab-ulary size and captures distributional uncertainty rather than point-wise confidence. Second, A3PO applies adaptive time-decaying modulation that gradually reduces the effect of its token weighting during training, while EAPO maintains continu-ous dual-clipped modulation throughout, preserv-ing the entropy-aware signal at all stages. Third, EAPO is grounded in the CMI credit bound (Propo-sition 1), which provides a principled justification for why entropy—rather than probability—is the appropriate axis for credit allocation.

While exact computation of I(oi,t ; ri|st) requires the hindsight distribution πhs , we can estimate a proxy quantity from the training data. Specifically, we compute the mutual information between the token entropy value Hi,t and the binary reward ri:

The proxy therefore serves as a necessary-condition detector :it reliably identifies positions where credit cannot 

reside (low entropy), while flagging high-entropy positions as candidates for high credit. This proxy can be directly computed from the entropy distribu-tions of positive and negative samples (Figure 4). The entropy distribution in Figure 4 shows that in the low-entropy regime , the distributions for positive and negative tokens are nearly identical, implying H(Hi,t |ri) ≈ H(Hi,t ) and thus bI ≈ 0.In the high-entropy tail , a significant divergence emerges—negative samples exhibit higher density at high entropy—yielding bI ≫ 0. This empirically confirms the theoretical prediction: low-entropy tokens carry negligible information about the re-ward, while high-entropy tokens are the dominant carriers of credit signal.

Low-Entropy Quadrants (PLR & NLR). Low entropy implies a peaked distribution where the model is confident in its choice ( πot ≈ 1). D.3 Connections to Related Theoretical Frameworks 

The CMI framework developed above provides a unifying lens through which several recent empiri-cal findings can be explained. 

Negative Reinforcement Effectiveness. Zhu et al. (2025) demonstrated the “surprising effec-tiveness” of negative reinforcement in LLM rea-soning, showing that NHR alone often matches or exceeds full GRPO performance. The CMI framework offers a principled explanation: at high-entropy positions where Hi,t is large, the credit bound I(oi,t ; ri|st) ≤ Hi,t permits substantial learning signal. Negative feedback at these posi-tions (NHR) effectively prunes erroneous branches by redistributing probability mass away from in-correct tokens, as shown in the gradient analysis (Equation 21). Conversely, negative feedback at low-entropy positions (NLR) targets tokens with near-zero CMI, explaining why it introduces noise rather than useful signal. Recent analysis of nega-tive gradient dynamics (Deng et al., 2025a) further corroborates this view, showing that the gradient contribution of negative samples is concentrated on tokens where the policy is most uncertain— precisely the high-CMI regime identified by Propo-sition 1. 

Token Hidden Reward and Implicit Credit Es-timation. The Token Hidden Reward (THR) framework (Deng et al., 2025b) identifies “reward-carrying” tokens through an importance-based scor-ing mechanism. Remarkably, the set of tokens identified as high-THR overlaps with high-entropy tokens by over 90% (Deng et al., 2025b). Our CMI analysis explains this empirical coincidence: since the credit upper bound I(oi,t ; ri|st) ≤ H i,t , high-entropy positions are precisely those capable of carrying substantial reward information. THR’s learned scoring function implicitly discovers the CMI-relevant positions, while EAPO achieves a similar effect through a training-free entropy-based proxy—a computationally cheaper approximation that avoids the overhead of learning an auxiliary reward model. 

Learning Dynamics and Entropy Collapse. 

Studies on the learning dynamics of LLM fine-tuning (Ren and Sutherland, 2025) have identified a “squeezing effect” whereby PLR training progres-sively collapses the entropy of already-confident tokens, ultimately reducing the model’s exploration capacity. This phenomenon is a direct consequence of the gradient structure in the low-entropy regime: as πot → 1, the gradient factor (1 − πot ) → 0,creating a self-reinforcing trap where confident to-kens receive diminishing updates but never escape their peaked distribution. From the CMI perspec-tive, PLR operates in a regime where Hi,t ≈ 0, so the credit bound guarantees near-zero information gain regardless of the applied advantage magnitude. This explains why PLR exhibits stagnating perfor-mance in our four-quadrant experiments (Figure 2): optimization effort is wasted on tokens that cannot, by information-theoretic necessity, contribute to improved reasoning. H Credit Assignment Analysis 

In this section, we compare our credit assignment strategy with three strong baselines: DAPO (Yu et al., 2025), EntroAdv (Cheng et al., 2025), and Forking (Wang et al., 2025c). Figure 8 visualizes the token-level advantage distributions assigned by each method. Briefly, DAPO (and GRPO) adopts a group-based reward assignment that treats all tokens within a trajectory equally, disregarding their individual contribution or uncertainty. En-troAdv modifies the advantage function by adding a gradient-detached entropy term, explicitly en-couraging exploration at high-uncertainty decision points. Forking implements a hard selection mech-anism, restricting policy gradient updates to the top 20% of tokens with the highest entropy, under the assumption that these tokens drive reasoning capabilities. As shown in Figure 8, GRPO/DAPO assigns uniform advantages, failing to distinguish criti-cal decision points from routine tokens. Forking improves upon this by filtering out low-entropy tokens, yet its binary masking remains a coarse-grained approximation. EntroAdv introduces con-tinuous entropy-based shaping, but it primarily fo-cuses on exploration in positive samples and lacks a distinct mechanism for handling negative feed-back. In contrast, our method implements a con-tinuous, four-quadrant modulation.
