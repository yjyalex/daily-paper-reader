# AI Achieves a Perfect LSAT Score 

Bonmu Ku 

bku@alumni.harvard.edu 

## Abstract 

This paper reports the first documented instance of a language model achieving a perfect score on an officially disclosed Law School Admission Test (LSAT). Controlled experiments on eight reasoning models show that varying the prompt, shuffling answer choices, and sampling multiple responses have no meaningful effect as drivers of performance. Ablating the thinking phase that models generate before answering, however, lowers frontier accuracy by up to 8 percentage points, predominantly in logical reasoning. Distilled models produce full thinking traces in the same format yet plateau far below frontier performance. A pilot process reward model fine-tuned via QLoRA on official LSAT explanations narrows this gap through Best-of-5 selection, with gains again predominantly in logical reasoning. The gatekeeper of elite legal education since 1948, the LSAT has not merely been passed but answered without a single error by models that reason. The upper bound of the cognitive capacities it has tested is no longer exclusive to human cognition. 

## 1 Introduction 

1.1 The LSAT as a Benchmark for Logical Reasoning 

The Law School Admission Test (LSAT) stands as the most rigorous standardized examination of logical reasoning. The test requires no prior legal training. It instead requires exceptional reasoning capability to identify logical structures, evaluate arguments, and draw valid inferences from complex linguistic input. This exclusive measure of inferential skill makes the LSAT a particularly clean benchmark for evaluating the reasoning capabilities of language models as well. Even the strongest human performers fall below the perfect score of 180, with a median of 174 among Harvard Law School admits. Language models have been measured against the same standard. GPT-3.5 scored 149 at the 40th percentile, and GPT-4 scored 163 at the 88th percentile [ 1]. Each generation of language models narrows the gap to the human ceiling. A perfect score, however, demands the capacity for extended multi-step reasoning and deliberation, a level of performance that has thus far remained unmet for language models. 

1.2 From Language Models to Reasoning Models 

The leap from GPT-4 to the models that followed was not a continuation of prior scaling trends [ 2, 3]. It marked the emergence of a fundamentally new inference paradigm: the reasoning model [ 4, 5, 6]. The central capability was generating extended thinking traces at inference time before producing a final answer [ 7, 8, 9, 10 ]. This advance developed incrementally, through three phases that moved explicit reasoning progressively deeper into the model. 

Reasoning via Prompting 

Chain-of-thought prompting [ 11 ] dramatically improved performance on reasoning tasks by eliciting intermediate reasoning steps from the model. Zero-shot chain-of-thought [ 12 ] extended this result to the setting without few-shot exemplars, and self-consistency [ 13 ] further improved accuracy by

majority-voting over multiple sampled reasoning paths. Still, the model did not yet internalize the reasoning process. Intermediate steps were elicited externally and remained sensitive to prompt phrasing. 

Reasoning via Training 

STaR [ 14 ] bootstrapped reasoning ability by generating chain-of-thought rationales, fine-tuning on those that yielded correct answers, and iterating. Quiet-STaR [ 15 ] generalized this by training models to produce internal rationales at every token position during general text prediction. On the evaluation side, outcome reward models (ORMs) [ 16 ] scored candidate solutions by final-answer correctness, while process reward models (PRMs) [ 17 ] scored each reasoning step independently and provided a stronger selection signal. 

Reasoning via Inference 

Modern reasoning models are trained end-to-end via reinforcement learning to generate an internal chain of thought before producing a final answer [ 4, 5]. Inference-time compute scaling [ 6] matched the gains of scaling model parameters by an order of magnitude when compute was optimally allocated. Group relative policy optimization (GRPO) [ 18 ], originally introduced for mathematical reasoning, was extended to general reasoning [ 5], showing that RL with rule-based outcome rewards alone sufficed to elicit complex reasoning behaviors including backtracking, self-questioning, and exploration of alternative solution paths. Theoretical analysis [ 19 ] showed that chain-of-thought lets bounded-depth transformers solve problems they otherwise could not. A growing body of work on inference-time compute control, including budget forcing [ 20 ], learned length control [ 21 ], and pause tokens [ 22 ], further establishes extended thinking traces as the observable product of model deliberation. The separability of the thinking trace from the final answer opens a distinctive analytical opportunity. On the LSAT, where each question demands a structured chain of inference, the thinking trace is what reveals whether a model has reasoned its way to the answer, or merely arrived at it.

## 3 Datasets 

All analyses group questions into two categories, LR (Logical Reasoning) and RC (Reading Compre-hension). 

Test set. Each year, the Law School Admission Council (LSAC) publicly discloses one previously administered LSAT [ 31 ]. The most recent disclosure, the April 2025 administration, postdates the knowledge cutoff of every model in this study, providing a test set free of contamination risk. It comprises 77 questions (50 LR, 27 RC). Hereafter referred to as the Official Test .

Validation set. Ten consecutive tests (PrepTests 150–159) comprising 1,037 questions (633 LR, 404 RC). This larger dataset is used for statistical power. 

Training set. Two tests (PrepTests 140–141) comprising 209 questions (128 LR, 81 RC) with official LSAC per-choice explanations, further reviewed by a human expert who scored a perfect 180 on the LSAT. This dataset is used exclusively for process reward model training in Section 5.5. No overlap exists with the Official Test or the validation set. Throughout all experiments, questions are presented individually, one per API call, with no cross-question context even for Reading Comprehension questions sharing the same passage. 

## 4 Methodology 

Prior work has shown that language models can be sensitive to prompt phrasing, answer-position artifacts, and variation across repeated samples. Reasoning models, with their dedicated thinking phase, may behave differently. Sections 4.1–4.3 test whether these factors influence performance at either the frontier or small scale. Section 4.4 isolates the role of thinking. Section 4.5 explores whether thinking quality can be externally improved via a process reward model. 

4.1 Prompt Sensitivity 

Language model performance can vary substantially with prompt phrasing, a phenomenon docu-mented across reasoning benchmarks, where even minor wording changes shift accuracy by several percentage points [ 32 , 33 ]. To test whether reasoning models exhibit similar sensitivity, all eight models are evaluated under three conditions. 4Condition A (Minimal Prompting). No system prompt. User message: “Here is an LSAT [Logical Reasoning / Reading Comprehension] question. What is the answer?” 

followed by the question text and five answer choices. 

Condition B (Structured Prompting). System prompt assigns an expert persona (an LSAT instructor with a perfect 180) and prescribes a structured chain-of-thought methodology, identifying premises and conclusions, eliminating each wrong answer with explicit reasoning, and confirming the final selection. 

Condition C (Constrained Prompting). System prompt: “Respond with ONLY the letter of the correct answer (A, B, C, D, or E). Nothing else.” 

For Conditions A and B, answers are extracted via a lightweight parser that identifies the selected letter without influencing model output. Cochran’s Q test compares accuracy across the three conditions; TOST equivalence testing ( ±2pp margin) is applied to pairwise comparisons on PT 150–159. Full prompt templates are provided in Appendix B. 

4.2 Position Bias 

Language models have been shown to exhibit position bias in multiple-choice settings, preferring answers placed in certain positions, typically early options such as (A) or late options such as (E), independent of content [ 34 , 35 ]. To test whether reasoning models are susceptible to this artifact, shuffled variants of both datasets (fixed seed across models) permute answer choices. McNemar’s exact test compares paired accuracy between original and shuffled orderings; χ2 goodness-of-fit tests letter-selection distributions against uniform. TOST equivalence testing ( ±2pp) is applied at the section level. 

4.3 Self-Consistency 

Self-consistency [ 13 ] improves language model accuracy by sampling multiple reasoning paths and selecting the most common answer via majority vote, exploiting the assumption that correct reasoning is more likely to converge than incorrect reasoning. To test whether this strategy yields meaningful gains for reasoning models, or whether their errors are too deterministic for majority voting to correct, each model generates five independent responses per question, following the smallest sample size evaluated in [ 13 ]. Self-consistency accuracy (SC@5) is computed via majority vote and compared against pass@1 (the first generated response) using McNemar’s exact test. Unanimity, the proportion of questions receiving 5/5 identical answers, is reported as a measure of response determinism. 

4.4 Ablation Study: Thinking 

The preceding experiments vary the input and decoding strategy. This experiment targets the reasoning process itself. To isolate the contribution of the thinking phase, GPT-5 ( reasoning_effort: none ) and Claude Opus 4 ( thinking: disabled ) are evaluated with thinking turned off, treating the API toggle as a binary ablation of the reasoning component. These are the only two models whose APIs permit disabling thinking. Gemini 2.5 Pro does not expose a toggle, and the open-source models do not provide a non-thinking inference mode. McNemar’s exact test compares thinking-on vs. thinking-off accuracy. Cohen’s h quantifies effect sizes. Results are broken down by section (LR vs. RC) to test whether thinking differentially benefits logical inference. 

4.5 Pilot Study: Process Reward Modeling 

The four preceding experiments target prompting, answer ordering, sample diversity, and the presence of a thinking phase, all of which apply uniformly across frontier and small scales. What none of them probes directly is the rigor of the reasoning carried out within the trace itself, the property that distinguishes a model that has reasoned its way to the answer from one that has merely arrived at it. This pilot study tests whether such rigor can be externally identified and selected for, by training a process reward model [ 36 , 17 ] on official LSAT explanations and using its scores to perform Best-of-N selection at inference time. The base model is DeepSeek-R1 Distill 7B, the smallest model in the evaluation and the scale most amenable to fine-tuning on a single GPU. 5Training signal construction. The training signal must score the thinking trace itself rather than the final answer letter, since whether the trace reaches the right letter and whether the trace reasons its way there are independent properties. The official LSAT explanations for PT 140 and PT 141, reviewed by a human expert, provide the supervision, since they label each of the five answer choices as correct or incorrect and give the underlying logical reason for that label. Each thinking trace is then graded against these explanations choice by choice through a two-pass procedure. In the first pass, the judgment that the trace expresses about each answer choice is classified as correct, incorrect, or not addressed, and one point is awarded when this judgment agrees with the official label. Choices the trace fails to address receive zero points by construction. In the second pass, for every choice on which the trace agreed with the official judgment, the reasoning that the trace offers for that choice is compared against the official explanation, and one further point is awarded when the trace captures the same underlying logical reason rather than aligning with the official label only nominally. The per-trace score sums across the five answer choices on a 0 to 10 scale and is then normalized to the unit interval [0 , 1] , so that a score of 1.0 requires the correct judgment together with the matching reason on every one of the five answer choices. Empirically, this score averaged 0.89 on traces leading to a correct final answer and 0.47 on those leading to an incorrect one. 

Self-PRM training. The same base model is fine-tuned as its own PRM via QLoRA (4-bit quantiza-tion, LoRA r = 8 ), using all 209 training examples from PT 140 and PT 141. Given a question and a thinking trace, the model learns to predict the reasoning rigor score. This self-PRM design avoids the cross-model distribution mismatch that arises when a verifier trained on the reasoning patterns of one model is applied to another [37]. The full training configuration is provided in Appendix C. 

Inference. At test time, the generator produces five candidate responses per question. The PRM scores each thinking trace and selects the highest-scoring response (Best-of-5). Under the same compute budget of five samples per question, this is compared against pass@1 (the first generated response) and SC@5 (majority vote) on the held-out Official Test. 

4.6 Statistical Framework 

All statistical tests are two-tailed with α = 0 .05 . McNemar’s exact test is used for paired binary comparisons, Cochran’s Q for three-condition comparisons, TOST ( ±2pp margin) for equivalence, and Cohen’s h for effect sizes. TOST is applied on PT 150–159, where the sample size ( N = 1 ,037 )provides sufficient power for the ±2pp equivalence margin. On the Official Test ( N = 77 ), the minimum detectable equivalence margin exceeds ±2pp, so only significance tests are reported.

## 6 Discussion 

6.1 A Perfect Score 

In early 2023, GPT-3.5 scored 149 on the LSAT [ 1 ]. Later that year, GPT-4 scored 163 [ 1 ]. Less than two years later, in this study, GPT-5 misses only one of 77 questions on the Official Test, a score that still maps to a perfect 180 on the LSAT scoring curve [ 31 ], and Kimi K2 Thinking goes one further by answering every question correctly for an unambiguous 180. Five of the six frontier models in this study exceed 97% across over 1,000 questions. This trajectory is not driven by scaling alone. Disabling the thinking phase reduces frontier accuracy by 3–8pp (Section 4.4), establishing that the thinking phase itself contributes substantively to frontier performance. A perfect LSAT score is not simply the product of a larger model. It is the product of one that reasons. 10 6.2 Reasoning Rigor as the Differentiator 

Three factors widely documented to shift language model performance prove inert on the LSAT. Prompt phrasing leaves frontier accuracy unchanged across minimal, structured, and constrained conditions (Section 4.1). Shuffling answer choices reveals no position bias at the frontier (Sec-tion 4.2). Self-consistency recovers only a handful of errors for frontier models, whose mistakes are reproduced identically across samples (Section 4.3). The standard toolkit for moving language model performance, in short, has little purchase on reasoning models at the top of the LSAT. What does move frontier accuracy is the thinking phase itself. Disabling thinking in GPT-5 and Claude Opus 4 reduces accuracy by 3–8pp, with the drop concentrating in logical reasoning (Section 4.4). Thinking is therefore doing substantive work on the LSAT, not merely adding latency or formatting structure. But it cannot be the only variable that matters, because the two distilled small models in Table 2 already produce full thinking traces in the same <think>...</think> format and still score below 64%. The presence of a thinking phase is therefore necessary but not sufficient, and the quality of the reasoning the trace carries matters at least as much as whether a trace is produced at all. Section 5.5 independently shows that improving reasoning quality via PRM selection lifts small-model accuracy by +11.7pp, with gains again concentrated in logical reasoning. Because the two experiments operate at different scales and via different interventions, the convergence should be read as a consistent empirical pattern rather than a direct causal demonstration. 

6.3 Limitations and Future Directions 

This study evaluates models at a single point in time, and rapid model development may quickly alter the performance landscape. The thinking ablation was possible for only two models, GPT-5 and Claude Opus 4, due to API constraints, limiting the generalizability of that finding. The primary test set (the Official Test, N = 77 ) provides limited statistical power for per-section analyses. The validation set (PT 150–159) provides greater power but predates all knowledge cutoffs and may therefore have been seen during pretraining. If a model had memorized these questions, it would score higher on PT 150–159 than on the post-cutoff Official Test, which no model could have encountered. For all frontier models and DS-R1 Distill 7B, the gap between the two datasets is within ±2.5pp, consistent with sampling variability rather than memorization. DS-R1 Distill Llama 8B shows a larger gap (53.2% vs. 63.2%), but given its high item-level variance (53–70% across prompt conditions), this likely reflects measurement noise rather than contamination. Overall, the close agreement between a dataset the models could have seen and one they could not provides reasonable evidence that the results reported here reflect genuine reasoning rather than recall of memorized answers. Finally, the evaluation is restricted to the multiple-choice format of the LSAT, and performance on constructed-response legal reasoning tasks may differ substantially. The process supervision pilot itself is the most acknowledged of these limitations, having been conducted on a single model at a single scale with only 209 training examples. The resulting self-PRM improves DeepSeek-R1 Distill 7B from 51.9% to 63.6% on the held-out Official Test, a ceiling that remains far from frontier performance. Scaling the candidate pool (Best-of-N with larger N ), expanding the PRM training set through synthetic explanation generation, and applying process supervision during fine-tuning rather than only at inference time are all natural next steps within the small-model regime. An equally compelling direction is applying process supervision to frontier models themselves. The small-model experiments were constrained to the scale at which fine-tuning remains feasible on a single GPU. Frontier models, which already score 97–99%, make only a handful of errors, but those errors are deterministic and resistant to self-consistency. If a self-PRM can identify superior thinking traces among 7B-scale candidates, the same approach applied to frontier-scale candidates, where the base reasoning rigor is far higher, may push accuracy closer to the theoretical ceiling. 

## 7 Conclusion 

For the first time, a reasoning model has answered every question correctly on a test that the legal industry has spent three-quarters of a century using to identify its most capable minds. 11 Decades of correlation studies identify LSAT scores as the strongest single predictor of first-year law-school grades, a finding replicated as recently as the 2020–2024 admission cycles [ 38 , 39 ]. Independent analyses trace the same time-pressured analytic signal into the in-class examinations students sit years later in law school itself [ 40 ]. A perfect 180 on this test is therefore among the most rigorously validated markers of elite human analytic capacity. That a reasoning model now attains the same score, however, removes the LSAT from its long-standing position as a threshold only the human mind could cross. This does not diminish the reasoning skills the LSAT measures, nor does it render the exam irrelevant for admissions. But it does mean that the ceiling the LSAT was designed to measure, the upper bound of logical reasoning ability, is no longer exclusive to human cognition. For law schools and the legal profession, this should prompt a reckoning with what remains distinctively human about the capacity their gatekeeping exam was designed to measure, and with the possibility that this capacity has ceased to be the exclusive preserve of the human mind.
