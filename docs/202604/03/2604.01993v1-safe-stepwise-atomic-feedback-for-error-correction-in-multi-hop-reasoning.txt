## Abstract 

Multi-hop QA benchmarks frequently reward Large Language Models (LLMs) for spurious correctness, masking ungrounded or flawed rea-soning steps. To shift toward rigorous reason-ing, we propose SAFE , a dynamic benchmark-ing framework that replaces the ungrounded Chain-of-Thought (CoT) with a strictly ver-ifiable sequence of grounded entities. Our framework operates across two phases: (1) train-time verification, where we establish an atomic error taxonomy and a Knowledge Graph (KG)-grounded verification pipeline to elimi-nate noisy supervision in standard benchmarks, identifying up to 14% of instances as unan-swerable, and (2) inference-time verification, where a feedback model trained on this veri-fied dataset dynamically detects ungrounded steps in real-time. Experimental results demon-strate that SAFE not only exposes the critical flaws of existing benchmarks at train-time, but also significantly outperforms standard base-lines, achieving an average accuracy gain of 8.4 pp while guaranteeing verifiable trajecto-ries at inference-time. 1

## 1 Introduction 

The frontier of Large Language Models (LLMs) has shifted towards complex reasoning, such as multi-hop question answering (QA), requiring mod-els to synthesize evidence across multiple docu-ments to derive a final answer. While Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting has substantially im-proved performance on these tasks (Wei et al., 2022), their reasoning traces are hard to ground or verify (Turpin et al., 2023). For example, in-termediate steps may contain unverified entities, unsupported relations, or implicit shortcuts that nevertheless lead to the correct answer.

Our goal is to make reasoning traces verifiable and thus eliminate spurious reasoning, by shift-ing from free-form thought generation to rigorous traversal using Knowledge Graph (KG). Specifi-cally, we formalize the reasoning process as a veri-fiable sequence of grounded entities, subject to the following strict requirements: (1) each step must be atomic, corresponding to a single valid KG triple; (2) all entities must be explicitly anchored in the provided context; and (3) every relation must be independently explainable and verifiable. To rigorously enforce these grounding con-straints on both train-time and inference-time, we build a dynamic benchmarking framework. For train-time verification, we execute two critical offline steps. First, we apply the above strict constraints to verify standard multi-hop bench-marks (Ho et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2018; Trivedi et al., 2022), where we remove up to 14% of noisy instances. Second, we formalize an atomic error taxonomy to construct targeted supervision sig-nals for training an external feedback model. At inference-time, this model dynamically verifies each atomic step of the multi-hop reasoning pro-cess, detecting and correcting any deviation from a valid KG path on-the-fly. Compared with LLM self-correction which is inherently error-prone (Huang et al., 2023; Tyen et al., 2024), this inference-time verification ensures strictly grounded reason-ing trajectories. Collectively, these train-time and inference-time phases constitute SAFE , a dynamic benchmarking framework. Our main contributions are as follows: • We propose a train-time verification pipeline to filter noise from standard benchmarks, and define an error taxonomy to construct targeted training signals. • We develop an inference-time verification mech-anism using a trained feedback model to detect and rectify reasoning deviations in real-time. • We introduce SAFE , a novel dynamic benchmark-

## 2 Related Works 

2.1 Multi-hop QA Benchmarks and Spurious Reasoning 

Multi-hop QA benchmarks such as 2WikiMulti-hopQA (Ho et al., 2020), HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018), and MuSiQue (Trivedi et al., 2022) have been widely adopted for complex reasoning and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) evaluation (Jimenez Gutierrez et al., 2024; Trivedi et al., 2023). However, Ishii et al. (2024) report that a non-trivial portion of correct answers on standard multi-hop QA datasets are spurious. To reduce spurious reasoning, KG has been used before (Fang et al., 2024) and after gener-ation (Nguyen et al., 2024). The former extracts the KG chain before generation and the latter evalu-ates CoT reasoning by mapping the generated steps to a KG. Closest to our work is REVEAL (Jacovi et al., 2024), a benchmark that assigns correctness labels to individual reasoning steps based on a spe-cialized error taxonomy. Hop, Skip, and Overthink (Yadav et al., 2025) is another post-hoc evaluation, curating failure modes to localize breakdowns. 

Our distinction: Unlike these post-hoc ap-proaches that focus on error diagnosis after a full trajectory is produced, SAFE diagnoses such errors during generation at inference-time and prevents them at train-time. 

2.2 Inference-Time Reasoning Feedback 

LLMs can be prompted to critique and revise their own outputs (Madaan et al., 2023) or accumulate reflective feedback across trials (Shinn et al., 2023). Approaches like Self-Refine (Madaan et al., 2023) demonstrate the appeal of self-critique as a mecha-nism for improving output quality without requir-ing additional training or external models. However, in the context of complex multi-hop reasoning, pure self-correction is unverifiable and fundamentally limited, as LLMs often inherit the same hallucinations and biases that caused the orig-inal error. Consequently, models struggle to self-correct reasoning flaws without external ground-truth signals (Huang et al., 2023). This is further supported by Tyen et al. (2024), who identify mis-take finding as the primary bottleneck, showing that unguided models consistently fail to rectify their own logical missteps. 

Our distinction: SAFE enhances self-correction by introducing a dedicated, externally trained feed-back model. Grounded in a rigorously verified dataset and a comprehensive error taxonomy, our external evaluator validates each atomic reason-ing step to enforce a strictly verifiable sequence of grounded entities, providing more reliable supervi-sion towards grounded reasoning. 

## 3 Method 

SAFE is structured into two phases: train-time and inference-time verification. During the train-time, we formalize the error taxonomy (Section 3.1) and systematically verify standard benchmarks (Sec-tion 3.2). For the inference-time, we introduce a dedicated feedback model, trained to dynamically verify reasoning trajectories (Section 3.3). 

3.1 Taxonomy of Reasoning Errors 

We formally define a valid reasoning trajectory as a strictly verifiable sequence of grounded entities. Unlike conventional CoT, this sequence operates exclusively as a traversal over a localized Knowl-edge Graph ( G), extending derived entities through explicitly grounded triples ( ehead , r, e tail ). Every intermediate entity in the reasoning path serves as a verifiable anchor, effectively preventing logical hallucinations. Toward this verification goal, we introduce a KG-grounded error taxonomy. As Section 2 contrasted, REVEAL (Jacovi et al., 2024) is a similar post-hoc taxonomy without grounding, but we aim to pro-vide a grounded verification per each entity. More specifically: First, while REVEAL treats a reasoning step as a free-form sentence, we enforce strict atomic attribution, anchoring each step to exactly one ev-idence passage and one KG triple, which ensures strict grounding and enables precise step-level vali-dation. Second, whereas REVEAL’s post-hoc nature re-stricts it to static benchmarking, our taxonomy ac-tively supports both training supervision and dy-namic inference-time correction. Specifically, we assign each atomic step exclu-sively to one error type from four mutually exclu-sive categories that we define: Procedural, Attribu-tion, Logical, and Final Answer (Table 1). 3.2 KG-grounded Benchmark Verification 

Having formalized the atomic operations and error taxonomy required for strict grounding, we must ensure that the evaluation environment is also veri-fiable. When applying our defined KG constraints to standard large-scale benchmarks, we observe pervasive noise such as missing relations, ambigu-ous entity mappings, and incomplete supporting facts with spurious correlations. Such noise inher-ently hinders the formation of a valid sequence of grounded entities (see Appendix Figure 6 and Table 6). To systematically diagnose these defi-ciencies and guarantee that every evaluation in-stance possesses a grounded reasoning trajectory, we introduce a KG-grounded benchmark verifica-tion pipeline. Throughout this pipeline, we utilize gpt-oss-120b (Agarwal et al., 2025) as our base LLM, selected for its state-of-the-art instruction-following capabilities and robust zero-shot relation extraction performance among open-weight mod-els (exact prompts are detailed in Appendix A.9). The pipeline actively enforces our atomic ground-ing constraints through the following steps: 1. Triple Extraction: We prompt the LLM to ex-tract atomic triples ( ehead , r, e tail ) from the gold passages, conceptualizing the context as a local-ized knowledge graph. 2. By explicitly filtering out these ungrounded instances, we establish a verified, strictly grounded testing environment where the efficacy of our real-time feedback model can be measured solely on valid reasoning challenges. 

3.3 Inference-time Feedback Model Defining Step-Level Feedback. To enforce the strict grounding paradigm during inference, we train a dedicated step-level feedback model that verifies reasoning trajectories and produces correc-tive guidance when violations occur. Formally, we model feedback generation as a conditional predic-tion task over reasoning prefixes. Let P denote the passages provided as context, and let a reasoning trajectory τ be represented as a sequence of atomic steps: 

τ = ( s1, s 2, . . . , s t)

where each step si corresponds to a KG operation (e.g., entity extraction or relational traversal) that must be explicitly grounded in P. The feedback model F receives the question q, the passages P,and the current reasoning prefix s1: t to predict: 

(yt, d t, g t) = F (q, P, s 1: t)

where: • yt denotes the specific error type (e.g., Unsup-ported, Logical Fallacy) assigned from the taxon-omy defined in Section 3.1. • dt represents the natural language diagnosis, ex-plicitly grounding why the step violates KG con-straints based on P.• gt provides actionable guidance, directing the generator on how to correct the trajectory and construct a valid continuous path. This formulation transforms feedback generation into a rigorous verification problem, where the model learns to detect deviations from valid ground-ing. 

Building Training Data. Training feedback model requires both verified reasoning trajectories and diverse failure modes. Using the verified en-vironment (Section 3.2), we construct 3.9K ideal trajectories strictly grounded to the passages P. To provide robust negative supervision, we then syn-thesize 7.6K negative examples through taxonomy-guided error injection. Starting from these valid trajectories, a high-capacity teacher model (gpt-oss-120b) systematically perturbs individual reasoning steps by introducing violations aligned with our error taxonomy. This yields corrupted trajectories where the precise error location and type are known, enabling explicit step-level supervision. While synthetic perturbations provide controlled coverage of error types, they may not fully capture the inherent path-finding failures and subtle mis-grounding behaviors specific to the target model during actual inference. To bridge this distribution gap, we adopt an iterative refinement strategy: we first train an initial feedback model on the synthetic dataset, then conduct targeted failure analysis to identify recurring weaknesses (e.g., confusing lexi-cally similar entities or failing at complex numeri-cal comparisons). For each identified weakness, we leverage the teacher model to generate corrected step-level feedback labels, and reintegrate these hard cases into the training pool. Through this cycle of train → diagnose → augment , 848 hard cases are added to the training pool. This progres-sively closes the distribution gap between synthetic supervision and real inference-time failures, ulti-mately yielding a final dataset of 11.5K instances and a verifier robust to diverse and subtle KG de-viations. We empirically validate the effectiveness of this refinement strategy in Section 5, where it consistently improves performance over training with synthetic data alone. The overall error statistics of this complete train-ing set are illustrated in Appendix Figure 7. Addi-tional details, including the positional distribution of error steps and dataset statistics, are provided in Appendix A.5. 

## 4 Experiments 

4.1 Experimental Setup Datasets. We evaluate our approach on three multi-hop QA benchmarks: 2WikiMul-tihopQA (Ho et al., 2020), HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018), and MuSiQue (Trivedi et al., 2022). To ensure rigorous evaluation, we apply our KG-grounded verification pipeline to the validation splits, extracting a sound pool from which we randomly sample 1,000 instances per benchmark (3,000 in total). Each question is evaluated in a constrained distractor setting provided with exactly 10 passages (including gold evidence). More details of data processing are provided in Appendix A.4. 

Evaluation Metric. Standard string-matching metrics, such as Exact Match (EM) and F1 scores, often underestimate model performance by failing to recognize semantic equivalence in surface-form variations or descriptive phrases. To ensure a more faithful assessment of the final output, we adopt an LLM-as-a-judge approach (Li et al., 2025) using gpt-oss-120b to evaluate the semantic equivalence of the generated answers against the ground truth.

## 6 Conclusion 

In this paper, we introduced SAFE , a dynamic benchmarking framework that shifts multi-hop rea-soning evaluation from static answer-matching to strictly verifiable grounding. During train-time, we formalized a rigorous error taxonomy and filtered ungrounded noise from standard benchmarks, pro-viding a verified foundation for our feedback model. Comprehensive experiments demonstrate that our inference-time verification yields substantial per-formance gains while fundamentally enhancing the faithfulness of reasoning trajectories. Ultimately, our work proves that strict step-by-step grounding is significantly more efficient and reliable than un-grounded self-correction, setting a new standard for trustworthy and explainable AI evaluation. 7 Limitations 

While we utilized a Knowledge Graph (KG) to ensure the quality of our verification pipeline, we acknowledge the potential for mislabeling due to inherent noise or incomplete coverage in the KG. To address this, rather than aggressively excluding instances that might contain valuable reasoning trajectories, we provide the original dataset with our feedback labels as an augmented resource. This non-destructive approach allows future researchers to audit, refine, or leverage the full context of the data while accounting for potential label noise in automated diagnostics. Furthermore, the efficacy of the SAFE frame-work inherently relies on the reasoning generator’s instruction-following capabilities. We observed instances where, despite the feedback model suc-cessfully producing accurate diagnoses and ex-plicit corrective guidance, the reasoning genera-tor failed to effectively incorporate these instruc-tions into its subsequent generation step. Such instruction-following failures can lead to uncor-rected errors or repeated mistakes within the iter-ative loop. This highlights a dependency on the base language model’s capacity to adhere to com-plex, stepwise constraints, suggesting that future work must address the alignment between correc-tive feedback and the generator’s execution. Finally, our current experimental setting operates under the assumption that all necessary gold pas-sages are present within the provided context. In practical open-domain multi-hop scenarios, initial retrievals may omit crucial evidence, necessitat-ing dynamic or iterative retrieval steps. The pri-mary scope of this study, however, is deliberately focused on isolating and evaluating the model’s rea-soning capabilities—specifically, how accurately it can synthesize and navigate multi-hop logic given that the required information is already available. Extending the SAFE framework to actively trig-ger external information retrieval when the feed-back model diagnoses a missing premise remains a promising direction for future work. 

## Ethics Statement 

This work introduces SAFE , a framework designed to enhance the reliability of large language mod-els (LLMs) in multi-hop reasoning tasks through stepwise atomic feedback and error-correction. We acknowledge several ethical considerations and po-tential broader impacts associated with its develop-ment and deployment. 

Transparency and Interpretability A persistent ethical challenge with LLMs is their "black-box" nature, which obscures the rationale behind gener-ated answers. SAFE directly addresses this opacity by decomposing the complex multi-hop reasoning process into a fine-grained, stepwise atomic frame-work. This design transforms an otherwise opaque generation process into an inherently explainable system. By isolating each reasoning step and its corresponding feedback, researchers and practition-ers can rigorously analyze the exact distribution of reasoning failures. This granular transparency not only facilitates precise, targeted error-correction but also promotes essential trust, accountability, and auditing capabilities in AI decision-making. 

Computational Efficiency and Environmental Impact Iterative reasoning and error-correction frameworks traditionally incur significant compu-tational overhead due to repeated generation steps. However, SAFE explicitly mitigates this issue by employing efficient KV caching mechanisms, ren-dering the additional inference-time cost negligible compared to standard single-pass generation. By achieving robust multi-hop reasoning without heav-ily inflating computational demands, our approach aligns with the growing ethical need for sustainable and energy-efficient AI. 

Bias and Fairness The performance and behav-ior of SAFE inherently depend on the underlying pre-trained LLMs and the external knowledge cor-pora (e.g., retrieved Wikipedia passages) used for reasoning. These base models and datasets may contain historical, cultural, or demographic biases. While our error-correction taxonomy meticulously targets procedural, logical, and attribution errors, it is not explicitly designed to detect or mitigate social biases. Users must remain cautious, as the system might inadvertently reproduce or amplify existing biases present in the source material.
