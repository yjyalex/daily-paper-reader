## Abstract 

We introduce Options LLM (OLLM), a simple, general method that replaces the single next-token prediction of standard LLMs with a set of learned options for the next token, indexed by a discrete latent variable. Instead of relying on temperature or sampling heuristics to induce diversity, OLLM models variation explicitly: a small latent space parametrizes multiple plausible next-token options which can be selected or searched by a downstream policy. Architecturally, OLLM is a lightweight "plug-in" that inserts two layers: an encoder and a decoder, before the output head, allowing almost any pretrained LLM to be converted with mini-mal additional parameters. We apply OLLM to a 1.7B-parameter backbone (only 

1.56% of parameters trainable) trained on OpenMathReasoning and evaluated on OmniMath. The SOTA LoRA-adapted baselines peak at 51% final answer correctness, while OLLM’s option set allows up to ∼ 70% under optimal latent selection. We then train a compact policy in the latent space that emits latents to control generation. Operating in a low-dimensional option space makes reward optimization far more sample-efficient and substantially reduces common mis-alignments (e.g., language switching or degenerate reasoning), as the policy is constrained to options learned during SFT. Crucially, this alignment arises from model structure rather than additional KL or handcrafted alignment losses. Our results demonstrate that optionized next-token modeling enhances controllability, robustness, and efficiency in math reasoning, and highlight latent-space policy learning as a promising direction for reinforcement learning in LLMs. 

## 1 Introduction 

Traditional LLMs model a single next-token distribution and rely on sampling heuristics (temperature, top-k/p) to produce diversity. In contrast, we propose Options LLM (OLLM), which learns a small latent option space that indexes multiple plausible next-token alternatives at each generation step. By making variation explicit in a compact latent manifold, OLLM enables a downstream policy to act in a low-dimensional space for controlled generation—rather than over the full vocabulary—improving sample efficiency and constraining exploration to behaviors seen during supervised fine-tuning (SFT). OLLM architecture is simple and involves adding two modules before the last layer (lm_head): encoder and decoder. With this small change, any existing LLM can be converted into an OLLM. Prior work has used compact latent spaces for attribute control and high-quality sampling [ 5 , 1 , 6 ] and studied how RLHF can induce capability drift and reward-hacking [ 9]; OLLM differs by (i) exposing per-token options, (ii) integrating a light encoder/decoder plug-in into the pretrained backbone, and

Using the latent space for controlling generations causes the output to be bound to the variations learned during SFT.

2.2 Architecture 

Let x1: t denote the observed prefix or context and yt+1 the ground-truth next token. A pretrained autoregressive model with parameters θ encodes the prefix into a hidden state ht ∈ Rd, which is normally mapped to logits ℓt = lm _head( ht) over the vocabulary V . To introduce options, we augment the model with two lightweight modules: an encoder Eψ and a decoder Dω .During supervised training, the target token yt+1 is first mapped through the model’s input embedding layer e(·) to obtain a compact representation e(yt+1 ) ∈ Rd. The encoder then conditions jointly on this embedding and the contextual hidden state ht to produce a latent distribution (Eq. 1), 

p(z) = Eψ (ht, e (yt+1 )) ∈ Z , (1) where Z is a small discrete latent space of options. This avoids scaling the encoder with the vocabulary size while still allowing it to condition on the identity of the target token. And conditioning on both the token and its context prevents the encoder from collapsing into a static partition of the vocabulary, and instead allows it to learn context-sensitive latent assignments (e.g., the same target token may correspond to different options depending on the reasoning trajectory). ## 3 Results 

We use the OpenMathReasoning dataset [ 11 ] and evaluate using the Omnimath dataset [ 3], which contains math problems of comparable difficulty. The latent space is set to K = 10 , allowing the model to represent up to ten token-level options at each step. Figure 4 reports evaluation curves. Baseline LoRA-adapted models achieve per-token accuracy of 

∼ 74% and final-answer correctness of ∼ 51% , but plateau early due to overfitting. In contrast, OLLM continues improving throughout training. Under optimal latent selection, OLLM reaches 

∼ 83% per-token accuracy and ∼ 70% final-answer correctness. This gain arises because token alternatives do not compete within a single probability simplex: separating them into latent options allows the model to preserve multiple plausible continuations without overfitting to a dominant mode. Consequently, even simple policies that operate in the low-dimensional latent space can substantially improve final-answer accuracy. We also provide qualitative examples of OLLM generations in Section C. These illustrate that OLLM learns genuinely diverse options when multiple continuations are plausible, but collapses to a consistent choice in deterministic cases (e.g., fixed symbol sequences). Notably, different options can also drive reasoning in distinct logical directions, underscoring the benefits of interpretability and controllability in the latent space. 

## 4 Discussion & Future Work 

Our results show that introducing latent options into LLMs improves both token-level accuracy and final-answer correctness in mathematical reasoning. A key advantage is that the latent space provides a compact and well-grounded action space. Unlike traditional RL fine-tuning, which operates over the entire vocabulary and risks issues such as language switching or spurious reasoning, OLLM constrains exploration to options learned during supervised training. This removes the need for explicit KL penalties, reduces variance in policy learning, and ensures that exploration remains within valid continuations. 4Looking ahead, we plan to train policies in this latent space using reinforcement learning, evaluating whether the theoretical efficiency gains translate into further improvements. The option structure also offers interpretability, as different latents can correspond to distinct reasoning trajectories, enabling controllable exploration of alternative solution paths. Extending OLLM to domains such as code generation, scientific QA, and multi-step planning represents a promising direction for future work. 5References

A growing body of research has explored the use of latent spaces to enhance control, efficiency, and interpretability in large language models (LLMs). One common direction leverages latent probability transformations for controllable text generation. PriorControl [ 5] and follow-up work on probability density estimation with normalizing flows [ 2] transform the complex, high-dimensional latent spaces of LLMs into structured Gaussian priors, enabling fine-grained single- and multi-attribute control. Similarly, compositional control via latent ODEs [ 10 ] shows that low-dimensional latent vectors permit efficient manipulation and composition of multiple operators (e.g., sentiment, tense, formality). A second line of work investigates reinforcement learning and critic-driven methods in the latent or token distribution space. CriticControl [ 7 ] combines actor-critic RL with weighted decoding to manipulate token probabilities without modifying base model parameters. More recently, the EAGLE framework [ 12 ] treats a pre-trained LLM as an environment and learns policies in the latent embedding space, demonstrating improved sample efficiency and alignment with domain-specific objectives. Other approaches seek lightweight adaptation mechanisms for controllable generation. Prefix-tuning [ 8 ] and related parameter-efficient tuning methods optimize small continuous prompts or adapters in latent space, offering strong performance while preserving the base model. Recent multi-aspect control methods [ 4] extend this by learning distributional intersections of attributes, reducing interference between controllers. Finally, recent work has investigated interpretability and causal structure in latent representations. JAM (Just A Move) [ 6] frames text generation control as cause-effect reasoning in latent space, highlighting latent vectors as fundamental control units. These approaches complement ongoing efforts to better understand and guide the internal option-like structures of LLMs. Our work is closest in spirit to these latent-space control frameworks but differs in its explicit formulation of options as discrete latent variables aligned with next-token distributions. This design directly reduces competition among plausible tokens, enabling both improved reasoning accuracy and a natural foundation for downstream reinforcement learning in a compact action space. 70 1 2 3 4 5 6 7 8
